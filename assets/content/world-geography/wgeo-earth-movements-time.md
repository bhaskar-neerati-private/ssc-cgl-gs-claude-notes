# Earth's Movements, Coordinates & Time

On the night of September 2, 1752, British citizens went to sleep and woke up on September 14. Overnight, eleven days had vanished — the British Empire had finally adopted the Gregorian calendar (already used in most of Europe since 1582), and the accumulated error of the old Julian calendar had to be corrected. People rioted in the streets, shouting "Give us back our eleven days!" The story is a reminder that how humans measure time is deeply tied to Earth's movements in space — and that those movements produce consequences as dramatic as vanishing days.

---

## Earth's Movements

### 1. Rotation (Earth spinning on its own axis)

- **Direction:** West to East (anti-clockwise when viewed from North Pole)
- **Duration:** 24 hours (solar day); 23 hours 56 minutes 4 seconds (sidereal day — relative to distant stars)
- **Speed at Equator:** ~1,670 km/h (decreases toward poles, zero at poles)
- **Axis tilt:** 23.5° from perpendicular (66.5° from orbital plane)

**Effects of Rotation:**
- Day and night cycle
- Apparent movement of Sun, Moon and stars from east to west
- Deflection of winds and ocean currents (**Coriolis Effect** — right in NH, left in SH)
- Tidal bulges follow the Moon
- Earth's equatorial bulge (centrifugal effect)

### 2. Revolution (Earth orbiting the Sun)

- **Duration:** 365 days 5 hours 48 minutes 46 seconds (~365.25 days) → hence leap year every 4 years
- **Path:** Elliptical orbit (**Kepler's First Law**)
- **Speed:** ~107,000 km/h (faster when closer to Sun — Kepler's Second Law: equal areas in equal time)
- **Perihelion:** Earth closest to Sun (~147 million km) — around **January 3** (northern hemisphere winter!)
- **Aphelion:** Earth farthest from Sun (~152 million km) — around **July 4** (northern hemisphere summer!)
- **Note:** Seasons are caused by axial tilt, NOT distance from Sun

## Seasons

| Season (NH) | Sun's Declination | Special Day |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Summer | Sun overhead at Tropic of Cancer (23.5°N) | **Summer Solstice — June 21** (longest day NH) |
| Autumn | Sun moves toward equator | **Autumnal Equinox — September 23** (equal day/night) |
| Winter | Sun overhead at Tropic of Capricorn (23.5°S) | **Winter Solstice — December 22** (shortest day NH) |
| Spring | Sun moves toward Tropic of Cancer | **Vernal (Spring) Equinox — March 21** (equal day/night) |

- Seasons in Southern Hemisphere are **opposite** to Northern Hemisphere
- At equinoxes (March 21, September 23): Sun is overhead at Equator; all places on Earth have ~12 hours day and 12 hours night

## Important Latitudes (Parallels)

| Latitude | Name | Significance |
|----------|------|--------------|
| 0° | Equator | Longest circle; divides Earth into NH and SH |
| 23.5°N | Tropic of Cancer | Sun overhead at June Solstice |
| 23.5°S | Tropic of Capricorn | Sun overhead at December Solstice |
| 66.5°N | Arctic Circle | 24-hour daylight at June solstice; 24-hour darkness at December solstice |
| 66.5°S | Antarctic Circle | 24-hour daylight at December solstice; 24-hour darkness at June solstice |
| 90°N | North Pole | — |
| 90°S | South Pole | — |

- **Torrid Zone:** Between Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn — highest solar energy
- **Temperate Zones:** Between 23.5° and 66.5° (both hemispheres)
- **Frigid Zones:** Above 66.5° (both hemispheres) — polar regions

## Longitude, Time and the Prime Meridian

- **Meridians:** Lines of longitude running pole to pole
- **Prime Meridian (0°):** Passes through **Greenwich, London** (established 1884 at International Meridian Conference)
- Earth has 360° of longitude; rotates 360° in 24 hours → **1° = 4 minutes**
- **15° = 1 hour difference** in local solar time
- East of Prime Meridian = ahead in time; West = behind

### International Date Line (IDL)
- Roughly follows 180° meridian (with deviations to keep island groups on same date)
- Crossing east (→ west to east) → **date decreases by 1 day** (gain a day going east)
- Actually: crossing from east to west, you **add 1 day**; crossing west to east, you **subtract 1 day**
- Zigzags around Fiji, Samoa, Kiribati to keep nations on convenient dates

### Indian Standard Time (IST)
- **82.5°E longitude** — passes through **Mirzapur/Allahabad, UP**
- **UTC + 5:30** (5 hours 30 minutes ahead of Greenwich)
- India has a single time zone despite wide longitudinal spread (~68°E to ~97°E)

## Time Zones of World

- World is divided into **24 standard time zones** (15° each)
- **Coordinated Universal Time (UTC):** Successor to GMT; based on atomic clocks
- Some countries have fractional time zones: India (+5:30), Nepal (+5:45), Iran (+3:30), Australia various
- **Greenwich Mean Time (GMT):** Mean solar time at Greenwich; precursor of UTC

---

## Grid Reference — Latitude and Longitude

- **Latitude:** Angle between a point and the equatorial plane (measured N or S, 0°–90°)
  - Parallels of latitude are **parallel circles**, getting smaller toward poles
- **Longitude:** Angle east or west of Prime Meridian (measured E or W, 0°–180°)
  - Meridians of longitude **converge at poles**
- **Location of a point** = Latitude first, then Longitude (e.g., Delhi ≈ 28.6°N, 77.2°E)
- **1° of latitude ≈ 111 km** (fairly constant)
- **1° of longitude varies** — 111 km at equator, 0 km at poles

## Leap Year Rules

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Divisible by 4 | Leap year (e.g., 2024) |
| Divisible by 100 | NOT a leap year (e.g., 1900) |
| Divisible by 400 | IS a leap year (e.g., 2000) |
| Reason | Corrects for Earth's 365.25-day orbit (Gregorian calendar system) |

---

## Coriolis Effect

- Caused by Earth's **rotation**
- In the **Northern Hemisphere:** winds and currents deflect to the **right**
- In the **Southern Hemisphere:** winds and currents deflect to the **left**
- Named after French mathematician Gaspard-Gustave de Coriolis (1835)
- Responsible for: cyclone rotation direction, trade wind deflection, ocean gyre direction

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Perihelion (Earth closest to Sun) is in January**, not summer — seasons are due to axial tilt, NOT distance.
2. **Summer Solstice = June 21** (longest day in NH); December 22 = Winter Solstice (shortest day NH).
3. **Vernal Equinox = March 21**; Autumnal Equinox = September 23 — equal day/night worldwide.
4. **IST is 82.5°E**, passes through Mirzapur — NOT 80°E or Allahabad city centre precisely.
5. **15° longitude = 1 hour time difference** (Earth rotates 15° per hour).
6. **IDL deviation:** The IDL does NOT exactly follow 180° — it zigzags to keep island nations together.
7. **Crossing IDL westward → add 1 day**; crossing eastward → subtract 1 day.
8. **Earth's rotation is west to east (eastward)** — Sun appears to move east to west because of this.
9. **1° latitude ≈ 111 km** but 1° longitude varies from 111 km (equator) to 0 km (poles).
10. **Leap year exception:** 1900 was NOT a leap year (divisible by 100 but not 400); 2000 WAS a leap year (divisible by 400).
11. **Sidereal day = 23 hr 56 min**, solar day = 24 hr — SSC sometimes asks which is shorter.
12. **Coriolis deflects right in NH, left in SH** — cyclones rotate anti-clockwise in NH, clockwise in SH.
13. **Equatorial rotation speed is ~1,670 km/h** — this is NOT the orbital speed around Sun (~107,000 km/h).
14. **Arctic Circle = 66.5°N** (not 65° or 67°); at this latitude, 24-hour daylight occurs on June 21.
15. **Southern Hemisphere seasons are opposite** — December is summer in Australia.
16. **GMT vs UTC:** GMT is a time zone; UTC is the global time standard — they differ by tiny fractions of a second but are treated as equivalent in most contexts.
