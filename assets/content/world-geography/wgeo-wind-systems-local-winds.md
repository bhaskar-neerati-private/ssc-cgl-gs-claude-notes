# Wind Systems & Local Winds

In the 15th century, Portuguese and Spanish explorers were baffled by how to reach Asia. They tried sailing south along Africa's coast, fighting headwinds all the way. Then Bartolomeu Dias rounded the Cape of Good Hope in 1488 and Vasco da Gama reached India in 1498 — not by fighting the wind, but by reading it. They swung far west into the Atlantic to catch the **Southeast Trade Winds**, which carried them south, then caught the **Westerlies** to blow them east around the Cape, then rode the Indian Ocean monsoon winds to Calicut. The wind systems of the world unlocked the Age of Exploration — and they have been a mandatory chapter in every geography curriculum since.

---

## What Drives Winds?

- **Primary cause:** Differences in air pressure
- Air moves from **high pressure → low pressure**
- **Coriolis Effect** deflects winds: right in Northern Hemisphere, left in Southern Hemisphere
- Wind speed: measured by **anemometer**
- Wind direction: measured by **wind vane** (direction = where wind comes FROM)
- **Beaufort Scale:** Measures wind speed in force numbers (0 = calm; 12 = hurricane)

## Pressure Belts and Wind Zones

Earth has a systematic pattern of pressure belts that drive the main (planetary/permanent) winds:

| Latitude | Pressure Belt | Wind Blowing Toward | Name |
|----------|--------------|---------------------|------|
| 0° (Equator) | Equatorial Low | — | Doldrums (calm zone; no wind) |
| 0°→30°N | — | Equator from NE | NE Trade Winds (North Hemisphere) |
| 0°→30°S | — | Equator from SE | SE Trade Winds (South Hemisphere) |
| 30° (Sub-tropical) | Sub-tropical High | — | Horse Latitudes (calm, dry; descending air) |
| 30°→60° (NH) | — | Toward 60°N from SW | SW Westerlies |
| 30°→60° (SH) | — | Toward 60°S from NW | NW Westerlies |
| 60° (Sub-polar) | Sub-polar Low | — | Convergence zone (Polar Front) |
| 60°→90° (NH) | — | From NE (polar) | NE Polar Easterlies |
| 60°→90° (SH) | — | From SE (polar) | SE Polar Easterlies |
| 90° | Polar High | — | — |

## Permanent (Planetary) Wind Belts in Detail

### Trade Winds
- Blow from sub-tropical highs (~30°N/S) toward the equatorial low (0°)
- **NE Trade Winds** in Northern Hemisphere
- **SE Trade Winds** in Southern Hemisphere
- Reliable and steady — used by ancient sailors (hence "trade" = regular passage)
- Converge at the **ITCZ (Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone):** Rising air, thunderstorms, rain
- ITCZ shifts north in June (Indian summer monsoon) and south in December (NE monsoon)

### Westerlies
- Blow from sub-tropical highs (~30°N/S) toward sub-polar lows (~60°N/S)
- **SW Westerlies** in Northern Hemisphere
- **NW Westerlies** in Southern Hemisphere (where they are strongest — "Roaring Forties," "Furious Fifties," "Screaming Sixties" in the Southern Ocean — 40°S, 50°S, 60°S)
- Bring year-round rainfall to west coasts of continents in temperate zones
- Drive depressions (low pressure systems) across North Atlantic and North Pacific

### Polar Easterlies
- Cold, dry air flowing from polar highs toward sub-polar lows
- **NE Polar Easterlies** in Northern Hemisphere
- **SE Polar Easterlies** in Southern Hemisphere
- Least regular of the three planetary wind systems

## Jet Streams

- Narrow bands of high-altitude winds (9–16 km altitude)
- Speed: 100–400 km/h
- Follow a wavy path around the globe; meander north and south

| Type | Latitude | Significance |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Sub-tropical Jet Stream** | ~30°N/S | Associated with sub-tropical highs |
| **Polar Jet Stream** | ~60°N/S (variable) | Drives surface weather; Indian monsoon; strong westerly wind |

- **Indian Monsoon Link:** In June, the Tibetan Plateau heats up and pushes the polar jet stream northward, removing the "lid" that kept the moist SW winds at bay — triggering the monsoon burst
- Jet streams are also used by aircraft to save fuel (flying with jet stream = shorter flight times)

## Monsoon Winds

| Season | Wind Direction | Effect on India |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Summer (June–September)** | SW Monsoon — from Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal toward land | Heavy rainfall; June onset in Kerala |
| **Winter (October–January)** | NE Monsoon — from Coromandel Coast toward sea | Rain on SE coast of India; Tamil Nadu |

- SW Monsoon reaches Kerala by June 1 (conventionally)
- **Arabian Sea branch:** Hits Western Ghats → heavy rain on windward side (Konkan, Goa, Kerala); rain shadow on leeward (Deccan)
- **Bay of Bengal branch:** Moves north → Assam, NE India (highest rainfall globally — Mawsynram), then west along Gangetic Plain
- **NE Monsoon (Retreating Monsoon):** October–November; rain for Tamil Nadu and Andhra (east coast)

## Local/Regional Winds

These are winds specific to particular regions and are heavily tested in SSC.

### Hot Local Winds

| Name | Origin/Type | Region | Effect |
|------|-------------|--------|--------|
| **Loo** | Hot, dry | North India (Punjab, Haryana, UP, Rajasthan) in summer | Hot, dusty; can be fatal; blows May–June |
| **Sirocco** | Hot, dry, dust-laden | From Sahara northward to Mediterranean (Italy, Spain) | Brings Saharan dust; can carry red/yellow rain |
| **Harmattan** | Hot, dry, dusty | From Sahara to West Africa (Ghana coast) | Called "Doctor" — dries humidity; relief from tropical heat |
| **Khamsin** | Hot, dry | Egypt/Sudan (North Africa) | Blows 50 days (Arabic "fifty"); dust storms |
| **Simoom** | Hot, dry, violent | Arabian Peninsula and Sahara | "Poison wind"; intensely hot |
| **Santa Ana** | Hot, dry | California, USA | Funnelled through mountain passes; fire risk |
| **Zonda** | Hot, dry | Argentina (east of Andes) | Foehn-type; Andean rain shadow |

### Cold Local Winds

| Name | Origin/Type | Region | Effect |
|------|-------------|--------|--------|
| **Mistral** | Cold, dry | Alps → Rhône Valley → Mediterranean (France) | Fierce; can damage crops |
| **Bora** | Cold, dry | Dinaric Alps → Adriatic coast (Croatia, Italy) | Violent; can exceed 170 km/h |
| **Tramontane** | Cold, dry | Pyrenees/Alps → Mediterranean (Spain/France) | |
| **Pampero** | Cold | Pampas, South America (Argentina/Uruguay) | Sudden temperature drops |
| **Blizzard** | Cold, snow-laden | High latitudes (North America, Russia) | Dangerous reduced visibility |
| **Norther** | Cold | Texas/Gulf Coast, USA | Sudden cold blast |

### Foehn-Type Winds (Warm on Leeward Side)

| Name | Mountain Range | Region | Notes |
|------|---------------|--------|-------|
| **Foehn** | Alps | Northern slopes → southern Europe / vice versa | Original foehn; air warms as it descends (adiabatic warming) |
| **Chinook** | Rockies | Eastern slopes → Great Plains (Canada/USA) | "Snow eater"; rapidly melts snow; farming benefit |
| **Berg Wind** | Drakensberg | South Africa | Hot descending; fire risk |
| **Canterbury Northwester** | Southern Alps | New Zealand | |

**How Foehn Works:**
1. Moist air rises on windward side → cools at moist adiabatic lapse rate (6°C/1,000 m) → loses moisture as rainfall
2. Air descends on leeward side → warms at dry adiabatic lapse rate (10°C/1,000 m) → arrives warmer and drier than it started

## ITCZ and Monsoon

- **ITCZ (Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone):** Band of converging trade winds near equator; low pressure; rising moist air → thunderstorms and heavy rainfall
- Shifts with the apparent movement of the Sun:
  - **June:** ITCZ at ~20°N → brings SW monsoon to India
  - **December:** ITCZ at ~20°S → NE trade winds bring rain to southern hemisphere tropics
- The **Indian Monsoon** is the world's most studied monsoon system; affects 1.4 billion people's food and water

---

## Wind Energy

- **Best wind power regions:** "Roaring Forties" latitude offshore, coastal plains, mountain passes
- **India's wind power:** Tamil Nadu (Muppandal), Gujarat (Kutch), Rajasthan, Karnataka
- India is the **4th largest wind power producer** in the world (after China, USA, Germany)
- Wind turbines: Horizontal-axis (most common) and Vertical-axis

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Loo blows in North India (UP, Rajasthan, Punjab)** — hot, dry, dusty, dangerous; May–June; do NOT confuse with any cold wind.
2. **Sirocco comes from Sahara northward** (hot, dusty) — NOT a cold Mediterranean wind.
3. **Mistral is cold (not hot)** — from Alps toward Mediterranean France; cold and dry.
4. **Chinook is warm** (leeward of Rockies, North America) — "snow eater"; opposite of what you'd expect from a mountain wind.
5. **Harmattan is hot and dry in West Africa** — called "Doctor" because it reduces humidity; NOT Mediterranean.
6. **Foehn effect:** Leeward side is WARMER and DRIER than windward — because dry adiabatic lapse rate (10°C/km) is steeper than moist (6°C/km).
7. **Trade Winds: NE trades in NH, SE trades in SH** — they both blow TOWARD the equator.
8. **Westerlies: SW in NH, NW in SH** — they blow AWAY from sub-tropical highs toward polar lows.
9. **Polar Easterlies: NE in NH, SE in SH** — cold, dry, blow from poles.
10. **Jet streams are in the upper troposphere** (~9–16 km) — NOT the mesosphere or stratosphere.
11. **Roaring Forties / Furious Fifties** — in Southern Ocean; strong westerlies; minimal land to slow them; used by historic sailing clippers.
12. **Doldrums at equator = calm, light winds** — opposite of popular "in the doldrums" meaning (though the expression comes from the frustration of becalmed sailing ships).
13. **Horse Latitudes at ~30° = calm descending air** — where sub-tropical high sits; ancient sailors allegedly threw horses overboard when becalmed.
14. **ITCZ shifts northward in June**, triggering Indian SW monsoon — Tibetan Plateau heating is also a key driver.
15. **NE Monsoon (retreating)** brings rain to Tamil Nadu and SE India — Tamil Nadu's rain season is October–December, opposite to most of India.
16. **Wind direction is named for where it comes FROM** — a "Westerly" comes from the west; a "NE trade wind" comes from the NE.
