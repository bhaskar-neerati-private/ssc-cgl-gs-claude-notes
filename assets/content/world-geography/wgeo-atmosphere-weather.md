# Atmosphere — Layers, Weather & Cyclones

On April 14, 1912, the *Titanic* sank in waters so cold that most victims died of hypothermia within minutes. What killed them was not just ice — it was a specific atmospheric pressure system that had pushed a high concentration of icebergs far south of their usual range. Ninety years later, scientists named the atmospheric phenomenon responsible: a "blocking high" pressure cell that rerouted the jetstream. The atmosphere that gives us life can also, in specific configurations, become a death trap. Understanding its layers and behaviour is what modern meteorology — and SSC CGL geography — is built on.

---

## Composition of the Atmosphere

| Gas | Percentage |
|-----|-----------|
| Nitrogen (N₂) | 78.09% |
| Oxygen (O₂) | 20.95% |
| Argon (Ar) | 0.93% |
| Carbon dioxide (CO₂) | ~0.04% |
| Others (Ne, He, CH₄, O₃, H₂O vapour) | Trace |

- **Water vapour:** Highly variable (0–4%); essential for weather formation
- **Ozone (O₃):** Concentrated in stratosphere (15–50 km); absorbs UV radiation
- Total mass of atmosphere: ~5.15 × 10¹⁸ kg; 99% within 30 km of Earth's surface

---

## Layers of the Atmosphere

| Layer | Height | Temperature | Key Feature |
|-------|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Troposphere** | 0–12 km (8 km at poles, 16 km at equator) | Decreases with altitude (~6.5°C per km) | All weather occurs here; contains 75% of atmosphere's mass |
| **Stratosphere** | 12–50 km | Increases with altitude (ozone absorbs UV) | Ozone layer (15–35 km); calm air; jet aircraft fly here |
| **Mesosphere** | 50–80 km | Decreases with altitude | Coldest layer (−100°C); meteors burn up here |
| **Thermosphere** | 80–700 km | Increases with altitude (up to 2,500°C) | Ionosphere here; radio waves reflect; auroras; ISS orbits here |
| **Exosphere** | 700–10,000 km | — | Outermost layer; satellites orbit; merges with space |

**Boundaries:**
- Tropopause: Top of troposphere
- Stratopause: Top of stratosphere
- Mesopause: Top of mesosphere (coldest point in atmosphere, ~−100°C)

## Atmospheric Pressure

- **Standard sea-level pressure:** 1013.25 mb (millibars) = 1 atm = 760 mm Hg
- Pressure **decreases with altitude** (less air above)
- Measured by **barometer** (mercury barometer invented by Torricelli 1643; aneroid barometer — no mercury)
- **Isobars:** Lines connecting points of equal atmospheric pressure on weather maps
- **High pressure (anticyclone):** Descending air; clear, calm weather; divergence at surface
- **Low pressure (cyclone/depression):** Rising air; cloudy, stormy weather; convergence at surface

### Pressure Belts (Latitudinal)

| Belt | Latitude | Type | Cause |
|------|----------|------|-------|
| Equatorial Low | 0° | Low | Thermal — intense solar heating, rising air |
| Sub-tropical High | 30°N/S | High | Dynamic — descending air from upper atmosphere |
| Sub-polar Low | 60°N/S | Low | Dynamic — warm/cold air convergence (polar front) |
| Polar High | 90°N/S | High | Thermal — extremely cold, dense, descending air |

## Winds

### Permanent (Planetary) Winds

| Wind | From | To | Characteristics |
|------|------|----|-----------------|
| **Trade Winds** | 30°N/S (sub-tropical high) | 0° (equatorial low) | NE in NH; SE in SH; steady and reliable |
| **Westerlies** | 30°N/S | 60°N/S | Blow west to east; NW in NH; SW in SH; bring rainfall to west coasts in temperate zones |
| **Polar Easterlies** | 90°N/S | 60°N/S | Cold, dry; blow east to west |

### Jet Streams
- High-altitude, narrow bands of fast-moving air (9–16 km altitude, in troposphere/lower stratosphere)
- Speed: 100–300 km/h
- **Sub-tropical jet stream:** ~30°N/S
- **Polar jet stream:** ~60°N/S; meanders north-south; drives weather systems
- Critical for: Indian monsoon (breaks when Tibetan plateau heats up, pushing jet stream north)

## Humidity and Precipitation

- **Absolute humidity:** Actual amount of water vapour in air (g/m³)
- **Relative humidity:** Ratio of actual to maximum possible moisture at that temperature (%)
- **Dew point:** Temperature at which air is saturated (100% RH); condensation begins
- **Precipitation types:** Rain, drizzle, snow, sleet, hail, frost
- **Rainfall types:**
  - **Orographic/Relief:** Moist air forced up mountain slope → cools → condenses → rains on windward side; leeward side (rain shadow) remains dry
  - **Convectional:** Intense surface heating → rising moist air → thunderstorms; common in tropics and interiors
  - **Frontal/Cyclonic:** Two air masses of different temperatures meet; warm air rises over cold air; steady rain along frontal systems

---

## Air Masses and Fronts

- **Air mass:** Large body of air with uniform temperature and humidity
- **Front:** Boundary between two air masses
  - **Cold front:** Cold air displacing warm air; steep slope; heavy showers
  - **Warm front:** Warm air rising over cold air; gentle slope; prolonged steady rain
  - **Occluded front:** Cold front catches up with warm front; complex weather
  - **Stationary front:** Front not moving significantly

## Cyclones

### Tropical Cyclone
- Form over warm ocean waters (sea temperature >26.5°C)
- **Energy source:** Latent heat released when moist air rises and condenses
- **Structure:** Eye (calm, clear, low pressure centre) → Eye wall (most violent) → Spiral rain bands
- **Rotation:** Anti-clockwise in NH; Clockwise in SH (Coriolis effect)
- **Regional names:**

| Name | Region |
|------|--------|
| Cyclone | Indian Ocean, Bay of Bengal, Arabian Sea, South Pacific |
| Hurricane | Atlantic Ocean, Eastern Pacific |
| Typhoon | Western Pacific, South China Sea |
| Willy-willy | Australia |

- **Saffir-Simpson Scale:** Classifies hurricanes 1–5 by wind speed
- **Most cyclone-prone coast of India:** East coast (Bay of Bengal) — more than west coast (Arabian Sea)

### Temperate/Extra-tropical Cyclone
- Form in mid-latitudes (40°–65°) along polar fronts
- Larger but less intense than tropical cyclones
- Have distinct fronts (warm, cold, occluded)
- Rotate anti-clockwise in NH

### Anticyclone
- High pressure system; air descends and diverges
- Clear skies, calm weather, low humidity
- **Rotate clockwise in NH; anti-clockwise in SH** (opposite of cyclones)

## Cloud Types

| Type | Level | Description |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Cirrus | High (>6 km) | Thin, wispy ice crystals; fair weather |
| Cumulus | Low-mid | Puffy, vertical development; fair weather or showers |
| Stratus | Low | Sheet-like grey layer; drizzle |
| Nimbus | — | Rain-bearing clouds |
| Cumulonimbus | Extends from low to high | Thunderstorm cloud; heavy rain, lightning, hail |

---

## Thunderstorms and Lightning

- **Formation:** Strong updrafts in cumulonimbus clouds cause charge separation; lightning discharges when potential difference is large enough
- **Thunder:** Sound from rapid expansion of air heated by lightning bolt
- **Lightning rod:** Invented by Benjamin Franklin; provides preferential path for lightning to reach ground safely

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **All weather phenomena occur in the troposphere**, not the stratosphere.
2. **Ozone layer is in the stratosphere** (15–35 km), not the mesosphere.
3. **Mesosphere is the coldest layer** (~−100°C at mesopause), not the thermosphere.
4. **Meteors burn up in the mesosphere**, not the troposphere.
5. **Temperature in troposphere decreases with altitude**; in stratosphere it INCREASES (due to ozone absorbing UV).
6. **Cyclones rotate anti-clockwise in NH** and clockwise in SH — anticyclones are OPPOSITE.
7. **Bay of Bengal is more cyclone-prone** than Arabian Sea (on India's east coast).
8. **Hurricane, Typhoon, Cyclone are the same phenomenon** — different regional names.
9. **Trade winds blow from high (30°) to low (0°) pressure** — NE trades in NH, SE trades in SH.
10. **Westerlies blow west TO east** (they are named for where they come FROM, not where they go).
11. **Orographic rainfall:** Windward slope = wet; leeward slope = dry (rain shadow).
12. **Equatorial low is thermal**, sub-tropical high is **dynamic** — SSC loves this distinction.
13. **Standard pressure at sea level = 1013.25 mb** (or 760 mm Hg or 1 atm).
14. **Relative humidity of 100% = dew point reached** — not necessarily foggy or raining.
15. **ISS (International Space Station) orbits in thermosphere** (~400 km altitude).
16. **Jet streams are in upper troposphere/lower stratosphere** — they drive surface weather systems and are key to Indian monsoon.
