# Earthquakes, Volcanoes & Natural Disasters

At 9:15 AM on December 26, 2004, a 9.1 magnitude earthquake ruptured along a 1,200-km fault off the coast of Sumatra. The energy released was equivalent to 23,000 Hiroshima atomic bombs. The Indian Ocean Tsunami that followed killed ~227,000 people across 14 countries in hours — making it the deadliest tsunami in recorded history. What made it catastrophic was not just the earthquake but the complete absence of a warning system. India, which lost ~10,700 people, had no coastal tsunami alert infrastructure. The disaster led directly to the creation of the **Indian Ocean Tsunami Warning System** in 2006. Natural disasters teach through tragedy — and SSC CGL tests every number and mechanism behind them.

## Plate Tectonics — Foundation of All Natural Disasters

### Theory
- Earth's lithosphere (crust + upper mantle) is broken into ~15 major **tectonic plates** that move on the semi-fluid asthenosphere
- Movement rate: 1–10 cm/year (similar to rate fingernails grow)
- **Alfred Wegener** proposed Continental Drift theory (1912) — observed that continents fit like puzzle pieces and had matching fossils across oceans
- **Plate Tectonics** (1960s) confirmed by seafloor spreading evidence (Harry Hess), magnetic striping

### Types of Plate Boundaries

| Boundary Type | Plate Movement | Features | Examples |
|---------------|----------------|----------|---------|
| **Convergent** | Plates moving toward each other | Mountains, ocean trenches, volcanoes, earthquakes | Himalayas (India–Eurasia); Andes; Mariana Trench |
| **Divergent** | Plates moving apart | Rift valleys, mid-ocean ridges, basaltic volcanism | Mid-Atlantic Ridge; East African Rift Valley |
| **Transform** | Plates sliding past each other | Earthquakes; no mountains or trenches | San Andreas Fault (California); North Anatolian Fault |

### Major Tectonic Plates
Indo-Australian, Eurasian, North American, South American, African, Pacific, Antarctic, Nazca, Caribbean, Philippine, Arabian, Juan de Fuca, Cocos, Caribbean

## Earthquakes

### How Earthquakes Occur
- Stress builds up along plate boundaries or faults until rock breaks suddenly
- **Focus (Hypocenter):** Point underground where earthquake originates
- **Epicenter:** Point on Earth's surface directly above focus
- Energy radiates as **seismic waves:**
  - **P-waves (Primary/Compressional):** Travel through solid + liquid; fastest
  - **S-waves (Secondary/Shear):** Travel only through solid; slower than P-waves
  - **Surface waves (L and R):** Travel along surface; cause most damage; slowest

### Measurement

| Scale | Measures | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| **Richter Scale** | Magnitude (energy released) | Logarithmic: each step = 10× amplitude = ~31× energy; developed by Charles Richter (1935) |
| **Moment Magnitude Scale (Mw)** | More accurate for large earthquakes | Now standard; replaces Richter for large events |
| **Modified Mercalli Scale (MMI)** | Intensity (felt effects at a location) | I (not felt) to XII (total destruction); subjective |

### Major Earthquakes in History

| Earthquake | Year | Magnitude | Deaths |
|------------|------|-----------|--------|
| **Valdivia, Chile** | 1960 | 9.5 | ~1,655 | Largest earthquake ever recorded |
| **Indian Ocean (Sumatra)** | 2004 | 9.1 | ~227,000 | Deadliest tsunami |
| **Haiti** | 2010 | 7.0 | ~160,000 | Most deadly by death toll (shallow, near city) |
| **Bhuj, Gujarat, India** | 2001 | 7.7 | ~20,000 | India's worst 21st-century earthquake |
| **Japan (Tōhoku)** | 2011 | 9.0 | ~15,899 | Triggered Fukushima nuclear disaster |
| **Nepal** | 2015 | 7.8 | ~8,900 | |

### Earthquake Zones in India
India has **four seismic zones (II, III, IV, V)** — removed Zone I in revised version:
- **Zone V (Very High Risk):** Kashmir, Uttarakhand, parts of NE India, Himachal Pradesh, Gujarat (Kutch)
- **Zone IV (High Risk):** Delhi, Bihar, parts of Maharashtra
- **Zone III (Moderate Risk):** Most of Deccan Plateau
- **Zone II (Low Risk):** Southern Tamil Nadu, parts of Rajasthan

## Volcanoes

### Types of Volcanoes

| Type | Shape | Eruption Style | Examples |
|------|-------|----------------|---------|
| **Shield Volcano** | Wide, gently sloping | Gentle, fluid basaltic lava | Mauna Loa (Hawaii) — widest; Deccan Traps |
| **Stratovolcano (Composite)** | Steep, cone-shaped | Violent, viscous lava + ash + gas | Mt. Fuji, Mt. Vesuvius, Krakatau, Mt. St. Helens |
| **Cinder Cone** | Small, steep cone | Short, explosive bursts | Paricutín (Mexico) |
| **Caldera** | Bowl-shaped depression | After massive eruption collapse | Yellowstone (supervolcano); Lake Toba |

### Activity Status
- **Active:** Currently erupting or recently erupted (e.g., Etna, Stromboli, Kilauea)
- **Dormant:** Not erupting but capable (e.g., Mt. Fuji — last erupted 1707)
- **Extinct:** No eruption in historical times; unlikely to erupt (e.g., Edinburgh's Arthur's Seat)

### Important Volcanoes

| Volcano | Country | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| **Mauna Loa** | Hawaii, USA | Largest shield volcano by volume; tallest from ocean floor |
| **Mauna Kea** | Hawaii, USA | Tallest mountain from base (10,210 m) when measured from ocean floor |
| **Mt. Fuji** | Japan | Iconic stratovolcano; 3,776 m; dormant |
| **Vesuvius** | Italy | Buried Pompeii in 79 AD; only active volcano mainland Europe |
| **Etna** | Sicily, Italy | Tallest active volcano in Europe (~3,350 m) |
| **Krakatau** | Indonesia | 1883 eruption heard 4,800 km away; triggered global temperature drop |
| **Pinatubo** | Philippines | 1991 eruption — largest 20th-century eruption; caused global cooling |
| **Yellowstone** | USA | Supervolcano; last erupted 640,000 years ago |
| **Barren Island** | Andaman Islands, India | Only active volcano in South Asia; Indian territory |
| **Narcondam** | Andaman Islands, India | Dormant volcano; forest-covered |

### Ring of Fire
- ~40,000 km arc around the Pacific Ocean
- 90% of world's earthquakes; ~75% of world's active volcanoes
- Countries include: Chile, Peru, Ecuador, Colombia, Mexico, USA (Alaska/Pacific NW), Japan, Philippines, Indonesia, New Zealand

## Tsunamis

- **Cause:** Undersea earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or landslides that displace large volumes of water
- **Speed in deep ocean:** ~700–900 km/h (speed of jet aircraft)
- **Wavelength:** Hundreds of kilometres in deep ocean; hard to detect
- **Height:** A few cm to 1 m in open ocean; rises dramatically when reaching shallow coastal waters (**shoaling**)
- **Warning signs:** Sea suddenly withdrawing from shore (runout)
- **Major Tsunamis:**
  - 2004 Indian Ocean: 14 countries; ~227,000 deaths
  - 2011 Japan (Tōhoku): ~15,899 deaths; nuclear disaster at Fukushima
  - 1755 Lisbon: Earthquake + tsunami + fire destroyed Lisbon

## Other Natural Disasters

### Cyclones
- See Atmosphere topic for detail
- **Tropical cyclones:** Over warm ocean water; strong winds + heavy rain + storm surge
- India's vulnerable coast: East coast (Bay of Bengal)
- **Odisha super-cyclone (1999):** ~15,000 deaths
- **Cyclone Fani (2019):** Category 5; Odisha; mass evacuation reduced deaths to ~89

### Floods
- Caused by: Heavy rainfall, dam failure, river overflow, storm surge
- **India flood-prone:** Brahmaputra valley (Assam), Bihar (Kosi River — "Sorrow of Bihar"), UP, Odisha
- **Kosi River** — breaks embankments frequently; shifts course; Bihar's annual disaster

### Droughts
- **Meteorological:** Rainfall deficit
- **Agricultural:** Soil moisture insufficient for crops
- **Hydrological:** Water table depletion
- **India's drought-prone areas:** Parts of Rajasthan, Gujarat, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh

### Landslides
- Triggered by: Heavy rain, earthquakes, deforestation on slopes
- **India vulnerable:** Western Ghats, Himalayas, NE India
- 2013 Kedarnath disaster: Cloudbursts + landslides + glacial lake outburst flood (GLOF); ~5,000 deaths

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Richter Scale is logarithmic:** A 7.0 is 10× the amplitude (NOT 10× the energy) of a 6.0; energy release is ~31×.
2. **P-waves travel through solids AND liquids**; S-waves travel through solids only — used to determine Earth's liquid outer core.
3. **Epicenter is on the surface**; Focus/Hypocenter is underground — distinguish clearly.
4. **Valdivia 1960 (Chile) = largest earthquake ever recorded** (9.5 Mw); NOT the Indian Ocean 2004.
5. **Indian Ocean 2004 = deadliest tsunami**, not the deadliest earthquake.
6. **Barren Island is India's only active volcano** — in Andaman Islands.
7. **Mauna Loa** is the world's largest volcano by volume (shield); **Mauna Kea** is tallest from ocean base.
8. **Vesuvius buried Pompeii in 79 AD** — NOT Herculaneum (which was also buried, but Pompeii is the famous one).
9. **Ring of Fire = Pacific** — NOT Atlantic or Indian Ocean.
10. **Tsunamis travel ~700–900 km/h in deep ocean** — fast but with very small wave height; dangerous only near shore.
11. **Pinatubo 1991** was the largest volcanic eruption in the 20th century (not Krakatau — that was 1883, 19th century).
12. **Yellowstone** is a supervolcano — a caldera, not a cone-shaped mountain.
13. **Shield volcanoes produce gentle eruptions** with fluid lava; **Stratovolcanoes produce violent explosive eruptions**.
14. **Kosi River** is called "Sorrow of Bihar" — frequently floods and shifts course.
15. **India's Seismic Zone V** (highest risk) includes Kashmir, NE India, Uttarakhand, parts of Gujarat.
16. **Bhuj earthquake (2001) was 7.7 magnitude** — Gujarat, India; one of India's most devastating recent earthquakes.
