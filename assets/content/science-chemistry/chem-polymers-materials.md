# Polymers, Plastics, Rubber, Soaps, Detergents & Synthetic Fibres

## The Century of Polymers

In 1907, Leo Baekeland poured phenol and formaldehyde into a pressure vessel, heated the mixture, and produced the world's first fully synthetic plastic — Bakelite. Over the next century, polymers transformed every aspect of human life. Today, humans produce over 400 million tonnes of plastics annually. The clothes on your back (polyester, nylon), the pipes in your walls (PVC), the non-stick pan in your kitchen (Teflon), and the tyre on every vehicle (vulcanised rubber) — all are polymer triumphs.

A polymer is simply a very long chain molecule built by joining thousands of small repeating units (monomers). The properties depend entirely on which monomers are used and how they are connected.

---

## What is a Polymer?

**Polymer** (Greek: poly = many, mer = unit): A large molecule (macromolecule) consisting of many repeating structural units (monomers) linked by covalent bonds.

**Monomer:** The small repeating unit. Example: ethylene (CH₂=CH₂) is the monomer for polyethylene.

**Degree of polymerisation (n):** Number of monomer units in one polymer chain. Can be thousands to millions.

**General:** Monomer + Monomer + ... → Polymer (via polymerisation reaction)

---

## Types of Polymerisation

| Type | Description | By-product | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Addition polymerisation** | Monomers with C=C double bond add to each other; double bond opens | None (no atoms lost) | Polyethylene, PVC, Teflon, polystyrene, polypropylene |
| **Condensation polymerisation** | Two different monomers with functional groups react, releasing a small molecule at each step | Water (H₂O) usually; sometimes HCl | Nylon, polyester, Bakelite, proteins, DNA |

---

## Thermoplastics vs Thermosets

| Property | Thermoplastics | Thermosets |
|---|---|---|
| Structure | Linear or branched chains; no cross-links | 3D cross-linked network |
| On heating | Soften and melt (can be reshaped) | Char/burn but do NOT melt |
| Recyclability | Recyclable | NOT recyclable |
| Examples | Polyethylene, PVC, polystyrene, nylon, Teflon | Bakelite, epoxy resin, vulcanised rubber, urea-formaldehyde resin |
| Solubility | Soluble in some organic solvents | Generally insoluble |
| Hardness | Generally softer | Generally harder and more rigid |

## Important Synthetic Polymers

### 1. Polyethylene (Polythene) — PE

**Monomer:** Ethylene (ethene, CH₂=CH₂)
**Reaction:** n(CH₂=CH₂) → (-CH₂-CH₂-)ₙ

| Type | Properties | Uses |
|---|---|---|
| LDPE (Low Density) | Soft, flexible, transparent | Plastic bags, cling film, squeeze bottles |
| HDPE (High Density) | Hard, opaque, stronger | Milk jugs, detergent bottles, pipes, toys |
| UHMWPE (Ultra High Molecular Weight) | Extremely tough | Bullet-proof vests, hip implants |

Most widely produced plastic in the world.

### 2. Polyvinyl Chloride (PVC)

**Monomer:** Vinyl chloride (CH₂=CHCl)
**Reaction:** n(CH₂=CHCl) → (-CH₂-CHCl-)ₙ

Properties: Hard, rigid, flame-resistant; can be made flexible by adding plasticisers.

**Uses:** Water pipes (rigid PVC), electrical cable insulation, flooring (vinyl flooring), window frames, blood bags (flexible PVC), raincoats, shoes.

### 3. Polypropylene (PP)

**Monomer:** Propylene (propene, CH₂=CHCH₃)

Properties: Lightweight, tough, heat-resistant (melts at ~160°C), chemically resistant.

**Uses:** Car bumpers, food containers (lunch boxes, yogurt cups), ropes, carpets, medical syringes.

### 4. Polystyrene (PS)

**Monomer:** Styrene (CH₂=CHC₆H₅)

- **Solid PS:** Hard, transparent, brittle; used in disposable cups, CD cases, model kits
- **Expanded PS (EPS / Styrofoam / Thermocol):** Foam of 95% air; excellent thermal insulation and packaging material; used in disposable coffee cups, packaging electronics, building insulation.

### 5. Polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE / Teflon)

**Monomer:** Tetrafluoroethylene (CF₂=CF₂)
**Reaction:** n(CF₂=CF₂) → (-CF₂-CF₂-)ₙ

Properties: Extremely non-stick (lowest coefficient of friction of any solid material), chemically inert (resists all acids, bases, and solvents), temperature-stable from -200°C to +260°C.

**Uses:** Non-stick cookware (frying pans, griddles — 'Teflon-coated'), electrical insulation, lubricants, biomedical implants, sealing tape (PTFE plumber's tape), aerospace components.

**Discovered accidentally** by Roy Plunkett (DuPont) in 1938.

### 6. Bakelite (Phenol-Formaldehyde Resin)

**Monomers:** Phenol (C₆H₅OH) + Formaldehyde (HCHO)
**Type:** Thermosetting (condensation polymerisation; water released)
**Reaction:** Phenol + HCHO → 3D cross-linked network + H₂O

Properties: Very hard, brittle, heat-resistant, electrical insulator; cannot be remoulded once set.

**Uses:** Electrical switchboards and sockets, telephone handsets (old), radio cabinets, billiard balls, chess pieces, dark brown/black laboratory instrument handles.

**First fully synthetic plastic;** invented by Leo Baekeland in 1907 in New York — revolutionised the materials industry.

### 7. Nylon (Polyamide)

**Type:** Condensation polymer; thermoplastic

| Type | Monomers | Linkage |
|---|---|---|
| **Nylon-6,6** | Hexamethylenediamine + Adipic acid (hexanedioic acid) | -CONH- (amide bond) |
| **Nylon-6** | Caprolactam (ring-opening polymerisation) | -CONH- |

Properties: Strong, tough, lightweight, abrasion-resistant, low moisture absorption.

**Uses:** Stockings and hosiery, ropes (stronger than steel of same weight), toothbrush bristles, parachutes, tyre cord, gear wheels, sports equipment (tennis strings), surgical sutures.

**Nylon was the first fully synthetic fibre** — introduced by DuPont in 1938; first commercial product was nylon stockings (1939) — sold out in days.

**Amide bond (-CONH-)** is the key linkage in nylon. The same amide bond (peptide bond) links amino acids in proteins — nylon was designed to mimic silk.

### 8. Polyester (PET — Polyethylene Terephthalate)

**Monomers:** Ethylene glycol (HO-CH₂-CH₂-OH) + Terephthalic acid
**Type:** Condensation polymer; thermoplastic
**Linkage:** -COO- (ester bond)

Properties: Strong, dimensionally stable, low moisture absorption, wrinkle-resistant.

**Uses:**
- **Fibres:** 'Dacron' or 'Terylene' — polyester fabric (shirts, pants, sarees); blended with cotton ('polycot')
- **Film:** Mylar film for recording tapes, food packaging
- **Bottles:** PET bottles for water, soft drinks, cooking oil (transparent, tough, lightweight)
- **Carpets and ropes**

### 9. Polyacrylonitrile (Acrylic / Orlon)

**Monomer:** Acrylonitrile (CH₂=CH-CN)

**Properties:** Soft, wool-like feel; resistant to sunlight (UV), chemicals.
**Uses:** Sweaters, blankets, upholstery fabric (sold as 'acrylic wool'); replaces natural wool.

### 10. Polymethyl Methacrylate (PMMA / Acrylic Glass / Perspex / Plexiglass)

**Properties:** Transparent (90% light transmission vs 92% for glass), lightweight (half the weight of glass), shatter-resistant.
**Uses:** Aircraft windows, aquarium tanks, optical lenses, advertising signboards, phone screens (older devices).

## Natural and Synthetic Rubber

### Natural Rubber

**Source:** Latex from rubber tree (*Hevea brasiliensis*) — white milky sap

**Chemical composition:** Polymer of isoprene (2-methyl-1,3-butadiene, C₅H₈)
Formula: (-CH₂-C(CH₃)=CH-CH₂-)ₙ (cis-polyisoprene)

**Properties of raw natural rubber:**
- Elastic at room temperature
- Becomes sticky and soft in heat; brittle and hard in cold
- Absorbs water; not chemically resistant
- NOT suitable for most engineering applications in raw form

### Vulcanisation of Rubber

**Discovered by:** Charles Goodyear (1839, accidentally — dropped rubber + sulphur on a hot stove)

**Process:** Natural rubber is heated with sulphur (3–5%) at ~140°C
**Chemistry:** Sulphur atoms form cross-links between polymer chains (S-S bridges between polyisoprene chains)

**Effect of vulcanisation:**

| Property | Raw Rubber | Vulcanised Rubber |
|---|---|---|
| Elasticity | Elastic but poor recovery | High elasticity, good recovery |
| Temperature sensitivity | Melts in heat; brittle in cold | Stable over wide range |
| Tensile strength | Low | Much higher |
| Chemical resistance | Poor | Good |
| Water absorption | High | Low |

**Uses of vulcanised rubber:** Tyres (car, truck, cycle), tubes, hoses, conveyor belts, shoe soles, gaskets, seals, gloves.

**More sulphur → harder rubber (ebonite/hard rubber):** 30–50% sulphur gives very hard, rigid rubber used for battery cases and electrical insulators.

### Synthetic Rubbers

| Name | Monomers | Properties | Uses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Neoprene (Polychloroprene)** | Chloroprene (CH₂=CCl-CH=CH₂) | Oil-resistant, flame-resistant, weather-resistant | Wetsuits, hoses, gaskets, protective gloves |
| **Buna-N (Nitrile rubber)** | Butadiene + Acrylonitrile | Excellent oil/fuel resistance | Fuel hoses, O-rings, seals in engines |
| **Buna-S (SBR, Styrene-Butadiene Rubber)** | Butadiene + Styrene | Cheaper, good abrasion resistance | Car tyres (most car tyres use SBR) |
| **Silicone rubber** | Siloxane (Si-O backbone) | Extreme temperature range (-60°C to +250°C) | Medical devices, bakeware, sealants |
| **Thiokol** | Ethylene dichloride + Na₂Sₓ | Chemical resistance | Rocket fuel binders, sealants |

## Soaps and Detergents

### Soaps

**Chemical nature:** Sodium or potassium salts of long-chain fatty acids (carboxylates)
- Sodium soap (hard soap, bar soap): R-COONa (where R = long hydrocarbon chain C₁₂–C₁₈)
- Potassium soap (soft soap, liquid soap): R-COOK

**Manufacture — Saponification:**
Fat/oil (triglyceride) + NaOH (or KOH) → Soap (sodium/potassium fatty acid salt) + Glycerol
**Triglyceride + 3NaOH → 3R-COONa + C₃H₅(OH)₃ (glycerol)**

**Sodium stearate** (C₁₇H₃₅COONa) is a typical soap molecule.

### How Soap Cleans — Micelle Formation

A soap molecule has two distinct parts:
- **Hydrophilic head** (-COONa): Water-loving; dissolves in water
- **Hydrophobic tail** (long carbon chain -C₁₇H₃₅): Water-fearing; oil-loving

**Cleaning mechanism:**
1. Soap molecules surround a grease/oil droplet with their hydrophobic tails pointing INTO the oil
2. Hydrophilic heads point OUTWARD into the water
3. This spherical structure is called a **MICELLE**
4. The oil-in-micelle is now suspended in water (emulsified) and can be rinsed away
5. Rubbing/agitation helps break the grease into smaller droplets for more micelles to form

### Soaps vs Detergents

| Property | Soaps | Synthetic Detergents |
|---|---|---|
| Chemical nature | Sodium/potassium salts of fatty acids | Sodium salts of sulphonic acids (R-SO₃Na) |
| Hard water | INEFFECTIVE — form insoluble scum (Ca/Mg salts of fatty acids) | EFFECTIVE — sulphonate salts of Ca/Mg ARE soluble |
| Acidic conditions | Decompose (fatty acid precipitates) | Stable in all pH |
| Biodegradability | Fully biodegradable | Variable (linear = biodegradable; branched = NOT) |
| Source | Natural fats/oils | Petroleum (synthetic) |
| Example | Sodium stearate | SDS (sodium dodecyl sulphate), ABS (alkyl benzene sulphonate) |
| pH | Slightly alkaline (8–10) | Can be neutral or alkaline |

**Why detergents work in hard water:** The calcium/magnesium sulphonate salts (Ca(SO₃R)₂) ARE soluble in water, unlike calcium stearate (soap scum). So detergents form micelles in hard water just as effectively as in soft water.

**Biodegradability of detergents:**
- Linear alkyl benzene sulphonate (LAS) — straight carbon chain → biodegraded by bacteria in sewage
- Branched ABS detergents (older) — branched chains → NOT biodegraded → caused foam in rivers; now banned

## Natural Fibres vs Synthetic Fibres

| Fibre | Type | Source/Composition | Key Properties |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cotton** | Natural (cellulose) | Cotton plant (*Gossypium*) | Soft, absorbent, breathable, biodegradable |
| **Silk** | Natural (protein) | Silkworm (*Bombyx mori*) | Lustrous, strong, smooth, luxury fibre |
| **Wool** | Natural (protein) | Sheep hair (keratin) | Warm, elastic, water-resistant |
| **Jute** | Natural (cellulose) | Jute plant | Coarse, biodegradable, cheap; sacking, bags |
| **Nylon** | Synthetic (polyamide) | Hexamethylenediamine + adipic acid | Strong, elastic, abrasion-resistant |
| **Polyester** | Synthetic (polyester) | Ethylene glycol + terephthalic acid | Wrinkle-resistant, quick-drying |
| **Acrylic** | Synthetic (polyacrylonitrile) | Acrylonitrile monomer | Wool-like, colourful, UV-resistant |
| **Rayon** | Regenerated (semi-synthetic) | Cellulose (wood pulp) treated with chemicals | Silk-like, absorbent; 'artificial silk' |

**Rayon ('Artificial Silk'):** Not truly synthetic — derived from natural cellulose (wood pulp) treated with NaOH + CS₂ (viscose process) → regenerated cellulose fibres; resembles silk in sheen and drape; cheaper than natural silk.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Bakelite is the FIRST fully synthetic plastic** — thermosetting; made from phenol + formaldehyde; cannot be remoulded; used in electrical switches, old telephone parts.
2. **Nylon was the FIRST fully synthetic FIBRE** — introduced by DuPont in 1938; nylon-6,6 from hexamethylenediamine + adipic acid; amide (-CONH-) bond linkage.
3. **Teflon (PTFE) is the most non-stick material known** — used in non-stick cookware; monomer = tetrafluoroethylene (CF₂=CF₂); extremely inert (resists all chemicals).
4. **Thermoplastics soften on heating (recyclable); thermosets char/burn on heating (NOT recyclable)** — Bakelite, epoxy, vulcanised rubber = thermosets. PVC, polythene, nylon, PET = thermoplastics.
5. **Vulcanisation of rubber was discovered by Charles Goodyear (1839)** — sulphur cross-links between polyisoprene chains make rubber durable; without vulcanisation, rubber tyres would not work.
6. **Natural rubber = polyisoprene (cis form); synthetic rubber SBR = butadiene + styrene** — most car tyres use SBR (styrene-butadiene rubber), not natural rubber.
7. **Soap does NOT work in hard water** — forms insoluble calcium/magnesium stearate (scum); detergents DO work in hard water (sulphonate salts are soluble).
8. **Soap cleans via MICELLE formation** — hydrophobic tails point into grease/oil; hydrophilic heads face outward into water; the micelle suspends oil in water (emulsification).
9. **Saponification = making soap** — Fat + NaOH → Sodium soap + Glycerol. Saponification value = mg KOH needed to saponify 1 g of fat.
10. **Rayon is NOT fully synthetic** — it is a 'regenerated' or 'semi-synthetic' fibre; it is made from natural cellulose (wood pulp) that is chemically dissolved and re-spun. Called 'artificial silk.'
11. **Polyester (PET) bottles are labelled with recycling code '1'** — most recyclable common plastic; PVC is code '3'; HDPE is code '2'; LDPE is code '4'; PP is code '5'; PS is code '6'.
12. **PVC pipes replace galvanised iron pipes in plumbing** — lighter, cheaper, corrosion-resistant; rigid PVC (uPVC) for drain/water pipes; flexible PVC for cables.
13. **Addition polymerisation = NO by-product; condensation polymerisation = H₂O by-product** — polyethylene, PVC, Teflon = addition; nylon, polyester, Bakelite = condensation.
14. **Neoprene rubber (polychloroprene) is oil-resistant and flame-resistant** — used in wetsuits and protective gloves; unlike natural rubber or SBR which are not oil-resistant.
15. **Acrylic (polyacrylonitrile) is the synthetic substitute for wool** — soft, lightweight, takes colour well; sold as 'acrylic wool' for sweaters and blankets.
16. **Ebonite/Hard rubber = natural rubber vulcanised with 30–50% sulphur** — very hard, rigid, excellent electrical insulator; used in battery cases and bowling balls.
