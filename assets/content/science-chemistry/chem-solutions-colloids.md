# Solutions, Colloids & Concentration — Tyndall Effect, Osmosis, Molarity & Suspension

## Why Your Morning Tea Tells a Story of Chemistry

When you make a cup of tea, you're performing colloidal chemistry without realising it. Drop a tea bag in hot water — the tea colour spreads as dissolved tannins and caffeine form a **true solution**. Add milk — you've now created a **colloid** (fat droplets suspended in water). Add a teaspoon of sugar — another true solution. Stir in chalk dust — it settles after a minute, making a **suspension**.

Three mixtures, three categories, one cup. The difference between them isn't just visible to the eye — it determines how they filter, how they scatter light, whether they can be separated by paper, and whether they obey osmotic pressure. Chemistry built an entire taxonomy around these three types — and SSC tests the boundary cases relentlessly.

---

## Part 1 — The Three Types of Mixtures

| Property | True Solution | Colloid | Suspension |
|----------|--------------|---------|------------|
| Particle size | < 1 nm (< 10⁻⁷ m) | 1–100 nm (10⁻⁷ to 10⁻⁵ m) | > 100 nm (> 10⁻⁵ m) |
| Appearance | Transparent, clear | May appear cloudy or opaque | Visibly cloudy/turbid |
| Filtration | Passes through filter paper AND ultrafiltration membrane | Passes through filter paper, NOT ultrafiltration membrane | Does NOT pass through filter paper |
| Settling | Does not settle | Does not settle (stable) | Settles on standing |
| Tyndall Effect | NO | YES | YES (but not a true colloid) |
| Examples | Salt in water, sugar in water, HCl in water | Milk, blood, fog, smoke, starch solution, paint | Chalk in water, muddy water, sand in water |

## Part 2 — True Solutions

A **solution** is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. The dissolved substance is the **solute**; the dissolving medium is the **solvent**. If the solvent is water, it is called an **aqueous solution**.

### Terminology
- **Saturated solution**: holds maximum solute at a given temperature; no more can dissolve
- **Unsaturated solution**: can dissolve more solute
- **Supersaturated solution**: holds more solute than its saturation limit (unstable; achieved by cooling a hot saturated solution)
- **Solubility**: maximum amount of solute that dissolves in 100 g of solvent at a given temperature

### "Like dissolves like" Rule
- **Polar solvents** (water) dissolve **polar/ionic solutes** (NaCl, HCl, KNO₃)
- **Non-polar solvents** (benzene, ether) dissolve **non-polar solutes** (fats, oils, grease)

### Effect of Temperature on Solubility
- **Solids in liquid**: solubility generally increases with temperature
- **Gases in liquid**: solubility **decreases** with temperature (that's why warm carbonated drinks go flat faster — CO₂ is less soluble in warm water)

### Henry's Law (for gases)
The solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid.  
**Application**: Carbonated beverages are bottled under pressure — CO₂ stays dissolved. When you open the bottle (pressure drops), CO₂ escapes.

## Part 3 — Concentration Units

How much solute is dissolved? Several ways to express this:

| Unit | Formula | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| **Molarity (M)** | Moles of solute / Litres of solution | Most common in chemistry; changes with temperature (volume changes) |
| **Molality (m)** | Moles of solute / kg of solvent | Temperature independent; used in colligative property calculations |
| **Normality (N)** | Equivalents of solute / Litres of solution | Used in acid-base and redox titrations; being phased out |
| **Mole fraction (x)** | Moles of component / Total moles in mixture | Dimensionless; used in vapour pressure calculations |
| **Mass percent (w/w%)** | (Mass of solute / Mass of solution) × 100 | Everyday labelling |
| **Parts per million (ppm)** | mg per kg (or mg per litre for water) | Very dilute solutions: pollutants, trace minerals |

**Important**: A 1 M solution contains 1 mole of solute per *litre of solution* (not per litre of solvent). Molarity depends on temperature; molality does not.

**Dilution formula**: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ — the moles of solute don't change when you add water to a solution.

## Part 4 — Colligative Properties (Properties That Depend on Number, Not Nature)

**Colligative properties** depend on the *number* of solute particles dissolved, NOT on their chemical identity. Add sugar or salt in equal molar amounts — same colligative effect.

### Four Colligative Properties

**1. Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure**
Adding a non-volatile solute lowers the vapour pressure of the solvent. Fewer solvent molecules at the surface can escape.  
**Application**: antifreeze in car coolant.

**2. Elevation of Boiling Point (ΔTb)**
Adding a solute raises the boiling point. Salt in cooking water raises the boiling point slightly (~0.52°C per mole for water).  
ΔTb = Kb × m (where Kb = ebullioscopic constant; m = molality)

**3. Depression of Freezing Point (ΔTf)**
Adding a solute lowers the freezing point.  
ΔTf = Kf × m (where Kf = cryoscopic constant; m = molality)  
**Applications**:
- Salting icy roads (NaCl lowers water's freezing point below 0°C)
- Antifreeze (ethylene glycol in car radiators)
- Salting fish to preserve (draws out water)

**4. Osmotic Pressure (π)**
Most important for SSC — see next section.

## Part 5 — Osmosis & Osmotic Pressure

**Osmosis**: The spontaneous movement of solvent (usually water) from a region of *lower solute concentration* (more dilute) to *higher solute concentration* (more concentrated) through a **semi-permeable membrane** (SPM).

The SPM allows water (solvent) to pass but blocks solute particles.

**Osmotic pressure (π)**: The minimum pressure needed to stop osmosis. For dilute solutions:  
π = CRT (where C = molar concentration, R = gas constant, T = temperature in Kelvin)

### Types of Solutions (Relative to Cells)

| Term | Meaning | Effect on Red Blood Cells |
|------|---------|--------------------------|
| **Isotonic** | Same concentration as cell interior | No change in cell size |
| **Hypotonic** | More dilute than cell | Water enters cell → **swells/bursts (haemolysis)** |
| **Hypertonic** | More concentrated than cell | Water leaves cell → **shrinks (crenation/plasmolysis)** |

**Memory**: Hypo = below (less concentrated) → water rushes IN. Hyper = above (more concentrated) → water rushes OUT.

### Osmosis Applications

- **IV drips**: must be isotonic (0.9% NaCl, "normal saline") — if hypotonic, RBCs burst; if hypertonic, RBCs shrink
- **Salting/pickling vegetables**: hypertonic salt draws water out of food (preservation)
- **Wilting of plants in dry soil**: roots cannot absorb water when soil solution is more concentrated than cell sap
- **Freshwater fish in ocean**: lose water by osmosis and die (cells are hypotonic to sea water)
- **Water purification**: **Reverse Osmosis (RO)** applies pressure GREATER than osmotic pressure to push water *against* the natural direction — water moves from salty side to pure side through a membrane

**Reverse Osmosis (RO)** = applying external pressure greater than osmotic pressure to push pure water out of a concentrated solution. Used in water purifiers and desalination plants.

## Part 6 — Colloids

### What is a Colloid?
A heterogeneous mixture where particles (1–100 nm) of one substance (**dispersed phase**) are distributed in another (**dispersion medium**) without settling.

### Classification of Colloids

| Dispersed Phase | Dispersion Medium | Name | Example |
|----------------|------------------|------|---------|
| Solid | Liquid | **Sol** | Paint, blood, starch paste, muddy water (not settled) |
| Solid | Gas | **Aerosol** (solid) | Smoke, dust in air |
| Liquid | Liquid | **Emulsion** | Milk, mayonnaise, cream |
| Liquid | Gas | **Aerosol** (liquid) | Fog, mist, cloud, hairspray |
| Gas | Liquid | **Foam** | Soap suds, whipped cream |
| Solid | Solid | **Solid sol** | Gem stones, alloys (some), coloured glass |

### Important Specific Colloids
- **Milk** = fat (dispersed) in water (medium) = emulsion
- **Butter** = water in fat = emulsion (reversed!)
- **Blood** = solid (proteins, cells) in liquid = sol
- **Fog/mist** = liquid droplets in gas = aerosol
- **Smoke** = solid particles in gas = aerosol
- **Cream/foam** = gas in liquid (also emulsion in some contexts)
- **Gelatin** = solid in liquid = sol/gel

### The Tyndall Effect

When a beam of light passes through a colloid, the colloidal particles **scatter** the light, making the beam visible. This scattering is the **Tyndall Effect** (discovered by John Tyndall, 1869).

**True solution**: light passes through undeviated → NO Tyndall Effect  
**Colloid**: light scattered → YES Tyndall Effect (beam visible from side)

**Examples of Tyndall Effect**:
- Headlight beams visible in foggy night air (fog = colloid)
- Light shaft visible in a dusty room when sunlight enters through a window
- Blue colour of the sky (Rayleigh scattering, a related but different phenomenon)
- Scattering of light through milk

**Test to distinguish colloid from true solution**: shine a light beam through the liquid and view from the side. If beam visible = colloid (Tyndall Effect). If invisible = true solution.

### Brownian Motion
Colloidal particles show random, zigzag movement — **Brownian motion** — caused by constant bombardment by solvent molecules. Observed by Robert Brown (1827) with pollen grains. This motion prevents settling and keeps the colloid stable.

### Coagulation/Precipitation of Colloids
Add an electrolyte (like alum) → neutralises surface charge on colloidal particles → they aggregate and settle.

**Applications**:
- **Alum in water purification**: AlSO₄ coagulates suspended clay particles → they settle
- **Deltaic formation**: river water (freshwater colloid) meets seawater (electrolyte) → clay particles coagulate → delta forms (Ganga-Brahmaputra Delta)
- **Blood clotting**: FeCl₃ is used to stop bleeding (coagulates blood sol)
- **Smoke precipitation**: Cottrell precipitator removes charged smoke particles using electric field (industrial)

### Emulsification & Emulsifiers

An **emulsion** (oil + water) is usually unstable — oil and water separate. Adding an **emulsifier** (surfactant) stabilises the emulsion:
- Emulsifier molecules have a hydrophilic head (loves water) and hydrophobic tail (loves oil)
- They coat oil droplets, keeping them dispersed in water

**Common emulsifiers**:
- **Soap** (R-COONa): used in cleansing
- **Lecithin** (in egg yolk): stabilises mayonnaise
- **Casein** (in milk proteins): keeps fat dispersed

## Part 7 — Important Industrial & Environmental Applications

**Water purification stages**:
1. Sedimentation (large particles settle)
2. **Coagulation** (alum added → colloidal clay coagulates)
3. Filtration (remaining solids removed)
4. Chlorination (disinfection)
5. Optional: **RO** (reverse osmosis for purification beyond chlorination)

**Dialysis** (for colloids): similar to kidney dialysis — uses a semi-permeable membrane to separate colloidal particles from true solution particles. Used to purify colloidal solutions.

**Desalination**: RO applied to seawater at very high pressure (~60 bar) to produce freshwater. India has plants in Chennai, Lakshadweep, and Jamnagar.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Colloidal particles (1–100 nm) do NOT settle on standing** — this distinguishes colloids from suspensions. True solutions also don't settle, but they are transparent and show no Tyndall Effect.

2. **Tyndall Effect is shown by colloids, NOT true solutions.** Blue colour of the sky is Rayleigh scattering — a related phenomenon — but the specific Tyndall Effect question always points to colloids.

3. **Milk is a colloid (emulsion)** — fat dispersed in water. Butter is the reverse: water dispersed in fat. Both are emulsions, but the dispersion medium is reversed.

4. **Osmosis** moves water from LOW concentration (dilute solution) to HIGH concentration (concentrated solution). It moves *with* the concentration gradient for water — which is *against* the solute concentration gradient.

5. **Reverse Osmosis** requires external pressure GREATER THAN osmotic pressure. It forces water to move against its natural direction. RO removes dissolved salts (including ions like Na⁺, Cl⁻) — it is the only filtration method that does this at home.

6. **Normal saline (0.9% NaCl) is isotonic** with blood. Injecting distilled water (hypotonic) into the bloodstream causes RBCs to burst (haemolysis) — medically dangerous.

7. **Solubility of gases decreases as temperature increases** — that's why warm cola goes flat, cold water holds more dissolved oxygen (why fish prefer cold water), and hot springs have less dissolved gas.

8. **Molarity (M) = moles per LITRE OF SOLUTION; Molality (m) = moles per KG OF SOLVENT.** Molarity changes with temperature (volume of solution changes); molality does not — it's preferred for colligative property calculations.

9. **Elevation of Boiling Point and Depression of Freezing Point** both depend on molality (m), NOT molarity. Salting water raises its boiling point (very slightly) AND lowers its freezing point.

10. **Coagulation** (precipitation of a colloid) is caused by adding an electrolyte, which neutralises the surface charge on colloidal particles. Higher valence of the added ion = more effective at coagulation (Hardy-Schulze rule).

11. **Alum (potassium aluminium sulphate, KAl(SO₄)₂·12H₂O)** is used as a coagulant in water treatment. The Al³⁺ ion is highly effective at coagulating negatively charged clay colloids.

12. **Delta formation at river mouths** happens because seawater (electrolyte) coagulates the clay colloid carried by the river — this is why the Ganga-Brahmaputra Delta is one of the world's largest. This is a direct application of colloidal coagulation.

13. **Brownian motion** keeps colloidal particles from settling — the constant zigzag motion due to bombardment by solvent molecules counteracts gravity. Robert Brown observed it in 1827.

14. **Dialysis** uses a membrane permeable to small solute molecules but not to colloidal particles — used to purify colloidal sols and is the basis of kidney dialysis machines (haemodialysis).

15. **Smoke is a colloid** (solid particles in gas = aerosol). Fog and mist are also colloids (liquid droplets in gas = aerosol). The Tyndall Effect is why car headlights form visible beams in fog.

16. **Supersaturated solutions** are unstable — adding a seed crystal (or dust) triggers rapid crystallisation. This is used in making rock candy and in certain industrial crystallisation processes. Honey is a naturally supersaturated sugar solution.
