# Metals, Non-metals, Metallurgy, Alloys, Reactivity Series & Corrosion

## The Civilisations of Metal

Human history is written in metal — the Stone Age gave way to the Copper Age (Chalcolithic), then the Bronze Age (~3300 BCE), then the Iron Age (~1200 BCE). Each transition represented a civilisational leap: harder tools, better weapons, more durable structures.

Today, we extract over 1.8 billion tonnes of iron and 20 million tonnes of aluminium annually. The entire modern world — from smartphones (aluminium, copper, lithium) to bridges (steel) to electrical grids (copper) — is built on the chemistry of metals. Understanding what metals are, where they come from, and how they behave is foundational chemistry.

## Properties of Metals vs Non-Metals

### Metals

| Property | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Physical state | Mostly solid at room temperature | All metals except mercury (Hg) |
| Lustre | Shiny/lustrous (reflect light) | Silver, gold, copper |
| Hardness | Generally hard (except alkali metals) | Iron, copper; Na, K are soft |
| Malleability | Can be beaten into sheets | Gold (most malleable), silver |
| Ductility | Can be drawn into wires | Gold (most ductile), copper |
| Tensile strength | High in most metals | Steel, iron, titanium |
| Heat conductivity | Good to excellent | Silver (best), copper, aluminium |
| Electrical conductivity | Excellent (free electrons) | Silver (best conductor), copper |
| Melting/Boiling point | Generally high | Tungsten (highest mp = 3422°C) |
| Density | Generally high | Osmium (densest), iridium |
| Reaction with O₂ | Form basic metallic oxides | 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO (basic) |
| Reaction with HCl | Produce H₂ gas (if above H in reactivity) | Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑ |
| Ions formed | Cations (lose electrons) | Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺, Cu²⁺, Na⁺ |

### Non-Metals

| Property | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Physical state | Solids, liquids, or gases | S, P (solid); Br₂ (liquid); O₂, N₂ (gas) |
| Lustre | Dull (except iodine and graphite) | Sulphur (yellow, dull) |
| Malleability/Ductility | Brittle if solid | Sulphur shatters if struck |
| Heat/Electrical conductivity | Poor (except graphite) | Diamond is an electrical insulator |
| Melting/Boiling point | Generally low | Most non-metals are gases/liquids |
| Reaction with O₂ | Form acidic oxides | C + O₂ → CO₂ (acidic); S + O₂ → SO₂ (acidic) |
| Ions formed | Anions (gain electrons) | Cl⁻, O²⁻, S²⁻, N³⁻ |

### Exceptions and Special Cases

| Substance | Special Property |
|---|---|
| **Graphite** (carbon non-metal) | Conducts electricity (only non-metal that does); lubricant |
| **Diamond** (carbon non-metal) | Hardest natural substance; electrical insulator but best thermal conductor |
| **Iodine** (non-metal) | Has metallic lustre (shiny grey-violet solid) |
| **Mercury (Hg)** (metal) | Only liquid metal at room temperature; does not conduct heat well |
| **Gallium** | Metal; melts in your hand (mp 29.8°C) |
| **Tungsten** | Metal with the HIGHEST melting point (3422°C) — used in light bulb filaments |
| **Osmium** | Metal with the HIGHEST density (22.59 g/cm³) |
| **Lithium** | Lightest metal (density 0.53 g/cm³ — lighter than water!); floats on water |
| **Silicon, Germanium** | Metalloids; semiconductors |

## Reactivity Series (Activity Series)

The reactivity series ranks metals from most to least reactive, based on their tendency to lose electrons (oxidise) and their reactions with water, acids, and steam.

**Most reactive (top) → Least reactive (bottom):**

| Position | Metal | Reaction Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **K** (Potassium) | Most reactive | Reacts explosively with cold water; stored in kerosene |
| **Na** (Sodium) | Very reactive | Reacts vigorously with cold water; stored in kerosene |
| **Ca** (Calcium) | Reactive | Reacts briskly with cold water |
| **Mg** (Magnesium) | Reactive | Reacts slowly with cold water; fast with steam; fast with dilute acids |
| **Al** (Aluminium) | Moderately reactive | Slow with steam; reacts with dilute acids; passive (oxide layer) |
| **Zn** (Zinc) | Moderate | Reacts with hot water/steam; dilute acids |
| **Fe** (Iron) | Moderate | Reacts with steam (not cold water); dilute HCl and H₂SO₄ |
| **Ni** (Nickel) | Low-moderate | Slow reaction with dilute acids |
| **Sn** (Tin) | Low | Slow reactions; can dissolve in hot HCl |
| **Pb** (Lead) | Low | Very slow reactions with dilute acids |
| **H** (Hydrogen) | Reference point | Metals above H displace H₂ from acids; below cannot |
| **Cu** (Copper) | Inactive | Does not react with dilute acids; reacts with conc. HNO₃/H₂SO₄ |
| **Ag** (Silver) | Inactive | Dissolves only in conc. HNO₃ |
| **Au** (Gold) | Least reactive | Dissolves only in aqua regia (HNO₃ + HCl) |
| **Pt** (Platinum) | Least reactive | Dissolves only in aqua regia |

**Key rules from the reactivity series:**
1. Metals above H₂ displace hydrogen from dilute acids; metals below H₂ do not.
2. More reactive metals displace less reactive metals from their salt solutions (displacement reactions).
3. Metals above Cu cannot be found in native (free) form in nature; Cu, Ag, Au can be found as free metals.
4. Alkali metals (K, Na) are too reactive to be displaced from compounds; extracted by electrolysis.

## Metallurgy — Extraction of Metals

Metallurgy is the process of extracting metals from their ores.

### Steps in Metallurgy

**1. Mining:** Extracting ore from the earth (open-cast/surface or underground mines).

**2. Ore Dressing (Beneficiation):** Removing gangue (impurities) from the ore:
- Crushing and grinding
- Gravity separation (dense ore sinks; light gangue floats in water)
- Froth flotation (ore particles stick to froth/bubbles and float; used for sulphide ores)
- Magnetic separation (magnetite, Fe₃O₄ is magnetic; used to separate from non-magnetic gangue)
- Roasting (heating sulphide ores in air → converts to oxide; eliminates S as SO₂)
- Calcination (heating carbonate/hydroxide ores in limited air → decomposes to oxide; eliminates CO₂)

**3. Reduction (Smelting):** Reducing the purified metal oxide to the free metal:

| Method | Used For | Key Reaction |
|---|---|---|
| **Carbon reduction (coke)** | Fe, Zn, Pb, Sn, Cu | Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂ (blast furnace) |
| **Thermite process (Al reduction)** | Cr, Mn (from very stable oxides) | Fe₂O₃ + 2Al → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe (thermite reaction; extremely exothermic) |
| **Electrolytic reduction** | Na, K, Al, Mg, Ca (very reactive) | 2Al₂O₃ → 4Al + 3O₂ (Hall-Héroult process, electrolysis of molten alumina) |
| **Hydrogen reduction** | W (tungsten), Mo | WO₃ + 3H₂ → W + 3H₂O |

**4. Refining (Purification):** Removing impurities from crude extracted metal:
- **Electrolytic refining** (most common; used for Cu, Ag, Au, Al): Crude metal = anode; Pure metal = cathode; Metal ions in solution deposit on cathode; impurities fall as anode mud
- Distillation (Zn, Hg — have low boiling points)
- Liquation (Sn, Pb — low melting points)
- Zone refining (Si, Ge — ultra-pure for semiconductors; impurities concentrated at one end)

### Blast Furnace (Iron Extraction)

**Raw materials:** Haematite (Fe₂O₃) + Coke (carbon, C) + Limestone (CaCO₃)

| Temperature Zone | Reactions |
|---|---|
| Bottom (tuyères, ~2000°C) | C + O₂ → CO₂; CO₂ + C → 2CO (reducing agent) |
| Middle | Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂ (main reduction) |
| Top | CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (limestone decomposes) |
| CaO | CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃ (slag — removes silica gangue) |

**Products:** Pig iron (molten iron, collected at bottom) + Slag (CaSiO₃, collected above molten iron)
**Pig iron** has ~4% carbon content → brittle; converted to steel (0.2–2% C) or cast iron.

### Hall-Héroult Process (Aluminium Extraction)

- Aluminium is most abundant metal but requires enormous energy to extract
- Al₂O₃ (alumina/corundum) is extracted from bauxite (by Bayer process)
- Molten Al₂O₃ (dissolved in cryolite, Na₃AlF₆, to lower melting point) is electrolysed
- Cathode: Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al (aluminium deposits)
- Anode: 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻ (oxygen released; burns carbon anode gradually)
- Cryolite (Na₃AlF₆) reduces electrolysis temperature from 2050°C to ~1000°C — major energy saving

## Alloys

An alloy is a mixture of a metal with one or more other metals or non-metals, designed to have superior properties than the pure metal.

### Important Alloys for SSC

| Alloy | Composition | Properties & Uses |
|---|---|---|
| **Steel** | Fe + 0.2–2% C | Stronger, harder than iron; construction, vehicles, machines |
| **Stainless Steel** | Fe + Cr (12–18%) + Ni (~8%) | Corrosion-resistant; cutlery, medical instruments |
| **Cast Iron** | Fe + 3.5–4.5% C | Very brittle; used in engine blocks, pipes |
| **Wrought Iron** | Fe + <0.02% C | Very low carbon; tough, ductile; decorative ironwork |
| **Bronze** | Cu + Sn (10–22%) | Harder than pure Cu; the original 'alloy'; used for statues, medals |
| **Brass** | Cu + Zn (20–40%) | Yellow, corrosion-resistant; musical instruments, locks, plumbing |
| **German Silver / Nickel Silver** | Cu + Zn + Ni | Looks like silver; used for cutlery and jewellery |
| **Duralumin** | Al + Cu + Mg + Mn | Very light, strong; aircraft, spacecraft structures |
| **Magnalium** | Al + Mg (2–10%) | Very light; aircraft parts |
| **Solder** | Pb + Sn (37:63 ratio for eutectic) | Low melting point; joins electrical wires |
| **Amalgam** | Mercury (Hg) + any metal | Dental fillings (Ag + Sn + Cu + Hg); old mirror backing |
| **Nichrome** | Ni + Cr + Fe | Very high resistance; used in heating elements (toasters, heaters) |
| **Invar** | Fe + Ni (36%) | Near-zero thermal expansion; scientific instruments, clocks |
| **Rose's metal** | Bi + Pb + Sn | Very low mp (~94°C); safety plugs in fire suppression systems |
| **Constantan** | Cu + Ni (60:40) | Constant electrical resistance regardless of temperature; electrical instruments |

**Key alloy facts:**
- Pure gold is 24 carats; 22K gold = 22/24 parts gold + 2/24 parts silver/copper (for jewellery)
- 18K gold = 75% gold; most common for fine jewellery
- "Sterling silver" = 92.5% silver + 7.5% copper (too pure silver is soft)

## Corrosion

### What is Corrosion?

Corrosion is the gradual deterioration of a metal by chemical or electrochemical reaction with its environment (usually oxygen, water, and/or acidic gases).

### Rusting of Iron (Most Important)

**Reaction:** 4Fe + 3O₂ + xH₂O → 2Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (hydrated iron oxide = rust; reddish-brown)

**Requirements for rusting:** Both oxygen AND moisture/water must be present.
- Dry air (no moisture) → very slow rusting
- Pure water (no dissolved oxygen) → very slow rusting
- Saltwater or acidic environment → extremely fast rusting (electrolyte accelerates the electrochemical corrosion)

**Rusting is an electrochemical process** — forms tiny galvanic cells on the iron surface where iron acts as anode (oxidised: Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻) and oxygen is reduced at the cathode (O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻).

### Prevention of Corrosion

| Method | How It Works | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Galvanisation** | Coating iron/steel with zinc; Zn acts as sacrificial anode (more reactive → corrodes instead of Fe) | GI (galvanised iron) pipes, roofing sheets, buckets |
| **Tinning** | Coating iron with tin; tin is less reactive, but if tin layer breaks, iron corrodes faster (galvanic corrosion) | Food cans (tin cans) |
| **Painting/varnishing** | Barrier coating to prevent air/moisture contact | Bridges, ships, structures |
| **Electroplating** | Coating with chromium, nickel, silver etc. using electrolysis | Car bumpers (Cr), jewellery (Au, Ag), cutlery (Ni) |
| **Alloying** | Adding Cr and Ni to iron makes stainless steel (resistant to corrosion) | Stainless steel cutlery, surgical instruments |
| **Cathodic protection** | Connecting iron to a more reactive metal (Zn, Mg) so the sacrificial metal corrodes; or making iron the cathode by passing electrical current | Underground pipelines, ship hulls |
| **Anodising** | Making aluminium the anode in an electrolytic cell to thicken its natural oxide layer | Aluminium windows, aerospace components |
| **Greasing/oiling** | Barrier prevents moisture contact | Machine parts, tools |

**Galvanisation vs Tinning:**
- Galvanised iron: Even if Zn layer scratches, zinc still sacrificially protects iron (Zn is above Fe in reactivity series)
- Tin-coated iron: If tin layer scratches, bare iron and tin form a galvanic cell → iron (anode) corrodes FASTER than uncoated iron. This is why a dented tin can rusts faster inside.

## Electroplating

Electroplating uses electrolysis to deposit a thin layer of a desirable metal onto an object.

**Setup:** Object to be plated = CATHODE; Coating metal = ANODE; Electrolyte = solution of coating metal's salt.

| Article | Plating Metal | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Jewellery | Gold, Silver | Aesthetic appearance at lower cost |
| Car bumpers | Chromium | Hardness, corrosion resistance, shine |
| Cutlery | Nickel, Silver | Corrosion resistance, appearance |
| Electronic circuits | Gold, Silver | Excellent conductivity, corrosion resistance |
| Iron parts | Zinc (galvanisation), Nickel, Chromium | Corrosion prevention |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Most malleable metal = Gold (Au); most ductile metal = also Gold** — can be beaten to 0.1 μm thick sheets; one gram drawn to 2.5 km wire. (Silver is second in both.)
2. **Best electrical conductor = Silver (Ag)** — not copper, though copper is far more commonly used due to lower cost. Gold is third.
3. **Hardest natural substance = Diamond** (a non-metal/allotrope of carbon), NOT a metal. Hardest metal = chromium.
4. **Only liquid metal at room temperature = Mercury (Hg)** — Group 12, transition metal; highly toxic.
5. **Lightest metal = Lithium (Li)** — density 0.53 g/cm³; floats on water; so reactive it floats AND reacts with water simultaneously.
6. **Haematite (Fe₂O₃) is the most important ore of iron; Bauxite (Al₂O₃·2H₂O) is the most important ore of aluminium** — these two are asked constantly.
7. **Iron is extracted in a blast furnace; aluminium is extracted by Hall-Héroult electrolysis** — different methods because Al is too reactive for carbon reduction.
8. **Galvanised iron is coated with ZINC (not tin)** — zinc is the sacrificial anode; if the zinc layer cracks, zinc still protects iron. Tin is used in food cans (tinning), and tin-coated iron corrodes FASTER if scratched.
9. **Stainless steel contains iron + chromium (12–18%) + nickel (~8%)** — the chromium forms a thin, self-healing Cr₂O₃ layer that prevents rust.
10. **Bronze = Copper + Tin; Brass = Copper + Zinc** — confusing these is a very common exam trap. Brass has a yellow colour; bronze is more reddish-brown.
11. **Duralumin = Al + Cu + Mg + Mn** — used in aircraft because it is very light yet strong; higher strength-to-weight ratio than pure aluminium or steel.
12. **Solder = Lead + Tin (Pb + Sn)** — low melting point; used in electrical/plumbing joints. Lead-free solders (tin + silver) are replacing Pb+Sn due to RoHS (hazardous substance) regulations.
13. **Zinc blende (ZnS) is the main ore of zinc; Galena (PbS) is the main ore of lead; Cinnabar (HgS) is the main ore of mercury** — all sulphide ores.
14. **Thermite reaction: Al + Fe₂O₃ → Fe + Al₂O₃** (iron is obtained; used in welding rail tracks in-situ — thermite welding produces ~3000°C heat). Aluminium is more reactive than iron and displaces it.
15. **Gold is found in native form (as free Au) because it is at the bottom of the reactivity series** — too unreactive to combine with oxygen or other non-metals under natural conditions.
16. **Nichrome (Ni + Cr + Fe) is used in heating elements** — in electric toasters, electric heaters, electric furnaces — due to its high electrical resistance and high melting point (doesn't melt when hot).
