# Acids, Bases, Salts & pH

## The pH of Everyday Life

Your stomach has a pH of about 1.5 — as corrosive as battery acid. Your blood maintains a near-perfect pH of 7.4 with the precision of a Swiss watch. Milk turns sour when lactic acid drops its pH below 4.6. Black tea is acidic (pH ~4.9), but coffee is more acidic (pH ~5.0). Baking soda in your kitchen is a base (pH ~8.3) that neutralises the acid in heartburn.

Acids and bases are not exotic laboratory chemicals — they are the foundation of every biological process, every industrial chemical, and every bite of food you eat. Understanding the pH scale is understanding the chemistry of life.

## Theories of Acids and Bases

### 1. Arrhenius Theory (1884)

| Term | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Acid | Substance that produces H⁺ (proton) ions in water | HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻ |
| Base | Substance that produces OH⁻ (hydroxide) ions in water | NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻ |

**Limitation:** Cannot explain NH₃ as a base (produces no OH⁻ directly); cannot explain reactions without water.

### 2. Brønsted-Lowry Theory (1923)

| Term | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Acid | Proton (H⁺) DONOR | HCl donates H⁺ to water → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻ |
| Base | Proton (H⁺) ACCEPTOR | NH₃ accepts H⁺ from water → NH₄⁺ + OH⁻ |

**Conjugate acid-base pairs:** Every acid has a conjugate base (after losing H⁺); every base has a conjugate acid (after gaining H⁺).

### 3. Lewis Theory (1923, most general)

| Term | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Acid | Electron pair ACCEPTOR (Lewis acid) | BF₃ (empty orbital, accepts electron pair) |
| Base | Electron pair DONOR (Lewis base) | NH₃ (lone pair donor) |

**Widest definition:** Explains reactions with no H⁺ transfer at all.

## Properties of Acids

### Physical and Chemical Properties

| Property | Acids |
|---|---|
| Taste | Sour (Latin: 'acidus' = sour) |
| Touch | Corrosive (especially strong acids) |
| Litmus paper | Turn blue litmus RED |
| pH | Less than 7 (lower pH = stronger acid) |
| React with metals | Produce H₂ gas: Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑ |
| React with carbonates | Produce CO₂ gas: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑ |
| React with bases | Neutralisation → salt + water |
| Conduct electricity | If dissolved in water (electrolytes) |

### Strong vs Weak Acids

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong acid** | Completely ionises in water (100% dissociation) | HCl (hydrochloric), H₂SO₄ (sulphuric), HNO₃ (nitric), HClO₄ (perchloric), HBr, HI |
| **Weak acid** | Partially ionises in water (< 100% dissociation) | CH₃COOH (acetic/ethanoic), H₂CO₃ (carbonic), H₃PO₄ (phosphoric), H₂S (sulphurous), HF (hydrofluoric) |

**Concentrated vs Dilute** refers to the amount (how much acid in water), while **Strong vs Weak** refers to degree of ionisation. Concentrated weak acid ≠ strong acid.

## Properties of Bases

### Physical and Chemical Properties

| Property | Bases |
|---|---|
| Taste | Bitter |
| Touch | Soapy/slippery |
| Litmus paper | Turn red litmus BLUE |
| pH | Greater than 7 (higher pH = stronger base) |
| React with acids | Neutralisation → salt + water |
| React with metals (amphoteric metals) | NaOH + Al + H₂O → NaAlO₂ + H₂↑ |
| React with non-metallic oxides | Form salt + water: 2NaOH + CO₂ → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O |
| Conduct electricity | If dissolved in water (electrolytes) |

### Alkalis vs Bases

**Alkali:** A base that is soluble in water. All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis.
- CuO is a base (insoluble in water) — not an alkali
- NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂, NH₄OH are alkalis (soluble bases)

### Strong vs Weak Bases

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong base** | Completely ionises in water | NaOH (caustic soda), KOH (caustic potash), Ca(OH)₂ (slaked lime), LiOH, Ba(OH)₂ |
| **Weak base** | Partially ionises in water | NH₃ (ammonia), NH₄OH (ammonium hydroxide), Mg(OH)₂ (milk of magnesia), Al(OH)₃ |

## pH Scale

The pH scale measures the concentration of H⁺ ions in a solution on a logarithmic scale from 0 to 14.

**pH = −log₁₀[H⁺]**

| pH | Nature | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Very strong acid | Battery acid (H₂SO₄ concentrated) |
| 1–2 | Strong acid | Stomach acid (HCl, ~1.5); lemon juice (~2.0) |
| 3–4 | Weak acid | Vinegar (~3.0); tomato (~4.0); coffee (~5.0) |
| 5–6 | Mildly acidic | Rain water (~5.6); milk (~6.5) |
| **7** | **Neutral** | **Pure water at 25°C** |
| 8 | Mildly alkaline | Baking soda (~8.3); sea water (~8.2) |
| 9–10 | Weakly alkaline | Soap (~9–10); detergent |
| 11–12 | Strongly alkaline | Ammonia solution (~11) |
| 13–14 | Very strong base | Caustic soda (NaOH, ~14) |

**Key rule:** Each unit of pH represents a 10-fold change in [H⁺]. pH 3 is 10× more acidic than pH 4; 100× more acidic than pH 5.

**pH of blood:** 7.35–7.45 (slightly alkaline; if it drops below 7.35 = acidosis; above 7.45 = alkalosis — both life-threatening).

## Indicators

Indicators are substances that change colour depending on pH.

| Indicator | Colour in Acid | Colour in Neutral | Colour in Base | Range |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Litmus** (natural) | Red | Purple | Blue | 5–8 |
| **Phenolphthalein** | Colourless | Colourless | Pink/Magenta | 8.2–10 |
| **Methyl orange** | Red | Orange | Yellow | 3.1–4.4 |
| **Universal indicator** | Red/orange → acid | Green (neutral) | Blue/violet → alkali | 1–14 |
| **Turmeric** (haldi) | Yellow | Yellow | Red/brown | Natural indicator |
| **China rose (Gudhal)** | Magenta/deep pink | — | Green | Natural indicator |

**Red vs Blue Litmus:**
- Red litmus turns BLUE in base (Red to Blue = acid to base)
- Blue litmus turns RED in acid (Blue to Red = base to acid)

**Phenolphthalein is colourless in acid/neutral, pink in base** — used to detect base (alkali) or as endpoint indicator in acid-base titrations.

## Salts

Salts are formed when an acid reacts with a base (neutralisation). The anion comes from the acid; the cation comes from the base.

### Types of Salts by Parent Acid-Base

| Parent Acid | Parent Base | Salt | pH of solution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strong acid + strong base | HCl + NaOH | NaCl (common salt) | ~7 (neutral) |
| Strong acid + weak base | HCl + NH₄OH | NH₄Cl (ammonium chloride) | < 7 (acidic) |
| Weak acid + strong base | CH₃COOH + NaOH | CH₃COONa (sodium acetate) | > 7 (basic) |
| Weak acid + weak base | CH₃COOH + NH₄OH | CH₃COONH₄ (ammonium acetate) | ~7 (nearly neutral) |

### Normal, Acidic, and Basic Salts

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Normal/Neutral salt | All replaceable H⁺ ions replaced | NaCl, Na₂SO₄, K₂CO₃ |
| Acidic salt | Some H⁺ ions still replaceable | NaHCO₃ (baking soda), NaHSO₄, KHSO₄ |
| Basic salt | Contains unreacted OH⁻ or oxide | Pb(OH)NO₃, Ca(OH)Cl (bleaching powder) |
| Double salt | Two salts combined in one crystal | Alum (K₂SO₄·Al₂(SO₄)₃·24H₂O) = potash alum |

## Special Topics

### Plaster of Paris (POP)

**Making POP:** CaSO₄·2H₂O (gypsum) → heat at 120°C → (CaSO₄)₂·H₂O (POP) + 3H₂O

**Setting POP:** (CaSO₄)₂·H₂O + 3H₂O → CaSO₄·2H₂O (gypsum) — expands slightly on setting → sharp impressions

POP is used for surgical casts, moulds, fire protection, building boards.

### Baking Soda and Baking Powder

**Baking soda (NaHCO₃):** On heating: 2NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂↑ (CO₂ makes dough rise; but Na₂CO₃ residue is bitter)

**Baking powder = Baking soda + Tartaric acid/citric acid (cream of tartar):** The acid neutralises Na₂CO₃ → no bitter taste; CO₂ still produced.

### Bleaching Powder

CaO + Cl₂ → CaOCl₂ (bleaching powder) — reaction of slaked lime with chlorine gas

On contact with CO₂ (or dilute HCl): CaOCl₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃ + Cl₂↑ — releases chlorine gas, which does the bleaching.

Bleaching powder is used for: disinfecting water supply (releases chlorine), bleaching cotton/linen fabrics, sterilising swimming pools.

### Water Softening

**Hard water:** Contains dissolved Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ salts (sulphates, bicarbonates, chlorides).
- **Temporary hardness:** Due to Ca(HCO₃)₂ and Mg(HCO₃)₂ — removed by boiling (precipitates as CaCO₃ = 'scale' in kettles)
- **Permanent hardness:** Due to CaSO₄ and MgSO₄ — cannot be removed by boiling; removed by adding washing soda (Na₂CO₃) or ion exchange resins

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Litmus changes: Blue litmus turns RED in acid; Red litmus turns BLUE in base** — remember it as the opposite of intuition (blue → red in acid sounds wrong but is correct).
2. **Phenolphthalein is colourless in acid and neutral, pink/magenta in base** — used to detect bases (alkaline solutions), NOT acids.
3. **pH 7 = neutral (pure water at 25°C); pH < 7 = acidic; pH > 7 = basic** — blood pH is 7.35–7.45 (slightly alkaline, NOT neutral).
4. **Strong acid ≠ concentrated acid**: strong acid (HCl) ionises 100% in water; concentrated acid has a high amount of acid dissolved; dilute HCl is still a strong acid.
5. **Aqua regia = 1 part HNO₃ + 3 parts HCl** — dissolves gold; "aqua regia" = royal water in Latin; the only medium that dissolves noble metals like gold and platinum.
6. **NaHCO₃ (baking soda) is a WEAK base / mild alkali, pH ~8.3** — often mistaken for a salt. It produces CO₂ with acids: NaHCO₃ + HCl → NaCl + H₂O + CO₂↑.
7. **Gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O) → heated → Plaster of Paris (CaSO₄)₂·H₂O** — setting POP reverses this (absorbs water back). POP expands on setting.
8. **Washing soda = Na₂CO₃·10H₂O; Baking soda = NaHCO₃** — both sodium compounds, very different uses. Washing soda softens water; baking soda leavens bread.
9. **Bleaching powder releases chlorine when exposed to CO₂ or dilute acid** — actual bleaching agent is nascent chlorine (Cl), not the bleaching powder itself.
10. **Formic acid (methanoic acid/HCOOH) is responsible for ant stings and nettle sting** — the pain of a bee/wasp sting is also partly formic acid (and methanoic acid).
11. **Tartaric acid is present in tamarind (imli); oxalic acid in tomatoes and spinach; citric acid in lemon and orange** — these are natural organic acids frequently asked.
12. **Lactic acid = responsible for sour taste of curd; also produced in muscles during anaerobic exercise** (causes muscle soreness/cramps).
13. **Hard water vs Soft water:** Hard water does NOT form lather with soap (CaSO₄ reacts with soap to form scum); Soft water forms lather easily.
14. **Temporary hardness removed by BOILING; permanent hardness removed by washing soda or ion exchange** — common distinction.
15. **Potash alum [KAl(SO₄)₂·12H₂O] purifies water by coagulation** — alum causes suspended impurities to clump (flocculate) and settle.
16. **Uric acid → gout when elevated in blood** — not to be confused with urea (waste nitrogen in urine) or urine (which contains urea, not uric acid as the primary component).
