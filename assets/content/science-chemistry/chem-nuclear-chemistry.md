# Nuclear Chemistry — Radioactivity, Isotopes, Fission, Fusion & Nuclear Energy

## The Invisible Fire Inside Every Atom

In December 1938, German chemists Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann bombarded uranium with neutrons in their Berlin laboratory — and found barium in the products. Uranium (atomic number 92) had somehow become barium (atomic number 56). Their colleague Lise Meitner, working in exile in Stockholm, did the calculation: the nucleus had *split*, releasing energy beyond anything chemistry had produced. She called it *fission* — Latin for splitting.

That single insight launched the Atomic Age. Within seven years, nuclear reactors were generating power, and two cities in Japan lay flattened by weapons the world had never imagined. Today, nuclear energy supplies about 10% of global electricity, and India operates 22 nuclear reactors producing 7,480 MW. Understanding the atom's nucleus — its stabilities, its instabilities, and its enormous hidden energy — is the subject of this lesson.

---

## Part 1 — The Nucleus: What's Inside?

The atom has a tiny, dense nucleus made of **protons** (positively charged) and **neutrons** (neutral). Together they are called **nucleons**.

| Particle | Location | Charge | Mass (amu) |
|----------|----------|--------|------------|
| Proton | Nucleus | +1 | 1 |
| Neutron | Nucleus | 0 | 1 |
| Electron | Shells | −1 | 1/1836 |

**Atomic number (Z)** = number of protons → defines the element  
**Mass number (A)** = protons + neutrons  
**Number of neutrons** = A − Z

The nucleus is held together by the **strong nuclear force** — the most powerful force in nature, acting over incredibly short distances (~10⁻¹⁵ m). It overwhelms the electrostatic repulsion between protons. But when there are too many protons or the proton-to-neutron ratio is wrong, the nucleus becomes **unstable** and undergoes **radioactive decay**.

## Part 2 — Radioactivity

**Radioactivity** is the spontaneous emission of radiation from an unstable nucleus. Discovered by **Henri Becquerel** in 1896 (uranium ores spontaneously exposed photographic plates). Investigated deeply by **Marie Curie** — she coined the term "radioactivity," discovered polonium (named for her homeland Poland) and radium, and won **two Nobel Prizes** (Physics 1903, Chemistry 1911).

### Three Types of Radiation

| Radiation | Symbol | Nature | Charge | Penetration | Stopped By |
|-----------|--------|--------|--------|-------------|------------|
| Alpha | α | He nucleus (2p + 2n) | +2 | Lowest | Paper / skin |
| Beta | β | High-speed electron (from n → p + e⁻) | −1 | Medium | Aluminium sheet |
| Gamma | γ | High-energy electromagnetic wave | 0 | Highest | Lead / thick concrete |

**Memory trick — "P-A-L":** Paper stops Alpha; Aluminium stops beta; Lead stops gamma.

### Key Radioactive Decay Facts
- **Alpha decay**: nucleus loses 2 protons + 2 neutrons → atomic number drops by 2, mass number drops by 4
- **Beta decay**: a neutron converts to a proton + electron → atomic number increases by 1, mass number unchanged
- **Gamma decay**: nucleus releases energy without changing proton/neutron count — accompanies alpha or beta

### Half-Life (t½)
The time for half of a radioactive sample to decay. Each element has a fixed half-life:

| Isotope | Half-Life | Use / Significance |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| Carbon-14 (¹⁴C) | 5,730 years | Radiocarbon dating of organic remains |
| Uranium-238 (²³⁸U) | 4.5 billion years | Dating rocks, Earth's age |
| Iodine-131 (¹³¹I) | 8 days | Thyroid cancer treatment |
| Cobalt-60 (⁶⁰Co) | 5.27 years | Cancer radiotherapy (gamma rays) |
| Strontium-90 (⁹⁰Sr) | 28.8 years | Nuclear fallout hazard |
| Plutonium-239 (²³⁹Pu) | 24,100 years | Nuclear weapons, reactors |

**After n half-lives → 1/2ⁿ of original remains.** After 3 half-lives: 1/8 remains.

## Part 3 — Isotopes, Isobars & Isotones

These three terms cause enormous exam confusion. Fix them with one table:

| Term | Same | Different | Example |
|------|------|-----------|---------|
| **Isotopes** | Atomic number (Z) = same element | Mass number (A) | ¹H, ²H (deuterium), ³H (tritium) |
| **Isobars** | Mass number (A) | Atomic number (Z) = different elements | ⁴⁰Ar and ⁴⁰Ca |
| **Isotones** | Number of neutrons | Atomic number & mass number | ¹⁴C and ¹⁶O (both 8 neutrons) |

### Important Isotopes to Know

**Hydrogen — the only element whose isotopes have individual names:**
- **Protium** (¹H): ordinary hydrogen, 1p, 0n
- **Deuterium** (²H or D): "heavy hydrogen", 1p, 1n — used in heavy water (D₂O), nuclear reactors (moderator)
- **Tritium** (³H or T): radioactive, 1p, 2n — used in thermonuclear weapons

**Uranium isotopes:**
- **Uranium-235** (²³⁵U): fissile (undergoes fission with slow neutrons), only 0.7% of natural uranium
- **Uranium-238** (²³⁸U): 99.3% of natural uranium, not directly fissile, fertile (can be converted to Pu-239)

**Carbon isotopes:**
- **Carbon-12** (¹²C): standard for atomic mass unit (1 amu = 1/12 mass of ¹²C)
- **Carbon-14** (¹⁴C): radioactive, used in radiocarbon dating

## Part 4 — Nuclear Fission

**Fission**: A heavy nucleus (like U-235 or Pu-239) absorbs a neutron and splits into two medium-sized nuclei + 2–3 neutrons + enormous energy.

```
²³⁵U + ¹n → ¹⁴¹Ba + ⁹²Kr + 3¹n + ENERGY (200 MeV per fission)
```

The 2–3 released neutrons can trigger further fissions — a **chain reaction**.

### Controlled vs Uncontrolled Chain Reaction

| Type | Description | Application |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Controlled** | Neutrons regulated; energy released steadily | Nuclear reactor (power plant) |
| **Uncontrolled** | Chain reaction accelerates exponentially | Atom bomb (Little Boy, Fat Man) |

### Critical Mass
The minimum amount of fissile material needed to sustain a chain reaction. For U-235: ~52 kg (sphere). For Pu-239: ~10 kg.

### Inside a Nuclear Reactor

| Component | Role | Material Used |
|-----------|------|---------------|
| **Fuel** | Fissile material | Enriched U-235 or Pu-239 |
| **Moderator** | Slows neutrons (thermal neutrons are better at causing fission) | Heavy water (D₂O), graphite, light water |
| **Control rods** | Absorb excess neutrons to control reaction rate | Cadmium (Cd) or boron (B) |
| **Coolant** | Transfers heat to turbine | Water, CO₂, liquid sodium |
| **Reflector** | Reflects escaping neutrons back | Beryllium, graphite |
| **Shielding** | Protects workers from radiation | Thick concrete, lead |

**India uses PHWR (Pressurised Heavy Water Reactor)** — moderated and cooled by heavy water (D₂O). Heavy water is produced at Kota, Rajasthan (Rawatbhata). India does NOT use enriched uranium in its civilian reactors — it uses natural uranium (0.7% U-235).

## Part 5 — Nuclear Fusion

**Fusion**: Two light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus + enormous energy.

```
²H + ³H → ⁴He + ¹n + 17.6 MeV
```

Deuterium + Tritium → Helium + neutron

**Fusion releases 3–4× MORE energy per kilogram than fission**, but requires temperatures of **100 million °C** (to overcome the electrostatic repulsion between positively charged nuclei). This is why it powers stars — the Sun's core is ~15 million °C, sustained by gravity's enormous compression.

### Fission vs Fusion Quick Comparison

| Feature | Fission | Fusion |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Process | Splitting heavy nucleus | Joining light nuclei |
| Fuel | U-235, Pu-239 | Deuterium, Tritium |
| Energy per kg | ~8 × 10¹³ J/kg | ~3 × 10¹⁴ J/kg |
| By-products | Radioactive waste | Helium (safe), neutrons |
| Technology | Commercially viable | Still experimental (ITER) |
| Weapon | Atom bomb | Hydrogen bomb (H-bomb) |

**H-bomb (thermonuclear weapon)**: Uses a fission bomb as the "trigger" to generate the extreme heat needed to ignite fusion. Far more powerful than atom bombs — Tsar Bomba (1961, Soviet) was 50 megatons.

**ITER** (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor) — under construction in Cadarache, France. Aims to produce 10× more energy than input (Q ≥ 10) by sustained fusion plasma. India is a member.

## Part 6 — Nuclear Energy in India

India's nuclear programme follows the **Three-Stage Nuclear Programme** designed by **Dr Homi J. Bhabha** (father of India's nuclear programme):

| Stage | Reactor Type | Fuel | By-product |
|-------|-------------|------|------------|
| **Stage 1** | Pressurised Heavy Water Reactors (PHWR) | Natural Uranium | Plutonium-239 |
| **Stage 2** | Fast Breeder Reactors (FBR) | Pu-239 + U-238 | U-233 (from thorium) |
| **Stage 3** | Advanced Heavy Water Reactors | U-233 + Thorium-232 | — |

**Why this three-stage plan?** India has the world's largest **thorium** reserves (~25% of world's total, in Kerala's beach sands — monazite ore). But thorium is not fissile — it must be converted to fissile U-233 in Stage 2 reactors. Stage 3 will then exploit India's thorium wealth.

### Key Nuclear Facts About India
- **First nuclear test**: Pokhran-I (Smiling Buddha), May 1974
- **Second nuclear test**: Pokhran-II (Operation Shakti), May 1998
- **Atomic Energy Commission (AEC)**: set up 1948, chaired by PM
- **Department of Atomic Energy (DAE)**: reports directly to PM
- **Nuclear Power Corporation of India (NPCIL)**: operates nuclear plants
- **BARC** (Bhabha Atomic Research Centre): Trombay, Mumbai — R&D hub
- **IGCAR** (Indira Gandhi Centre for Atomic Research): Kalpakkam — FBR research
- **TIFR** (Tata Institute of Fundamental Research): basic nuclear research
- **Kudankulam Nuclear Power Plant**: 2 × 1,000 MW Russian VVER reactors, Tamil Nadu — largest in India
- **First nuclear plant**: Tarapur Atomic Power Station (TAPS), Maharashtra (1969)

## Part 7 — Radiation Units & Effects

| Unit | Measures | Context |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Becquerel (Bq)** | Activity (decays per second) | SI unit |
| **Curie (Ci)** | Activity (older unit) | 1 Ci = 3.7 × 10¹⁰ Bq |
| **Gray (Gy)** | Absorbed dose (energy per kg) | SI unit |
| **Sievert (Sv)** | Effective dose (biological harm) | SI unit for radiation safety |
| **Röntgen (R)** | Exposure to ionising radiation | Older unit |

**Background radiation** = natural radiation we all receive from cosmic rays, radon gas, potassium-40 in body, thorium in soil. Average ~2.4 mSv/year globally.

### Health Effects of Radiation
- Low dose: increased cancer risk over time
- High dose: **Acute Radiation Syndrome** (ARS) — nausea, hair loss, immune failure
- **LD₅₀**: 4–5 Sv (lethal dose for 50% of humans in 30 days without treatment)
- Bone marrow (blood cell production) and gut lining most sensitive to radiation

### Nuclear Accidents to Know
| Accident | Year | Country | Reactor |
|----------|------|---------|---------|
| Three Mile Island | 1979 | USA | PWR (partial meltdown) |
| **Chernobyl** | 1986 | USSR (Ukraine) | RBMK-1000 (graphite moderated) |
| **Fukushima Daiichi** | 2011 | Japan | BWR (tsunami-triggered) |

Chernobyl and Fukushima are both rated **Level 7** (maximum) on the INES (International Nuclear Event Scale).

## Part 8 — Applications of Radioactivity (Beyond Power)

**Medical:**
- **PET scan**: Positron Emission Tomography — radioactive glucose (F-18) injected, tumors glow
- **Iodine-131**: thyroid cancer treatment (iodine accumulates in thyroid)
- **Cobalt-60**: cancer radiotherapy (gamma knife)
- **Technetium-99m**: most widely used medical radioisotope — bone, brain scans
- **X-rays**: ionising EM radiation (not nuclear decay, but same ionising-radiation family)

**Industrial / Scientific:**
- **Radiocarbon dating** (C-14): age organic remains up to ~50,000 years
- **Uranium-lead dating** (U-238): age rocks and Earth (~4.54 billion years old)
- **Nuclear irradiation**: sterilise medical equipment, food preservation
- **Smoke detectors**: Americium-241 (alpha emitter) ionises air; smoke disrupts current

**Agricultural:**
- Induce mutations to develop high-yield crop varieties
- Track nutrient uptake using radioactive phosphorus (P-32) as tracer

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Becquerel discovered radioactivity** (1896) by accident with uranium ore and photographic plates — NOT Marie Curie. Curie coined the term and discovered polonium and radium.

2. **Marie Curie won TWO Nobel Prizes** — Physics (1903, with Pierre Curie and Becquerel) and Chemistry (1911). She remains the only person to win Nobel in two different sciences.

3. **Isotopes** = same atomic number, different mass number. **Isobars** = same mass number, different atomic number. Isobars are different elements.

4. **Alpha particles** = helium nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons), charge +2. They are the LEAST penetrating but MOST ionising. Stopped by a sheet of paper.

5. **Gamma rays** are not particles — they are electromagnetic waves (like X-rays but higher energy). No charge, no mass. MOST penetrating.

6. **Beta decay**: neutron → proton + electron (beta particle). Atomic number increases by 1, mass number unchanged.

7. **Carbon-14 half-life is 5,730 years** (not 5,568 — that was the old Libby value, updated in 1962). Used for dating organic material up to ~50,000 years.

8. **Control rods in a nuclear reactor are made of cadmium or boron** — they absorb neutrons to slow/stop the chain reaction. NOT graphite (graphite is the moderator in some reactors).

9. **India's nuclear reactors use heavy water (D₂O) as moderator**, not graphite. The Chernobyl reactor used graphite moderation — graphite fire made the disaster worse.

10. **Nuclear fusion powers the Sun** and stars — not fission. The Sun fuses hydrogen (protons) into helium via the proton-proton chain. Fission is used in nuclear power plants on Earth.

11. **H-bomb (thermonuclear bomb) is fusion-based**, triggered by a fission (atom) bomb. It is far more powerful than an atom bomb alone.

12. **Homi J. Bhabha** is the father of India's nuclear programme. He designed the Three-Stage plan exploiting India's thorium reserves. He died in a plane crash (1966, Mont Blanc).

13. **India's first nuclear test** was Pokhran-I "Smiling Buddha" in **May 1974** under PM Indira Gandhi. Pokhran-II "Operation Shakti" was in **May 1998** under PM Vajpayee.

14. **Tarapur** (Maharashtra) was India's first nuclear power plant (1969). **Kudankulam** (Tamil Nadu, Russian collaboration) is the largest.

15. **Chernobyl (1986) and Fukushima (2011)** are both rated Level 7 on INES — the maximum level. Three Mile Island (1979) was Level 5.

16. **Technetium-99m** is the most widely used diagnostic radioisotope in medicine — used in bone scans, cardiac imaging, brain scans. It emits gamma rays with ideal 6-hour half-life (short enough to minimise patient dose, long enough to complete the scan).
