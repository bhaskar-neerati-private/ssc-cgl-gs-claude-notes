# Fertilizers, Pesticides & Agricultural Chemistry — Urea, DAP, NPK, DDT & Organophosphates

## The Nitrogen Paradox

In 1909, Fritz Haber stood in a laboratory in Karlsruhe, Germany, and demonstrated something that would alter the course of human history: he made nitrogen gas from the air into ammonia. Nitrogen makes up 78% of the atmosphere, but it sits in an almost inert triple bond (N≡N) that almost nothing in nature can break — except a few bacteria and Haber's iron catalyst at 400–500°C.

That ammonia became urea, then fertilizer. **Without the Haber Process**, scientists estimate the Earth's soil could sustain roughly 3.5 billion people. The world's current population is 8 billion. The difference — roughly half of humanity's food today — exists because of the chemistry of fertilizers.

But that same chemistry created a darker legacy: nitrogen fertilizers cause river dead zones, DDT wiped out eagle populations, and organophosphate pesticides poisoned farmworkers. Agricultural chemistry is inseparable from both feeding humanity and poisoning it. That tension is what SSC tests — and what this lesson unpacks.

## Part 1 — Plant Nutrients: What Plants Need

Plants need 17 essential nutrients, divided into macronutrients and micronutrients:

### Macronutrients (needed in large amounts)

| Nutrient | Symbol | Role | Deficiency |
|----------|--------|------|-----------|
| **Nitrogen** | N | Leaf growth, chlorophyll, proteins | Yellowing of older leaves (chlorosis) |
| **Phosphorus** | P | Root development, flowering, energy (ATP, DNA) | Purple/red tints, stunted roots |
| **Potassium** | K | Water regulation, enzyme activation, stem strength | Brown leaf edges, weak stems |
| **Calcium** | Ca | Cell walls, root tip development | Tip burn, blossom end rot |
| **Magnesium** | Mg | Central atom of chlorophyll | Interveinal chlorosis |
| **Sulphur** | S | Amino acids, proteins | Yellowing of young leaves |
| **Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen** | C, H, O | From air/water — basic building blocks | N/A |

### Micronutrients (needed in trace amounts)
Iron (Fe), Manganese (Mn), Zinc (Zn), Copper (Cu), Boron (B), Molybdenum (Mo), Chlorine (Cl)

**The three most commonly limiting nutrients**: **N, P, K** — hence fertilizers are classified as N, P, K fertilizers and the NPK ratio on bags.

## Part 2 — Types of Fertilizers

### Chemical (Synthetic) Fertilizers

**Nitrogenous Fertilizers** (supply N):

| Fertilizer | Chemical Formula | N Content | Key Facts |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Urea** | CO(NH₂)₂ | 46% N — highest | Most widely used N fertilizer in India; made from NH₃ + CO₂; synthetic organic compound |
| **Ammonium Sulphate** | (NH₄)₂SO₄ | 21% N | Also supplies sulphur; makes soil acidic |
| **Ammonium Nitrate** | NH₄NO₃ | 33% N | Explosive risk; restricted in India; fast-acting |
| **Calcium Ammonium Nitrate (CAN)** | Ca(NO₃)₂ + NH₄NO₃ | 26% N | Safer than pure ammonium nitrate |
| **Anhydrous Ammonia** | NH₃ | 82% N — pure | Injected directly into soil; requires specialised equipment |

**Phosphatic Fertilizers** (supply P):

| Fertilizer | Key Facts |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Single Superphosphate (SSP)** | Ca(H₂PO₄)₂ + CaSO₄; 16% P₂O₅; also supplies sulphur and calcium |
| **Triple Superphosphate (TSP)** | Ca(H₂PO₄)₂; 46% P₂O₅; most concentrated P fertilizer |
| **Di-Ammonium Phosphate (DAP)** | (NH₄)₂HPO₄; 46% P₂O₅ + 18% N; **most popular complex fertilizer in India** |
| **Rock Phosphate** | Ca₃(PO₄)₂; slow-release; used in acidic soils |

**DAP (Di-Ammonium Phosphate)** — the most used phosphatic fertilizer in India and one of the most widely used globally. Used at sowing to promote root development. Approved for subsidised distribution in India.

**Potassic Fertilizers** (supply K):

| Fertilizer | Chemical | K₂O Content |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| **Muriate of Potash (MOP)** | KCl | 60% K₂O — cheapest and most common |
| **Sulphate of Potash (SOP)** | K₂SO₄ | 50% K₂O — also supplies sulphur; for chloride-sensitive crops |

### NPK Complex Fertilizers

These contain two or three of the N, P, K nutrients in one granule — more uniform distribution:
- **10:26:26** (N:P:K) — for general use
- **12:32:16** — for rainfed crops
- **19:19:19** — balanced formulation

### Biofertilizers

Living microorganisms that enhance soil fertility naturally:

| Biofertilizer | Organism | Function |
|--------------|---------|---------|
| **Rhizobium** | Bacteria (root nodules of legumes) | Nitrogen fixation — converts N₂ to NH₃/NO₃⁻ |
| **Azotobacter** | Free-living bacteria | Nitrogen fixation in soil |
| **Azospirillum** | Bacteria | N₂ fixation in grass/cereal roots |
| **Blue-Green Algae (BGA)** | Cyanobacteria (Anabaena, Nostoc) | N₂ fixation; used in paddy fields |
| **Mycorrhiza** | Fungi + plant roots | Enhance phosphorus absorption |
| **Phosphate Solubilising Bacteria (PSB)** | Various bacteria | Convert insoluble P to soluble form |

**Rhizobium** is the most important biofertilizer — it forms nodules on roots of legumes (pea, soybean, groundnut, lentil) and fixes atmospheric nitrogen. This is why rotating crops with legumes restores soil nitrogen.

### Organic Fertilizers (Manures)

| Type | Source | Benefits |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Farmyard Manure (FYM)** | Cattle dung, urine, straw | Slow-release NPK; improves soil structure |
| **Compost** | Decomposed organic waste | Improves soil texture, water retention |
| **Vermicompost** | Earthworm-processed organic matter | Rich in nutrients and growth hormones |
| **Green manure** | Leguminous crop ploughed back | Adds N; improves organic matter |
| **Biogas slurry** | Spent material from biogas plant | Rich in N; excellent liquid manure |

## Part 3 — Fertilizer Policy in India

- **Urea is subsidised** by the Indian government and is the most consumed fertilizer
- **NBS (Nutrient Based Subsidy)** policy: subsidy given per kg of nutrient (N, P, K, S) in non-urea fertilizers
- India imports large quantities of DAP and MOP (limited domestic production)
- **IFFCO (Indian Farmers Fertiliser Cooperative)** and **KRIBHCO** are major cooperative fertilizer producers
- **Neem-coated urea**: mandatory since 2015 — neem coating slows nitrogen release (reduces losses, prevents diversion to industrial use)

## Part 4 — Pesticides

**Pesticide** = any substance used to destroy, suppress, or repel pests (insects, weeds, fungi, rodents, nematodes).

### Classification by Target

| Type | Target | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Insecticide** | Insects | DDT, Malathion, Chlorpyrifos, Endosulfan |
| **Herbicide** | Weeds | 2,4-D, Glyphosate, Paraquat |
| **Fungicide** | Fungi | Mancozeb, Carbendazim, Copper oxychloride |
| **Rodenticide** | Rats/rodents | Zinc phosphide, Brodifacoum |
| **Nematicide** | Nematodes (soil worms) | Carbofuran (also insecticide) |
| **Acaricide** | Mites/ticks | Dicofol |

### Classification by Chemical Nature

**1. Organochlorines (Chlorinated Hydrocarbons)**

| Pesticide | Full Name | Status | Notes |
|-----------|----------|--------|-------|
| **DDT** | Dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane | **Banned** globally (Stockholm Convention 2001) | First widely used synthetic insecticide; Paul Müller Nobel Prize 1948; persistent in environment; biomagnification; thins eggshells |
| **BHC/Lindane** | Benzene Hexachloride | Banned | Soil and seed treatment |
| **Endosulfan** | — | Banned in India (2011 SC order) | Used in cashew; caused Kasargod tragedy |
| **Dieldrin, Aldrin** | — | Banned | Very persistent |

**2. Organophosphates**

| Pesticide | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| **Malathion** | Least toxic to mammals; used in mosquito control |
| **Chlorpyrifos** | Widely used in agriculture; concerns about neurotoxicity to children |
| **Parathion** | Highly toxic; restricted |
| **Dichlorvos (DDVP)** | Used in mosquito coils, vapourisers |

Organophosphates work by inhibiting **acetylcholinesterase** — the enzyme that breaks down acetylcholine at nerve synapses. Without this enzyme, acetylcholine accumulates, causing continuous nerve firing → muscle paralysis, convulsions. Used as nerve agents in chemical warfare (e.g., Sarin, VX are weaponised organophosphates).

**3. Carbamates**
- **Carbofuran, Carbaryl (Sevin)**: inhibit acetylcholinesterase (similar mechanism to organophosphates) but less persistent
- Carbofuran: banned in EU and USA; still used in some developing countries

**4. Pyrethroids (Synthetic)**
- Derived from natural pyrethrin (from chrysanthemum flowers)
- **Permethrin, Cypermethrin, Deltamethrin**
- Low toxicity to mammals; widely used in mosquito nets, household sprays
- Knock-down insecticides — fast-acting

**5. Herbicides**
- **2,4-D** (2,4-Dichlorophenoxyacetic acid): selective herbicide — kills broadleaf weeds, safe for grasses/cereals; was a component of Agent Orange (2,4-D + 2,4,5-T)
- **Glyphosate** (Roundup): non-selective; kills almost all plants; WHO classified as "probably carcinogenic" in 2015; widely debated
- **Paraquat**: highly toxic to humans; banned in many countries

## Part 5 — Biomagnification (Bioaccumulation)

**Biomagnification** = the progressive increase in concentration of a persistent pollutant as it moves up the food chain.

Classic example — DDT in aquatic ecosystems:

```
Water (0.000003 ppm) → Phytoplankton (0.04 ppm) → Fish (2 ppm) → Fish-eating birds (25 ppm)
```

Multiplication factor: ~10 million times from water to eagles.

**Why DDT was banned**: It accumulated in bird fat, causing thinning of eggshells (calcium metabolism interference) → eggs broke before hatching → population crash of bald eagles, peregrine falcons, ospreys. Rachel Carson's 1962 book **"Silent Spring"** documented this — it launched the modern environmental movement and led to DDT being banned in the USA in 1972.

**Stockholm Convention (2001)**: International treaty to eliminate or restrict the **Dirty Dozen** — 12 most persistent organic pollutants (POPs) including DDT, dieldrin, aldrin. India is a signatory.

## Part 6 — Environmental Impacts of Agricultural Chemicals

### Soil Degradation
- Excess N fertilizer → soil acidification (ammonium oxidises to NO₃⁻, releasing H⁺)
- Heavy pesticide use → kills beneficial soil organisms, earthworms
- **Salinisation** from excess irrigation (not fertilizer, but related agricultural problem)

### Water Pollution
- **Eutrophication**: excess N and P from fertilizer runoff → algal blooms → algae die → decomposition consumes O₂ → dead zone where fish cannot survive
- **Blue baby syndrome (Methemoglobinaemia)**: excess nitrates in drinking water (>50 ppm) → infants convert NO₃⁻ to NO₂⁻ → binds haemoglobin more tightly than O₂ → impairs oxygen transport → bluish skin

### Organic Farming
- Avoids synthetic pesticides and fertilizers
- Relies on FYM, compost, biofertilizers, crop rotation, biological pest control
- **National Programme for Organic Production (NPOP)**: India's certification body
- India is world's largest organic farmer by number (but small % of total farmland)

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Urea has the highest nitrogen content (46%)** among common fertilizers. It is the most widely used nitrogenous fertilizer in India. Chemically it is an organic compound (CO(NH₂)₂) — notably the first organic compound synthesised in a lab by Wöhler (1828).

2. **DAP (Di-Ammonium Phosphate)** is the most popular phosphatic fertilizer in India. It provides both N (18%) and P (46% as P₂O₅). It is used at sowing time to promote root development.

3. **DDT was discovered by Paul Müller**, who won the Nobel Prize in Medicine/Physiology in 1948. It was the first widely used synthetic insecticide. Despite its effectiveness against malaria-carrying mosquitoes, it was banned due to environmental persistence and biomagnification.

4. **Organophosphates inhibit acetylcholinesterase** — the enzyme that breaks down acetylcholine at nerve junctions. This causes acetylcholine to accumulate, leading to continuous nerve firing. Sarin, VX (nerve agents) and Malathion, Chlorpyrifos (pesticides) work by the same mechanism.

5. **Biomagnification** (bioaccumulation through food chain) applies to fat-soluble, persistent chemicals like DDT, PCBs, mercury. Water-soluble chemicals are excreted and do not biomagnify. The highest concentrations appear at the TOP of the food chain (apex predators: hawks, dolphins, humans).

6. **Rachel Carson's "Silent Spring" (1962)** documented DDT's effect on bird populations — thinning eggshells — and launched the modern environmental movement. It directly led to the US DDT ban in 1972.

7. **Rhizobium** bacteria live in root nodules of legumes (peas, beans, soybean, groundnut) and fix atmospheric nitrogen. This is why legumes are used in crop rotation — they naturally enrich soil nitrogen, reducing need for fertilizer.

8. **Blue-Green Algae (BGA/Cyanobacteria)** like Anabaena and Nostoc fix nitrogen and are used as biofertilizers in paddy (rice) fields. They simultaneously fix N₂ and produce oxygen through photosynthesis.

9. **Endosulfan was banned in India in 2011** by a Supreme Court order following the Kasargod tragedy (Kerala), where aerial spraying on cashew plantations caused severe health problems — birth defects, neurological disorders — in nearby villages.

10. **Stockholm Convention (2001)** targets Persistent Organic Pollutants (POPs) — the "Dirty Dozen" originally, now expanded. DDT is on the list (with a specific exception for malaria control in disease-endemic countries where alternatives are not available).

11. **Muriate of Potash (MOP) = Potassium Chloride (KCl)** — the most common and cheapest potassic fertilizer. Sulphate of Potash (SOP = K₂SO₄) is used for chloride-sensitive crops like tobacco, grapes, potatoes.

12. **Eutrophication** = nutrient pollution of water (excess N and P from fertilizer runoff) → algal bloom → algae die → decomposers consume O₂ → hypoxic dead zone. Major problem in India's lakes and coastal areas near agricultural regions.

13. **Neem-coated urea** was made mandatory in India (2015) to slow nitrogen release (improves efficiency, reduces volatilisation loss) and also to prevent diversion of subsidised urea for industrial use (neem makes it unpalatable for industry).

14. **2,4-D herbicide** selectively kills broadleaf (dicot) weeds and is safe for cereals (monocots). It was a component of Agent Orange used in Vietnam War. It works by mimicking a plant hormone (auxin) and causing uncontrolled growth.

15. **Glyphosate (Roundup)** is a non-selective, broad-spectrum herbicide — it kills virtually all plants by inhibiting the EPSPS enzyme (in the shikimate pathway). It is safe for "Roundup Ready" genetically modified crops engineered to resist it. WHO's IARC classified it as "probably carcinogenic" in 2015 (Group 2A).

16. **Phosphorus in soil is often "fixed" (made insoluble)** — P readily binds with Ca, Al, Fe to form insoluble compounds plants cannot absorb. Phosphate Solubilising Bacteria (PSB) convert insoluble P to soluble forms. This is why raw rock phosphate isn't efficient — SSP and TSP are processed to make P available.
