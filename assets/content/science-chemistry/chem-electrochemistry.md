# Electrochemistry — Electrolysis, Electroplating, Galvanic Cells & Batteries

## Alessandro Volta's Revenge on Luigi Galvani

In 1780, Luigi Galvani touched the leg of a dead frog with two different metals and watched it twitch. He called it "animal electricity" — he believed electricity was a property of living tissue. His friend and rival Alessandro Volta disagreed: the electricity came from the *metals*, not the frog. In 1800, Volta stacked zinc and silver discs separated by cloth soaked in brine, and demonstrated the world's first electric battery — the **Voltaic Pile**. The frog was just a very sensitive galvanometer.

Galvani was honoured with the unit for electric current (galvanism, later "current" in general), and Volta got the unit of electric potential — the **volt**. Both were right in their way: chemistry really can drive electricity, and electricity can drive chemistry. This two-way relationship is **electrochemistry**.

## Part 1 — Electrolytes and Electrolytic Conduction

**Electrolyte**: A substance that conducts electricity in dissolved or molten form by the movement of ions.

| Type | Example | Behaviour |
|------|---------|-----------|
| **Strong electrolyte** | HCl, NaOH, KNO₃, H₂SO₄ | Fully ionises in water — highly conducting |
| **Weak electrolyte** | CH₃COOH (acetic acid), NH₃, HF | Partially ionises — weakly conducting |
| **Non-electrolyte** | Glucose, ethanol, urea | Does not ionise — does not conduct |

**Electronic conduction** (metals): electrons carry charge, metal is not consumed.  
**Electrolytic conduction** (electrolytes): ions carry charge, electrodes may be consumed or products form.

## Part 2 — Electrolysis

**Electrolysis** = using an external electric current to drive a **non-spontaneous** chemical reaction. Current is forced through an electrolytic solution, decomposing it.

### Components of an Electrolytic Cell

| Component | Role |
|-----------|------|
| **Electrolyte** | Ionic solution/melt that conducts current |
| **Electrodes** | Conductors dipped in electrolyte (usually carbon, platinum, or metal) |
| **Anode** | Connected to + terminal; oxidation occurs here (**OA** — Oxidation at Anode) |
| **Cathode** | Connected to − terminal; reduction occurs here (**RC** — Reduction at Cathode) |
| **Power supply** | Provides the driving force (voltage) |

**Memory trick**: "An Ox and Red Cat" — **An**ode → **Ox**idation; **Red**uction → **Cat**hode.

### Electrolysis of Water (H₂O)
Electrolyte = dilute H₂SO₄ (H₂O alone is too poor a conductor)

- **Cathode (−)**: 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂↑ (hydrogen gas)
- **Anode (+)**: 2H₂O → O₂↑ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ (oxygen gas)
- **Volume ratio**: H₂ : O₂ = 2:1 (twice as much hydrogen as oxygen)

### Electrolysis of Brine (NaCl solution) — Chlor-Alkali Process
- **Cathode**: 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂↑ + 2OH⁻ → **NaOH** forms
- **Anode**: 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂↑ + 2e⁻ → **Chlorine gas** forms
- Three products: NaOH, Cl₂, H₂ → all industrially valuable

### Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

**First Law**: Mass of substance deposited at an electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity (charge) passed.  
m ∝ Q, where Q = It (current × time)

**Second Law**: When the same quantity of electricity passes through different electrolytes, masses deposited are proportional to their equivalent weights (molar mass/valence).

**Michael Faraday** (1791–1867) — discoverer of electromagnetic induction AND electrolysis laws. The SI unit of charge, the **Faraday (F)**, equals 96,485 coulombs — the charge carried by one mole of electrons.

## Part 3 — Electroplating

**Electroplating** = depositing a thin layer of one metal over another using electrolysis, to improve appearance, prevent corrosion, or add hardness.

### How Electroplating Works

- **Object to be plated** = Cathode (connected to −ve terminal)
- **Metal that forms the coating** = Anode (connected to +ve terminal)
- **Electrolyte** = Solution of the coating metal's salt

**Example — Silver plating a spoon:**
- Cathode: spoon (iron/steel)
- Anode: pure silver rod
- Electrolyte: silver nitrate (AgNO₃) solution
- Result: Ag deposits uniformly on the spoon

### Common Electroplating Applications

| Object | Plated With | Reason |
|--------|------------|--------|
| Jewellery (copper base) | Gold, silver | Appearance |
| Car bumpers, taps | Chromium (chrome plating) | Hardness, appearance, corrosion resistance |
| Steel cans (tin cans) | Tin | Non-toxic, prevents rusting |
| Electrical connectors | Gold | Conductivity, corrosion resistance |
| Engine parts | Nickel | Hardness, heat resistance |
| Galvanisation | Zinc | Sacrificial protection of iron/steel |

**Galvanisation** (zinc coating on iron) is done by **hot-dip galvanisation** (not electroplating) OR by electrogalvanisation. Zinc acts as a **sacrificial anode** — it corrodes preferentially, protecting the underlying iron even if the coating is scratched.

## Part 4 — Galvanic (Voltaic) Cells

A **galvanic cell** converts chemical energy → electrical energy through a **spontaneous** redox reaction. The opposite of an electrolytic cell.

### The Daniell Cell — Classic Example

Invented by John Frederic Daniell in 1836.

```
Zn | ZnSO₄ (aq) || CuSO₄ (aq) | Cu
  Anode (−)   salt bridge    Cathode (+)
```

- **Anode (Zn, −)**: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ (oxidation; zinc rod dissolves)
- **Cathode (Cu, +)**: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (reduction; copper deposits)
- **EMF**: ~1.1 V
- **Salt bridge** (KCl or KNO₃ in agar): maintains electrical neutrality by allowing ions to migrate between half-cells without mixing the solutions

### Electrode Potential & EMF

**Standard Electrode Potential (E°)**: measured relative to the **Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)** which is assigned E° = 0.00 V.

| Electrode | E° (V) | Tendency |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| F₂/F⁻ | +2.87 | Strongest oxidising agent |
| Au³⁺/Au | +1.50 | |
| Cu²⁺/Cu | +0.34 | |
| H⁺/H₂ (SHE) | 0.00 | Reference |
| Zn²⁺/Zn | −0.76 | |
| Li⁺/Li | −3.05 | Strongest reducing agent |

**EMF of cell** = E°(cathode) − E°(anode)  
For Daniell cell: 0.34 − (−0.76) = **+1.10 V**

Positive EMF = spontaneous reaction. Negative EMF = non-spontaneous (needs external power = electrolysis).

## Part 5 — Batteries

A battery is one or more galvanic cells connected in series. Here are the batteries that SSC loves to test:

### Primary Batteries (non-rechargeable — used once then discarded)

**Dry Cell / Leclanché Cell** (1866, Georges Leclanché):
- Anode: Zinc (Zn) can
- Cathode: Carbon (graphite) rod surrounded by MnO₂ paste
- Electrolyte: NH₄Cl paste (not a liquid — that's why it's "dry")
- Voltage: ~1.5 V
- Used in: torches, remote controls, clocks

**Alkaline Battery**:
- Similar to dry cell but uses KOH (potassium hydroxide) instead of NH₄Cl
- Longer lasting, performs better in cold
- Anode: Zn powder; Cathode: MnO₂
- Voltage: 1.5 V

**Mercury Cell (Button cell)**:
- Anode: Zinc amalgam; Cathode: Mercury oxide (HgO)
- Electrolyte: KOH
- Constant voltage: 1.35 V
- Used in: hearing aids, watches, calculators

### Secondary Batteries (rechargeable — can be restored)

**Lead-Acid Battery** (invented 1859 by Gaston Planté):
- Anode: Lead (Pb); Cathode: Lead dioxide (PbO₂)
- Electrolyte: Dilute H₂SO₄ (sulphuric acid)
- Each cell: 2 V; Car battery = 6 cells × 2V = **12 V**
- During use: Pb oxidises at anode; PbO₂ reduced at cathode; both form PbSO₄
- Recharging reverses the reaction
- Used in: **car batteries, inverters** — the workhorse rechargeable battery

**Nickel-Cadmium (Ni-Cd) Battery**:
- Anode: Cadmium (Cd); Cathode: NiO(OH)
- Voltage: 1.2 V per cell
- Rechargeable; problem: cadmium is toxic, "memory effect"

**Lithium-Ion (Li-ion) Battery** (commercialised 1991, Sony; Nobel Prize Chemistry 2019):
- Anode: Graphite (Li intercalated); Cathode: LiCoO₂
- Electrolyte: Lithium salt in organic solvent
- Voltage: 3.6 V per cell
- Advantages: high energy density, no memory effect, long cycle life
- Used in: smartphones, laptops, electric vehicles
- Nobel Prize 2019: John Goodenough, M. Stanley Whittingham, Akira Yoshino

**Fuel Cell** (invented 1839, William Grove):
- Not a battery in traditional sense — fuel (H₂) is continuously supplied
- Anode: H₂ → 2H⁺ + 2e⁻; Cathode: O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O
- Electrolyte: phosphoric acid or polymer membrane (PEM)
- By-product: only **water** (clean!)
- Used in: NASA space missions, hydrogen vehicles (Toyota Mirai)

## Part 6 — Corrosion as an Electrochemical Process

Rusting of iron is an **electrochemical process** — it is essentially a galvanic cell operating on the iron surface, with different regions acting as anode and cathode:

**Anode** (iron surface): Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ (iron dissolves)  
**Cathode** (oxygen-rich region): O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O  
**Overall**: Iron is oxidised; in the presence of moisture, Fe²⁺ further oxidises to Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (rust)

Requirements for rusting:
1. Presence of **oxygen (O₂)**
2. Presence of **moisture (H₂O)**
3. Electrolyte helps (salt water accelerates rusting)

**Cathodic protection / Sacrificial anode**: connect a more active metal (Zn, Mg) to the iron. The active metal acts as anode and corrodes instead, protecting the iron (cathode). Used to protect **ship hulls**, **underground pipelines**, **oil rigs**.

## Part 7 — Conductance in Electrolytes

| Term | Definition | Unit |
|------|-----------|------|
| **Resistance (R)** | Opposition to flow of current | Ohm (Ω) |
| **Conductance (G)** | = 1/R | Siemens (S) or mho |
| **Specific conductance (κ)** | Conductance of unit cube of solution | S·m⁻¹ |
| **Molar conductance (Λm)** | Conductance of solution containing 1 mol of electrolyte between electrodes 1 cm apart | S·cm²·mol⁻¹ |

**Kohlrausch's Law**: At infinite dilution, each ion contributes independently and additively to the total molar conductance. This allows calculation of molar conductance of weak electrolytes (which cannot actually be measured at infinite dilution, as they are never completely ionised).

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Oxidation occurs at the ANODE; reduction occurs at the CATHODE** — this is true in BOTH electrolytic cells AND galvanic cells. "An Ox Red Cat."

2. **In a galvanic cell, the ANODE is negative** (it gives up electrons) and the **cathode is positive**. In an electrolytic cell, the anode is connected to the **positive** terminal of the power supply.

3. **Electrolysis is non-spontaneous** — it requires an external power supply. Galvanic cells are **spontaneous** — they produce electricity from a spontaneous reaction.

4. **In electrolysis of water, hydrogen is collected at the cathode, oxygen at the anode**. Volume ratio H₂:O₂ = 2:1. If you get 20 mL O₂, you get 40 mL H₂.

5. **Galvanisation** = coating iron with zinc. Zinc acts as a *sacrificial anode* — it corrodes preferentially even when the coating is scratched, because Zn is more active than Fe. Tinning (Sn coating on steel cans) — if scratched, iron corrodes instead (Sn is less active than Fe).

6. **Faraday** discovered both electromagnetic induction (physics) AND the laws of electrolysis. The Faraday constant (96,485 C/mol) = charge per mole of electrons.

7. **Car battery (lead-acid)**: 6 cells of 2 V each = 12 V. Electrolyte is dilute H₂SO₄. During use, both electrodes become PbSO₄ — battery "dies." Charging reverses this.

8. **Dry cell** electrolyte is ammonium chloride (NH₄Cl) paste — it's called "dry" because it's paste, not liquid. Voltage = 1.5 V.

9. **Lithium-ion battery won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry 2019** (John Goodenough, Whittingham, Yoshino). Li-ion has no "memory effect" unlike Ni-Cd.

10. **Fuel cells** produce electricity from hydrogen and oxygen; the only by-product is **water**. Used in NASA spacecraft and hydrogen fuel cell vehicles.

11. **Daniel cell EMF ≈ 1.1 V** — Zn-Cu cell. Zn is the anode (oxidised, dissolves); Cu is the cathode (reduced, Cu deposits). Salt bridge maintains charge neutrality.

12. **Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)** has E° = 0.00 V by convention. It is the reference against which all other electrode potentials are measured.

13. **Electroplating object = cathode**. The object to be plated must be connected to the *negative* terminal — it is the site of reduction where metal deposits. Anode = the metal being used for coating.

14. **Rusting requires both oxygen AND moisture** — in the absence of either, iron does not rust. Iron in dry air doesn't rust; iron in de-aerated water doesn't rust. Salt (electrolyte) speeds it up.

15. **Mercury (button) cells** provide a constant voltage of 1.35 V. Used in medical devices, watches. Problem: mercury toxicity when disposed. Being phased out due to environmental concerns.

16. **Chromium plating** (chrome) is used for car parts and taps for hardness and a bright finish. **Tin plating** is used for food cans because tin is non-toxic and food-safe. **Nickel plating** for engine components and coins.
