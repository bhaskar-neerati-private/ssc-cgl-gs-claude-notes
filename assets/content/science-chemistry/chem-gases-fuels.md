# Key Gases & Fuels — Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen, CO₂, Ozone, LPG & CNG

## The Invisible Architects

Gases are the most ephemeral of matter, yet they quietly run everything. The nitrogen surrounding us keeps fires from burning too rapidly. The oxygen we inhale drives every cell in our bodies. The carbon dioxide from our breath heats the planet. The ozone 25 km above us shields every living creature from UV radiation. The methane in a LPG cylinder cooks 500 million meals every day in India.

Understanding these gases — their chemistry, properties, and roles — is essential for SSC chemistry.

## Key Atmospheric Gases

### Composition of Dry Air

| Gas | % by Volume | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Nitrogen (N₂) | 78.09% | Dilutes oxygen; biologically inert in air; fixed by bacteria → nitrates |
| Oxygen (O₂) | 20.95% | Supports combustion and respiration |
| Argon (Ar) | 0.93% | Noble gas; used in welding, light bulb filling |
| Carbon dioxide (CO₂) | ~0.04% (and rising) | Photosynthesis; greenhouse gas; fire extinguishing |
| Neon, Helium, etc. | Traces | Noble gases; lighting, balloons |
| Water vapour (H₂O) | 0–4% (variable) | Not included in 'dry air' |

## Hydrogen (H₂)

### Properties
- Lightest element and lightest gas (density = 0.09 g/L; 14× lighter than air)
- Colourless, odourless, tasteless; highly flammable
- Burns with a pale blue flame: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O (releases 286 kJ/mol — highest energy per kg of any fuel)
- Does not support combustion (the burning gas itself is the fuel, not an oxidiser)
- Reacts with almost all non-metals at appropriate conditions

### Industrial Preparation
- **Steam reforming of methane** (natural gas): CH₄ + H₂O → CO + 3H₂ (Ni catalyst, 700–1000°C) — primary industrial source
- **Electrolysis of water**: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (clean if renewable electricity used)
- **Coal gasification**: C + H₂O → CO + H₂ (water gas)

### Uses
| Use | Details |
|---|---|
| Haber Process | N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ (fertilisers — most important use) |
| Hydrogenation of oils | Liquid vegetable oils + H₂ (Ni catalyst) → solid vanaspati/margarine |
| Welding and cutting | Atomic hydrogen welding (~4000°C); oxy-hydrogen flame |
| Hydrogen fuel cells | H₂ + O₂ → H₂O + electricity (zero-emission power; used in fuel-cell vehicles) |
| Balloon/airship filling | Much safer now replaced by helium (H₂ is flammable — Hindenburg disaster, 1937) |
| Petroleum refining | Hydrocracking, desulphurisation of crude oil |

**Hydrogen economy:** Hydrogen as a clean future fuel — burned in engines or fuel cells, produces only water. India has a National Hydrogen Mission targeting 5 million tonnes of green hydrogen by 2030.

## Oxygen (O₂)

### Properties
- Colourless, odourless, tasteless gas; slightly heavier than air (density 1.43 g/L)
- Supports combustion: most substances burn more vigorously in pure O₂ than in air
- Is NOT itself flammable (it's an oxidiser, not a fuel)
- Second most abundant gas in atmosphere (20.95%)
- Forms ozone (O₃) in the stratosphere
- Boiling point: −183°C; liquid oxygen (LOX) is pale blue

### Industrial Preparation
- **Fractional distillation of liquid air** — primary industrial source (air is cooled to −200°C; N₂ boils off first at −196°C; O₂ remains at −183°C)
- **Electrolysis of water**: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂

### Laboratory Preparation
- 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂↑ (MnO₂ catalyst; decomposition of hydrogen peroxide)
- 2KClO₃ → 2KCl + 3O₂↑ (heated with MnO₂ catalyst)
- 2KMnO₄ → K₂MnO₄ + MnO₂ + O₂↑ (heating potassium permanganate)

### Uses
| Use | Details |
|---|---|
| Medical | Oxygen cylinders for patients with breathing difficulties; operating theatres |
| Steel making | Blown through molten iron to burn off carbon and impurities (basic oxygen furnace) |
| Oxy-acetylene cutting/welding | Pure O₂ + acetylene → 3500°C flame to cut steel |
| Rocket propellant | Liquid oxygen (LOX) as oxidiser in rockets (Saturn V, ISRO GSLV) |
| Water treatment | Dissolved O₂ supports aerobic bacteria that break down sewage |
| Chemical synthesis | Oxidation reactions in chemical plants |

## Nitrogen (N₂)

### Properties
- Colourless, odourless, tasteless; very slightly lighter than air (density 1.25 g/L vs air 1.29 g/L)
- Most abundant gas in atmosphere (78.09%)
- Very unreactive at room temperature (N≡N triple bond, 945 kJ/mol — very strong)
- Does NOT support combustion (neither burns nor allows other things to burn in N₂)
- Liquefies at −196°C (liquid nitrogen, LN₂)

### Industrial Preparation
- Fractional distillation of liquid air (N₂ boils off first at −196°C, before O₂ at −183°C)

### Uses
| Use | Details |
|---|---|
| Haber Process | N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ (most important use — fertilisers) |
| Inert atmosphere | Food packaging (chips, biscuits) — prevents oxidation and bacterial growth |
| Liquid nitrogen (LN₂) | Cryogenic applications: rapid food freezing; storage of biological samples (sperm, embryos, stem cells); cryotherapy (treating skin lesions by freezing) |
| Tyre inflation | Aircraft tyres inflated with N₂ (no moisture, no oxidation of wheel rim, no pressure change with temperature change as much as air) |
| Electric bulb filling | Mixed with argon in incandescent bulbs — prevents filament oxidation and evaporation |
| Fire suppression | Nitrogen atmosphere smothers fires (displaces O₂); used in server rooms, archives |

## Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

### Properties
- Colourless, odourless gas; heavier than air (density 1.98 g/L vs air 1.29 g/L)
- Does NOT support combustion; does NOT burn itself → used in fire extinguishers
- Turns limewater milky: CO₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O (key test for CO₂)
- Dissolves in water to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) — makes solutions slightly acidic
- Sublimes at −78.5°C (dry ice): solid CO₂ → gas directly (no liquid phase at atmospheric pressure)
- **Greenhouse gas**: absorbs IR radiation emitted by Earth's surface, trapping heat

### Sources of CO₂
- **Natural:** Respiration (all living organisms); volcanic eruptions; ocean out-gassing; decomposition of organic matter
- **Anthropogenic (human-caused):** Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas), deforestation, cement production (CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂)

### Uses
| Use | Details |
|---|---|
| Fire extinguishing | CO₂ fire extinguishers — smothers fire by excluding O₂; ideal for electrical and oil fires |
| Carbonated drinks | CO₂ dissolved under pressure → fizzy drinks (sodas, sparkling water, beer) |
| Dry ice | Solid CO₂ (−78.5°C) — used for refrigeration in transport; stage fog effects |
| Photosynthesis | 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ (primary raw material for all plant growth) |
| Urea production | CO₂ + 2NH₃ → CO(NH₂)₂ + H₂O (urea fertiliser) |
| Supercritical CO₂ | Decaffeinating coffee; dry cleaning; extracting essential oils |

## Ozone (O₃)

### Properties
- Allotrope of oxygen: three oxygen atoms (triatomic); unstable; reverts to O₂ over time
- Pale blue gas with pungent, sharp smell ('fresh' smell after lightning storms — produced by electrical discharge)
- Strong oxidising agent
- Two roles: 
  - **Stratospheric ozone (good ozone):** Ozone layer at 15–30 km altitude; absorbs harmful UV-B and UV-C radiation from the sun
  - **Tropospheric ozone (bad ozone):** Formed at ground level from NOₓ + VOCs + sunlight; a major air pollutant; causes respiratory damage and crop damage

### The Ozone Layer and Its Depletion

**Ozone formation (stratosphere):**
O₂ + UV radiation → 2O (atomic oxygen)
O + O₂ → O₃ (ozone)

**Ozone destruction by CFCs:**
CCl₂F₂ (CFC-12, Freon) + UV → CClF₂ + Cl (atomic chlorine)
Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂ (ozone destroyed)
ClO + O → Cl + O₂ (Cl is regenerated — acts as catalyst)
**One Cl atom can destroy ~100,000 ozone molecules.**

**Key facts:**
- **Montreal Protocol (1987):** International treaty to phase out CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons) and other ozone-depleting substances (ODSs); most successful global environmental treaty
- **Ozone hole:** Most severe over Antarctica in spring (August–October); 2023 hole among the largest recorded
- **HCFCs and HFCs:** Transitional replacements for CFCs; HCFCs still deplete ozone slightly; HFCs do not deplete ozone but ARE potent greenhouse gases
- **Kigali Amendment (2016):** Added HFCs (powerful greenhouse gases) to Montreal Protocol phase-down schedule

### Uses of Ozone
- Water purification (ozonation — strong oxidiser kills bacteria, viruses; no chlorine taste/smell)
- Bleaching textiles and paper (ozone bleaching instead of chlorine)
- Air purifiers (ozone generators — but tropospheric ozone at high concentration is harmful to lungs)

## Fuels

### Classification of Fuels

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Solid fuels** | Wood, coal, charcoal, coke, dung cake |
| **Liquid fuels** | Petrol (gasoline), diesel, kerosene, ethanol, methanol |
| **Gaseous fuels** | LPG (propane + butane), CNG (methane), hydrogen, biogas |

### Calorific Value

**Calorific value** = heat energy released per unit mass/volume of fuel on complete combustion.

| Fuel | Calorific Value (kJ/g) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hydrogen | ~143 | Highest of any fuel by mass |
| Methane (CNG) | ~55 | Main component of natural gas |
| LPG (butane) | ~49 | Propane + butane mixture |
| Petrol | ~45 | Mixture of C₅–C₁₂ hydrocarbons |
| Diesel | ~44 | Mixture of C₁₄–C₁₉ hydrocarbons |
| Kerosene | ~43 | C₁₁–C₁₄ hydrocarbons |
| Coal | ~28–35 | Depends on type (anthracite > bituminous > lignite) |
| Wood | ~17 | Moisture content reduces effective value |
| Cow dung cake | ~6–7 | Very low; inefficient fuel |

### LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas)

**Composition:** Primarily propane (C₃H₈) + butane (C₄H₁₀); more butane in Indian domestic LPG
**State:** Gas at room temperature and atmospheric pressure; liquefies under moderate pressure (~6–7 bar) → stored as liquid in steel cylinders
**Odour:** Odourless in pure form; **ethyl mercaptan (C₂H₅SH)** or thiophane added as odorant for leak detection
**Uses:** Domestic cooking (most common in India), vehicles (LPG-fuelled taxis), industrial heating

| Fuel | Composition | Calorific Value | Primary Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **LPG** | Propane + Butane (C₃–C₄) | ~49 kJ/g | Cooking, vehicles |
| **CNG** | Mainly Methane (C₁, ~90%) | ~55 kJ/g | Vehicles (buses, autorickshaws) |
| **Biogas** | ~50–70% Methane + CO₂ + traces | ~22 kJ/g | Rural cooking (Gobar gas plants) |
| **PNG** (Piped Natural Gas) | Methane (~90%) | ~55 kJ/g | Domestic cooking in cities |
| **Hydrogen (fuel cell)** | H₂ | ~143 kJ/g | Fuel cell vehicles (future) |

### CNG (Compressed Natural Gas)

**Composition:** Mainly methane (CH₄, ~85–95%) + small amounts of ethane, propane
**Storage:** Compressed at 200–250 bar in steel cylinders (gaseous; does NOT liquefy at room temperature unlike LPG)
**Octane rating:** ~120–130 (very high) → very resistant to knocking → smooth engine operation
**Advantages over petrol/diesel:**
- Cleaner burning → 25% less CO₂ than petrol; 40% less CO; negligible SO₂; fewer particulates
- Cheaper fuel cost per km (India)
- Higher octane number
**Disadvantages:**
- Lower energy density by volume → fewer km per equivalent fuel volume
- Heavy steel cylinders reduce vehicle payload
- Less refuelling infrastructure

### Biogas

**Production:** Anaerobic digestion of organic waste (cow dung, agricultural waste, food waste, sewage sludge) by methanogenic bacteria in biogas plants (digesters)
**Composition:** ~55–65% methane (CH₄) + ~35–45% carbon dioxide (CO₂) + traces of H₂S, N₂
**Slurry by-product:** Rich in nitrogen and phosphorus — excellent organic fertiliser
**Plants:** 'Gobar gas' plants in rural India; MNRE (Ministry of New and Renewable Energy) promotes through National Biogas Programme

### Hydrogen as Fuel

- **Advantages:** Highest calorific value (143 kJ/g); burns to give ONLY water (zero CO₂, zero pollutants); abundant element (in water)
- **Disadvantages:** Currently produced mainly from fossil fuels (grey hydrogen); electrolysis using renewable energy gives 'green hydrogen' but is expensive; storage is a challenge (hydrogen is extremely light and diffuses through metals)
- **Fuel cells:** H₂ + O₂ → H₂O + electricity (via electrochemical reaction; no combustion; ~60% efficiency vs ~25–30% for internal combustion engines)

## Greenhouse Gases and Global Warming

| Gas | GWP (100-year, relative to CO₂) | Main Source |
|---|---|---|
| CO₂ | 1 (reference) | Fossil fuel burning, deforestation |
| CH₄ (Methane) | 28 | Cattle, landfills, natural gas leaks, rice paddies |
| N₂O (Nitrous oxide) | 273 | Fertilisers, vehicle exhaust, industrial |
| HFCs | 1,000–10,000 | Refrigerants, air conditioners |
| SF₆ (Sulphur hexafluoride) | ~24,000 | Electrical switchgear |
| Ozone (tropospheric) | ~variable | NOₓ + VOC photochemistry |

**GWP = Global Warming Potential** — how much heat a gas traps relative to CO₂ over 100 years.

**India's commitments:** NDC under Paris Agreement (2015): 45% reduction in emissions intensity of GDP by 2030 (compared to 2005); 500 GW renewable energy by 2030; Net zero by 2070.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Nitrogen is 78.09% of atmosphere; Oxygen is 20.95%** — nitrogen is most abundant; oxygen is second; argon (0.93%) is more abundant than CO₂ (0.04%).
2. **CO₂ does NOT support combustion and does NOT burn** — CO (carbon monoxide) is flammable and toxic; CO₂ is the fully oxidised, non-flammable form.
3. **Dry ice = solid CO₂ (−78.5°C); sublimes directly to gas** — no liquid phase at atmospheric pressure; used for refrigeration and stage fog effects.
4. **The limewater test for CO₂: Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃↓ (white precipitate turns limewater milky)** — excess CO₂ clears the milky solution (CaCO₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca(HCO₃)₂, which is soluble).
5. **LPG = propane + butane; CNG = mainly methane** — LPG is stored as liquid under pressure; CNG is compressed gas.
6. **Ethyl mercaptan (or thiophane) is added to odourless LPG as an odorant** — the 'gas smell' when there's a leak is this additive, not the LPG itself.
7. **Biogas is mainly methane (55–65%) + CO₂ (35–45%)** — produced by anaerobic digestion; also called gobar gas; slurry by-product is an organic fertiliser.
8. **Hydrogen has the highest calorific value (143 kJ/g)** — higher than any other fuel; burns to give only water; the fuel of the future.
9. **Ozone (O₃) layer is in the STRATOSPHERE (15–30 km altitude)** — absorbs UV-B and UV-C; tropospheric ozone (at ground level) is a harmful pollutant.
10. **CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons) destroy the ozone layer** — banned by Montreal Protocol (1987); one Cl atom can destroy ~100,000 ozone molecules via a catalytic cycle.
11. **Methane (CH₄) is a greenhouse gas 28× more potent than CO₂ per molecule** — comes from cattle digestion, rice paddies, landfills, natural gas leaks.
12. **Oxygen supports combustion but does NOT itself burn** — it is an oxidising agent, not a fuel. It helps other things burn faster and more completely.
13. **Nitrogen gas (N₂) is used in food packaging (chips, biscuits) to prevent oxidation** — replaces air; nitrogen is inert, prevents rancidity, helps packaging stay puffed.
14. **CNG has a very high octane rating (~120)** — this means it resists 'knocking' (pre-ignition) better than petrol (~87–95); engines run more smoothly on CNG.
15. **Coal types by carbon content:** Anthracite (> 90% C, best quality) > Bituminous (60–80% C, most common) > Lignite/Brown coal (25–35% C) > Peat (lowest quality).
16. **Water gas = CO + H₂ (made from C + H₂O)** — an important industrial fuel gas and hydrogen source; NOT the same as hydrogen gas or natural gas.
