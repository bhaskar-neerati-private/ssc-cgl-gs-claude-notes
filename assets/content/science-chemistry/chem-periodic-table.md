# Periodic Table — Elements, Classification, Groups, Periods & Trends

## The Dream That Organised Chemistry

In 1869, the Russian chemist Dmitri Mendeleev was struggling to write a chemistry textbook. He had 63 elements to cover and needed a logical order. According to legend, the answer came to him in a dream — he saw all the elements fall into place in a table, arranged by atomic mass, with chemical properties repeating in a pattern.

He was so confident in his table that he left gaps — predicting that undiscovered elements would fill them. When Gallium was discovered in 1875, its properties matched Mendeleev's prediction (which he'd named "eka-aluminium") almost exactly. Chemistry had its master organising principle.

## Historical Development

| Scientist | Year | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Döbereiner** | 1817 | "Law of Triads" — groups of 3 elements with similar properties; middle element's atomic mass = average of other two (e.g., Li, Na, K) |
| **Newlands** | 1865 | "Law of Octaves" — every 8th element had similar properties (like musical octaves); worked only for the first 17 elements |
| **Mendeleev** | 1869 | Arranged 63 elements by increasing **atomic mass**; left gaps for undiscovered elements; predicted properties of missing elements |
| **Moseley** | 1913 | Discovered the concept of **atomic number** (Z) from X-ray spectra; showed Z, not mass, determines element position — the basis of the modern periodic law |

**Modern Periodic Law (Moseley):** The properties of elements are a periodic function of their **atomic number** (not atomic mass as Mendeleev thought).

## Structure of the Modern Periodic Table

- **118 elements** discovered (as of 2024)
- **7 Periods** (horizontal rows)
- **18 Groups** (vertical columns)
- **4 Blocks:** s, p, d, f

### Periods (Rows)

| Period | Elements | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Period 1 | 2 (H, He) | Shortest period |
| Period 2 | 8 (Li to Ne) | Short period |
| Period 3 | 8 (Na to Ar) | Short period |
| Period 4 | 18 (K to Kr) | Long period (includes d-block) |
| Period 5 | 18 (Rb to Xe) | Long period |
| Period 6 | 32 (Cs to Rn) | Longest period (includes f-block lanthanides) |
| Period 7 | 32 (Fr to Og) | Longest period (includes f-block actinides); all elements beyond Z=92 are man-made |

**Rule:** Period number = number of electron shells in the atom (H has 1 shell, Na has 3 shells).

### Groups (Columns) — Key Groups

| Group | Name | Elements | Characteristic |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | Alkali Metals | Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr | 1 valence electron; soft; react violently with water → H₂ + alkali; stored in kerosene/oil |
| **2** | Alkaline Earth Metals | Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra | 2 valence electrons; harder than Group 1; less reactive |
| **3–12** | Transition Metals | Fe, Cu, Zn, Ni, Cr, Mn, Co, etc. | d-block; variable valency; form coloured compounds; good catalysts |
| **13** | Boron Family | B, Al, Ga, In, Tl | 3 valence electrons |
| **14** | Carbon Family | C, Si, Ge, Sn, Pb | 4 valence electrons |
| **15** | Nitrogen Family (Pnictogens) | N, P, As, Sb, Bi | 5 valence electrons |
| **16** | Oxygen Family (Chalcogens) | O, S, Se, Te, Po | 6 valence electrons |
| **17** | Halogens | F, Cl, Br, I, At | 7 valence electrons; most reactive non-metals; form salts with metals |
| **18** | Noble Gases (Inert Gases) | He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn | Full outer shells; almost completely unreactive; used in lighting |

**Lanthanides (Rare Earths):** La to Lu (Z=57–71) — Period 6, f-block; separated at bottom of table
**Actinides:** Ac to Lr (Z=89–103) — Period 7, f-block; all radioactive; many man-made (transuranic)

## Blocks of the Periodic Table

| Block | Groups | Characteristic | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **s-block** | 1 and 2 | Outermost electrons in s-orbital | Na, K, Mg, Ca |
| **p-block** | 13 to 18 | Outermost electrons in p-orbital | C, N, O, F, Cl, Ne, Si |
| **d-block** | 3 to 12 | Transition metals; fill d-orbitals | Fe, Cu, Zn, Cr, Mn |
| **f-block** | Lanthanides + Actinides | Fill f-orbitals; placed separately | Ce, Th, U |

## Periodic Trends

As you move across a period (left to right) and down a group (top to bottom), atomic properties change in predictable ways.

### 1. Atomic Radius

| Direction | Trend | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Across a period (L→R)** | **Decreases** | More protons added → greater nuclear charge pulls electrons closer |
| **Down a group (↓)** | **Increases** | New electron shells added; electrons farther from nucleus |

Largest atom: **Francium (Fr)** (bottom-left corner)
Smallest atom: **Helium (He)** (top-right, noble gas); among reactive elements: **Fluorine**

### 2. Ionisation Energy (IE)

Energy required to remove the outermost electron from a gaseous atom.

| Direction | Trend | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Across a period (L→R)** | **Increases** | Smaller atomic radius + more nuclear charge → electron held more tightly |
| **Down a group (↓)** | **Decreases** | Larger radius; outer electrons farther from nucleus + shielded by inner electrons |

**Highest IE:** Helium (He)
**Lowest IE:** Francium (Fr) (or Caesium)

### 3. Electronegativity

Tendency of an atom to attract shared electrons in a covalent bond (Pauling scale).

| Direction | Trend | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Across a period (L→R)** | **Increases** | More nuclear charge; smaller size |
| **Down a group (↓)** | **Decreases** | Larger size; greater shielding of nucleus |

**Most electronegative:** Fluorine (F) = 3.98
**Least electronegative:** Francium (Fr) = 0.7
**Noble gases:** Not assigned electronegativity (don't usually form bonds)

### 4. Metallic Character

| Direction | Trend | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **Across a period (L→R)** | **Decreases** | Metals on left; non-metals on right; metalloids in between |
| **Down a group (↓)** | **Increases** | Easier to lose electrons (lower IE) |

### 5. Electron Affinity

Energy released when a neutral atom gains an electron.

**Highest electron affinity:** Chlorine (Cl) — *not* fluorine (fluorine is most electronegative but Cl has higher EA due to less repulsion in the larger 3rd shell)

## Metals, Non-metals, and Metalloids

| Category | Count | Position | Properties |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Metals** | ~94 | Left + centre of table | Lustrous, malleable, ductile, good conductors, lose electrons |
| **Non-metals** | 18 | Top right | Dull, brittle (if solid), poor conductors (except graphite), gain electrons |
| **Metalloids (Semimetals)** | 7 | Diagonal staircase | Properties intermediate; B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te, At |
| **Noble gases** | 6 | Group 18 | Chemically inert; full outer shells |

**Metalloids are semiconductors** — their electrical conductivity can be controlled; silicon (Si) and germanium (Ge) are the basis of all modern electronics and computer chips.

## Key Group Properties

### Group 1 — Alkali Metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr)
- Softest metals; cut with a knife
- React vigorously with water: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑ (hydrogen gas produced)
- **Reactivity increases down the group** (Cs > Rb > K > Na > Li)
- Stored under oil/kerosene (to prevent air/water contact)
- Flame colours: Li = crimson red; Na = golden yellow; K = violet; Rb = red-violet; Cs = blue

### Group 17 — Halogens (F, Cl, Br, I, At)
- Most reactive non-metals
- 7 valence electrons; gain 1 electron → halide ion (F⁻, Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻)
- **Reactivity decreases down the group** (F > Cl > Br > I)
- Form salts with metals (hence "halogen" = "salt-former" in Greek)
- States at room temperature: F₂ and Cl₂ = gas; Br₂ = liquid; I₂ = solid
- Used as disinfectants (chlorine in water treatment), bleaching agents

### Group 18 — Noble Gases (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn)
- Completely filled outer shells → negligible reactivity
- **Helium:** Lightest noble gas; used in balloons (safer than H₂); deepwater diving mixtures
- **Neon:** Red-orange glow in neon signs
- **Argon:** Most abundant noble gas in atmosphere (0.93%); used in incandescent bulb fillings; welding
- **Krypton, Xenon:** High-intensity lights, anaesthetics
- **Radon:** Radioactive; formed from uranium decay; health concern in buildings over granite rock

## Hydrogen — The Odd One Out

Hydrogen occupies a unique position:
- **Group 1** (1 valence electron, like alkali metals) — but it is a non-metal, not a metal
- Can also be placed in **Group 17** (like halogens, it needs 1 electron to fill its shell)
- At ordinary conditions: **colourless, odourless, lightest gas** — H₂
- **Most abundant element in the universe** (~75% by mass)
- Not found free in Earth's atmosphere (too light; escapes gravity)

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Mendeleev arranged elements by atomic MASS; the Modern Periodic Law uses atomic NUMBER (Moseley, 1913)** — this distinction is fundamental; Mendeleev came first (1869), Moseley corrected him.
2. **Most electronegative element = Fluorine (F)** — not oxygen, not chlorine. Fluorine is at the top right of the periodic table (excluding noble gases).
3. **Most abundant element in Earth's crust = Oxygen (O)**, not silicon or iron. Most abundant metal = Aluminium (Al).
4. **Most abundant element in the universe = Hydrogen (H)** — stars are mostly hydrogen plasma.
5. **Atomic radius decreases across a period (left to right)** — more protons pull electrons closer; **increases down a group** — more electron shells.
6. **Ionisation energy increases across a period; decreases down a group** — inverse of atomic radius trend.
7. **Noble gases (Group 18) are almost completely inert** — full outer electron shells; used as protective atmospheres, lighting, cooling.
8. **Alkali metals (Group 1) react violently with water, producing hydrogen gas** — reactivity increases down the group (Cs > K > Na > Li).
9. **Halogens (Group 17) are the most reactive non-metals** — reactivity decreases down the group (F > Cl > Br > I).
10. **Bromine (Br₂) is the only liquid non-metal at room temperature** — fluorine and chlorine are gases; iodine is solid.
11. **Mercury (Hg) is the only liquid metal at room temperature** — it is in Group 12 (transition metal). Gallium melts just above room temperature (29.8°C).
12. **Silicon and Germanium are metalloids and semiconductors** — the foundation of modern electronics (chips, transistors). Silicon = most important semiconductor.
13. **Hydrogen placed in Group 1 but is a non-metal** — it does not have the properties of alkali metals; can also be considered to fit Group 17 (needs 1 electron to fill shell).
14. **Period number = number of electron shells** — Na (Period 3) has 3 shells; Ca (Period 4) has 4 shells.
15. **Group number for s and p-block elements = number of valence electrons** — Group 1 = 1 valence electron; Group 17 = 7 valence electrons; Group 18 = 8 (or 2 for He).
16. **Lanthanides and Actinides are placed separately at the bottom of the table** — they belong to Period 6 and 7 respectively (f-block). All actinides beyond uranium (Z=92) are artificially produced (transuranic elements).
