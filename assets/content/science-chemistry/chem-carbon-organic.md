# Carbon — Allotropes, Organic Chemistry, Hydrocarbons & Functional Groups

## The Element That Built Life

Carbon is element number 6 — modest in atomic number but extraordinary in versatility. It forms more compounds than all other elements combined. Every living organism is built around carbon chains. Petroleum, coal, natural gas, plastics, medicines, textiles, food — virtually everything that makes modern civilisation possible traces back to carbon chemistry.

What makes carbon so special? It forms four covalent bonds (tetravalent), allowing it to chain to itself indefinitely — short chains, long chains, rings, branches, double bonds, triple bonds, aromatic rings. This structural diversity is the basis of organic chemistry.

## Allotropes of Carbon

**Allotropes** are different physical forms of the same element, having different structural arrangements and hence different properties.

### 1. Diamond

**Structure:** Each carbon atom is bonded to 4 other carbon atoms in a rigid, three-dimensional tetrahedral arrangement (sp³ hybridisation). No free electrons.

| Property | Value/Explanation |
|---|---|
| Hardness | Hardest natural substance (10 on Mohs scale) |
| Melting point | ~3550°C (very high — strong covalent bonds in 3D) |
| Electrical conductivity | Insulator (no free electrons) |
| Thermal conductivity | Highest of any natural material (~2000 W/m·K) |
| Refractive index | 2.42 (high — responsible for brilliance/sparkle) |
| Appearance | Colourless, transparent, lustrous |
| Crystal structure | Face-centred cubic (3D covalent lattice) |

**Uses:** Cutting tools (diamond-tipped drill bits, saws), grinding/polishing abrasives, gemstones, windows in high-pressure equipment, scientific research, heat sinks in electronics.

### 2. Graphite

**Structure:** Each carbon atom is bonded to 3 other carbon atoms in flat, hexagonal layers (sp² hybridisation). The 4th valence electron of each carbon is delocalised → free to move within the layers.

| Property | Value/Explanation |
|---|---|
| Hardness | Very soft (1–2 on Mohs scale); layers slide over each other |
| Melting point | ~3600°C (sublimes) — within-layer bonds very strong; sublimes at atmospheric pressure |
| Electrical conductivity | **Good conductor** (delocalised electrons between layers) — only non-metal that conducts |
| Thermal conductivity | Good within layers |
| Appearance | Dark grey/black, shiny metallic lustre |
| Crystal structure | Layered (planar sheets stacked; weak van der Waals between layers) |

**Uses:** Pencil 'lead' (graphite + clay), lubricant (layers slide easily), electrodes in electrolysis and batteries, nuclear reactor moderator (slows neutrons), electrical contacts, crucibles for high-temperature work.

**Why graphite is a lubricant:** The weak van der Waals forces between graphite layers allow the sheets to slide over each other easily, providing lubrication without the chemical reactivity of oil.

**Why graphite conducts electricity:** The delocalised π electrons move freely within the hexagonal layers under an electric field — conducting electricity along the layers. (Graphite does NOT conduct well perpendicular to the layers.)

### 3. Fullerenes

**Structure:** Carbon atoms arranged in hollow spherical (ball-like) or cylindrical (tube-like) structures.

| Type | Formula | Structure | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Buckminsterfullerene (Buckyball)** | C₆₀ | Spherical — 60 C atoms in 20 hexagons + 12 pentagons; like a soccer ball | Discovered 1985 (Kroto, Curl, Smalley — Nobel Prize 1996) |
| **Carbon nanotubes (CNT)** | Variable | Cylindrical graphene sheet rolled up | Extremely strong (100× stronger than steel at ¼ the weight); conducting or semiconducting |
| **Graphene** | Single layer | Single atomic layer of graphite (one atom thick hexagonal sheet) | Strongest material known; excellent conductor; Nobel Prize 2010 (Geim, Novoselov) |

**Applications:** Drug delivery (hollow fullerene cage can carry drugs), lubricants, nanotechnology, electronics (CNTs in transistors), composite materials.

### Comparison of Carbon Allotropes

| Property | Diamond | Graphite | Fullerene (C₆₀) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hybridisation | sp³ | sp² | sp² |
| Structure | 3D tetrahedral | 2D layered | 3D cage/sphere |
| Conductivity | Insulator | Good conductor | Semiconductor |
| Hardness | Very hard | Soft | Moderate |
| Colour | Colourless | Black/grey | Brown-black |

## Introduction to Organic Chemistry

**Organic chemistry** is the study of compounds containing carbon-hydrogen (C-H) bonds (and often C-C, C-O, C-N bonds).

### Why So Many Organic Compounds?

1. **Catenation:** Carbon's unique ability to form long chains by bonding to itself (C-C bonds are strong and stable)
2. **Tetravalency:** Carbon forms 4 bonds, allowing enormous structural diversity
3. **Multiple bond types:** Single bonds, double bonds (C=C), triple bonds (C≡C) are all stable
4. **Ring formation:** Carbon can form stable rings (benzene, cyclohexane, glucose ring)
5. **Bonding with other elements:** C bonds readily with H, O, N, S, halogens, P

### Carbon Compounds Classification

```
Organic Compounds
├── Hydrocarbons (only C and H)
│   ├── Aliphatic (open chain)
│   │   ├── Alkanes (only single bonds, -ane)
│   │   ├── Alkenes (one double bond, -ene)
│   │   └── Alkynes (one triple bond, -yne)
│   └── Aromatic (contains benzene ring)
│       └── Benzene (C₆H₆) and its derivatives
└── Heteroatom compounds (C, H + O, N, S, halogens)
    ├── Alcohols (-OH group)
    ├── Carboxylic acids (-COOH group)
    ├── Aldehydes (-CHO group)
    ├── Ketones (C=O in middle)
    ├── Esters (-COO- group)
    ├── Ethers (C-O-C)
    ├── Amines (-NH₂)
    └── Halides (-F, -Cl, -Br, -I)
```

## Hydrocarbons

### 1. Alkanes (Paraffins) — Saturated Hydrocarbons

General formula: **CₙH₂ₙ₊₂**

All C-C bonds are single bonds (saturated). Relatively unreactive — only undergo combustion and substitution reactions.

| Name | Formula | Structural Formula | Boiling Point | State (at 25°C) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Methane | CH₄ | CH₄ | -161°C | Gas |
| Ethane | C₂H₆ | CH₃-CH₃ | -89°C | Gas |
| Propane | C₃H₈ | CH₃-CH₂-CH₃ | -42°C | Gas |
| Butane | C₄H₁₀ | CH₃-(CH₂)₂-CH₃ | -1°C | Gas |
| Pentane | C₅H₁₂ | CH₃-(CH₂)₃-CH₃ | 36°C | Liquid |
| Hexane | C₆H₁₄ | — | 69°C | Liquid |
| Decane | C₁₀H₂₂ | — | 174°C | Liquid |
| Hexadecane | C₁₆H₃₄ | — | 287°C | Liquid |
| Octadecane | C₁₈H₃₈ | — | 317°C | Solid (wax) |

**Sources:** Petroleum/crude oil (mixture of alkanes); Natural gas (mostly methane, ~85%); LPG (liquefied petroleum gas) = propane + butane mixture.

### 2. Alkenes (Olefins) — Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

General formula: **CₙH₂ₙ**

Contain one C=C double bond. More reactive than alkanes — undergo addition reactions.

| Name | Formula | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Ethene (Ethylene) | C₂H₄ | Simplest alkene; ripening fruit hormone; make polyethylene |
| Propene (Propylene) | C₃H₆ | Makes polypropylene plastics |
| Butene | C₄H₈ | Used in synthetic rubber |
| Isoprene | C₅H₈ | Natural rubber monomer |

**Reactions of alkenes:** Addition reactions (Br₂ in CCl₄ — bromine water decolorises = test for double bond); Hydrogenation (H₂ + catalyst Ni → alkane); Hydration (H₂O + acid → alcohol); Polymerisation (many alkene monomers → polymer).

### 3. Alkynes — Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

General formula: **CₙH₂ₙ₋₂**

Contain one C≡C triple bond. Most reactive hydrocarbons — undergo addition reactions with two molecules.

| Name | Formula | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Ethyne (Acetylene) | C₂H₂ | Simplest alkyne; used in oxy-acetylene welding/cutting (burns at ~3500°C) |
| Propyne | C₃H₄ | — |

### 4. Aromatic Hydrocarbons (Arenes)

Based on benzene ring (C₆H₆) — a 6-carbon ring with alternating double bonds (resonance). All C-C bonds equivalent due to delocalisation of π electrons.

| Compound | Formula | Uses |
|---|---|---|
| Benzene | C₆H₆ | Solvent, starting material for dyes, drugs, plastics |
| Toluene | C₆H₅CH₃ | Solvent, TNT (trinitrotoluene explosive) |
| Naphthalene | C₁₀H₈ | Mothballs |
| Anthracene | C₁₄H₁₀ | Dyes |
| Styrene | C₆H₅CH=CH₂ | Monomer for polystyrene |

## Functional Groups and Homologous Series

A **functional group** is an atom or group of atoms that gives an organic compound its characteristic chemical properties.

### Important Functional Groups

| Functional Group | Formula/Symbol | Class of Compound | Example | Common Name |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hydroxyl | -OH | Alcohol | C₂H₅OH | Ethanol (drinking alcohol) |
| Carboxyl | -COOH | Carboxylic acid | CH₃COOH | Acetic/Ethanoic acid (vinegar) |
| Aldehyde | -CHO | Aldehyde | HCHO | Formaldehyde (methanal) |
| Ketone | C=O (middle) | Ketone | CH₃COCH₃ | Acetone (propanone) |
| Ester linkage | -COO- | Ester | CH₃COOC₂H₅ | Ethyl acetate |
| Ether linkage | -O- | Ether | C₂H₅OC₂H₅ | Diethyl ether (anaesthetic) |
| Amino | -NH₂ | Amine | C₆H₅NH₂ | Aniline |
| Amide | -CONH₂ | Amide | — | Nylon linkage |
| Nitro | -NO₂ | Nitro compound | C₆H₅NO₂ | Nitrobenzene |
| Halide | -X (F, Cl, Br, I) | Alkyl halide | CH₃Cl | Chloromethane |
| Sulphonic acid | -SO₃H | Sulphonic acid | C₆H₅SO₃H | Benzene sulphonic acid |

### Key Compounds

**Alcohols:**
- Methanol (CH₃OH) — 'wood spirit'; extremely toxic; used as fuel additive, solvent; causes blindness even in small amounts
- Ethanol (C₂H₅OH) — 'drinking alcohol'; beverages (fermentation of sugars); antiseptic (70% solution); solvent; fuel
- Glycerol/Glycerine (C₃H₈O₃) — three -OH groups; moisturiser; soap making; nitroglycerin precursor

**Aldehydes:**
- Formaldehyde (HCHO, methanal) — disinfectant; formalin (40% solution in water) used to preserve biological specimens
- Acetaldehyde (CH₃CHO, ethanal) — industrial chemical; used in making acetic acid

**Ketones:**
- Acetone (CH₃COCH₃, propanone) — nail polish remover; solvent; makes plastics

**Carboxylic Acids:**
- Formic acid (HCOOH) — ant stings
- Acetic acid (CH₃COOH) — vinegar
- Lactic acid (C₃H₆O₃) — curd, sour milk, muscle cramps
- Citric acid (C₆H₈O₇) — citrus fruits
- Stearic acid (C₁₈H₃₆O₂) — soap making (sodium stearate = soap)

**Esters:**
- General formula: Alcohol + Acid → Ester + Water (Fischer esterification)
- Esters have pleasant fruity aromas — used in perfumes and artificial flavours
- Ethyl acetate = nail polish remover / solvent
- Amyl acetate = banana flavour
- Ethyl butyrate = pineapple flavour

## Isomerism

**Isomers** are compounds that have the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements, and therefore different properties.

### Types of Isomerism (Simple)

**Structural isomers — same molecular formula, different connectivity:**

Example: C₄H₁₀ (butane) has two isomers:
1. n-Butane: CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-CH₃ (straight chain)
2. Isobutane: (CH₃)₃CH (branched chain)

As carbon number increases, the number of isomers increases dramatically (C₁₀H₂₂ has 75 isomers).

## Petroleum and Fractional Distillation

Petroleum (crude oil) is a complex mixture of hydrocarbons. It is separated by fractional distillation based on different boiling points:

| Fraction | Boiling Range | Carbon Chain | Uses |
|---|---|---|---|
| Petroleum gas | < 30°C | C₁–C₄ | LPG; cooking fuel |
| Petrol/Gasoline | 40–200°C | C₅–C₁₂ | Petrol engines, dry cleaning |
| Naphtha | 60–100°C | C₅–C₁₀ | Petrochemical feedstock |
| Kerosene | 150–250°C | C₁₁–C₁₄ | Aviation fuel (jet fuel), cooking (kerosene stoves) |
| Diesel | 200–350°C | C₁₄–C₁₉ | Diesel engines |
| Lubricating oil | > 300°C | C₂₀–C₃₅ | Machine lubrication |
| Paraffin wax | > 400°C | C₂₀–C₄₅ | Candles, polishes |
| Bitumen/Tar | Residue | > C₄₅ | Road surfacing (asphalt), waterproofing |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Diamond is hardest but graphite is softest — both are pure carbon allotropes** — same element, completely different properties due to different bonding structure.
2. **Graphite conducts electricity because of DELOCALISED π electrons in its layers** — it is the only non-metal that conducts electricity (important exception to "non-metals don't conduct").
3. **Diamond does NOT conduct electricity (no free electrons) but IS the best thermal conductor** — often asked as "which statement about diamond is false."
4. **Fullerene (C₆₀, Buckminsterfullerene) was discovered in 1985; Nobel Prize given in 1996** — discovered by Kroto, Curl, and Smalley; named after architect Buckminster Fuller who designed geodesic dome structures.
5. **Alkanes have formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂; Alkenes CₙH₂ₙ; Alkynes CₙH₂ₙ₋₂** — each unsaturation (double or triple bond or ring) reduces hydrogen count by 2.
6. **Bromine water test for alkenes**: alkenes decolourise bromine water (Br₂/CCl₄) via addition reaction; alkanes do not.
7. **Acetylene (ethyne, C₂H₂) is used in oxy-acetylene welding** — burns at ~3500°C, hot enough to cut and weld steel.
8. **Methanol is toxic; ethanol is drinkable** — methanol causes blindness and death even in small amounts; ethanol in beverages can be denatured (made undrinkable) by adding methanol or other toxic substances.
9. **Ethylene (ethene) is a plant hormone that ripens fruits** — used commercially to artificially ripen bananas, mangoes, tomatoes shipped while unripe.
10. **Formaldehyde (HCHO) + water (40% solution) = Formalin** — used to preserve biological specimens in biology labs; causes cancer; being phased out.
11. **Urea = CO(NH₂)₂ — the first organic compound synthesised from inorganic material** (Wöhler, 1828 — overturned the vitalist theory that organic compounds could only be made by living organisms).
12. **Acetone (propanone) = nail polish remover, also solvent** — a ketone; the simplest ketone; smells like nail polish remover.
13. **DDT was first used as a pesticide, was very effective against malaria mosquitoes, but is now globally banned** due to biomagnification in food chains and its persistence in the environment (remains active for years).
14. **Benzene (C₆H₆) has a planar hexagonal ring with alternating single and double bonds (resonance)** — Kekulé's dream (1865) where he saw a snake eating its own tail, suggesting the ring structure.
15. **The -COOH group is in carboxylic acids; -CHO is in aldehydes; C=O in the middle chain is in ketones** — these three oxygen-containing groups are a very common source of exam questions.
16. **Biogas is mainly methane (CH₄)** — produced by anaerobic bacteria from organic waste; used as cooking fuel in rural areas; Gobar gas (cow dung) produces biogas. Methane is also a potent greenhouse gas (25× more powerful than CO₂ per molecule over 100 years).
