# Chemical Bonding & Types of Chemical Reactions

## Why Atoms Hold Hands

Imagine you're attending a party where single people feel unstable and uncomfortable. Noble gases arrived alone and are perfectly happy вЂ” they've already found their inner peace (full electron shells). Everyone else is desperate to pair up, and they do so in three fundamentally different ways: some transfer electrons outright (ionic bonds), some share them (covalent bonds), and some pool them into a communal fund (metallic bonds). The result is everything from table salt to diamond to iron.

Chemistry is mostly the story of atoms trying to achieve the electronic configuration of the nearest noble gas вЂ” this drive is the engine of all chemical bonding.

---

## Octet Rule

**The Octet Rule:** Atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve a stable outer electron configuration of **8 electrons** (like noble gases from Ne onward). Hydrogen is satisfied with **2 electrons** (like helium вЂ” the 'duet rule').

This rule, proposed by Gilbert Lewis (1916) and Irving Langmuir (1919), explains why: Na loses 1 electron в†’ NaвЃє (becomes like Ne); Cl gains 1 electron в†’ ClвЃ» (becomes like Ar); Carbon shares 4 electrons with 4 hydrogen atoms в†’ CHв‚„.

## Type 1 вЂ” Ionic (Electrovalent) Bond

### How It Forms

An **ionic bond** is formed by the complete **transfer of electrons** from a metal atom to a non-metal atom. The result is two oppositely charged ions (cation + anion) held together by electrostatic attraction.

**Example вЂ” Sodium Chloride (table salt, NaCl):**
- Na (2,8,1) в†’ loses 1 electron в†’ NaвЃє (2,8) вЂ” stable, like Neon
- Cl (2,8,7) в†’ gains 1 electron в†’ ClвЃ» (2,8,8) вЂ” stable, like Argon
- NaвЃє + ClвЃ» в†’ NaCl (the + and в€’ charges attract strongly)

### Conditions for Ionic Bond Formation

| Factor | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Electronegativity difference | Large (> ~1.7 on Pauling scale) |
| Who donates? | Low ionisation energy metals (alkali/alkaline earth metals) |
| Who accepts? | High electron affinity non-metals (halogens, oxygen) |

### Properties of Ionic Compounds

| Property | Why |
|---|---|
| High melting and boiling point | Strong electrostatic forces between ions; lots of energy needed to break them |
| Soluble in water (polar solvent) | Water molecules surround and separate ions (hydration) |
| Conduct electricity in solution/melt | Free ions carry charge; solid doesn't conduct (ions locked in lattice) |
| Hard but brittle | Ions in fixed positions; when struck, like charges align and repel в†’ shatters |
| Crystalline solids | Regular, repeating 3D lattice structure |

## Type 2 вЂ” Covalent Bond

### How It Forms

A **covalent bond** is formed by the **sharing of electron pairs** between two atoms (usually between two non-metals, or a non-metal and hydrogen). Neither atom fully acquires the electrons; instead, the shared pair belongs to both.

**Example вЂ” Water (Hв‚‚O):**
- O (2,6) needs 2 electrons; each H needs 1 electron
- Two H atoms each share 1 electron with O в†’ O gets 2 shared pairs в†’ stable octet

**Bond types by number of shared pairs:**

| Bond | Shared Pairs | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Single covalent bond (вЂ”) | 1 | HвЂ“H (Hв‚‚), HвЂ“O in Hв‚‚O |
| Double covalent bond (=) | 2 | O=O (Oв‚‚), C=O in COв‚‚ |
| Triple covalent bond (в‰Ў) | 3 | Nв‰ЎN (Nв‚‚), Cв‰ЎC in acetylene (Cв‚‚Hв‚‚) |

More shared pairs = stronger and shorter bond.

### Properties of Covalent Compounds

| Property | Why |
|---|---|
| Low melting and boiling point (mostly) | Weak van der Waals forces between molecules (not between atoms) |
| Usually gases, liquids, or soft solids | Weak intermolecular forces |
| Generally don't conduct electricity | No free ions or electrons |
| Soluble in non-polar solvents | 'Like dissolves like' |
| Some polar covalent compounds soluble in water | If the molecule has a partial charge (e.g., HCl, NHв‚ѓ) |

A special type of covalent bond where **both shared electrons come from one atom** (the donor). Example: NHв‚„вЃє (ammonium ion) вЂ” after NHв‚ѓ forms normally, the N atom donates both electrons to HвЃє; the result is the same as a normal NвЂ“H bond once formed.

## Type 3 вЂ” Metallic Bond

### How It Forms

In metals, atoms lose their valence electrons to form a **'sea of delocalised electrons'** вЂ” a quantum soup of electrons free to roam throughout the entire metal structure. The positive metal ions (cations) are embedded in this electron sea and held together by the attraction between the positive lattice and the negative electron cloud.

**No specific atoms are bonded to specific other atoms** вЂ” the bond is collective.

### Properties Explained by Metallic Bond

| Property | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Electrical conductivity** | Free electrons carry charge through the metal under an electric field |
| **Thermal conductivity** | Free electrons carry kinetic energy (heat) through the lattice |
| **Lustre (shine)** | Free electrons absorb and re-emit light photons at the surface |
| **Malleability & ductility** | Positive ions can slide over each other; electron sea 'flows' with them; the bond doesn't break |
| **High melting points** (usually) | Strong electrostatic attraction between positive ions and electron sea |

## Type 4 вЂ” Hydrogen Bond

A special, weak type of interaction (not a full chemical bond but stronger than van der Waals forces) that forms when a hydrogen atom, already covalently bonded to a highly electronegative atom (F, O, or N), is attracted to another electronegative atom nearby.

**Why it matters for SSC:**
- **Water's anomalous properties**: high boiling point (100В°C), high surface tension, ice floats (H-bonds in solid ice make it less dense than liquid water) вЂ” all due to hydrogen bonding between water molecules
- **DNA double helix**: the two strands of DNA are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs (A-T, G-C)
- **Protein structure**: secondary and tertiary protein structures maintained by H-bonds
- **Cellulose structure**: cotton fibres get strength from H-bonds

## Exam Traps вЂ” 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Ionic bonds = electron TRANSFER; covalent bonds = electron SHARING** вЂ” mixing these up is the most common error. Ionic bonds form between metals and non-metals; covalent bonds between non-metals.
2. **Ionic compounds conduct electricity in SOLUTION or MELT, not in solid state** вЂ” in solid form, ions are fixed in the lattice and cannot move.
3. **Metallic bond = 'sea of delocalised electrons'** вЂ” this explains ALL properties of metals at once (conduction, malleability, lustre).
4. **Water has anomalously high boiling point for its molecular size because of hydrogen bonds** вЂ” without H-bonds, water would boil at approximately -80В°C (like Hв‚‚S).
5. **OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons); Reduction Is Gain** вЂ” in redox, the reducing agent loses electrons and gets oxidised; the oxidising agent gains electrons and gets reduced.
6. **Rusting of iron is an oxidation (redox) reaction and is exothermic** вЂ” 4Fe + 3Oв‚‚ + xHв‚‚O в†’ 2Feв‚‚Oв‚ѓВ·xHв‚‚O (rust requires both oxygen and moisture).
7. **Combustion is exothermic; photosynthesis is endothermic** вЂ” energy is released in combustion; absorbed from sunlight in photosynthesis.
8. **Double displacement = ion exchange, often producing a precipitate or gas** вЂ” the 'precipitation test' for sulphate ions uses BaClв‚‚ (white BaSOв‚„ precipitate).
9. **Iron displaces copper from copper sulphate solution** вЂ” blue solution (CuSOв‚„) turns pale green (FeSOв‚„); copper metal deposits on iron nail. This proves Fe > Cu in reactivity.
10. **AgBr decomposes on exposure to light (photolysis)** вЂ” this property was the basis of traditional film photography.
11. **CaCOв‚ѓ (limestone) decomposes on heating to CaO (quicklime) + COв‚‚** вЂ” this decomposition is used in cement making and to test COв‚‚ (lime water turns milky).
12. **Combination reaction produces ONE product; decomposition breaks ONE into MANY** вЂ” they are exact opposites.
13. **CO (carbon monoxide) forms from INCOMPLETE combustion** вЂ” in sufficient oxygen, complete combustion gives COв‚‚; inadequate Oв‚‚ gives toxic CO.
14. **In coordinate bond, BOTH electrons come from one atom (donor)** вЂ” this is unlike regular covalent bonds where each atom contributes one electron. Example: NHв‚„вЃє.
15. **Triple bond in Nв‚‚ (Nв‰ЎN) makes nitrogen very unreactive** вЂ” this strong bond requires high energy to break; explains why atmosphere is ~78% nitrogen without it all reacting.
16. **Electronegativity difference determines bond type**: 0 = pure covalent (between identical atoms); small (< 0.5) = non-polar covalent; moderate (0.5вЂ“1.7) = polar covalent; large (> 1.7) = ionic.
