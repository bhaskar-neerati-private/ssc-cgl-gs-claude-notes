# Matter, Atoms & Atomic Structure — States, Models & Electronic Configuration

## An Idea 2,400 Years in the Making

In 400 BC, the Greek philosopher Democritus sat by the sea and asked: if you kept cutting a piece of matter in half — and half again and half again — would you ever reach something that could not be divided further? He decided: yes. He called this indivisible particle **atomos** — "uncuttable." He had no proof. No instrument. Just reasoning.

For two millennia, the atom was a philosophical idea, not a scientific one. Then, in 1808, John Dalton gave atoms a scientific basis with his atomic theory. In 1897, J.J. Thomson shattered the idea of indivisibility by discovering the electron — something *inside* the atom. And in 1911, Ernest Rutherford showed that most of the atom was empty space, with a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus at the centre.

The atom had turned out to be anything but simple.

## What Is Matter?

**Matter** is anything that has mass and occupies space. Everything around you — the air you breathe, the book you read, your own body — is made of matter.

### States of Matter

| State | Shape | Volume | Particle arrangement | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Solid** | Fixed | Fixed | Tightly packed; strong intermolecular forces; vibrate in place | Ice, iron, salt |
| **Liquid** | Takes container shape | Fixed | Loosely packed; moderate forces; flow freely | Water, mercury, oil |
| **Gas** | Takes container shape | Fills container | Very far apart; weak forces; high kinetic energy | Steam, oxygen, air |
| **Plasma** | Takes container shape | Fills container | Highly ionised gas; nuclei and electrons separate | Stars, lightning, neon signs |

**Plasma is the 4th state of matter** — most common state in the universe (stars are plasma) but rare naturally on Earth.

### Phase Changes

```
Solid ←(Melting/Fusion)→ Liquid ←(Vaporisation/Evaporation)→ Gas
Solid ←(Sublimation)→ Gas (direct)
```

| Change | Process | Energy |
|---|---|---|
| Solid → Liquid | Melting (Fusion) | Absorbs heat |
| Liquid → Solid | Freezing (Solidification) | Releases heat |
| Liquid → Gas | Vaporisation (Evaporation/Boiling) | Absorbs heat |
| Gas → Liquid | Condensation | Releases heat |
| Solid → Gas | **Sublimation** | Absorbs heat |
| Gas → Solid | Deposition | Releases heat |

**Substances that sublime:** Dry ice (CO₂), Iodine (I₂), Camphor, Naphthalene (mothballs), Ammonium chloride (NH₄Cl).

## Physical vs Chemical Changes

## Elements, Compounds, and Mixtures

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Element** | Pure substance; one type of atom; cannot be broken down chemically | Fe (iron), Au (gold), O₂, N₂ |
| **Compound** | Two or more elements chemically combined in fixed ratios | H₂O (water), NaCl (salt), CO₂ |
| **Mixture** | Two or more substances physically combined; no fixed ratio; components retain properties | Air, seawater, alloys, soil |

**Mixtures — two types:**
- **Homogeneous (Solution):** Uniform composition throughout; cannot see components (salt water, brass, air)
- **Heterogeneous:** Non-uniform; components visible or separable (sand + iron filings, oil + water)

### Separation Techniques

| Technique | Separates | Principle |
|---|---|---|
| **Filtration** | Insoluble solid from liquid | Particle size difference |
| **Evaporation** | Dissolved solid from liquid | Volatile solvent evaporates |
| **Distillation** | Liquids with different boiling points | Difference in volatility |
| **Chromatography** | Components of a mixture | Different rates of movement on stationary phase |
| **Centrifugation** | Denser components from lighter | Centrifugal force separates by density |
| **Magnetic separation** | Magnetic from non-magnetic | Magnetism |
| **Sublimation** | Sublime substance from non-sublime | Direct solid → gas |

## Dalton's Atomic Theory (1808)

John Dalton revived and scientifically grounded Democritus's idea:
1. All matter is made of tiny, indivisible particles called **atoms**
2. Atoms of the same element are identical in mass and properties
3. Atoms of different elements differ in mass and properties
4. Atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form compounds
5. Atoms cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction

**Limitations:** Atoms are divisible (subatomic particles); isotopes have same element but different masses; Dalton's theory did not explain electrical nature of matter.

## Laws of Chemical Combination

| Law | Statement | Discoverer |
|---|---|---|
| **Law of Conservation of Mass** | Total mass of reactants = Total mass of products; matter can neither be created nor destroyed | **Lavoisier (1789)** |
| **Law of Definite Proportions (Constant Composition)** | A chemical compound always contains the same elements in the same ratio by mass | **Proust (1799)** |
| **Law of Multiple Proportions** | If two elements A and B form more than one compound, then the masses of B that combine with a fixed mass of A are in a simple ratio | **Dalton (1803)** |
| **Gay-Lussac's Law of Gaseous Volumes** | Gases combine in simple whole-number ratios by volume | **Gay-Lussac (1808)** |
| **Avogadro's Law** | Equal volumes of all gases at the same temperature and pressure contain the same number of molecules | **Avogadro (1811)** |

## Discovery of Subatomic Particles

### Electron — J.J. Thomson (1897)
Thomson passed an electric discharge through a gas at very low pressure in a cathode ray tube. The rays were deflected by both electric and magnetic fields, showing they were made of **negatively charged particles** much lighter than any atom. He called these **electrons** (or corpuscles initially).

**Key fact:** Thomson discovered that all atoms contain electrons — and since atoms are electrically neutral, there must also be positive charge to balance.

### Proton
- Canal rays (positive rays) were discovered by **Goldstein (1886)** using a modified discharge tube
- **Rutherford (1919)** confirmed that when nitrogen was bombarded with alpha particles, hydrogen nuclei (protons) were expelled — establishing the proton as a fundamental particle
- Proton: mass ≈ 1836 × mass of electron; charge = +1

### Neutron — James Chadwick (1932)
Bombarding beryllium with alpha particles produced an unknown neutral radiation. Chadwick showed these were uncharged particles of mass ~1 amu — the **neutron**.

| Particle | Symbol | Charge | Relative mass | Location |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Proton | p⁺ | +1 | 1 amu | Nucleus |
| Neutron | n⁰ | 0 | 1 amu | Nucleus |
| Electron | e⁻ | -1 | 1/1836 amu | Orbitals/shells |

## Atomic Models — The Evolution of Understanding

### Thomson's Plum Pudding Model (1904)
The atom is a sphere of diffuse positive charge with electrons embedded in it like plums in a pudding. **Disproved** by Rutherford's gold foil experiment.

### Rutherford's Nuclear Model (1911) — The Gold Foil Experiment
Rutherford directed a beam of alpha particles (He²⁺) at a thin gold foil:
- Most particles passed straight through → atom is mostly **empty space**
- A small fraction were deflected at large angles → a small, dense, positively charged **nucleus** exists at the centre
- A very few bounced straight back → the nucleus is very small but extremely dense

**Rutherford's model:** Dense positive nucleus surrounded by electrons orbiting at a distance.

**Limitation:** A charged particle in circular motion should continuously emit radiation and lose energy → electrons should spiral into the nucleus within nanoseconds. Atoms would not be stable. (Classical electromagnetism predicts this — but atoms clearly ARE stable.)

### Bohr's Model (1913) — Quantised Orbits
Niels Bohr resolved Rutherford's stability problem by introducing quantum ideas:
1. Electrons move in fixed circular orbits (shells) around the nucleus — each orbit has a fixed energy
2. As long as an electron stays in its orbit, it does not radiate energy
3. Electrons can jump to a higher orbit by absorbing a specific quantum of energy (photon); they fall back by emitting a photon of the same energy
4. This emission/absorption gives rise to the **line spectrum of hydrogen**

**Shells (Energy levels):** K, L, M, N, O, P, Q (or 1, 2, 3, 4...)

**Limitation:** Only accurate for hydrogen; fails for multi-electron atoms; does not explain fine structure of spectra.

### Quantum Mechanical Model (Schrödinger, 1926)
Electrons do not move in fixed paths but exist in **orbitals** — regions of probability where the electron is likely to be found. Described by quantum numbers.

## Atomic Number and Mass Number

| Term | Definition | Symbol |
|---|---|---|
| **Atomic Number (Z)** | Number of protons in nucleus = Number of electrons in neutral atom | Z |
| **Mass Number (A)** | Number of protons + Number of neutrons | A |
| **Number of neutrons** | A − Z | — |

**Notation:** ᴬ_Z X  e.g., ²³_₁₁Na → sodium has 11 protons, 12 neutrons (23−11), 11 electrons

## Isotopes, Isobars, and Isotones

### Isotopes — Same Z, Different A
Same element (same number of protons), different mass number (different neutrons).

| Element | Isotopes | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Hydrogen** | ¹H (Protium), ²H (Deuterium/D), ³H (Tritium/T) | D₂O = Heavy water; used in nuclear reactors |
| **Carbon** | ¹²C (98.9%), ¹³C, ¹⁴C (radioactive) | ¹⁴C used in radiocarbon dating |
| **Uranium** | ²³⁵U (fissile), ²³⁸U (most abundant) | ²³⁵U used in nuclear reactors/bombs |
| **Chlorine** | ³⁵Cl (75%), ³⁷Cl (25%) | Average atomic mass = 35.5 |
| **Oxygen** | ¹⁶O (99.76%), ¹⁷O, ¹⁸O | — |

### Isobars — Same A, Different Z
Different elements with the same mass number.
- ⁴⁰Ar (Z=18) and ⁴⁰Ca (Z=20) and ⁴⁰K (Z=19) — all have A=40

### Isotones — Same number of neutrons, Different Z
- ¹⁴C (Z=6, N=8) and ¹⁵N (Z=7, N=8)

## Electronic Configuration

**Rules:**
- Maximum electrons per shell: **2n²** (K=2, L=8, M=18, N=32)
- **Aufbau principle:** Fill lowest energy orbitals first
- **Octet rule:** Atoms tend to gain/lose/share electrons to achieve 8 electrons in outermost shell (noble gas configuration)

| Element | Z | Configuration |
|---|---|---|
| Hydrogen | 1 | 1 |
| Helium | 2 | 2 |
| Lithium | 3 | 2, 1 |
| Carbon | 6 | 2, 4 |
| Nitrogen | 7 | 2, 5 |
| Oxygen | 8 | 2, 6 |
| Sodium | 11 | 2, 8, 1 |
| Magnesium | 12 | 2, 8, 2 |
| Aluminium | 13 | 2, 8, 3 |
| Silicon | 14 | 2, 8, 4 |
| Chlorine | 17 | 2, 8, 7 |
| Argon | 18 | 2, 8, 8 |
| Calcium | 20 | 2, 8, 8, 2 |

**Valence electrons:** Electrons in the outermost shell — determine chemical behaviour and bonding capacity.

## Valency

**Valency** = the combining capacity of an element; number of electrons gained, lost, or shared to achieve a stable configuration.

| Element | Symbol | Valency | How achieved |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hydrogen | H | 1 | Loses or shares 1e⁻ |
| Carbon | C | 4 | Shares 4e⁻ |
| Nitrogen | N | 3 | Shares 3e⁻ (or gains 3e⁻) |
| Oxygen | O | 2 | Gains or shares 2e⁻ |
| Sodium | Na | 1 | Loses 1e⁻ |
| Magnesium | Mg | 2 | Loses 2e⁻ |
| Aluminium | Al | 3 | Loses 3e⁻ |
| Chlorine | Cl | 1 | Gains 1e⁻ |
| Sulphur | S | 2 (or 4, 6) | Variable |
| Iron | Fe | 2 or 3 | Variable (ferrous/ferric) |

## The Mole Concept

**1 mole** = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, ions)
**Avogadro's number (Nₐ) = 6.022 × 10²³ mol⁻¹** — named after Amedeo Avogadro

**Molar mass** = mass of 1 mole of a substance in grams = numerically equal to atomic/molecular mass in amu
- 1 mole of C = 12 g; 1 mole of H₂O = 18 g; 1 mole of NaCl = 58.5 g

**Why the mole?** Atoms are too small to count individually. The mole allows chemists to count atoms/molecules by weighing.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **J.J. Thomson discovered the ELECTRON (1897)** — not the atom; not the proton; not the neutron. His cathode ray tube experiment.
2. **Chadwick discovered the NEUTRON (1932)** — the last of the three subatomic particles to be found; bombarded beryllium with alpha particles.
3. **Rutherford's gold foil experiment** proved the atom has a small, dense, positively charged NUCLEUS; most of the atom is empty space.
4. **Atomic number (Z) = number of PROTONS** (not electrons, not mass). In a neutral atom, Z also equals the number of electrons.
5. **Mass number (A) = Protons + Neutrons** — electrons are ignored in mass number (they weigh almost nothing).
6. **Isotopes: same atomic number (Z), different mass number (A)** — same element, different neutrons. Hydrogen's isotopes: Protium (¹H), Deuterium (²H), Tritium (³H).
7. **Isobars: same mass number (A), different atomic number (Z)** — different elements. Example: ⁴⁰Ar and ⁴⁰Ca.
8. **Heavy water = D₂O (deuterium oxide)** — used as a moderator in CANDU nuclear reactors; NOT ordinary water with heavy minerals dissolved in it.
9. **Avogadro's number = 6.022 × 10²³** — number of atoms/molecules in one mole of any substance.
10. **Maximum electrons in a shell = 2n²** (K=2, L=8, M=18, N=32).
11. **Bohr's model explained the hydrogen spectrum** — electrons jump between fixed energy levels; each jump emits/absorbs a specific wavelength of light.
12. **Thomson's "Plum Pudding" model was disproved by Rutherford's gold foil experiment (1911)** — the sequence matters; Thomson came first (1904), Rutherford second (1911).
13. **Sublimation = solid directly converts to gas** — examples: dry ice (CO₂), iodine, camphor, naphthalene. SSC frequently asks which substances sublime.
14. **Plasma = 4th state of matter** — ionised gas; found in stars, the Sun, lightning, neon signs. Most common state of matter in the universe.
15. **Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier, 1789):** mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction — the foundational law of chemistry.
16. **Valency of Carbon = 4** (tetravalent) — this is why carbon can form millions of compounds; forms 4 bonds simultaneously.
