# Ray Optics — Reflection, Refraction, TIR, Lenses, Mirrors & Optical Instruments

## The Arab Scholar Who Explained Sight

For centuries, the Greeks believed that vision worked because the eye *emitted* rays of light that struck objects and bounced back — the eye was the lantern. It took an Iraqi mathematician born in 965 CE to overturn this ancient theory entirely. Ibn al-Haytham (Latinised as Alhazen) locked himself in a darkened room in Cairo, let a single beam of light enter through a tiny hole, and wrote the *Book of Optics* — the first systematic treatise on how light actually works, how the eye perceives, and how lenses bend light. He concluded that the eye *receives* light from objects, not the reverse.

Alhazen's camera obscura principle is in every smartphone. His experiments with refraction underlie every spectacle lens worn by 2.5 billion people worldwide. And his methodology — observe, experiment, repeat — made him one of the first true scientists.

## Part 1 — Nature of Light

Light is an electromagnetic wave (also has particle/photon nature — dual nature, covered in Modern Physics). In ray optics, we treat light as straight-line rays for geometric analysis.

**Speed of light in vacuum**: c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s (approximately 300,000 km/s)

Light travels in straight lines (rectilinear propagation) — evidenced by shadows, eclipses, and the pinhole camera.

## Part 2 — Reflection of Light

**Reflection**: light bouncing off a surface.

**Laws of Reflection:**
1. Angle of incidence (i) = Angle of reflection (r) — measured from the normal to the surface
2. Incident ray, reflected ray, and normal all lie in the same plane

### Plane Mirror
- Image is **virtual** (behind the mirror), **erect** (same side up), **laterally inverted** (left-right reversed)
- Image is the **same size** as the object
- Image distance = object distance (behind mirror = in front of object)

**Applications**: rear-view mirrors in rooms; periscope uses two plane mirrors.

### Spherical Mirrors

| Property | Concave Mirror (converging) | Convex Mirror (diverging) |
|----------|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| Shape | Curved inward | Curved outward |
| Focus | Real, in front of mirror | Virtual, behind mirror |
| Image (far object) | Real, inverted | Virtual, erect, diminished |
| Uses | Shaving/makeup mirror, solar cooker, headlights, dentist's mirror, telescopes | Rear-view mirrors in vehicles, shop security mirrors |

**Mirror Formula**: **1/f = 1/v + 1/u**
- f = focal length, v = image distance, u = object distance
- Sign convention: distances measured from pole; direction of incident light = positive

**Magnification**: m = −v/u (negative = inverted image; positive = erect image)

**For a concave mirror**: focal length = R/2 (R = radius of curvature); real focal point in front of mirror

## Part 3 — Refraction of Light

**Refraction**: bending of light as it passes from one medium to another (due to change in speed).

**Laws of Refraction (Snell's Law):**
1. The incident ray, refracted ray, and normal lie in the same plane
2. **n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r** (Snell's Law) — where n is the refractive index, i is angle of incidence, r is angle of refraction

**Refractive Index (n)** = speed of light in vacuum / speed of light in medium = c/v

| Medium | Refractive Index (n) |
|--------|---------------------|
| Vacuum | 1.00000 |
| Air | 1.0003 ≈ 1.00 |
| Water | 1.33 |
| Glass (crown) | 1.52 |
| Diamond | 2.42 (highest among natural materials) |

**Rules:**
- Light going from less dense (lower n) to more dense (higher n) medium: bends **toward** the normal, slows down
- Light going from more dense to less dense medium: bends **away** from normal, speeds up

**Apparent depth**: objects in water appear shallower than they are (apparent depth < real depth). A spoon in a glass of water appears bent — classic refraction demonstration.

## Part 4 — Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

**Total Internal Reflection**: when light travels from a denser medium to a less dense medium at an angle greater than the **critical angle**, ALL light is reflected — none is transmitted.

**Critical angle (θ_c)**: sin(θ_c) = n₂/n₁ (when n₂ < n₁)

For glass-air: θ_c ≈ 42°; For water-air: θ_c ≈ 49°; For diamond-air: θ_c ≈ 24° (very small — hence diamonds sparkle at many angles)

**Applications of TIR:**
- **Optical fibre**: light is guided along glass/silica fibre by repeated TIR at the fibre wall. Used in internet cables, endoscopy
- **Diamond sparkle**: very small critical angle (24°) means light entering a diamond undergoes TIR many times before escaping → brilliant sparkle
- **Mirage**: hot air near desert ground refracts light from sky continuously, eventually exceeding critical angle → TIR → image of sky appears on ground (looks like water)
- **Periscope of submarine**: prisms use TIR instead of mirrors (more efficient)

## Part 5 — Lenses

**Lens**: a transparent refracting material shaped to converge or diverge light.

| Property | Convex (Converging) Lens | Concave (Diverging) Lens |
|----------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Shape | Thicker at centre | Thicker at edges |
| Action | Converges parallel rays to real focus | Diverges parallel rays (virtual focus) |
| Image of distant object | Real, inverted, at focus | Virtual, erect, diminished |
| Uses | Magnifying glass, camera, projector, eye (for hypermetropia), microscope objective | Correcting myopia (short-sightedness) |

**Lens Formula**: **1/f = 1/v − 1/u** (same sign convention as mirror but different formula)

**Power of a lens (P)** = **1/f** (f in metres)  
**Unit: Dioptre (D)** — 1 D = 1 m⁻¹

- Convex lens: positive power (converging)
- Concave lens: negative power (diverging)
- Combined lenses: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ ...

**Optometrist prescriptions** use power in dioptres (e.g., −2.5 D = concave lens for myopia; +1.5 D = convex lens for hypermetropia).

## Part 6 — Human Eye and Vision Defects

**The Human Eye:**
- **Cornea**: outermost transparent layer; does most of the refraction
- **Aqueous humour**: fluid behind cornea
- **Iris**: controls pupil size (adjusts light entering)
- **Pupil**: hole in iris; dilates in dim light, contracts in bright light
- **Lens**: convex biconvex lens; fine-tunes focus via ciliary muscles (accommodation)
- **Vitreous humour**: gel inside eyeball
- **Retina**: light-sensitive screen; contains rods (dim light) and cones (colour)
- **Fovea**: point of sharpest vision (most cones)
- **Blind spot**: optic nerve exit point; no rods or cones
- **Optic nerve**: carries signals to brain

**Accommodation**: the ability of the lens to change its focal length (by changing shape via ciliary muscles) to focus on objects at different distances.

### Vision Defects and Corrections

| Defect | Problem | Cause | Correction |
|--------|---------|-------|-----------|
| **Myopia** (short-sightedness / near-sightedness) | Cannot see distant objects clearly | Image forms in front of retina; eyeball too long | **Concave lens** (diverging) |
| **Hypermetropia** (long-sightedness / far-sightedness) | Cannot see near objects clearly | Image forms behind retina; eyeball too short | **Convex lens** (converging) |
| **Astigmatism** | Blurred vision at all distances | Cornea has uneven curvature | Cylindrical lens |
| **Presbyopia** | Loss of near vision with age | Ciliary muscles weaken; lens loses flexibility | Bifocal lens (reading + distance) |
| **Colour blindness** | Cannot distinguish certain colours | Absence/malfunction of cone types; inherited (X-linked) | No cure; special lenses/glasses can help |

## Part 7 — Optical Instruments

### Simple Microscope (Magnifying Glass)
- Single convex lens; object placed within focal length
- Produces a virtual, erect, magnified image
- Magnification = 1 + D/f (D = 25 cm = least distance of distinct vision)

### Compound Microscope
- Two convex lenses: **objective** (short focal length, high power, near object) + **eyepiece** (acts as magnifying glass)
- Very high magnification (100×–2000×)
- Image is final virtual, magnified, inverted

### Refracting Telescope (Astronomical Telescope)
- Two convex lenses: **objective** (long focal length, large diameter) + **eyepiece** (short focal length)
- Objective forms real image; eyepiece magnifies
- Final image is inverted (acceptable for astronomy)
- Galileo's telescope (1609): used this principle to discover Jupiter's moons

### Reflecting Telescope (Newtonian)
- Uses concave mirror (objective) + convex lens (eyepiece)
- Avoids chromatic aberration; can be very large (no sagging issue as with large lenses)
- World's largest optical telescopes use mirrors: Keck Observatory (10 m), TMT (proposed 30 m)

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Concave mirror uses**: shaving/makeup (close, magnified, erect image); headlights and torches (parallel beam from source at focus); solar cookers; doctor's head mirror. **Convex mirror uses**: rear-view mirrors (wide field of view, virtual erect diminished image).

2. **Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection** — both measured from the **normal** (perpendicular to surface), not from the surface itself. This is the most common error in reflection problems.

3. **Refractive index of diamond (2.42) is the highest** of common materials. Small critical angle (~24°) means TIR occurs at many angles inside the stone — that's why diamonds sparkle brilliantly.

4. **Power of lens in dioptres (D)**: P = 1/f (f in metres). A 50 cm focal length convex lens: P = 1/0.5 = +2 D. A 25 cm focal length concave lens: P = −4 D. Negative dioptre = concave = myopia correction.

5. **Myopia = short-sightedness**: near objects seen clearly; distant blurred. Corrected by **concave (diverging) lens**. Hypermetropia = long-sightedness: distant objects seen clearly; near blurred. Corrected by **convex (converging) lens**.

6. **Total Internal Reflection (TIR) requires**: (1) light going from denser to less dense medium, AND (2) angle of incidence > critical angle. Both conditions must be met simultaneously.

7. **Optical fibres work by TIR**: light is repeatedly reflected inside the fibre and travels long distances without significant loss. Used in telecommunications (internet backbone) and medical endoscopes (viewing inside the body without surgery).

8. **The human eye lens is biconvex** — it's a converging (convex) lens. The lens does fine focusing; the cornea does ~70% of the total refraction. Near point of normal eye = 25 cm (least distance of distinct vision).

9. **Least distance of distinct vision = 25 cm** for a normal adult eye. Objects closer than 25 cm cannot be seen clearly without a magnifying aid (the eye cannot accommodate that much).

10. **Mirror formula vs Lens formula**: Mirror: 1/f = 1/v + 1/u. Lens: 1/f = 1/v − 1/u. The sign convention (New Cartesian) is the same for both. For a mirror, object in front → u is negative; for a lens, object on left → u is negative.

11. **Mirage is a TIR phenomenon**, not a reflection from the actual ground. Hot air near the desert floor is less dense (lower refractive index) than cool air above — light from the sky bends and eventually undergoes TIR, appearing as a "pool" of reflected sky on the ground.

12. **Presbyopia (age-related vision loss)**: the eye lens loses flexibility and ciliary muscles weaken with age — the eye can no longer accommodate for near objects. A person may need reading glasses (convex lens) in addition to distance glasses → bifocal lenses.

13. **Compound microscope vs telescope**: in a microscope, the objective is SHORT focal length and the object is very close. In a telescope, the objective is LONG focal length and the object is very far (essentially at infinity). Both use a short-focal-length eyepiece.

14. **Snell's Law: n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r**. When light goes from air (n=1) to glass (n=1.5), angle of refraction < angle of incidence (light bends toward normal). When going from glass to air, angle of refraction > angle of incidence (light bends away from normal).

15. **Colour of sky (Rayleigh scattering) is NOT covered here** — that's Topic 10 (Light Phenomena). Do not confuse the sky's blue colour with reflection/refraction. The blue sky is due to preferential scattering of blue light (shorter wavelength) by air molecules.

16. **Virtual image vs Real image**: real image is formed where light rays actually meet (can be projected on a screen). Virtual image is formed where light rays appear to diverge from (cannot be projected; seen by looking through the mirror/lens). Plane mirror and convex mirror always produce virtual images. Concave mirror produces virtual image only when object is between focus and mirror.
