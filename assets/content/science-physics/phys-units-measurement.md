# Units, Measurement & Dimensions — SI Units, Scalars, Vectors & Significant Figures

## The $125 Million Lesson in Units

On 23 September 1999, NASA's Mars Climate Orbiter vanished. It had travelled 286 million miles over nine months, cost $327 million, and was supposed to study Martian weather. The culprit? One engineering team was using metric units (Newton-seconds); another was using imperial units (pound-force-seconds). The navigation data was off by a factor of 4.45. The spacecraft entered the Martian atmosphere at the wrong angle and burned up.

A $125 million loss — because no one agreed on units.

This story captures exactly why physics starts with measurement. Before you can describe the universe — whether you're landing a spacecraft or timing a race — you need a shared language. That language is the **International System of Units (SI)**, the global standard adopted in 1960. Master this foundation and every other chapter of physics becomes clearer.

## Part 1 — Physical Quantities: Fundamental & Derived

A **physical quantity** is anything that can be measured. Every physical quantity has a **magnitude** (numerical value) and a **unit**.

### Fundamental (Base) Quantities

These seven quantities cannot be expressed in terms of others. Everything else is derived from them.

| Quantity | SI Unit | Symbol | Dimension |
|----------|---------|--------|-----------|
| **Length** | Metre | m | [L] |
| **Mass** | Kilogram | kg | [M] |
| **Time** | Second | s | [T] |
| **Electric current** | Ampere | A | [I] |
| **Temperature** | Kelvin | K | [θ] |
| **Amount of substance** | Mole | mol | [N] |
| **Luminous intensity** | Candela | cd | [J] |

**Memory trick**: "My Kind Teacher Is Amazingly Nice, Clearly" → Mass, K (Kelvin), Time, I (current), A (Amount), N? — actually just remember the 7: metre, kilogram, second, ampere, kelvin, mole, candela.

### Derived Quantities

All other physical quantities are combinations of the base seven:

| Derived Quantity | Formula | SI Unit | Dimension |
|----------------|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Area** | length × length | m² | [L²] |
| **Volume** | length³ | m³ | [L³] |
| **Velocity/Speed** | length/time | m/s | [LT⁻¹] |
| **Acceleration** | velocity/time | m/s² | [LT⁻²] |
| **Force** | mass × acceleration | Newton (N) = kg·m/s² | [MLT⁻²] |
| **Pressure** | force/area | Pascal (Pa) = N/m² | [ML⁻¹T⁻²] |
| **Energy/Work** | force × distance | Joule (J) = N·m | [ML²T⁻²] |
| **Power** | energy/time | Watt (W) = J/s | [ML²T⁻³] |
| **Frequency** | cycles/second | Hertz (Hz) | [T⁻¹] |
| **Electric charge** | current × time | Coulomb (C) = A·s | [IT] |
| **Voltage/EMF** | energy/charge | Volt (V) | [ML²T⁻³I⁻¹] |
| **Resistance** | voltage/current | Ohm (Ω) | [ML²T⁻³I⁻²] |
| **Magnetic flux** | — | Weber (Wb) | [ML²T⁻²I⁻¹] |

## Part 3 — Scalars and Vectors

### Scalar Quantities
Have only **magnitude** (no direction).

Examples: mass, distance, speed, time, temperature, energy, power, pressure, volume, density, work.

**Distance** = total path length (scalar)  
**Speed** = distance/time (scalar)

### Vector Quantities
Have both **magnitude** AND **direction**.

Examples: displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum, weight, electric field, magnetic field, torque.

**Displacement** = shortest path from start to end (vector)  
**Velocity** = displacement/time (vector)

### Key Scalar vs Vector Pairs (SSC loves to confuse these)

| Scalar | Vector |
|--------|--------|
| Distance | Displacement |
| Speed | Velocity |
| Mass | Weight (force due to gravity) |
| Energy | Momentum |
| Temperature | — |
| Power | Force |

**A body can travel a large distance with zero displacement** — run around a circular track and return to start. Distance = perimeter of track; displacement = 0.

## Part 6 — Dimensional Analysis

Every physical quantity can be expressed in terms of fundamental dimensions [M], [L], [T], [I], [θ], [N], [J].

**Uses of Dimensional Analysis:**
1. Check the correctness of an equation (dimensions must balance on both sides)
2. Convert between unit systems
3. Derive relations between physical quantities

**Dimensionless quantities** — quantities with dimension [M⁰L⁰T⁰]:
- Angle (radian), strain, refractive index, relative density, Reynolds number, Mach number

**Important dimensional formulas:**

| Quantity | Dimension |
|---------|-----------|
| Force | [MLT⁻²] |
| Work/Energy | [ML²T⁻²] |
| Power | [ML²T⁻³] |
| Pressure | [ML⁻¹T⁻²] |
| Momentum | [MLT⁻¹] |
| Impulse | [MLT⁻¹] |
| Torque | [ML²T⁻²] |
| Surface tension | [MT⁻²] |
| Viscosity (η) | [ML⁻¹T⁻¹] |
| Planck's constant (h) | [ML²T⁻¹] |
| Gravitational constant (G) | [M⁻¹L³T⁻²] |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Light year is a unit of DISTANCE, not time.** It is the distance light travels in one year (~9.46 × 10¹⁵ m). SSC routinely asks this as a trap — don't say it's a unit of time.

2. **Parsec is the largest unit of distance** among those commonly listed: 1 parsec = 3.26 light years. Astronomers use it for stellar distances. 1 AU < 1 ly < 1 parsec.

3. **The Ångström (Å = 10⁻¹⁰ m)** is used for atomic radii, bond lengths, and wavelengths of visible light (380–750 nm = 3800–7500 Å). It is NOT an SI unit but widely used.

4. **Kilogram is the only SI base unit with a prefix (kilo)** in its name. This is a unique quirk — gram is not the base unit; kilogram is.

5. **Speed is scalar; velocity is vector.** A car going 60 km/h is its speed. A car going 60 km/h towards north is its velocity. Equal speeds but different velocities produce different outcomes.

6. **Weight is a vector (force), mass is a scalar.** Weight = mg (force due to gravity). Mass doesn't change; weight changes on the Moon (g is ~1/6th of Earth's).

7. **Displacement can be zero even if distance is non-zero.** If you run around a track and return to start: total distance = perimeter; displacement = 0. This directly tests scalar/vector understanding.

8. **Ammeter is connected in SERIES; voltmeter in PARALLEL.** Ammeter has very low resistance (to not disturb current). Voltmeter has very high resistance (to not draw current).

9. **Hydrometer measures density/specific gravity of liquids.** Hygrometer measures humidity. These two are frequently confused. Lactometer is a specialised hydrometer for milk.

10. **Sphygmomanometer** = blood pressure instrument. Stethoscope listens to heart/lungs. Electrocardiogram (ECG/EKG) records electrical activity of the heart.

11. **Candela is the SI unit of luminous intensity** (brightness of a light source in a given direction). Do not confuse with Lumen (luminous flux = total light emitted) or Lux (illuminance = light per unit area).

12. **Mole is the SI unit of amount of substance.** 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number). It is one of the 7 base units, not a derived unit.

13. **Radian is a supplementary unit** (not base, not derived) — it is the SI unit of plane angle. Steradian is the SI unit of solid angle. Both are dimensionless.

14. **Vernier callipers give precision to 0.1 mm (0.01 cm)**; a screw gauge/micrometer gives precision to 0.01 mm (0.001 cm). A metre rule gives precision to 1 mm.

15. **Pressure (Pa) = N/m²; Pascal's unit** is named after Blaise Pascal. Standard atmospheric pressure = 101,325 Pa ≈ 1.013 × 10⁵ Pa. 1 bar = 10⁵ Pa. 1 atmosphere ≈ 1.013 bar.

16. **The SI system has exactly 7 base units** — no more, no less. A common wrong answer is "8" (people add radian/steradian which are supplementary/dimensionless). The 7 are: metre, kilogram, second, ampere, kelvin, mole, candela.
