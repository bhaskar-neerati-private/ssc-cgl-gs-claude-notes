# Work, Energy & Power — Kinetic/Potential Energy, Conservation & Simple Machines

## James Watt's Horse and the Birth of the Watt

In 1782, James Watt needed to convince mine owners to buy his steam engines. How could he compare his engine to the thing it was replacing — horses? He ran an experiment: a horse working at a mill lifted weights over a pulley. It could raise 33,000 pounds by 1 foot in one minute. Watt defined this as "1 horsepower" and showed his engine could produce several horsepower continuously. The mine owners bought the engines.

His name now defines the SI unit of power. But the deeper insight behind that commercial sales pitch is physics: **energy** can be measured, **work** can be quantified, and machines that do work can be compared on a universal scale. This chapter builds that scale from first principles.

---

## Part 1 — Work

**Work** is done when a force causes displacement in the direction of the force.

**W = F × d × cos θ**

where F = applied force, d = displacement, θ = angle between force and displacement.

| θ | cos θ | Work |
|---|-------|------|
| 0° | 1 | Maximum positive work (force and displacement in same direction) |
| 90° | 0 | **Zero work** (force perpendicular to displacement) |
| 180° | −1 | Negative work (force opposes displacement) |

**SI unit of work**: Joule (J) = N·m = kg·m²/s²

### When is Work Zero?

- Carrying a suitcase at constant height on a level path (force is vertical, displacement horizontal → θ = 90°)
- A satellite moving in a circular orbit (gravity is centripetal; displacement is tangential → θ = 90° at every point)
- A man pushing a wall that doesn't move (displacement = 0)
- Normal force on a body moving horizontally (normal force is vertical)

### Positive and Negative Work

- **Positive work**: Engine on a car (force and motion same direction)
- **Negative work**: Friction on a moving body (friction opposes motion)
- **Negative work**: Brakes on a car (decelerating force, displacement is forward)

## Part 2 — Energy

**Energy** = capacity to do work. It is a scalar quantity.

**SI unit**: Joule (J)  
Other units: calorie (1 cal = 4.18 J), electron volt (1 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J), kWh (1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J)

**1 kWh (kilowatt-hour)** = the energy consumed by a 1 kW device in 1 hour. This is the unit used in electricity bills ("1 unit of electricity").

### Kinetic Energy (KE)

Energy due to motion:  
**KE = ½mv²**

- Depends on speed (v²) and mass (m)
- Always positive (scalar)
- Doubling speed quadruples KE; doubling mass doubles KE

**Work-Energy Theorem**: Net work done on a body = change in its kinetic energy  
W_net = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mu²

### Potential Energy (PE)

Energy due to position or configuration:

**Gravitational PE = mgh**  
(mass m at height h above reference level, g = gravitational acceleration)

**Elastic PE = ½kx²**  
(spring compressed/stretched by x, spring constant k — Hooke's Law: F = kx)

### Law of Conservation of Energy

**"Energy can neither be created nor destroyed; it can only be converted from one form to another. The total energy of an isolated system remains constant."**

**Mechanical energy** = KE + PE = constant (in absence of non-conservative forces like friction)

**Example — ball falling from height H:**
- At top: PE = mgH, KE = 0
- Halfway: PE = mg(H/2), KE = mg(H/2) → total = mgH
- At bottom: PE = 0, KE = mgH

**Pendulum**: At extreme position (maximum height) → maximum PE, zero KE. At mean position (bottom) → maximum KE, zero PE. Total energy constant.

### Forms of Energy

| Form | Description | Examples |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Kinetic** | Energy of motion | Moving car, wind, river |
| **Potential** | Energy of position/configuration | Water at height, compressed spring, stretched bow |
| **Thermal/Heat** | Energy of random molecular motion | Hot tea, steam |
| **Chemical** | Energy stored in chemical bonds | Food, fuel, batteries |
| **Nuclear** | Energy in atomic nucleus | Nuclear fuel, Sun |
| **Electrical** | Energy of moving charges | Current in wire |
| **Electromagnetic/Radiant** | Energy carried by EM waves | Light, X-rays, radio waves |
| **Sound** | Mechanical energy in pressure waves | Music, noise |

## Part 3 — Power

**Power** = rate of doing work = work done per unit time

**P = W/t = F × v** (for constant force and velocity)

**SI unit**: Watt (W) = J/s  
**1 horsepower (hp)** = 746 W ≈ 0.746 kW  
(James Watt's original unit: 1 hp = 33,000 ft·lb/min = 550 ft·lb/s)

**Kilowatt (kW)** = 1000 W  
**Megawatt (MW)** = 10⁶ W (used for power plant output)

## Part 4 — Simple Machines

A **simple machine** is a device that changes the magnitude or direction of a force to make work easier. It does NOT reduce the total work done — it makes it easier by trading force for distance (or vice versa).

**Mechanical Advantage (MA)** = Load / Effort = distance moved by effort / distance moved by load

**Efficiency (η)** = (Work output / Work input) × 100%  
For ideal (frictionless) machine: η = 100%, MA = Velocity Ratio (VR)

### Six Types of Simple Machines

**1. Lever**

A rigid bar pivoting on a fulcrum. Three classes:

| Class | Fulcrum Position | MA | Examples |
|-------|-----------------|-----|---------|
| **First class** | Between load and effort | Can be >1, =1, or <1 | Seesaw, scissors, crowbar, pliers, claw hammer |
| **Second class** | Load between fulcrum and effort | Always >1 | Wheelbarrow, nutcracker, bottle opener |
| **Third class** | Effort between fulcrum and load | Always <1 (speed advantage) | Tweezers, tongs, fishing rod, forearm when lifting |

**2. Inclined Plane**
A sloped surface. MA = L/h (length of slope / height).  
Reduces force needed to raise a load but increases the distance through which effort acts.  
Examples: ramp for wheelchair, loading dock, road on a hill.

**3. Wedge**
Two inclined planes joined back-to-back. Converts downward force into sideways/outward force.  
Examples: axe, knife, nail, door stopper, zipper.

**4. Screw**
A circular inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder. Each full rotation advances by the pitch (thread spacing).  
Examples: screw, bolt, jar lid, vise, Archimedes screw (water lifting).

**5. Pulley**

Uses a rope over a wheel to change direction or multiply force.

| Type | MA | Examples |
|------|-----|---------|
| **Fixed (single)** | MA = 1, only changes direction | Flag pole |
| **Movable (single)** | MA = 2 | Lifting loads in construction |
| **Block and tackle** | MA = number of rope segments supporting load | Cranes, elevators |

**6. Wheel and Axle**
Large wheel attached to small axle; turning wheel applies force through axle (or vice versa).  
MA = radius of wheel / radius of axle  
Examples: steering wheel, screwdriver, windlass, doorknob.

### Important Machine Principles for SSC

- **No machine can have efficiency > 100%** — this would violate conservation of energy
- **Friction always reduces efficiency** below 100%
- **Compound machines** = combinations of simple machines (bicycle, clock, crane)
- **Effort** = input force; **Load** = output force

## Part 5 — Elasticity and Hooke's Law

**Hooke's Law**: Within the elastic limit, the restoring force in a spring is proportional to the extension/compression:  
**F = −kx**  
(k = spring constant, N/m; x = extension; negative sign = restoring direction)

- **Elastic limit**: maximum stress beyond which material doesn't return to original shape permanently deforms
- **Elastic PE** stored in spring = ½kx²

Two springs in series: 1/k_eff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂ (effective k is less than either)  
Two springs in parallel: k_eff = k₁ + k₂ (effective k is sum)

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Work is zero when force is perpendicular to displacement (θ = 90°)**. A coolie carrying a load on their head walking horizontally does ZERO work on the load (force is vertical, displacement horizontal). However, the coolie does do work against friction on the floor.

2. **Kinetic energy depends on v²** — doubling speed quadruples KE. This is why speed limits matter so much: a car at 100 km/h has 4× the KE of a car at 50 km/h, meaning it needs much more distance to stop.

3. **Work-Energy Theorem**: Net work done = change in KE. If KE doesn't change (constant speed), net work = 0. If speed increases, positive work was done. If speed decreases (brakes applied), negative work was done.

4. **1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J = 3.6 MJ**. This is the unit on your electricity bill — "1 unit" = 1 kilowatt-hour. It is a unit of ENERGY, not power. 1 kW for 1 hour = 1 kWh.

5. **1 horsepower = 746 W** (not 735 W as some older texts state — SI standard is 745.7 W). James Watt defined it from horse experiments; the watt (SI unit of power) is named after him.

6. **A simple machine cannot create energy** — it cannot have efficiency >100%. Machines only redirect or multiply force at the expense of distance (or vice versa). Total work in = work out + work against friction.

7. **Lever classes**: First class has fulcrum BETWEEN load and effort (seesaw, scissors). Second class has load between fulcrum and effort (wheelbarrow, nutcracker). Third class has effort between fulcrum and load (tweezers, human forearm).

8. **The human forearm is a third-class lever** (effort at elbow/bicep, load in hand, fulcrum at elbow joint). This gives mechanical disadvantage (MA < 1) — the muscle must exert more force than the load — but gain in speed and range of movement.

9. **Conservation of mechanical energy** applies only when no non-conservative forces (friction, air resistance) act. A real pendulum eventually stops because friction converts mechanical energy to heat. In ideal (frictionless) scenarios, PE + KE = constant.

10. **Potential energy is always relative** to a chosen reference level. PE = mgh is zero at the ground (h = 0), positive above ground, and can be negative if h < 0 (below reference level). Only changes in PE are physically meaningful.

11. **Elastic collision conserves BOTH kinetic energy and momentum.** Inelastic collision conserves only momentum (KE is lost). Perfect elastic collisions occur between gas molecules and between billiard balls (approximately). Most real collisions are partially inelastic.

12. **Spring constant (k) is larger for stiffer springs.** Two springs in parallel are harder to stretch (k_eff = k₁ + k₂). Two springs in series are easier to stretch (1/k_eff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂). A stiffer spring stores more energy for the same extension (PE = ½kx²).

13. **Negative work is done by friction** on a moving body — friction opposes motion, so the angle between friction force and displacement is 180°. Negative work means energy is removed from the body (converted to heat).

14. **Power = Force × velocity** (P = Fv) for a constant force. A car's engine power determines its maximum speed (when engine power = power lost to friction/drag). More powerful engine → higher maximum speed for same car.

15. **Electron volt (eV) is a unit of energy**, not voltage. 1 eV = energy gained by one electron accelerated through 1 volt of potential difference = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J. Used in nuclear and particle physics.

16. **A moving satellite does zero work** against gravity. Gravity provides centripetal force (perpendicular to velocity in circular orbit), so θ = 90° and work by gravity = 0. This is why satellites don't need continuous thrust to maintain circular orbits.
