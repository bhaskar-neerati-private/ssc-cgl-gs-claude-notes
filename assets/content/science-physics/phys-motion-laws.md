# Motion & Newton's Laws — Equations of Motion, Momentum, Forces & Friction

## Galileo's Heresy and Newton's Universe

In 1589, Galileo Galilei climbed the Leaning Tower of Pisa — or so the legend goes — and dropped two balls of different masses. They hit the ground simultaneously. This shattered Aristotle's 2,000-year-old belief that heavier objects fall faster. Aristotle's physics was common sense; Galileo's was experiment. It cost Galileo his freedom (house arrest by the Inquisition) but launched modern physics.

Eighty-seven years later, a 23-year-old Isaac Newton — hiding from the plague in rural England — watched an apple fall (or so Newton later claimed) and asked why the moon didn't fall the same way. The answer he worked out became the three laws of motion and the law of universal gravitation — the most successful scientific framework ever built, valid from falling apples to spacecraft trajectories.

## Part 2 — Kinematics: Describing Motion

**Kinematics** = description of motion without asking why (no forces).

### Key Definitions

| Term | Definition | Unit | Type |
|------|-----------|------|------|
| **Distance** | Total path length covered | m | Scalar |
| **Displacement** | Shortest path from start to end (with direction) | m | Vector |
| **Speed** | Distance/time | m/s | Scalar |
| **Velocity** | Displacement/time | m/s | Vector |
| **Acceleration** | Change in velocity/time | m/s² | Vector |
| **Retardation** | Negative acceleration (slowing down) | m/s² | Vector |

**Average speed** = total distance / total time  
**Average velocity** = total displacement / total time

A body can have zero velocity but non-zero acceleration (instantaneous rest during deceleration). A body moving in a circle at constant speed has **changing velocity** (direction changes) → it has acceleration.

### Equations of Motion (for uniform acceleration in a straight line)

These three equations connect initial velocity (u), final velocity (v), acceleration (a), displacement (s), and time (t):

| Equation | Form | Relates |
|----------|------|---------|
| **First** | v = u + at | v, u, a, t |
| **Second** | s = ut + ½at² | s, u, a, t |
| **Third** | v² = u² + 2as | v, u, a, s |

**Free fall** — special case where a = g = 9.8 m/s² (acceleration due to gravity):
- u = 0 (dropped from rest)
- v = gt (velocity at time t)
- h = ½gt² (height fallen in time t)
- v² = 2gh (velocity after falling height h)

### Projectile Motion

A body launched at an angle θ to the horizontal; no air resistance:
- **Horizontal**: uniform velocity (no force horizontally) → x = u·cosθ·t
- **Vertical**: uniform acceleration downward (g) → y = u·sinθ·t − ½gt²

| Property | Formula |
|----------|---------|
| **Time of flight** | T = 2u·sinθ / g |
| **Maximum height** | H = u²·sin²θ / 2g |
| **Horizontal range** | R = u²·sin2θ / g |
| **Maximum range** | At θ = 45° → R_max = u²/g |

**Complementary angles** give the same range: θ and (90° − θ). A ball thrown at 30° travels the same range as one thrown at 60° (if speed is the same).

## Part 3 — Newton's Laws of Motion

### First Law — Law of Inertia

**"Every body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external unbalanced force."**

- **Inertia** = resistance to change in state of motion
- Inertia depends on **mass** — heavier objects are harder to start/stop/turn
- No force needed to maintain uniform motion; force is needed to *change* motion

**Examples of First Law:**
- Passenger thrown forward when brakes are suddenly applied (inertia of motion)
- Passenger pushed back when car accelerates suddenly (inertia of rest)
- Dust flies off a blanket when shaken (blanket moves, dust's inertia keeps it in place)
- A ball rolled on ice travels far (little friction, so little external force, little change in motion)

**Galileo's contribution**: It was Galileo who first articulated the concept of inertia; Newton formalized it into the first law.

### Second Law — Law of Force and Acceleration

**"The rate of change of momentum of a body is directly proportional to the applied force and takes place in the direction of the force."**

F = ma (Force = mass × acceleration)

- 1 Newton = force that gives 1 kg an acceleration of 1 m/s²
- **Momentum (p)** = mass × velocity = mv (vector, SI unit: kg·m/s)
- F = Δp/Δt = rate of change of momentum

**Impulse** = Force × time = Δp (change in momentum)

**Examples of Second Law:**
- It is harder to stop a heavy truck than a bicycle at the same speed (greater momentum)
- Cricket fielder pulls hands back while catching — extends time, reduces force (F = Δp/Δt; increase t → decrease F)
- Crumple zones in cars extend collision time, reducing force on occupants

### Third Law — Law of Action and Reaction

**"For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction."**

Forces always come in pairs — if A exerts force on B, then B simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on A. These forces act on DIFFERENT bodies.

**Examples of Third Law:**
- Rocket propulsion: gases expelled backward (action) → rocket pushed forward (reaction)
- Walking: foot pushes backward on ground (action) → ground pushes forward on foot (reaction)
- Boat moves backward when you jump forward from it
- Gun recoil: bullet pushed forward → gun pushed backward
- Bird flies: wings push air downward → air pushes bird upward

**Why don't action and reaction cancel?** They act on different bodies. The action force acts on one body; the reaction acts on the other.

## Part 4 — Types of Forces

| Force | Nature | Examples |
|-------|--------|---------|
| **Gravitational** | Attractive, always | Earth-Moon, Earth-apple |
| **Normal force (N)** | Perpendicular to surface | Table pushing book up |
| **Tension** | Along rope/string | Weight on a string |
| **Friction** | Opposing relative motion | Brakes, grip of shoes |
| **Applied force** | External push/pull | Pushing a box |
| **Spring force** | Restoring (Hooke's Law: F = −kx) | Spring returning to original length |
| **Buoyant force** | Upward in fluid | Wood floating |
| **Centripetal force** | Toward centre of circular path | Planetary orbit, car on curve |

### Friction

**Friction** = force that opposes relative motion between two surfaces in contact.

| Type | When Occurs | Magnitude |
|------|------------|-----------|
| **Static friction** | Before sliding begins; prevents motion | Up to maximum static friction (μₛN) |
| **Kinetic (sliding) friction** | When surfaces slide against each other | μₖN (< max static friction) |
| **Rolling friction** | When one body rolls on another | Much less than sliding friction |

**Coefficient of friction (μ)** = Friction force / Normal force

- **μ** depends on the nature of surfaces (rough/smooth), NOT on the area of contact or weight
- Kinetic friction < Static friction: that's why it's easier to keep something moving once it's started than to start it
- Rolling friction << Sliding friction: that's why wheels were invented

**Increasing friction**: grooves in tyres, ridges on soles, sand on ice  
**Decreasing friction**: lubricants (oil, grease), ball bearings, smooth surfaces, streamlining

**Angle of friction (λ)**: angle between resultant of normal force and friction force and the normal = arctan(μ)

## Part 5 — Circular Motion

When a body moves in a circle at constant speed, its direction changes continuously → velocity changes → there is acceleration.

**Centripetal acceleration** = v²/r (directed toward centre)  
**Centripetal force** = mv²/r (directed toward centre, provided by some agent)

| Situation | What Provides Centripetal Force |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Planet orbiting Sun | Gravitational force |
| Car taking a curve | Friction between tyres and road |
| Ball on a string swung in circle | Tension in the string |
| Electron orbiting nucleus (classical) | Electrostatic force |
| Banking of roads (without friction) | Component of normal force |

**There is NO centrifugal force** in an inertial frame. "Centrifugal force" (the outward push you feel) is a pseudo-force in a rotating (non-inertial) reference frame.

### Banking of Roads
Roads at curves are banked (outer edge raised) so vehicles can take the curve without relying on friction:  
tan(θ) = v²/(rg), where θ = banking angle, v = safe speed, r = radius of curve, g = gravity.

## Part 6 — Conservation of Linear Momentum

**"In the absence of an external force, the total linear momentum of a system remains constant."**

This is a consequence of Newton's Third Law applied to a system of bodies.

**p_total = constant** (when no external force)

**Applications:**
- Recoil of a gun (total initial momentum = 0; bullet goes forward, gun goes backward; momenta are equal and opposite)
- Rocket propulsion (initially at rest; hot gases ejected backward, rocket moves forward)
- Explosion: fragments fly in different directions with momenta that add to zero
- Collision analysis

### Types of Collisions

| Type | Kinetic Energy | Momentum | Example |
|------|---------------|---------|---------|
| **Elastic** | Conserved | Conserved | Billiard balls, gas molecule collisions |
| **Inelastic** | NOT conserved (some converted to heat/sound) | Conserved | Car crash |
| **Perfectly inelastic** | Maximum KE lost | Conserved | Clay balls sticking together |

In perfectly inelastic collision: both bodies stick and move together with common final velocity.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Newton's First Law defines inertia** — it says that objects resist change in their state of motion. Inertia is NOT a force; it is a property of matter (its mass). The greater the mass, the greater the inertia.

2. **Newton's Second Law: F = ma** means force is needed to change velocity (acceleration), NOT to maintain constant velocity. A car at constant speed on a straight level road has zero net force (engine force balances friction exactly).

3. **Newton's Third Law forces act on DIFFERENT bodies** — they never cancel each other because they don't act on the same body. A horse and cart example: the cart pulls the horse backward (reaction) with the same force the horse pulls the cart forward (action).

4. **Momentum is a vector** (p = mv). If two identical balls move in opposite directions at the same speed, their total momentum is ZERO (not 2mv). Direction matters.

5. **Impulse = change in momentum** (J = Δp = FΔt). A fielder pulling hands back while catching extends time (Δt) → reduces force (F). This is why it hurts more to fall on concrete than on a mattress.

6. **In a perfectly inelastic collision, kinetic energy is NOT conserved** but momentum IS. The two bodies stick together. This is the maximum possible loss of kinetic energy in any collision.

7. **A ball thrown at 45° has maximum horizontal range** under gravity (for a given initial speed). Complementary angles (e.g., 30° and 60°) give the same range.

8. **Centripetal force always points toward the centre** of the circle. It is NOT a separate force — it is provided by friction, gravity, tension, etc. depending on the situation. The "outward centrifugal force" is a fictitious force in the rotating frame.

9. **Static friction is GREATER than kinetic friction** for the same pair of surfaces. Once motion starts, friction drops. This is why your car's antilock braking system (ABS) prevents wheels from locking — to keep static (rolling) friction rather than kinetic friction.

10. **Friction does NOT depend on the area of contact** between surfaces — it depends only on the normal force (weight component perpendicular to surface) and μ. A wide tyre and narrow tyre of the same material on the same surface have the same friction force for the same load.

11. **Rolling friction << Sliding friction** — that's the fundamental reason wheels are useful. Ball bearings in machinery replace sliding contact with rolling contact to reduce friction dramatically.

12. **Rockets work on Newton's Third Law** — high-speed exhaust gases ejected backward (action) produce a forward thrust on the rocket (reaction). Rockets work in vacuum (space) because they carry their own oxidiser, not because they "push against" anything.

13. **The equations of motion (v=u+at, etc.) apply only to UNIFORM acceleration** — constant acceleration in a straight line. They do NOT apply to circular motion or motion with varying acceleration.

14. **Distance can only be zero or positive; displacement can be negative** (opposite to chosen positive direction). Velocity can be negative (moving in negative direction); speed is always positive.

15. **Galileo's contribution** to Newton's Laws: Galileo first proposed the concept of inertia and disproved Aristotle's idea that heavier objects fall faster. Newton built on Galileo's experimental foundation to formulate the laws mathematically.

16. **Recoil velocity of a gun**: if M = gun mass, m = bullet mass, v = bullet velocity, then gun recoil velocity V = mv/M (in opposite direction). A heavier gun has smaller recoil (conservation of momentum: MV = mv, so V = mv/M).
