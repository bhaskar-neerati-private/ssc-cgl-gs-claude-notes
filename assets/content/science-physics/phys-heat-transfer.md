# Heat Transfer & Calorimetry — Temperature Scales, Specific Heat, Latent Heat & Modes of Transfer

## The Celsius-Fahrenheit War and the Ice Point That Settled It

Anders Celsius, a Swedish astronomer, proposed his temperature scale in 1742 — but with 0° at boiling and 100° at freezing (reversed from what we use today). His colleague Carl Linnaeus flipped it. Meanwhile, across Europe, Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit was using a scale he'd devised around 1724, setting 0° as the coldest temperature he could achieve with a saltwater-ice mixture and 96° as human body temperature (later revised to 98.6°F). Two brilliant men. Two incompatible scales. Three centuries of international argument.

The solution came from a different direction entirely: William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) in 1848 proposed an absolute scale anchored to absolute zero — the coldest possible temperature, where molecular motion stops. Today the Kelvin scale underlies all scientific temperature measurement. But Celsius and Fahrenheit live on in everyday life, and SSC tests conversions between all three relentlessly.

## Part 1 — Temperature Scales and Conversions

**Temperature** = measure of the average kinetic energy of molecules in a substance.  
**Heat** = total thermal energy transferred from a hotter body to a cooler body.

| Scale | Freezing Point of Water | Boiling Point of Water | Absolute Zero |
|-------|------------------------|----------------------|---------------|
| **Celsius (°C)** | 0°C | 100°C | −273.15°C |
| **Fahrenheit (°F)** | 32°F | 212°F | −459.67°F |
| **Kelvin (K)** | 273.15 K | 373.15 K | 0 K |
| **Rankine (°R)** | 491.67°R | 671.67°R | 0°R |

### Conversion Formulas

| From | To | Formula |
|------|-----|---------|
| Celsius → Fahrenheit | °F | °F = (9/5 × °C) + 32 |
| Fahrenheit → Celsius | °C | °C = (°F − 32) × 5/9 |
| Celsius → Kelvin | K | K = °C + 273.15 |
| Kelvin → Celsius | °C | °C = K − 273.15 |

**Key values to memorise:**
- 0°C = 32°F = 273 K
- 100°C = 212°F = 373 K
- −40°C = −40°F (the only point where both scales coincide!)
- Body temperature: 37°C = 98.6°F = 310 K
- Absolute zero: −273.15°C = −459.67°F = 0 K

**Absolute zero (0 K)** = temperature at which molecular motion is minimum (not entirely zero — zero-point quantum energy remains). No substance can reach absolute zero (Third Law of Thermodynamics).

## Part 2 — Thermal Expansion

When heated, most substances expand. The amount of expansion depends on the material and the temperature change.

### Three Types of Thermal Expansion

| Type | Definition | Formula | Coefficient |
|------|-----------|---------|------------|
| **Linear** | Expansion in length | ΔL = αLΔT | α (coefficient of linear expansion) |
| **Area/Superficial** | Expansion in area | ΔA = βAΔT | β = 2α |
| **Volume/Cubical** | Expansion in volume | ΔV = γVΔT | γ = 3α |

**Relation**: γ = 3α = 3β/2 (for isotropic materials)

### Applications of Thermal Expansion
- **Railways**: gaps left between rail sections to allow expansion; now welded rails use controlled pre-tension
- **Bridges**: expansion joints allow expansion without buckling
- **Bimetallic strip** (thermostat): two metals with different α bonded together. When heated, one expands more → strip bends → can break/make electrical contact → temperature control
- **Thermometer**: liquid mercury or alcohol expands with temperature
- **Loose jar lid**: briefly running hot water over it expands the lid more than the glass jar (metals have higher α than glass) → lid loosens

### Anomalous Expansion of Water
Water behaves unusually between 0°C and 4°C: it **contracts as it warms** from 0°C to 4°C (density increases). Above 4°C, water expands normally with heating.

**Water is densest at 4°C** (density = 1,000 kg/m³ exactly).

**Consequences:**
- Ice floats on water (ice at 0°C is less dense than water at 0°C)
- Lakes freeze from the surface downward, not from the bottom up — allowing aquatic life to survive winters
- Water pipes burst in winter — freezing water expands and cracks the pipe

## Part 3 — Specific Heat Capacity

**Specific heat capacity (c)** = heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C).

**Q = mcΔT** (Heat = mass × specific heat × temperature change)

**SI unit**: J/(kg·K) or J/(kg·°C)

| Substance | Specific Heat (J/kg·K) |
|----------|----------------------|
| **Water** | **4,186** (highest of common liquids) |
| Ice | 2,090 |
| Seawater | 3,900 |
| **Aluminium** | **900** |
| Iron | 450 |
| Copper | 385 |
| **Lead** | **128** (very low) |
| Air | ~1,005 |
| Hydrogen gas | 14,300 (highest of gases) |

**Water has the highest specific heat of all common substances** — it takes a lot of heat to raise its temperature (and it releases a lot of heat as it cools). This makes water an ideal:
- Coolant in car radiators, nuclear reactors, power plants
- Climate moderator — coastal areas have milder temperatures than inland areas (the sea absorbs and releases heat slowly)
- Storage medium in solar heaters

## Part 4 — Latent Heat

**Latent heat** = heat absorbed or released during a phase change (change of state) at constant temperature.

**Q = mL** (Heat = mass × latent heat)

| Phase Change | Heat | Name |
|-------------|------|------|
| Solid → Liquid (melting) | Absorbed | Latent heat of fusion |
| Liquid → Solid (freezing) | Released | Latent heat of fusion |
| Liquid → Gas (boiling/vaporisation) | Absorbed | Latent heat of vaporisation |
| Gas → Liquid (condensation) | Released | Latent heat of vaporisation |
| Solid → Gas (sublimation) | Absorbed | Latent heat of sublimation |

### Latent Heat Values for Water

| Process | Latent Heat |
|---------|------------|
| Fusion (melting/freezing at 0°C) | 334,000 J/kg = 334 kJ/kg = 80 cal/g |
| Vaporisation (boiling/condensing at 100°C) | 2,260,000 J/kg = 2,260 kJ/kg = 540 cal/g |

**Latent heat of vaporisation >> Latent heat of fusion:**  
Boiling requires ~7 times more heat than melting (for the same mass of water).

**Why steam burns more than boiling water at 100°C:**  
Steam at 100°C must release its latent heat of vaporisation (2,260 kJ/kg) before it can even start cooling. So it delivers far more heat energy to skin than equal mass of 100°C water.

### Applications of Latent Heat
- **Sweating**: evaporation of sweat absorbs latent heat from body → cooling effect (evaporative cooling)
- **Ice in drinks**: ice absorbs latent heat of fusion from the drink → cools it effectively (cools even before melting)
- **Refrigerators/AC**: refrigerant evaporates inside (absorbs heat = cooling) and condenses outside (releases heat)
- **Dry ice** (solid CO₂): sublimes directly at −78.5°C; used for cooling without liquid mess

## Part 5 — Modes of Heat Transfer

### 1. Conduction

Heat transfer through a solid by direct molecular contact (no bulk movement of material).

**Fourier's Law**: Q/t = kA(ΔT/Δx)  
(Rate of heat flow = thermal conductivity × area × temperature gradient)

**Thermal conductivity (k)**: (W/m·K)

| Material | k (W/m·K) | Conductor/Insulator |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Silver | 429 | Best metallic conductor |
| Copper | 401 | |
| Aluminium | 237 | |
| Iron | 80 | |
| Glass | 1.0 | |
| Water | 0.6 | |
| Wood | 0.1–0.3 | |
| Air | 0.025 | Excellent insulator |
| Wool/fur | ~0.04 | Excellent insulator (trapped air) |

**Metals conduct heat well** (free electrons transfer energy). **Non-metals** (wood, glass, rubber) are poor conductors (insulators). **Air is an excellent insulator** — double-glazed windows, thermos flasks, and woollen clothing all trap air to minimise conduction.

**Why cooking pots have metal bases but plastic/wooden handles:** metal = good conductor (transfers heat quickly to food); plastic/wood = insulator (protects hands).

### 2. Convection

Heat transfer through **bulk movement of fluid** (liquid or gas). Hotter fluid rises (less dense), cooler fluid sinks — setting up convection currents.

**Natural (free) convection**: driven by density differences (sea breeze, monsoon winds, room heating)  
**Forced convection**: fluid is moved by fans/pumps (car radiator fan, HVAC, blood circulation)

**Applications:**
- **Sea breeze/land breeze**: during day, land heats faster than sea → air rises over land → sea breeze blows landward. At night, reverse.
- **Monsoon**: large-scale convection driven by differential heating of land and ocean
- **Ventilation**: hot air rises and escapes from ceiling vents; cool air enters below
- **Oceanic circulation (thermohaline)**: drives global climate by convection of warm/cold water

### 3. Radiation

Heat transfer through **electromagnetic waves** (infrared radiation), requiring NO medium (works in vacuum).

All bodies above absolute zero emit radiation. **Stefan-Boltzmann Law**:  
**P = εσAT⁴** (Power radiated = emissivity × Stefan constant × area × T⁴)

**Stefan-Boltzmann constant (σ)** = 5.67 × 10⁻⁸ W/m²·K⁴

**Emissivity (ε)**: 0 to 1. A **perfect blackbody** has ε = 1 (absorbs/emits all radiation). A perfect reflector has ε = 0.

| Surface | Emission/Absorption |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Black/dark, rough** | Good emitter and absorber |
| **White/shiny, smooth** | Poor emitter, poor absorber (good reflector) |

**Good absorbers are also good emitters** (Kirchhoff's Law of Radiation)

**Applications:**
- **Solar cooker**: dark surface absorbs radiation efficiently; glass cover prevents convection loss
- **Thermos flask (vacuum flask)**: silvered walls → reduce radiation; vacuum → eliminate conduction and convection; double glass walls
- **White clothes in summer**: reflect radiation → stay cooler
- **Dark clothes in winter**: absorb radiation → stay warmer
- **Greenhouse effect**: glass/CO₂ allows short-wave solar radiation in but traps long-wave infrared emitted by Earth → warming

## Part 6 — Newton's Law of Cooling

**"The rate of cooling of a body is proportional to the temperature difference between the body and its surroundings"** (for small temperature differences).

dT/dt ∝ (T − T_surroundings)

This approximates Newton's Law of Cooling and explains why a hot cup of tea cools quickly at first, then more slowly as it approaches room temperature.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **−40°C = −40°F** — the ONLY temperature at which Celsius and Fahrenheit scales give the same reading. Useful to know for trick questions. Body temperature: 37°C = 98.6°F.

2. **Water is densest at 4°C** — not at 0°C. Between 0°C and 4°C, water contracts as it warms (anomalous expansion). Below 0°C, it expands as it freezes into ice. Ice is less dense than water at 0°C.

3. **Latent heat of vaporisation > Latent heat of fusion** (for water: 2,260 kJ/kg vs 334 kJ/kg). Steam burns more severely than boiling water at the same temperature because steam releases its latent heat of vaporisation upon condensing.

4. **Sweating cools the body** through evaporative cooling — sweat absorbs latent heat of vaporisation from the skin surface. Dry desert heat feels cooler than humid coastal heat because sweat evaporates faster in dry air.

5. **Heat flows from higher temperature to lower temperature** — always. This is the Second Law of Thermodynamics. Heat never spontaneously flows from cold to hot without external work (as in a refrigerator, which requires electricity).

6. **Conduction requires direct contact** (or very close proximity); **convection requires a fluid** (liquid or gas); **radiation requires neither medium nor contact** (works in vacuum). The Sun heats Earth by radiation through the vacuum of space.

7. **Air is an excellent thermal insulator** (k ≈ 0.025 W/m·K). Woollen clothing, double-glazed windows, thermos flasks, and foam insulation work by trapping pockets of still air. The wool/foam itself is a poor conductor too, but trapped air does most of the insulation work.

8. **Thermos flask (vacuum flask)** uses all three mechanisms: silvered walls reduce radiation; vacuum between walls eliminates conduction and convection. Invented by James Dewar (1892), first commercially sold as "Thermos" (1904).

9. **Bimetallic strip** curves toward the metal with lower coefficient of thermal expansion when heated. Used in thermostats, circuit breakers, fire alarms. When temperature rises, the strip bends and either breaks or makes contact.

10. **Specific heat of water (4,186 J/kg·K) is very high** — water resists temperature change. This moderates coastal climates (sea stays cool in summer, warm in winter) and is why car radiators use water/coolant. Metals have low specific heat — they heat and cool quickly.

11. **Good absorbers are good emitters** (Kirchhoff's Law). A black surface absorbs and emits radiation best. A shiny white surface reflects radiation and emits poorly. This is why solar collectors are painted black and white clothes are worn in summer.

12. **Radiation is T⁴ dependent** (Stefan-Boltzmann Law). Doubling absolute temperature increases radiation by 2⁴ = 16 times. The Sun (surface T ≈ 5,778 K) radiates about 6.3 × 10⁷ W/m². This extreme dependence on T explains why very hot objects glow brightly.

13. **Kelvin scale is absolute** — all thermodynamic calculations use Kelvin. Gas laws (PV = nRT) always use K, never °C or °F. You cannot have negative Kelvin temperature (absolute zero is the minimum).

14. **Latent heat of ice (334 kJ/kg)**: when 1 kg of ice melts, it absorbs 334 kJ without changing temperature. This is why ice is more effective for cooling drinks than the same mass of cold water — the phase change absorbs far more heat.

15. **Land heats and cools faster than water** (lower specific heat of soil/rock vs water). During the day, land becomes hotter → sea breeze blows from sea to land. At night, land cools faster → land breeze blows from land to sea. The sea breeze cools coastal areas in summer.

16. **Phase changes occur at constant temperature** — temperature doesn't rise while ice is melting or water is boiling (all added heat goes into breaking intermolecular bonds, not raising temperature). On a heating curve, horizontal sections (plateaus) indicate phase transitions.
