# Waves & Sound — Frequency, Speed, Doppler Effect, Echo, Ultrasound & Resonance

## The Bridge That Danced Itself to Death

On 7 November 1940, the Tacoma Narrows Bridge in Washington state began to oscillate wildly in a 67 km/h wind — not because the wind was unusually strong, but because the wind hit the bridge at the bridge's own natural frequency. Like a child on a swing pumped at exactly the right moment, the bridge resonated. Within four hours, it tore itself apart and collapsed into Puget Sound. Engineers had built this beautiful, slender structure — only to discover that resonance could destroy it.

Sound is vibration. Waves are energy moving without matter moving. These simple truths explain everything from why bats navigate in total darkness to why certain notes shatter glass, why submarines use sonar to detect enemies 500 km away, and why a doctor's ultrasound machine can "see" inside your body without a single incision.

## Part 1 — What is a Wave?

A **wave** is a disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transferring matter.

### Types of Waves

| Basis | Types | Examples |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **By medium requirement** | Mechanical (need medium) | Sound, water waves, seismic waves |
| | Electromagnetic (no medium needed) | Light, radio, X-rays, UV, IR |
| **By vibration direction** | Transverse (vibration ⊥ to propagation) | Light, water waves, EM waves |
| | Longitudinal (vibration ∥ to propagation) | Sound waves, compression waves |

**Sound is a mechanical, longitudinal wave** — it requires a medium (cannot travel in vacuum) and vibrates in the direction of propagation through compressions and rarefactions.

### Wave Parameters

| Parameter | Symbol | Definition | SI Unit |
|-----------|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Amplitude** | A | Maximum displacement from equilibrium | m |
| **Frequency** | f or ν | Oscillations per second | Hz (hertz) |
| **Time Period** | T | Time for one complete oscillation | s |
| **Wavelength** | λ | Distance between two successive crests/troughs | m |
| **Wave Speed** | v | Distance covered per unit time | m/s |

**Fundamental relation**: **v = fλ** (wave speed = frequency × wavelength)

**T = 1/f** (period is reciprocal of frequency)

## Part 2 — Sound: Properties and Speed

### Nature of Sound
Sound travels as longitudinal waves — alternating **compressions** (high pressure regions) and **rarefactions** (low pressure regions).

**Sound requires a medium** — it CANNOT travel in vacuum (hence, "in space, no one can hear you scream" — a scientifically accurate movie tagline).

### Speed of Sound

Speed of sound depends on the medium:

| Medium | Speed at ~20°C |
|--------|---------------|
| **Air (at 0°C)** | **332 m/s** |
| Air (at 20°C) | ~343 m/s |
| Water | ~1,500 m/s |
| Sea water | ~1,533 m/s |
| Iron/Steel | ~5,000–5,960 m/s |
| Glass | ~5,000–6,000 m/s |

**Key facts about speed of sound:**
- **v_sound in solid > v_sound in liquid > v_sound in gas** (solids are more elastic)
- Speed in air **increases with temperature**: increases ~0.6 m/s per °C rise
- Speed is **independent of frequency** and amplitude
- Speed **does NOT change** when frequency changes (v = fλ, so if f increases, λ decreases proportionally)
- Speed in air **does not depend on pressure** at constant temperature (ideal gas)

### Frequency Ranges of Sound

| Range | Frequency | Examples |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Infrasound** | < 20 Hz | Earthquakes, elephants, whales, volcanic activity |
| **Audible range** | 20 Hz – 20,000 Hz (20 kHz) | Human hearing |
| **Ultrasound** | > 20,000 Hz (> 20 kHz) | Bats, dolphins, dogs; medical imaging |

## Part 3 — Echo and Reverberation

**Echo**: a reflected sound heard distinctly after the original sound.

**Conditions for echo:**
1. Reflecting surface must be large and rigid
2. Minimum distance between source and reflector: **17 metres** (at ~340 m/s, sound travels 34 m in 0.1 s — the minimum time for persistence of sound in ear; 34/2 = 17 m one way)

**Reverberation**: multiple reflections causing prolonged persistence of sound. Undesirable in auditoriums (causes jumbled sound); controlled using sound-absorbing materials (curtains, carpets, acoustic tiles, perforated ceilings).

**SONAR** (Sound Navigation And Ranging): uses echo of ultrasonic pulses to detect underwater objects, depth of ocean, submarine detection.

**Formula for depth (d)**: d = v × t / 2 (v = speed of sound in water, t = time for echo to return; divide by 2 because sound travels to object and back)

## Part 4 — Doppler Effect

**Doppler Effect**: the apparent change in frequency (pitch) of a wave when the source and observer are in relative motion.

- **Source approaching observer**: apparent frequency **increases** (higher pitch)
- **Source moving away from observer**: apparent frequency **decreases** (lower pitch)
- **Classic example**: ambulance siren — higher pitch as it approaches, lower as it passes

**Applications of Doppler Effect:**
- **Radar speed guns**: police use reflected radio waves; Doppler shift gives vehicle speed
- **Weather radar (Doppler radar)**: tracks storm movement and rainfall
- **Astronomy**: redshift (galaxies moving away) and blueshift (approaching stars) — Edwin Hubble used this to discover the Universe is expanding
- **Medical Doppler ultrasound**: measures blood flow speed in vessels
- **Fighter jets**: sonic boom occurs when aircraft speed exceeds sound speed (v > 332 m/s in air at 0°C)

## Part 5 — Characteristics of Sound (Musical Sound vs Noise)

Three characteristics distinguish sounds:

| Characteristic | Depends On | Example |
|---------------|-----------|---------|
| **Loudness (volume)** | Amplitude | Turning up speakers → more amplitude → louder |
| **Pitch** | Frequency | High-pitched = high frequency (violin); low-pitched = low frequency (bass) |
| **Quality (timbre/tone)** | Waveform/harmonics | Why same note sounds different on piano vs violin |

**Decibel (dB)**: unit of sound intensity.

| Sound | Decibels |
|-------|---------|
| Threshold of hearing | 0 dB |
| Whisper | ~30 dB |
| Normal conversation | ~60 dB |
| Traffic | ~80 dB |
| Rock concert | ~110 dB |
| Jet engine (threshold of pain) | ~120–130 dB |

## Part 6 — Resonance

**Resonance**: when an object vibrates at the same frequency as an external driving force, energy transfer is maximised — amplitude grows rapidly.

**Natural frequency**: every object has a frequency at which it naturally vibrates (e.g., a tuning fork, a guitar string).

**Resonance occurs** when driving frequency = natural frequency of the object.

**Examples:**
- **Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse (1940)**: wind drove the bridge at its natural frequency → catastrophic resonance
- **Glass shattering by sound**: opera singer hitting the natural frequency of a crystal glass
- **MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)**: nuclear resonance — atomic nuclei absorb radio waves at specific frequencies
- **Microwave oven**: microwaves at 2.45 GHz resonate with water molecules → heats food
- **Pendulum synchronisation** (Huygens's pendulum clocks on same wall)
- **Radio tuning**: tuning a radio adjusts the resonant frequency of its circuit to match the broadcast frequency

## Part 7 — Ultrasound Applications

**Ultrasound**: sound waves with frequency > 20,000 Hz (20 kHz).

| Application | How It Works |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Medical imaging (USG/sonography)** | High-frequency sound reflects from different tissues; computer constructs image |
| **Prenatal scanning** | Images foetus without radiation risk (unlike X-rays) |
| **SONAR** (submarine detection) | Echoes from underwater objects; navy uses for enemy submarine detection |
| **Ultrasonic cleaning** | High-frequency vibration dislodges dirt from delicate instruments, jewellery |
| **Kidney stone treatment (lithotripsy)** | Focused ultrasound waves break kidney stones without surgery |
| **Welding (ultrasonic welding)** | Used in plastics and electronic assembly |
| **Bat echolocation** | Bats emit ultrasonic pulses (20–200 kHz) and use echoes to navigate and hunt in darkness |
| **Dolphin/whale communication** | Dolphins use echolocation (clicks up to 120 kHz) |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Sound is a mechanical longitudinal wave** — it requires a medium to travel. It CANNOT travel in vacuum. This is why there is no sound in space. Light can travel in vacuum; sound cannot.

2. **Speed of sound in air at 0°C = 332 m/s** (approximately 340 m/s at room temperature). Speed is greater in solids than liquids than gases (v_solid > v_liquid > v_gas). Sound travels fastest in steel (~5,960 m/s).

3. **Minimum distance for echo = 17 metres** (at 340 m/s). The human ear cannot distinguish two sounds less than 0.1 seconds apart — so sound must travel at least 34 m (17 m there + 17 m back) to be heard as a distinct echo.

4. **Infrasound**: frequency < 20 Hz (below human hearing range). Produced by earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, and some animals (elephants, whales). Can travel very long distances and penetrate walls.

5. **Ultrasound**: frequency > 20,000 Hz. Used in medical imaging, SONAR, lithotripsy, and bat echolocation. Bats navigate using ultrasonic echolocation (not vision).

6. **Doppler Effect**: when source moves toward observer, frequency (pitch) appears higher. When source moves away, frequency appears lower. This is why an ambulance siren sounds higher-pitched approaching and lower-pitched receding.

7. **Sonic boom**: when an aircraft travels faster than sound (v > v_sound), it creates a shock wave — a cone of compressed air that creates a loud boom when it passes. Concorde and military jets break the sound barrier.

8. **v = fλ**: wave speed = frequency × wavelength. If frequency increases (shorter wavelength), speed does not change — only wavelength shrinks. Speed of sound in a medium is fixed at a given temperature.

9. **Reverberation** is the persistence of sound due to multiple reflections in an enclosed space. Too much reverberation in a concert hall makes speech unintelligible. Acoustic panels absorb excess reflections.

10. **Loudness depends on amplitude**; **pitch depends on frequency**; **quality (timbre) depends on waveform**. These three independently describe any musical sound.

11. **SONAR formula**: depth = (v × t)/2. If ultrasonic pulse returns in 0.5 seconds and speed in water is 1500 m/s, depth = (1500 × 0.5)/2 = 375 m.

12. **Speed of sound increases with temperature** — approximately 0.6 m/s per °C. So sound is faster on a hot day than a cold day. But speed is independent of pressure (at constant temperature) — this is why speed at sea level and mountain top are roughly equal at the same temperature.

13. **Resonance** occurs when driving frequency matches natural frequency of object. Tacoma Narrows bridge, marching soldiers ordered to break step on bridges, and glass-shattering opera singers are all resonance examples SSC has asked about.

14. **Ultrasound is safe for medical imaging** (no ionising radiation) — that's why it's used for prenatal scanning. X-rays carry ionising radiation risk and are not used for foetal imaging.

15. **Musical interval**: frequency ratio 2:1 = one octave. A note at 440 Hz (A4) and 880 Hz (A5) differ by one octave. Higher frequency = higher pitch.

16. **Audible frequency range of humans = 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz**. This range decreases with age (older adults can't hear high frequencies well). Dogs can hear up to ~65,000 Hz; bats up to ~200,000 Hz.
