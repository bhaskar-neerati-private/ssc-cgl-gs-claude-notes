# Light Phenomena — Dispersion, Spectrum, Rainbow, Scattering, Laser & EM Spectrum

## Newton's Prism and the Colour of Everything

In 1666, Cambridge University was closed due to the plague, and a 23-year-old Isaac Newton was sent home to the village of Woolsthorpe. Bored and brilliant, he drilled a small hole in his window shutter, let in a single beam of sunlight, and placed a glass prism in its path. What appeared on the opposite wall astonished him: a strip of colour — violet, indigo, blue, green, yellow, orange, red (VIBGYOR) — stretched across the room.

The common belief at the time was that prisms "added" colour to white light. Newton disagreed. He placed a second prism in the path of the spectrum: the seven colours recombined into white light. He had proved that white light is not pure but a mixture of all colours — and that prisms don't add colour; they separate what is already there. This discovery became the foundation of spectroscopy, which today tells us what distant stars are made of without anyone ever going there.

## Part 1 — Dispersion of Light

**Dispersion**: the separation of white light into its component colours when it passes through a prism (or any refracting medium).

**Cause**: different colours (wavelengths) of light travel at different speeds in a medium — so they have different refractive indices. Violet light slows the most (highest refractive index), red light slows the least (lowest refractive index).

**VIBGYOR**: the order of colours in the visible spectrum:

| Colour | Wavelength (approx.) | Refractive Index in Glass | Position |
|--------|---------------------|--------------------------|----------|
| **Violet** | ~380–450 nm | Highest (≈1.532) | Most deviated |
| **Indigo** | ~445–455 nm | | |
| **Blue** | ~455–492 nm | | |
| **Green** | ~492–577 nm | | |
| **Yellow** | ~577–597 nm | | |
| **Orange** | ~597–622 nm | | |
| **Red** | ~622–780 nm | Lowest (≈1.513) | Least deviated |

**Memory trick for order**: **V**ery **I**mportant **B**ooks **G**ive **Y**our **O**ranges **R**ich taste (VIBGYOR) — or just ROY G BIV in reverse.

**Key distinction**: violet light has the shortest wavelength (most energetic visible light) and is deviated/refracted the most. Red light has the longest wavelength and is deviated the least.

## Part 2 — Rainbow

A rainbow is nature's most spectacular demonstration of dispersion.

**Formation of a primary rainbow:**
1. Sunlight enters a spherical raindrop
2. Refraction at entry bends different colours by different amounts (dispersion)
3. Total Internal Reflection occurs at the back of the drop
4. Refraction again at exit further separates the colours
5. **Red** appears on the **outside (top)** of the rainbow (least deviated, longest wavelength)
6. **Violet** appears on the **inside (bottom)** of the primary rainbow (most deviated)

**Conditions**: observer must have the sun behind them, rain in front; rainbow is always seen as a semicircle with the observer at the centre.

**Secondary rainbow**: formed by two internal reflections inside the drop:
- Less bright than primary rainbow
- Colours are **reversed** (violet outside, red inside)
- Appears above the primary rainbow

**Alexander's Dark Band**: dark region between primary and secondary rainbow (no light directed into this region).

## Part 3 — Scattering of Light

**Scattering**: when light hits very small particles (air molecules, dust, colloidal particles), it scatters in all directions.

### Rayleigh Scattering (by very small particles smaller than wavelength of light)

**Rayleigh's Law**: Scattering intensity ∝ 1/λ⁴

This means shorter wavelengths scatter **much more** than longer wavelengths.

| Phenomenon | Explanation |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Sky appears blue** | Air molecules (N₂, O₂) scatter blue light (~450 nm) much more than red (preferential Rayleigh scattering). We see scattered blue from all directions overhead → blue sky |
| **Sunset/sunrise appears red/orange** | Near the horizon, sunlight travels through more atmosphere. Most blue light is scattered away; only the least-scattered red/orange light reaches our eyes → red/orange sunset |
| **Sun appears white at noon** | Sunlight travels through minimum atmosphere; relatively little scattering — most colours reach us, and white sunlight is seen |
| **Danger signals are red** | Red light scatters the least → penetrates fog and dust best → seen from greatest distance. Hence traffic signals, brake lights, fire engines use red |

### Tyndall Effect (by larger colloidal particles)
Scattered light makes a beam visible from the side (the "Tyndall beam"). This distinguishes colloids from true solutions:
- Torch beam visible in fog/smoke = Tyndall Effect
- Headlights in fog = Tyndall scattering
- Explained in Chemistry (Solutions & Colloids) but also tested in Physics context

## Part 4 — Electromagnetic Spectrum

Electromagnetic waves are transverse waves that do not require a medium — they travel at c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum.

| Type | Wavelength Range | Frequency Range | Key Properties / Uses |
|------|-----------------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Radio waves** | > 1 mm → km | 3 Hz – 300 GHz | AM/FM radio, TV broadcasting, mobile phones |
| **Microwaves** | 1 mm – 10 cm | 300 MHz – 300 GHz | Microwave ovens (2.45 GHz heats water), satellite comms, RADAR |
| **Infrared (IR)** | 700 nm – 1 mm | 430 THz – 300 GHz | Heat radiation, thermal cameras, TV remotes, night vision, IR spectroscopy |
| **Visible light** | 380 – 780 nm | 430 THz – 750 THz | Human vision (VIBGYOR) |
| **Ultraviolet (UV)** | 10 – 380 nm | 750 THz – 30 PHz | Sun tanning, sterilisation, vitamin D synthesis; blocked by ozone layer; UV lamps detect fake currency |
| **X-rays** | 0.01 – 10 nm | 30 PHz – 30 EHz | Medical imaging (bone), airport security, crystallography |
| **Gamma rays** | < 0.01 nm | > 30 EHz | Nuclear decay, cancer treatment (radiotherapy), most energetic EM radiation |

**Key ordering** (increasing frequency / increasing energy / decreasing wavelength):
**Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible → UV → X-ray → Gamma ray**

Memory trick: **R**ather **M**any **I**ndians **V**iew **U**pcoming **X**- **G**ames (Radio Microwave IR Visible UV X-ray Gamma)

## Part 5 — Laser

**LASER**: Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation.

### Properties of Laser Light (vs ordinary light)
| Property | Laser | Ordinary Light |
|----------|-------|---------------|
| **Coherence** | Highly coherent (waves in phase) | Incoherent |
| **Monochromaticity** | Single wavelength/colour | Multiple wavelengths |
| **Directionality** | Tight, narrow beam — diverges very little | Spreads in all directions |
| **Intensity** | Extremely high intensity in narrow beam | Low intensity spread over large area |

### Types and Applications

| Laser Type | Medium | Wavelength | Application |
|-----------|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **He-Ne laser** | Helium-Neon gas | 632.8 nm (red) | Barcode scanners, holography |
| **CO₂ laser** | Carbon dioxide gas | 10.6 μm (IR) | Industrial cutting, surgical laser |
| **Ruby laser** | Ruby crystal | 694 nm (red) | First laser (Maiman 1960); medical applications |
| **Semiconductor/Diode laser** | GaAs, etc. | Varies | Laser printers, CD/DVD/Blu-ray readers, fibre optic comms |
| **Nd:YAG laser** | Neodymium glass | 1064 nm (IR) | Eye surgery (retinal repair), industrial |
| **Excimer laser** | ArF, KrF | UV range | LASIK eye surgery, semiconductor chip lithography |

**Theodore Maiman** created the first working laser in 1960 using a ruby crystal.

**Applications of Lasers:**
- LASIK eye surgery (reshapes cornea)
- Barcode scanners in supermarkets
- Fibre optic communication (laser light in optical fibres)
- CD/DVD/Blu-ray data reading/writing
- Industrial cutting and welding
- Cancer treatment (photodynamic therapy)
- Laser rangefinders and LIDAR (used in autonomous vehicles, survey)
- Nuclear fusion research (National Ignition Facility — lasers compressed hydrogen pellet to achieve fusion)
- Holography

## Part 6 — Colours and Mixing

### Additive Colour Mixing (Light — Primary Colours of Light)
**Primary colours of light**: **Red (R), Green (G), Blue (B)**
- R + G + B = **White**
- R + G = **Yellow**
- G + B = **Cyan**
- R + B = **Magenta**
- Used in: TV/computer screens, LED displays, projectors

### Subtractive Colour Mixing (Pigments — Primary Colours of Pigment)
**Primary colours of pigment**: **Cyan (C), Magenta (M), Yellow (Y)** (CMY)
- C + M + Y = **Black** (subtracts all colours)
- Used in: printing (CMYK — with Black/Key plate), painting, photography

**Why grass appears green**: it absorbs red and blue light, reflects green light.  
**Why sky is blue**: Rayleigh scattering (see above — not because the sky absorbs other colours).

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **VIBGYOR order**: Violet-Indigo-Blue-Green-Yellow-Orange-Red. In a prism, violet is deviated the MOST (highest refractive index); red is deviated the LEAST. In a rainbow, red is at the TOP of the primary rainbow (outermost), violet is at the bottom (innermost).

2. **Sky is blue due to Rayleigh scattering** (not TIR, not reflection, not refraction). Air molecules scatter shorter wavelengths (blue, violet) more intensely. Our eyes are less sensitive to violet, so we see the sky as blue.

3. **Sunset is red/orange** because sunlight at the horizon travels through more atmosphere, scattering away blue and leaving red/orange. Same principle: "red in morning, sailor's warning; red at night, sailor's delight."

4. **Rainbow: red outside, violet inside** (primary). In a secondary rainbow (above primary), colours are REVERSED (violet outside, red inside). The secondary rainbow forms from two internal reflections.

5. **EM spectrum ordering** (low to high frequency/energy): Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible → UV → X-ray → Gamma. All travel at c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum regardless of frequency.

6. **Infrared radiation is heat radiation** — all warm objects emit IR. TV remotes use IR (not visible light). Thermal cameras "see" IR emitted by the body. Night vision equipment detects IR.

7. **UV radiation from the Sun causes sunburn** and produces vitamin D in skin. The ozone layer absorbs most incoming UV (particularly harmful UV-B and UV-C). Ozone depletion → increased UV at Earth's surface.

8. **X-rays are produced when high-energy electrons strike a metal target** (Röntgen, 1895 — Nobel 1901). X-rays are ionising radiation — they can knock electrons off atoms, causing cell damage. That's why excess X-ray exposure is harmful.

9. **Gamma rays** are the most energetic and most penetrating form of EM radiation. They come from nuclear decay. Used in radiotherapy to kill cancer cells (focused beams of gamma rays from Cobalt-60).

10. **LASER**: Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. Key properties: monochromatic (one wavelength), coherent (waves in phase), directional (negligible divergence). First laser built by Theodore Maiman (1960) using ruby crystal.

11. **Barcode scanners use He-Ne laser** (red, 632.8 nm). LASIK surgery uses excimer (UV) lasers to reshape the cornea. Blu-ray uses shorter wavelength (blue) laser than DVD (red) → more data on the same disc size.

12. **Primary colours of light (RGB)** are Red, Green, Blue. Red + Green + Blue = White. These are ADDITIVE colours (used in screens). Primary pigment colours (CMY) are Cyan, Magenta, Yellow — SUBTRACTIVE mixing. Do not confuse paint mixing (CMY) with light mixing (RGB).

13. **Microwaves used in radar (RADAR)** = Radio Detection And Ranging. RADAR sends microwave pulses; the time delay of the echo gives the distance to an aircraft or weather system. Speed of the target causes Doppler shift in returned frequency.

14. **Mirage** is due to Total Internal Reflection (covered in Ray Optics) caused by variation in air density near a hot road surface — but the concept connects to refraction and bending of light. The sky's image appears on the road, not actual water.

15. **Visible light wavelength range**: 380 nm (violet) to 780 nm (red). This is the narrow band humans can see. The full EM spectrum spans from kilometres (radio) to sub-picometres (gamma).

16. **Danger signals are red**: red light has the longest wavelength among visible colours → scatters least in fog/dust → travels furthest while remaining visible → used for emergency signals, traffic stop lights, and ship/aircraft lights.
