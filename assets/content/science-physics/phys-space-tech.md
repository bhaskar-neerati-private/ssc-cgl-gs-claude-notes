# Space Technology & Defence — ISRO Missions, Satellites, Missiles, NAVIC & Radar

## Sputnik and the Day the Sky Changed

On 4 October 1957, the Soviet Union launched a polished metal sphere about the size of a basketball — 83 kg, four radio antennae, a battery, and nothing else. Sputnik 1 became the first artificial satellite to orbit Earth, completing one orbit every 96 minutes at an altitude of ~500 km. Amateur radio operators worldwide could tune to 20.005 MHz and hear it: *beep… beep… beep*.

The Americans were stunned. If the Soviets could put a satellite in orbit, they could put a nuclear warhead on a rocket that could reach any city in 30 minutes. The Space Age had begun — and it began in terror. The US response was to create NASA (1958), pour money into science education, and eventually put Neil Armstrong on the Moon (1969). India's response, a decade later, was to create a space programme that would one day land a spacecraft near the Moon's south pole for less than the cost of a Hollywood film.

## Part 1 — Basics of Satellites and Orbits

### Types of Orbits

| Orbit | Altitude | Period | Key Feature |
|-------|----------|--------|------------|
| **LEO** (Low Earth Orbit) | 160–2,000 km | ~90–120 min | ISS (400 km), spy satellites, weather satellites |
| **MEO** (Medium Earth Orbit) | 2,000–35,786 km | 2–24 hrs | GPS (20,200 km), NAVIC, GLONASS, Galileo |
| **GEO** (Geostationary Orbit) | **35,786 km** | **24 hours** | Communications satellites (INSAT, DigiSat); appear stationary over fixed point |
| **SSO** (Sun-Synchronous Orbit) | 500–800 km | ~100 min | Remote sensing (Cartosat, ResourceSat); passes over same spot at the same local solar time daily |
| **HEO** (Highly Elliptical Orbit) | Varies | Varies | Molniya orbit; covers polar regions for extended periods |

**Geostationary Orbit (GEO)**: exactly 35,786 km altitude, equatorial orbit, eastward, period = 24 hours. Satellite appears stationary relative to Earth → ideal for TV broadcasting, weather monitoring. First proposed by Arthur C. Clarke (1945) — also called Clarke Orbit.

**Escape Velocity**: 11.2 km/s — needed to break free from Earth's gravity.  
**Orbital Velocity (LEO)**: ~7.9 km/s (first cosmic velocity).

## Part 2 — Launch Vehicles (Rockets)

### ISRO Launch Vehicles

| Vehicle | Full Name | Payload to LEO | Notable Uses |
|---------|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| **SLV-3** | Satellite Launch Vehicle-3 | 40 kg | India's first successful satellite launch (Rohini, 1980) |
| **ASLV** | Augmented Satellite Launch Vehicle | 150 kg | Testing vehicle; retired |
| **PSLV** | Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle | 3,800 kg (LEO) / 1,750 kg (GEO) | **Workhorse of ISRO**; 100+ consecutive successes; Chandrayaan-1, MOM (Mars Orbiter), IRNSS |
| **GSLV Mk I/II** | Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle | 2,500 kg (GTO) | Communication satellites to GEO; uses cryogenic engine |
| **GSLV Mk III / LVM3** | Launch Vehicle Mark 3 | 4,000 kg (GTO) / 10,000 kg (LEO) | Heaviest rocket; Chandrayaan-2, Chandrayaan-3, OneWeb satellites |
| **SSLV** | Small Satellite Launch Vehicle | 500 kg (LEO) | Quick, low-cost launches for small satellites |

**Cryogenic engine**: uses liquid oxygen (LOX) and liquid hydrogen (LH₂) as propellants; India developed its own cryogenic engine after Russia supplied early engines. India is now among a handful of nations with indigenous cryogenic engine capability.

**PSLV configurations**: QL (Core Alone), DL, XL (with different numbers of strap-on booster motors). PSLV-XL is the most powerful PSLV variant.

## Part 4 — INSAT and Communication Satellites

**INSAT Series** (Indian National Satellite System): multipurpose geostationary satellites for telecommunications, broadcasting, and meteorology.

**GSAT Series**: dedicated communication satellites (TV broadcasting, internet, VSAT).

**EDUSAT / GSAT-3**: dedicated education satellite — broadcasts educational programmes across India.

**IRNSS / NAVIC**: navigation satellites (see below).

**Oceansat, Resourcesat, Cartosat**: earth observation satellites in SSO for remote sensing, agriculture, disaster management.

## Part 5 — NAVIC (Navigation with Indian Constellation)

**NAVIC** (formerly IRNSS — Indian Regional Navigation Satellite System):
- India's own GPS-like navigation system
- 7 satellites (3 geostationary + 4 inclined geosynchronous)
- Covers India and 1,500 km region around it
- Accuracy: better than 20 metres for standard positioning; better than 10 metres for restricted/military use
- Signal frequencies: L5 and S-band
- Applications: terrestrial, aerial, and marine navigation; disaster management; vehicle tracking; mobile phones (India mandated NAVIC chip in new smartphones from 2023)

**Global comparison:**
| System | Owner | Coverage |
|--------|-------|---------|
| GPS (Global Positioning System) | USA | Global |
| GLONASS | Russia | Global |
| Galileo | EU | Global |
| BeiDou | China | Global |
| **NAVIC** | **India** | **Regional** (India + 1,500 km) |
| QZSS | Japan | Regional |

## Part 6 — Missile Systems (India's Defence)

**IGMDP (Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme)**: launched 1983 under Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam; aimed to make India self-reliant in guided missiles.

### Five IGMDP Missiles (mnemonic: **P**eople **N**eed **A**ctive **T**iger **A**rmy)

| Missile | Type | Range | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|-------|
| **Prithvi** | Surface-to-Surface Ballistic | 150–350 km | India's first indigenously developed ballistic missile; three variants |
| **Agni** | Surface-to-Surface Ballistic | Agni-1: 700 km → Agni-V: 5,000+ km | Agni-V is India's first ICBM-class missile; MIRV-capable Agni-5 tested 2024 |
| **Trishul** | Surface-to-Air | 9 km | Short-range air defence; now retired |
| **Akash** | Surface-to-Air | 25–45 km | Medium-range air defence; replacing Soviet-era systems |
| **Nag** | Anti-Tank Guided | 4–7 km | Third-generation anti-tank missile; HELINA (helicopter-launched variant) |

### Other Key Missile Systems

| Missile | Type | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| **BrahMos** | Supersonic Cruise Missile | India-Russia joint venture; speed Mach 2.8+; range ~300 km (land/ship/submarine/air versions); fastest supersonic cruise missile in operation |
| **Astra** | Air-to-Air | India's first indigenous beyond-visual-range air-to-air missile; range 100+ km |
| **Prahar** | Surface-to-Surface | Quick reaction tactical missile (200 km range) |
| **Shaurya** | Surface-to-Surface Hypersonic | Hypersonic (~Mach 7.5); range 700–1900 km; can be stored in canister |
| **Nirbhay** | Cruise Missile | Long-range (1000+ km) subsonic cruise missile; India's "Tomahawk"; still in development |
| **K-15 Sagarika / K-4** | Submarine-Launched Ballistic Missile (SLBM) | Launched from Arihant class nuclear submarine; India's nuclear triad |

## Part 7 — RADAR

**RADAR**: Radio Detection And Ranging

**Principle**: sends out microwave/radio pulses; measures time for echo to return from an object; calculates distance and (using Doppler Effect) velocity.

**d = ct/2** (distance = speed of light × time / 2)

**Applications:**
- **Defence**: tracking enemy aircraft, ships, missiles; air traffic control
- **Weather (Doppler RADAR)**: detecting rainfall, tracking storms (Cyclone monitoring uses RADAR at stations along coastline)
- **Speed guns**: police radar guns measure vehicle speed using Doppler shift
- **Airport**: ATC (Air Traffic Control) radar monitors all aircraft within hundreds of km
- **SAR (Synthetic Aperture Radar)**: used on satellites (RISAT, NISAR) to image Earth's surface through clouds and night — crucial for disaster monitoring (floods, cyclones, glacier changes)
- **Marine RADAR**: ship navigation, collision avoidance

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Chandrayaan-3 landed on 23 August 2023** near the Moon's south pole. India became the 4th nation to achieve a soft landing (after USSR, USA, China) and the first to land at the lunar south pole. Lander = Vikram; rover = Pragyan.

2. **MOM (Mangalyaan) 2013**: India's first Mars mission; became the first Asian country (and first to succeed on maiden attempt) to reach Mars; entered Mars orbit in September 2014. Cost ₹450 crore (~$74 million) — cheaper than many Hollywood films.

3. **Aditya-L1**: India's first dedicated solar observation mission; placed at Sun-Earth Lagrange point L1, approximately 1.5 million km from Earth. Lagrange points are positions where gravitational forces between two large bodies balance — an object placed there requires minimal fuel to maintain position.

4. **PSLV = Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle** — ISRO's workhorse; over 100 consecutive successful missions; placed Chandrayaan-1, MOM, and IRNSS constellation satellites. **GSLV Mk III / LVM3** = India's heaviest rocket; placed Chandrayaan-2 and Chandrayaan-3.

5. **Geostationary orbit = 35,786 km** altitude; orbital period exactly 24 hours; appears stationary over one point on equator. First proposed by Arthur C. Clarke (1945) — hence "Clarke orbit." Used for TV broadcasting, weather, and communication satellites (INSAT, GSAT).

6. **NAVIC**: India's regional navigation system; 7 satellites; covers India + 1,500 km surrounding region; accuracy better than 20 m. Global systems: GPS (USA), GLONASS (Russia), Galileo (EU), BeiDou (China).

7. **BrahMos**: India-Russia joint venture supersonic cruise missile; fastest supersonic cruise missile in operational service (Mach 2.8+); can be launched from land, sea, submarine, and air. Named after Brahmaputra (India) and Moskva (Russia) rivers.

8. **Agni-V**: India's first inter-continental ballistic missile (ICBM)-class missile; range ~5,000+ km; surface-to-surface; India's strategic deterrence capability. MIRV-capable Agni-5 tested in 2024.

9. **IGMDP five missiles**: Prithvi (surface-to-surface), Agni (ballistic), Akash (surface-to-air), Trishul (surface-to-air, retired), Nag (anti-tank). Led by Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam. India now exports Akash and BrahMos.

10. **Dr. Vikram Sarabhai** = Father of India's space programme. **Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam** = Missile Man of India (led SLV-3, IGMDP). **K. Sivan** = ISRO Chairman during Chandrayaan-2 (2019). **S. Somanath** = ISRO Chairman during Chandrayaan-3 (2023).

11. **RADAR uses microwave pulses**: time of echo arrival gives distance (d = ct/2). Doppler shift in frequency gives velocity. Doppler radar detects rainfall and tracks storm systems — crucial for weather forecasting in India's monsoon monitoring.

12. **SAR (Synthetic Aperture Radar)** on satellites (RISAT-1, RISAT-2, upcoming NISAR with NASA) can image Earth's surface through clouds and at night — unlike optical satellites which need clear skies and daylight. Essential for flood mapping, cyclone monitoring, glacier monitoring.

13. **Aryabhata (1975)**: India's first satellite, launched by the Soviet Union. **Rohini (1980)**: first satellite launched by an Indian rocket (SLV-3). Named after Aryabhata, the 5th century Indian mathematician-astronomer.

14. **Gaganyaan programme**: India's first crewed space mission. The Vehicle D abort test (TV-D1) was successfully conducted in October 2023. Gaganyaan will use LVM3 rocket; planned crew of 3 for a 3-day orbital mission. India will be the 4th nation with independent human spaceflight capability (after USSR/Russia, USA, China).

15. **Escape velocity from Earth = 11.2 km/s**. Orbital velocity for LEO ≈ 7.9 km/s. Speed of light = 3 × 10⁸ m/s. Distance from Earth to Moon ≈ 384,400 km. Distance from Earth to Sun ≈ 150 million km (1 AU). These are frequently tested numerical values.

16. **India's nuclear triad**: ability to deliver nuclear weapons from land (Agni missiles), sea (K-15/K-4 SLBMs on Arihant submarines), and air (Rafale aircraft with ASMP-A cruise missiles). India's nuclear doctrine: No First Use (NFU) — India will not use nuclear weapons first, but will retaliate massively if attacked.
