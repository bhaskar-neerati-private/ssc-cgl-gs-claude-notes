# 1991 LPG Reforms, Globalisation, Make in India & PLI Scheme

## The Day India Changed Course

In July 1991, India's new Finance Minister, Dr. Manmohan Singh, rose to deliver the Union Budget in Parliament. The country was in economic crisis — nearly bankrupt, its gold pledged abroad, its currency under attack, its international credit exhausted. What he said that day — and what followed — transformed India from a closed, state-controlled economy into a dynamic, market-driven one.

"No power on earth can stop an idea whose time has come," he quoted Victor Hugo.

The idea was **liberalisation** — freeing the economy from the iron grip of state licenses, bureaucratic permissions, and import barriers. The 1991 reforms, collectively called the **LPG reforms** (Liberalisation, Privatisation, Globalisation), are the defining economic event of modern India. Everything before 1991 and everything after is measured against this pivot.

---

## The World Before 1991: License Raj

To understand 1991, you must understand what India was escaping.

**The License Raj** was a system where virtually every economic decision — opening a factory, expanding capacity, importing machinery, hiring workers — required a government license or permit. The system was built on socialist principles: the state would plan, coordinate, and control economic activity for the public good.

In practice, it created:
- Bureaucratic bottlenecks and massive corruption (licenses were bought and sold)
- Inefficient industries protected from competition by high import tariffs
- A private sector afraid to grow (MRTP Act limited large businesses)
- Chronically low growth — **the "Hindu Rate of Growth"** of ~3.5% annually, a term coined by economist **Raj Krishna** in 1978

**Key Laws of the License Raj Era:**
| Law | Year | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **MRTP Act** | 1969 | Monopolies and Restrictive Trade Practices Act — restricted size of private businesses; prevented expansion beyond certain assets |
| **FERA** | 1973 | Foreign Exchange Regulation Act — strict criminal controls on forex; severely limited FDI |
| Industrial Development and Regulation Act | 1951 | Required license for any new industry or expansion |

---

## The 1991 Crisis: The Trigger

**Why did India need to reform?**

By mid-1991, India faced a full-blown balance of payments crisis:
- **Forex reserves fell to ~$1 billion** — barely 2 weeks of imports
- India secretly **airlifted 67 tonnes of gold** to Bank of England and Union Bank of Switzerland as collateral for an emergency IMF loan
- Fiscal deficit had ballooned to **~8% of GDP**
- Gulf War (1990–91) spiked oil prices and caused Indian workers to return from the Gulf (reducing remittances)
- Credit agencies were about to downgrade India's sovereign rating — threatening complete financial isolation

This was the crisis that forced India to liberalise. Without reform, India could not access IMF funds. The reform was both a necessity and an opportunity.

## The LPG Reforms: Three Pillars

**Architect:** Finance Minister Dr. Manmohan Singh (under PM P.V. Narasimha Rao)
**Announced in:** New Industrial Policy 1991 and Union Budget 1991–92

---

### L — Liberalisation

**Industrial Delicensing:**
- Most industries freed from licensing requirement; only a few strategic sectors retained licensing
- The "reserved for public sector" list was cut from **17 industries to 8** (later reduced to just 3 — atomic energy, railways, some defence)
- Companies could expand capacity without government permission
- Entry barriers slashed — new businesses could start freely

**MRTP Act diluted, later replaced:**
- MRTP Act's growth-limiting provisions were removed in 1991
- MRTP Act 1969 was eventually **replaced by the Competition Act, 2002**
- Competition Commission of India (CCI) established — focus shifted from limiting size to preventing anti-competitive behaviour (cartels, abuse of dominance, anti-competitive mergers)

**Financial Sector Liberalisation:**
- Interest rate deregulation
- New private banks licensed (1993) — ICICI Bank, HDFC Bank, Axis Bank entered
- Capital market reforms; SEBI given statutory status (1992)

---

### P — Privatisation

**Disinvestment Programme:**
- Government began selling equity stakes in PSUs (Public Sector Undertakings)
- From public monopoly → competitive market with government as one player
- Created **DIPAM (Department of Investment and Public Asset Management)** to manage government equity and disinvestment

**Types of Disinvestment:**
| Type | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Minority disinvestment** | Government sells small stake; retains majority and control |
| **Strategic sale** | Government sells majority stake + management control to private buyer |

**Notable disinvestments:**
- VSNL (now Tata Communications) — Tata Group, 2002
- Balco — Vedanta Group, 2001
- Air India — Tata Group, January 2022 (completed under Modi government)

**PSU Status Categories (Navratna system):**
- **Navratna** (9 gems): granted more financial and operational autonomy (e.g., BHEL, NTPC, SAIL, ONGC, IOC)
- **Miniratna** (smaller jewels): limited autonomy
- These ratings allow PSUs to invest and expand without case-by-case government approval

---

### G — Globalisation

**Trade Liberalisation:**
- Import tariffs slashed from extremely high levels (some > 300%) to much lower rates
- Quantitative restrictions (QRs) on imports phased out
- India joined WTO as a founding member in **1995** — bound by WTO rules

**Rupee Convertibility:**
- 1994: Rupee made **fully convertible on Current Account** — anyone can buy/sell rupees for trade in goods and services
- Capital Account convertibility is still partial (not fully free capital movement)

**FDI Liberalisation:**
- Automatic approval route introduced for many sectors
- FDI caps raised across industries
- Sectors previously closed to foreign companies opened up

**FERA replaced by FEMA:**
- **FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act), 1999** replaced **FERA (Foreign Exchange Regulation Act), 1973**
- Critical shift: Under FERA, forex violations were **criminal offences** (imprisonment). Under FEMA, they became **civil offences** (fines) — business-friendly transformation

## Post-1991 Impact

| Indicator | Pre-1991 | Post-1991 (by 2000s) |
|---|---|---|
| GDP growth | ~3.5% ("Hindu rate") | 6–8% sustained |
| India's global rank | ~11th by GDP | 5th largest (2024) |
| FDI inflows | Minimal | Billions annually |
| Exports | Closed, import-substituting | $770 billion+ (2022-23) |
| Private sector | Constrained | Dominant in most sectors |
| IT/Software | Negligible | $250 billion+ industry |

---

## Competition Commission of India (CCI)

- **Competition Act, 2002** (replaced MRTP Act 1969)
- **CCI became operational: 2009**
- **HQ:** New Delhi
- Functions: Investigate anti-competitive agreements, abuse of dominant position, regulate mergers and acquisitions
- The shift: from "don't let businesses get too big" (MRTP) to "let them grow but stop anti-competitive behaviour" (CCI)

---

## IBC — Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016

A landmark reform of the Modi era that completed what 1991 started:

- **IBC 2016:** Time-bound resolution of insolvency — 180 days (+ 90-day extension maximum)
- **NCLT (National Company Law Tribunal):** Adjudicating authority for IBC
- **IBBI (Insolvency and Bankruptcy Board of India):** Regulator under IBC
- Impact: Banks could now resolve NPAs in a structured, time-bound manner — major clean-up of the banking sector

## Make in India (2014)

**Launched:** 25 September 2014
**PM:** Narendra Modi
**Goal:** Transform India into a global manufacturing hub; raise manufacturing's share of GDP to **25%** from ~15%

**Focus sectors:** Initially 25 sectors including automobiles, aviation, biotechnology, chemicals, defence, electrical machinery, IT, leather, pharmaceuticals, textiles, tourism

**Pillars:**
1. **Ease of Doing Business** — reduce regulatory burden
2. **FDI liberalisation** — open more sectors at higher caps
3. **Infrastructure development** — industrial corridors, smart cities
4. **Innovation and IP protection** — stronger patent regime

**Ease of Doing Business Rankings (World Bank):**
- India 2014: **142nd rank**
- India 2019: **63rd rank** — dramatic improvement
- World Bank discontinued the index in 2021 (methodology concerns); India now tracks its own Business Reforms Action Plan

---

## Startup India (2016)

**Launched:** 16 January 2016
**DPIIT recognises startups** — must be ≤10 years old, not formed by splitting existing business, turnover < ₹100 crore
**Benefits:** Tax exemptions (3 years under Section 80-IAC), simplified compliance, self-certification on 6 labour + 3 environment laws, faster patent review
**Fund of Funds:** SIDBI manages a ₹10,000 crore Fund of Funds to invest in venture capital funds that back startups

---

## Atmanirbhar Bharat (2020)

**Launched:** May 2020 during COVID-19 crisis
**PM:** Narendra Modi
**Package:** ₹20 lakh crore economic stimulus (largest in India's history)
**Theme:** Self-reliance — reduce dependence on imports, especially from China

**Five Pillars of Atmanirbhar Bharat:**
1. Economy — quantum jump, not incremental growth
2. Infrastructure — world-class
3. System — technology-driven, efficient
4. Vibrant Demography — turning demographic dividend into strength
5. Demand — activating domestic demand

**Key Atmanirbhar initiatives:**
- PLI Schemes (14 sectors)
- MSME redefinition (raised turnover limits)
- Defence indigenisation (negative import lists for defence items)
- Coal sector opening to private mining
- Agriculture reforms (later withdrawn)

---

## PLI — Production Linked Incentive Scheme

**Introduced:** 2020 (first 3 sectors: mobile phones, pharma APIs, medical devices)
**Expanded to:** 14 sectors by 2021–22
**Administered by:** Ministry of Finance + relevant sector ministries

### How PLI Works

Government pays companies a **cash incentive based on incremental production** above a base year threshold:

```
PLI Incentive = (Sales in current year − Sales in base year) × Incentive Rate (%)
```

Companies must invest a minimum amount and achieve a minimum production level to qualify.

### 14 PLI Sectors

| Sector | Ministry |
|---|---|
| Mobile phones & electronic components | Electronics & IT |
| Pharmaceuticals (API + finished doses) | Pharmaceuticals |
| Medical devices | Health |
| Automobiles & auto components | Heavy Industries |
| Advanced Chemistry Cell (ACC) batteries | Heavy Industries |
| Speciality steel | Steel |
| Telecom & networking products | Telecom |
| Textile (MMF & technical textiles) | Textiles |
| Food processing | Food Processing |
| Solar PV modules | New & Renewable Energy |
| White goods (ACs, LED lights) | Consumer Affairs |
| Drone manufacturing | Civil Aviation |
| Semi-conductors | Electronics & IT |

**Target:** Generate ₹5 lakh crore in additional production; ₹35,000 crore in incentives over 5 years; create millions of jobs

**Why PLI matters:** It is designed to attract the **"China+1" wave** — multinational companies diversifying supply chains away from China post-COVID. Apple's manufacturing (Foxconn, Tata) in India expanded under PLI.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **1991 crisis: forex = 2 weeks; 67 tonnes gold pledged; FM = Manmohan Singh; PM = PV Narasimha Rao** — all four facts tested together.
2. **LPG = Liberalisation + Privatisation + Globalisation** — the full form; no individual letter should be confused.
3. **"Hindu Rate of Growth" (~3.5%) coined by Raj Krishna, 1978** — not by Manmohan Singh or Nehru.
4. **MRTP Act 1969 replaced by Competition Act 2002** — MRTP limited business size; Competition Act limits anti-competitive behaviour — a philosophical shift.
5. **CCI operational from 2009** (Act was 2002; there was a 7-year gap before it became operational).
6. **FERA 1973 (criminal offence) → FEMA 1999 (civil offence)** — nature of forex violation changed from criminal to civil.
7. **Industries reserved for public sector cut from 17 to 8 to 3** — the progressive reduction in public sector monopoly.
8. **Rupee current account convertibility: 1994** — current account (trade) is freely convertible; capital account is not fully convertible yet.
9. **India joined WTO as founding member in 1995** — a direct result of globalisation; Uruguay Round commitment.
10. **Make in India launched 25 September 2014** — date is tested; lion-with-cogs logo; manufacturing target of 25% of GDP.
11. **India's Ease of Doing Business: 142nd (2014) → 63rd (2019)** — dramatic jump; World Bank discontinued index in 2021.
12. **Startup India = 16 January 2016** — date; DPIIT recognises startups; 3-year tax exemption under Section 80-IAC.
13. **Atmanirbhar Bharat = May 2020** — COVID-19 context; ₹20 lakh crore package; self-reliance philosophy.
14. **PLI = cash incentive on INCREMENTAL production** — not on total production; above a base year threshold.
15. **PLI covers 14 sectors** — mobile phones, pharma, auto, batteries, semiconductors, drones, solar are all tested individually.
16. **Air India privatised to Tata Group, January 2022** — a landmark; Air India was nationalised in 1953; returned to Tatas 69 years later.
