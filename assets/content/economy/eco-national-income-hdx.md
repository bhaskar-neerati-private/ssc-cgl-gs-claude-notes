# National Income Measurement, HDI, PPP & Savings

## Three Ways to Count the Same Thing

The beauty of national income accounting is that three completely different approaches — counting all output, counting all incomes, or counting all spending — must arrive at the same GDP. Like measuring a cube from different faces: same volume, different vantage point.

---

## Three Methods of GDP Measurement

### 1. Output / Value Added Method (Production Method)

Count GVA (Gross Value Added) at each stage of production across all sectors, then add net taxes:

**GDP = GVA (Primary + Secondary + Tertiary) + Net Taxes on Products**

This is India's primary GDP estimation method under the 2011-12 base year.

### 2. Income Method

Count all incomes earned by factors of production (labour, land, capital, entrepreneurship):

**GDP = Compensation of Employees + Gross Operating Surplus + Gross Mixed Income + Net Taxes on Products**

- **Compensation of Employees:** Wages, salaries, social contributions paid by employers
- **Gross Operating Surplus:** Profits, rent, interest earned by firms
- **Gross Mixed Income:** Earnings of the self-employed (mixed because it combines labour + capital)

### 3. Expenditure Method (Demand Side)

Count all final spending in the economy:

**GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)**

| Component | Full Form | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|
| **C** | Private Final Consumption Expenditure | Household spending on goods and services |
| **I** | Gross Capital Formation | Investment in fixed assets + change in inventories |
| **G** | Government Final Consumption Expenditure | Government spending on goods and services (not transfers) |
| **X** | Exports | Foreign spending on Indian goods and services |
| **M** | Imports | Indian spending on foreign goods and services |
| **(X−M)** | Net Exports | India's trade balance |

**In India:** Private Consumption (C) is by far the largest component — approximately **55–60% of GDP**. This makes India's growth highly dependent on domestic consumer demand.

**Capital Formation (I) has two parts:**
- **Gross Fixed Capital Formation (GFCF):** Investment in fixed assets — machinery, buildings, roads, equipment. This is the most watched investment indicator.
- **Change in Inventories:** Increase/decrease in unsold stock.

## Savings, Investment and the Savings Rate

### The Savings-Investment Identity

In a **closed economy:** National Savings = National Investment (no external borrowing)

In an **open economy** like India: Domestic Savings + Net Foreign Borrowing = Investment

**India's Gross Domestic Savings Rate:** ~30–32% of GDP — one of the highest among developing countries, though it has been declining from the peak of ~36% in 2007-08.

High savings → high investable resources → high capital formation → higher growth capacity.

### Harrod-Domar Growth Model

An early mathematical growth theory:

**Growth Rate = Savings Rate / ICOR**

Where **ICOR (Incremental Capital-Output Ratio)** = units of new capital needed to generate one additional unit of GDP.

- Lower ICOR = more efficient use of capital (less investment needed for the same growth)
- Higher savings rate → higher growth (holding ICOR constant)

India's ICOR has historically been around 4–5, meaning ₹4–5 of investment generates ₹1 of GDP growth.

## Human Development Index (HDI)

### Origin and Purpose

The HDI was created by **Mahbub ul Haq** (Pakistani economist) and **Amartya Sen** (Indian economist and Nobel laureate) for the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP). First published in the **Human Development Report of 1990**.

The insight: GDP per capita tells you how much money people have, not whether they are healthy or educated. HDI brings health and education into the frame.

**Published by:** UNDP (United Nations Development Programme)
**Report:** Human Development Report (HDR) — published annually

### Three Dimensions of HDI

| Dimension | Indicator | What It Captures |
|---|---|---|
| **Long and Healthy Life** | Life expectancy at birth | Overall health of the population |
| **Knowledge** | Mean years of schooling + Expected years of schooling | Educational achievement and access |
| **Decent Standard of Living** | GNI per capita (PPP $) | Average income, adjusted for purchasing power |

**HDI = Geometric Mean of Three Dimension Indices**

(Before 2010, HDI used the arithmetic mean; since 2010 it uses the geometric mean — this penalises imbalances between dimensions more severely.)

### HDI Score Ranges

| Category | HDI Range |
|---|---|
| Very High Human Development | ≥ 0.800 |
| High Human Development | 0.700 – 0.799 |
| Medium Human Development | 0.550 – 0.699 |
| Low Human Development | < 0.550 |

**India's HDI (~2022/23 report):** ~0.644, rank ~134 out of 191 countries → **Medium Human Development**

**Top HDI countries:** Switzerland, Norway, Iceland, Hong Kong SAR, Australia

### Related UNDP Indices

| Index | Full Name | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|
| **GDI** | Gender Development Index | HDI calculated separately for males and females; ratio reveals gender gap in human development |
| **GII** | Gender Inequality Index | Gender gaps in reproductive health, empowerment (parliamentary seats, secondary education), labour force participation; 0 = equality, 1 = full inequality |
| **MPI** | Multidimensional Poverty Index | Joint UNDP-Oxford (OPHI); 10 deprivations across health, education, living standards; replaced the old HPI in 2010 |
| **PHDI** | Planetary Pressures-Adjusted HDI | HDI discounted for carbon emissions and material footprint; new green development indicator |

## Physical Quality of Life Index (PQLI)

Proposed by **Morris David Morris** in 1979 — one of the earliest attempts to measure development beyond income.

**Three components (equal weight):**
1. Infant Mortality Rate (IMR)
2. Life Expectancy at age 1
3. Basic Literacy Rate

**Scale:** 0 to 100 (100 = best)

PQLI predates HDI but shares the same philosophy — development is more than income. HDI largely superseded PQLI in global use.

## Inequality: Lorenz Curve and Gini Coefficient

### Lorenz Curve

Plot the cumulative share of income received (Y-axis) by the cumulative share of population sorted from poorest to richest (X-axis).

- **Line of Perfect Equality (45° diagonal):** Bottom 10% earns 10%, bottom 50% earns 50%, etc.
- **Actual Lorenz Curve:** Bows below the diagonal — the poorer half of the population earns less than 50% of income.
- **Greater the bow** (further from diagonal) = greater inequality.

### Gini Coefficient

**Gini = Area between diagonal and Lorenz Curve / Total area under diagonal**

| Gini Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **0** | **Perfect equality** — everyone earns exactly the same |
| **1** | **Perfect inequality** — one person earns everything |
| 0.25–0.35 | Moderate equality (Nordic countries) |
| 0.35–0.45 | Moderate inequality (India ~0.36; USA ~0.39) |
| > 0.50 | High inequality (many Sub-Saharan African countries) |

India's Gini coefficient: approximately **0.35–0.37** — moderate inequality, but rising urban-rural and caste-based gaps are concerns.

## PPP — Purchasing Power Parity

### The Problem with Market Exchange Rates

India's GDP in 2023 was approximately $3.7 trillion at market exchange rates (₹1 = ~$0.012). But a haircut that costs $20 in New York costs ₹200 (~$2.4) in Delhi. If you convert Indian incomes to dollars at the market rate, they look tiny — even though Indians can actually afford local goods and services.

**PPP solves this:** It adjusts exchange rates to reflect what identical baskets of goods actually cost in each country. A "PPP dollar" has the same purchasing power everywhere.

### India's Rankings: Nominal vs PPP

| Basis | India's Rank | Countries Ahead |
|---|---|---|
| **Nominal GDP (market exchange rates)** | **5th** | USA, China, Germany, Japan |
| **PPP-adjusted GDP** | **3rd** | USA, China |

India jumps two places on PPP because Indian prices are far lower than American or European prices — so the same rupee income buys more real goods in India than its dollar equivalent suggests.

**International Comparison Programme (ICP):** The World Bank coordinates this global exercise of comparing price levels across countries to compute PPP-adjusted GDP. Conducted every few years.

## Critiques of GDP as a Welfare Measure

GDP is essential but incomplete as a measure of a society's well-being:

| Limitation | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Ignores inequality** | GDP can rise while the bottom 50% gets poorer if gains accrue to the top |
| **Ignores informal economy** | India's informal sector (domestic work, subsistence farming, barter) is large and unrecorded |
| **Ignores environmental damage** | Cutting a forest raises GDP (timber sale) but destroys future value |
| **Ignores health and education** | A country with rising GDP but worsening child mortality looks fine in GDP data |
| **Ignores happiness** | **Bhutan's Gross National Happiness (GNH)** index — the most famous alternative — measures psychological well-being, cultural preservation, environmental sustainability, and good governance, not output |

**Green GDP / Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI):** Adjusted GDP that deducts environmental and social costs. India has not yet officially adopted these alternatives.

## Historical Pioneers of National Income Estimation in India

| Person | Contribution |
|---|---|
| **Dadabhai Naoroji** | First estimate of India's national income (1868); used it to quantify the "Drain of Wealth" theory |
| **V.K.R.V. Rao** | Pioneered scientific national income estimation in India (1940 doctoral thesis at Cambridge) — considered the "Father of India's National Income Statistics" |
| **P.C. Mahalanobis** | Chaired the **National Income Committee (1949)** — India's first official national income estimation body; also designed India's Second Five-Year Plan strategy |

## GDP Advance Estimates — Release Calendar

India's NSO (under MoSPI) releases GDP estimates at multiple points in the year:

| Estimate | Released | Based On |
|---|---|---|
| **First Advance Estimate** | **January** (9 months into FY) | Partial data; extrapolated |
| **Second Advance Estimate** | February | More data |
| **Provisional Estimate** | **May** (2 months after FY ends) | More complete |
| First Revised Estimate | January next year | Revised data |
| Final Estimate | 2+ years later | Complete data |

The January Advance Estimate is used by the Finance Ministry for Union Budget preparation.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Three methods of GDP: Output/Value Added, Income, Expenditure (C+I+G+NX)** — all must give the same GDP; exams ask which method is which.
2. **Largest GDP component in India = Private Consumption (C) at ~55–60%** — India's growth is consumption-driven.
3. **HDI was created by Mahbub ul Haq and Amartya Sen for UNDP (1990)** — not World Bank, not IMF.
4. **HDI = geometric mean** of three dimension indices (changed from arithmetic mean in 2010) — old books say arithmetic; new is geometric.
5. **Three HDI dimensions: Life expectancy + Education (mean + expected years of schooling) + GNI per capita (PPP)** — GNI, not GDP, is used in HDI.
6. **India at PPP = 3rd largest economy (USA, China, India)**; at Nominal = 5th (USA, China, Germany, Japan, India).
7. **Gini = 0 = perfect equality; Gini = 1 = perfect inequality** — students reverse these frequently.
8. **Lorenz curve further from the diagonal = more inequality** (not the other way around).
9. **PQLI proposed by Morris David Morris (1979)** — uses IMR + Life Expectancy + Literacy; precedes HDI.
10. **GDI ≠ GII:** GDI = gender gap in HDI (ratio); GII = gender inequality index (reproductive health + empowerment + labour market) — two different UNDP indices.
11. **MPI (Multidimensional Poverty Index) = joint UNDP + Oxford (OPHI)** product; 10 indicators, 3 dimensions; replaced the old HPI in 2010.
12. **First Advance Estimate of GDP released in January** — nine months into the fiscal year, before it closes in March; used for Union Budget preparation.
13. **V.K.R.V. Rao = pioneer of India's national income estimation** (1940); Dadabhai Naoroji = first estimate ever (1868).
14. **Bhutan's GNH (Gross National Happiness)** — the most famous non-GDP development indicator; used by SSC to test knowledge of development alternatives.
15. **PPP uses a common basket of goods to compare real purchasing power** — not market exchange rates; World Bank's ICP computes this.
16. **GFCF (Gross Fixed Capital Formation) = investment in fixed assets** — most important investment indicator watched by economists; part of "I" in C+I+G+NX.
