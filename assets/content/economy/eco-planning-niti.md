# Five-Year Plans, Planning Commission & NITI Aayog

## India's Tryst with Planning

In 1947, India inherited a shattered economy — decades of colonial extraction, the trauma of Partition, and a population where most people lived on the edge of subsistence. The new government faced a choice: let markets find their own way, or plan the economy's direction from the top. Nehru, inspired by the Soviet Union's spectacular industrialisation through Five-Year Plans, chose planning.

On 15 March 1950 — just weeks after the Constitution came into force — the **Planning Commission** was established by an executive resolution. Not an Act of Parliament. Not a constitutional provision. An executive order. This matters for SSC.

For 64 years, the Planning Commission drafted Five-Year Plans, allocated funds, and guided India's development trajectory. In 2014, Narendra Modi abolished it and replaced it with **NITI Aayog** — a think tank with no fund-allocation powers, built on the premise that the top-down Soviet-style planning model had outlived its relevance in a market economy.

---

## The Planning Commission

**Established:** 15 March 1950, by an **executive resolution** (not constitutional; not by any Act — extra-constitutional body)

**Chairperson:** Prime Minister (ex-officio) — always the PM by convention
**Deputy Chairman:** The effective working head; ranked a Cabinet Minister
**Members:** Economists, technocrats, administrators

**Functions:**
- Prepare Five-Year Plans
- Allocate plan funds between Centre and States (**this is the key power Planning Commission had that NITI Aayog lacks**)
- Define priorities and targets for the national economy
- Evaluate implementation of plans

---

## National Development Council (NDC)

**Established:** 6 August 1952 (also by executive order — not constitutional)
**Chairperson:** Prime Minister
**Members:** All Union Cabinet Ministers + All State Chief Ministers + Lt. Governors of UTs

**Function:** The NDC approved Five-Year Plans after the Planning Commission drafted them. It was the federal layer — states had a say in approving national plans. NDC also reviewed plan performance.

Effectively replaced now by NITI Aayog's **Governing Council**.

## Five-Year Plans: The Complete Journey

India completed **12 Five-Year Plans** (1951–52 to 2016–17). Interspersed were "Plan Holidays" and "Annual Plans" during political crises.

### 1st Plan (1951–56)
**Theme:** Agriculture, irrigation, power
**Model:** Harrod-Domar growth model
**Priority:** Food security after Partition's disruption; rehabilitate refugees
**Key projects:** Bhakra-Nangal Dam, Damodar Valley Project, Hirakud Dam
**Target:** 2.1% growth → **Achieved: 3.6%** (overshot — good monsoons helped)

### 2nd Plan (1956–61)
**Theme:** Heavy industrialisation
**Model:** **P.C. Mahalanobis model** (statistician at ISI Kolkata; inspired by Soviet planning)
**Strategy:** Invest in capital goods industries (steel, heavy machinery) to build industrial foundations
**Key projects:** Steel plants at **Bhilai** (Soviet help), **Rourkela** (West German help), **Durgapur** (British help)
**"Socialist Pattern of Society"** declared as India's goal
**Target:** 4.5% → **Achieved: 4.2%**

### 3rd Plan (1961–66) — The Failed Plan
**Theme:** "Self-reliant and self-generating economy"
**Also called:** Gadgil Plan (D.R. Gadgil was Deputy Chairman)
**Shattered by:** 1962 India-China War, 1965 India-Pakistan War, 1965–66 severe drought
**Target:** 5.6% → **Achieved: 2.8%** — worst performance; India had to import food under PL-480 from USA

### Plan Holiday (1966–69) — Three Annual Plans
After the 3rd Plan's failure, conditions were too unstable for a long-term plan. Three Annual Plans were prepared instead.
**Events:** Rupee devalued (1966), green revolution beginning, political consolidation

### 4th Plan (1969–74)
**Theme:** "Growth with stability"
**Coincided with:** Bank nationalisation (1969), Garibi Hatao slogan (Indira Gandhi)
**Target:** 5.7% → **Achieved: 3.3%** — 1971 war, oil shock 1973 disrupted

### 5th Plan (1974–79)
**Theme:** Garibi Hatao; Minimum Needs Programme; 20-Point Programme
**Terminated early:** Janata Party won 1977 elections and scrapped the Congress plan in 1978
**Target:** 4.4% → **Achieved: 4.8%**

### Rolling Plan (1978–80)
Janata government's alternative — no fixed five-year target; plans revised annually based on conditions

### 6th Plan (1980–85)
Two competing 6th Plans: Janata's (1978–83) and Congress's (post-1980 return)
**Theme:** Poverty alleviation, minimum needs
**Target:** 5.2% → **Achieved: 5.5%**
**Notable:** NABARD established (1982), IRDP (Integrated Rural Development Programme)

### 7th Plan (1985–90)
**Theme:** "Food, Work, Productivity" — Rajiv Gandhi era
**Notable:** Most successful plan — overshot targets significantly
**Target:** 5.0% → **Achieved: 6.0%** — landmark achievement

### Annual Plans (1990–92)
Political instability — VP Singh, Chandra Shekhar governments; 8th Plan delayed

### 8th Plan (1992–97)
**Theme:** Human development, privatisation, liberalisation
**Context:** 1991 LPG reforms; India opened up
**Approach:** Shift from state control to market; human development indicators added
**Target:** 5.6% → **Achieved: 6.7%** — second most successful

### 9th Plan (1997–2002)
**Theme:** "Growth with social justice and equity"
**Target:** 6.5% → **Achieved: 5.5%** — 1997 Asian financial crisis and political instability

### 10th Plan (2002–07)
**Theme:** "Doubling per capita income in 10 years"
**Innovation:** First plan with **Monitorable Targets** — specific goals for literacy, infant mortality, forest cover
**Target:** 8.0% → **Achieved: 7.6%** — close to target; India's growth story began

### 11th Plan (2007–12)
**Theme:** "Inclusive Growth" — Manmohan Singh era
**Fastest-growing plan period** — India briefly approached 9% growth
**Target:** 9.0% → **Achieved: ~8.0%**
**Global Financial Crisis (2008)** impacted second half

### 12th Plan (2012–17) — The Last Five-Year Plan
**Theme:** "Faster, Sustainable and More Inclusive Growth"
**Target:** 8.0% → **Achieved: ~6.7%** — fell short; structural challenges
**End of an era:** Planning Commission abolished in 2014; 12th Plan completed under NITI Aayog administration
**No 13th Plan** — Five-Year Plans discontinued

## Gadgil Formula

Named after **D.R. Gadgil** (Deputy Chairman of Planning Commission, 1967–71), the Gadgil Formula was the basis for allocating central plan assistance to states.

- Weighted criteria: population, per capita income, tax effort, special problems
- Favoured poorer states (giving them a larger share relative to size)
- Modified several times — "Gadgil-Mukherjee Formula" (1990s)

With the abolition of Planning Commission and plan expenditure category (2017), the Gadgil Formula is now defunct.

## NITI Aayog

**Full form:** National Institution for Transforming India
**Established:** **1 January 2015** (replaced Planning Commission with immediate effect)
**Announced by:** PM Narendra Modi

### Leadership
- **Chairperson:** Prime Minister (ex-officio) — same as Planning Commission
- **Vice-Chairperson:** Appointed by PM (e.g., Arvind Panagariya, Rajiv Kumar, Suman Bery)
- **CEO:** Appointed by PM; heads day-to-day operations (different post from VC)
- **Full-time Members:** Domain experts in health, agriculture, economy, etc.
- **Ex-officio Members:** Selected Union Ministers
- **Special Invitees:** State Ministers

### Governing Council
Replaces the NDC:
- PM + All State Chief Ministers + Lt. Governors of all UTs (including those without legislature)
- Meets periodically to discuss national priorities

### What NITI Aayog CANNOT Do
**NITI Aayog has NO power to allocate funds** — this is the single most important difference from the Planning Commission. Budget allocations go through the Ministry of Finance (Department of Expenditure). NITI Aayog recommends; Finance Ministry decides.

### Planning Commission vs NITI Aayog

| Feature | Planning Commission | NITI Aayog |
|---|---|---|
| **Established** | 1950 | 2015 |
| **Basis** | Executive resolution | Executive resolution |
| **Fund allocation** | **Yes** (key power) | **No** |
| **Approach** | Top-down; Centre dictates to states | Bottom-up; states as equal partners |
| **Nature** | Command-and-control | Advisory / Think tank |
| **Five-Year Plans** | Drafted them | Does not make Five-Year Plans |
| **National body** | National Development Council | Governing Council |

### Key NITI Aayog Initiatives
- **Aspirational Districts Programme:** 112 most backward districts given special focus; ranked monthly on health, education, water/sanitation indicators
- **Atal Innovation Mission (AIM):** promotes entrepreneurship and innovation; runs Atal Tinkering Labs in schools
- **SDG India Index:** ranks states on progress towards 17 Sustainable Development Goals
- **India Innovation Index:** ranks states by innovation ecosystem
- **15-Year Vision, 7-Year Strategy, 3-Year Action Agenda** (instead of Five-Year Plans)

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Planning Commission established 15 March 1950 by executive resolution** — NOT an Act of Parliament, NOT a constitutional body. This is the most common trap.
2. **Chairperson of Planning Commission = Prime Minister** (ex-officio) — but the **Deputy Chairman** was the effective working head (ranked Cabinet Minister).
3. **2nd Plan = Mahalanobis model** (PC Mahalanobis, ISI Kolkata) — heavy industry focus; Soviet-inspired.
4. **Steel plants of 2nd Plan: Bhilai (Soviet), Rourkela (West Germany), Durgapur (Britain)** — foreign collaborator for each plant is tested.
5. **3rd Plan failed** due to 1962 China War + 1965 Pakistan War + drought — triple blow.
6. **Plan Holiday (1966–69) = 3 Annual Plans** — not a "Zero Plan" or any other term.
7. **5th Plan terminated early by Janata government in 1978** — parties cancelling rival party plans is historically significant.
8. **7th Plan was most successful** — achieved 6% vs 5% target under Rajiv Gandhi.
9. **12th Plan (2012–17) was the last Five-Year Plan** — no 13th Plan was made; replaced by NITI Aayog framework.
10. **NITI Aayog established 1 January 2015** — date is tested; January 1, not March 15 (that was Planning Commission).
11. **NITI Aayog CANNOT allocate funds** — biggest functional difference from Planning Commission; Finance Ministry does the allocation.
12. **NITI Aayog CEO ≠ Vice Chairperson** — two separate positions; PM appoints both.
13. **Governing Council of NITI Aayog** = replaces NDC; includes all CMs + LGs of UTs.
14. **NDC established 1952** (2 years after Planning Commission, 1950) — both by executive resolution, neither constitutional.
15. **Aspirational Districts Programme = 112 districts** — poorest districts ranked monthly; PM-level initiative under NITI Aayog.
16. **Atal Innovation Mission (AIM) is under NITI Aayog** — not DST, not DPIIT; important for current affairs GK.
