# Fiscal Policy, Union Budget & Deficit Types (FRBM Act)

## The Government's Chequebook

Every household has a budget — income from salary, expenses on rent and food, and if there's a gap, a loan from the bank. A government runs the same logic, only at a scale of tens of lakh crores of rupees and with consequences for 1.4 billion people.

**Fiscal policy** is the government's use of its own spending and taxation to steer the economy. When private spending collapses during a recession, the government steps in to spend more — a stimulus. When the economy overheats, the government pulls back — austerity. The annual **Union Budget** is the formal statement of how the government plans to collect and spend money in the coming year.

For SSC, the key is mastering the deficit types (fiscal, revenue, primary, effective revenue) and the FRBM Act — these appear in almost every exam. The budget terminology (consolidated fund, vote on account, appropriation act) is equally high-yield.

---

## The Union Budget: Constitutional & Procedural Basics

**Constitutional basis:** Article 112 — the Union Budget is formally called the **Annual Financial Statement**, a statement of estimated receipts and expenditure for the financial year (1 April to 31 March).

**Who presents it:** Finance Minister in the Lok Sabha.

**When:** **1 February** since 2017 (changed from the last working day of February by Finance Minister Arun Jaitley). The earlier date gives ministries more time to operationalise budgetary allocations before the financial year begins.

**Railway Budget:** Merged with Union Budget in **2017** after 92 years of a separate Railway Budget (which existed since 1924, following the recommendation of the Acworth Committee).

**Plan vs Non-Plan distinction:** Abolished from **2017-18** budget. The new classification is purely **Revenue vs Capital**.

---

## The Budget's Two Halves

The budget has two halves, each with receipts and expenditure:

```
UNION BUDGET
├── Revenue Budget
│   ├── Revenue Receipts  (tax + non-tax)
│   └── Revenue Expenditure  (salaries, subsidies, interest payments)
│
└── Capital Budget
    ├── Capital Receipts  (borrowings, disinvestment)
    └── Capital Expenditure  (infrastructure, defence equipment, loans to states)
```

---

## Government Receipts

### Revenue Receipts (non-debt, non-repayable)

**Tax Revenue:**
- **Direct Taxes:** Income Tax, Corporate Tax (collected by CBDT under Ministry of Finance)
- **Indirect Taxes:** GST, Customs Duty, Central Excise (collected by CBIC)

**Non-Tax Revenue:**
- Interest received on loans given to states/PSUs
- Dividends from PSUs (e.g., RBI's dividend to government)
- Fees, fines, forfeitures
- External grants

### Capital Receipts (create a liability or reduce an asset)

| Capital Receipt | Nature |
|---|---|
| **Market Borrowings** | Government sells bonds/T-bills; largest source of capital receipts |
| **Small Savings** | PPF, NSC, KVP, Sukanya Samriddhi — collected via post offices/banks |
| **Disinvestment** | Government sells PSU equity stakes |
| **External Borrowings** | Loans from World Bank, ADB, IMF, bilateral |
| **Recovery of Loans** | Repayments by states/PSUs of earlier government loans |

---

## Government Expenditure

### Revenue Expenditure (does NOT create assets — consumed)
- Salaries and pensions of government employees
- Interest payments on government debt (single largest revenue expenditure item)
- Subsidies (food, fertiliser, fuel)
- Grants to states, UTs, autonomous bodies
- Defence operational costs (pay, maintenance)
- Police, judiciary operational costs

### Capital Expenditure (creates physical assets or reduces liabilities)
- Roads, bridges, railways, ports, airports (infrastructure)
- Defence equipment, aircraft, ships (capital procurement)
- Loans given to states and PSUs
- Repayment of borrowings (principal)

**Why the distinction matters:**
Revenue expenditure = money spent and gone (no lasting asset). Capital expenditure = money that creates something durable. A government that runs a large **revenue deficit** is borrowing to pay salaries and subsidies — economically problematic. A government borrowing for **capital expenditure** (roads, railways) is investing in productive capacity.

## The Three Public Funds

| Fund | Constitutional Article | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Consolidated Fund of India** | **Article 266** | All revenue receipts + loan proceeds go in; all expenditure drawn from here. Requires Parliamentary approval (via Appropriation Act). Most important. |
| **Contingency Fund of India** | **Article 267** | Emergency fund (₹500 crore) operated by the President. No prior Parliamentary approval needed; to be ratified later. |
| **Public Account of India** | **Article 266** | Government acts as banker/trustee — Provident Fund, small savings, postal deposits. Parliament's prior approval not required. |

**Mnemonic: 266, 267, 266** — Consolidated & Public Account share Article 266; Contingency Fund alone has 267.

## Types of Budget Deficits

This is the highest-yield area in SSC economy questions. Master each deficit type precisely.

---

### 1. Revenue Deficit

**Revenue Deficit = Revenue Expenditure − Revenue Receipts**

When the government's regular (day-to-day) spending exceeds its regular income.

- Indicates the government is borrowing to meet consumption needs (salaries, subsidies, interest) — not investment.
- A persistent revenue deficit is fiscally unhealthy — you are taking loans just to meet daily expenses, not to build assets.
- Revenue deficit = 0 → government's regular income covers all regular expenses.

---

### 2. Fiscal Deficit

**Fiscal Deficit = Total Expenditure − Total Receipts (excluding borrowings)**

OR equivalently:
**Fiscal Deficit = Revenue Deficit + Capital Expenditure − Non-Debt Capital Receipts**

The **most important and most discussed deficit.**

- Represents the **total borrowing requirement** of the government in a year.
- Financed primarily through: market borrowings (selling G-secs), small savings, external borrowings.
- Expressed as % of GDP — the internationally comparable benchmark.
- Higher fiscal deficit = more government borrowing = **crowds out private investment** (government competes for the same pool of savings, driving up interest rates).
- Fiscal deficit also signals inflation risk — large deficits can lead to monetisation (money printing).

**Current FRBM target for fiscal deficit: 3% of GDP**

---

### 3. Primary Deficit

**Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit − Interest Payments**

Strips out the interest burden on past debt to show whether the government's **current operations** are in deficit.

- If **Primary Deficit = 0**: the government is only borrowing to pay interest on old debt; its current spending (excluding interest) is covered by revenues.
- If **Primary Deficit > 0**: even before interest, the government is spending more than it earns — a deeper fiscal problem.
- Primary deficit reveals whether the government's current policy choices (not inherited debt) are fiscally disciplined.

---

### 4. Effective Revenue Deficit (ERD)

**Effective Revenue Deficit = Revenue Deficit − Grants for Capital Asset Creation**

Introduced in the **2011-12 Budget**.

The logic: some revenue expenditure (grants given to states for PMGSY — roads, PMAY — housing) actually funds capital asset creation at the state level. These grants are technically "revenue expenditure" in the Centre's books but fund durable assets. Deducting these grants gives a more realistic picture of the true "consumptive" deficit.

---

### Summary Table — Deficit Types

| Deficit | Formula | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|
| **Revenue Deficit** | Revenue Exp − Revenue Receipts | Day-to-day income vs spending gap |
| **Fiscal Deficit** | Total Exp − Total Non-Borrowing Receipts | Total borrowing requirement |
| **Primary Deficit** | Fiscal Deficit − Interest Payments | Current-year operational deficit (excluding debt burden) |
| **Effective Revenue Deficit** | Revenue Deficit − Grants for Capital Assets | True consumptive gap (excludes capital-creating grants) |

**Relationship:**
Effective Revenue Deficit < Revenue Deficit < Fiscal Deficit

## FRBM Act, 2003

**Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management Act, 2003** — India's law to put fiscal discipline on a legal footing.

**Core mandate:**
- Reduce **fiscal deficit to 3% of GDP**
- Eliminate **revenue deficit** (bring it to zero)
- Government cannot borrow directly from RBI by issuing **ad hoc Treasury Bills** (monetisation of deficit prohibited)

**History of targets (repeatedly revised):**
- Original target: 3% fiscal deficit + zero revenue deficit by 2008-09
- Derailed by 2008 global financial crisis → targets relaxed
- NK Singh Committee (2016-17): recommended replacing the fiscal deficit target with a **Debt/GDP ratio target** (60% for Centre + States combined; 40% for Centre alone) and allowing "escape clauses" — relaxing targets by 0.5% during national security threats, natural disasters, or agrarian crises.
- COVID-19 (2020-21): fiscal deficit touched ~9% of GDP — FRBM targets suspended.
- Current trajectory: glide path back toward 3%.

**FRBM's most important rule for SSC:** After FRBM, the RBI cannot directly purchase Government bonds in the primary market (no direct monetisation). Government must borrow from markets. Exception: a temporary emergency window (the WMA — Ways and Means Advances).

## Key Budget Terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Appropriation Act** | Law passed by Parliament giving legal sanction to withdraw funds from the Consolidated Fund |
| **Finance Bill** | Contains proposals for new taxes and changes to existing taxes; must be passed as a Money Bill |
| **Vote on Account** | Interim budget approval (typically 2 months) to allow government spending before the full budget is passed — used in election years |
| **Guillotine** | Voting all remaining budget demands together without debate when time runs out in Parliament |
| **Disinvestment** | Sale of government's equity stake in PSUs; strategic disinvestment = sale of majority + management control |
| **Zero-Based Budgeting** | Every expense must be justified from scratch each year (not just last year's + increment) |
| **Outcome Budget** | Links expenditure to outcomes/results (introduced 2005-06) |

## Fiscal Policy: Expansionary vs Contractionary

| Situation | Policy | Tools |
|---|---|---|
| **Recession / Slow growth** | Expansionary fiscal policy | Increase government spending; cut taxes; larger fiscal deficit acceptable |
| **Overheating / High inflation** | Contractionary fiscal policy | Reduce government spending; raise taxes; reduce fiscal deficit |

**Fiscal vs Monetary Policy:**
- **Fiscal policy** = government's tool (tax + spend)
- **Monetary policy** = RBI's tool (interest rates + money supply)
- Both can be used simultaneously — but they can also conflict (expansionary fiscal + tight monetary = mixed signals)

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Union Budget presented on 1 February** (since 2017) — previously last working day of February; date change by Arun Jaitley.
2. **Article 112 = Annual Financial Statement** — the constitutional name for the Union Budget. Not "Budget Act" or any other term.
3. **Railway Budget merged with Union Budget in 2017** — existed separately for 92 years (since 1924, Acworth Committee).
4. **Consolidated Fund of India = Article 266** — all revenues and loans go in; all expenditure from here; Parliament's approval mandatory.
5. **Contingency Fund = Article 267** — emergency fund, ₹500 crore; President can draw without prior Parliamentary approval; later ratified.
6. **Public Account of India = Article 266** — government acts as trustee; PF, small savings; Parliament approval not mandatory.
7. **Fiscal Deficit = Total Expenditure − Total Receipts (excluding borrowings)** — NOT just revenue deficit; it includes capital expenditure too.
8. **Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit − Interest Payments** — strips out inherited debt burden; shows current-year operational gap.
9. **Revenue Deficit = Revenue Expenditure − Revenue Receipts** — a revenue deficit means borrowing for day-to-day consumption.
10. **Effective Revenue Deficit (2011-12)** = Revenue Deficit minus grants for capital asset creation — the most recently introduced deficit concept.
11. **FRBM Act 2003 target: Fiscal Deficit = 3% of GDP** — and elimination of revenue deficit. Monetisation (ad hoc treasury bills) banned under FRBM.
12. **NK Singh Committee (2016-17)** recommended debt/GDP target + escape clauses to replace rigid FRBM fiscal deficit target.
13. **Largest single item of Revenue Expenditure = Interest Payments** — the government spends more on servicing old debt than on any other single category.
14. **Disinvestment = capital receipt** (reduces government's asset — its PSU equity); NOT a revenue receipt.
15. **Vote on Account ≠ Interim Budget** — Vote on Account only gets Parliament's approval to spend; Interim Budget has full budget details but before an election. In practice, the two terms are often used interchangeably in pre-election budgets.
16. **Fiscal deficit finances itself via market borrowings (G-secs)** — not by printing money (that would be monetisation, prohibited under FRBM). Higher fiscal deficit = more G-sec supply = higher interest rates if private demand doesn't match.
