# Inflation — Types, CPI vs WPI, GDP Deflator & Causes

## The Invisible Tax That Hits Everyone

Imagine you put ₹10,000 under your mattress in 2010. In 2024, you pull it out — still ₹10,000. But that ₹10,000 buys perhaps half of what it used to. The notes haven't changed, but money's power has shrunk. That shrinkage is **inflation** — a sustained rise in the general price level that erodes the purchasing power of every rupee you hold.

Economists call inflation the "cruelest tax" because it is not voted on, not legislated — it just happens. And it hits the poor and fixed-income earners hardest, while benefiting debtors and asset holders.

For SSC, inflation is tested from multiple angles: the different terms (stagflation, hyperinflation, disinflation), the price indices (CPI vs WPI vs GDP Deflator), the causes (demand-pull vs cost-push), and the measures to control it. Master these systematically.

---

## Key Definitions: The Inflation Family

| Term | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Inflation** | Sustained rise in general price level; fall in money's purchasing power | India's CPI inflation 2022: ~7% |
| **Deflation** | Sustained fall in price level | Great Depression (1930s); Japan (1990s) |
| **Disinflation** | Inflation rate falling (still positive, but slowing down) | India 2023: CPI fell from 7% to 5% |
| **Stagflation** | High inflation + high unemployment + low/stagnant growth | USA 1970s oil shock |
| **Reflation** | Deliberate policy to raise prices/growth after a deflationary recession | Post-COVID stimulus packages |
| **Hyperinflation** | Extremely rapid, out-of-control inflation (often >50%/month) | Weimar Germany 1923; Zimbabwe 2008 |
| **Skewflation** | Price rise concentrated in specific sectors, not economy-wide | Food inflation while manufactured goods stay stable |
| **Core Inflation** | Headline inflation minus food and fuel prices (strips out volatile components) | RBI's deeper indicator |
| **Headline Inflation** | Overall CPI-Combined figure (includes food and fuel) | The number reported in news |

**The "dis-" distinction is a classic SSC trap:**
- **Deflation** = prices actually falling (negative inflation rate)
- **Disinflation** = inflation rate falling but still positive (e.g., from 7% to 4%)

## Types of Inflation by Cause

### 1. Demand-Pull Inflation
**"Too much money chasing too few goods"**

When aggregate demand in the economy exceeds aggregate supply, buyers bid prices up. Classic causes:
- Government spending too much (fiscal stimulus)
- RBI keeping interest rates too low for too long (easy money)
- Strong export demand
- Rising household incomes

**Think of it as:** Post-COVID, people had savings but couldn't shop during lockdowns. When restrictions lifted, demand exploded — prices shot up worldwide.

### 2. Cost-Push Inflation
**"Higher input costs pushed into consumer prices"**

When the cost of producing goods rises — labour (wages), raw materials (steel, cotton), energy (oil) — producers pass costs on to consumers.

Classic example: **Oil price shocks**. When OPEC raised crude prices in 1973, everything that used energy got more expensive — transport, manufacturing, agriculture. Inflation hit without any rise in demand.

**India-specific:** A weak rupee → more expensive oil imports → petrol/diesel prices rise → all transport-dependent goods become costlier → inflation, even if domestic demand is flat.

### 3. Structural Inflation
Caused by supply-side bottlenecks — poor roads (food spoils before reaching market), insufficient cold storage, weak agricultural infrastructure. India's food inflation often has a structural component.

### 4. Imported Inflation
When global prices of imported commodities rise (crude oil, edible oils, fertilisers), the cost increase flows into the domestic economy.

## Price Indices: Measuring Inflation

India has three major price indices. Each measures a different "layer" of prices in the economy.

---

### Consumer Price Index (CPI)

CPI measures price changes for a **fixed basket of goods and services** consumed by a typical household at **retail prices**.

**Base Year: 2012** (i.e., CPI = 100 in 2012; if CPI = 150 today, prices have risen 50% since 2012)

**Published by:** Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation (MoSPI) — released monthly

**Variants:**

| Variant | Who It Covers | Published By |
|---|---|---|
| **CPI-Combined (CPI-C)** | Combined urban + rural; the headline measure | MoSPI |
| **CPI-Urban (CPI-U)** | Urban households | MoSPI |
| **CPI-Rural (CPI-R)** | Rural households | MoSPI |
| **CPI-IW** | Industrial Workers | Labour Bureau, Min. of Labour (Base: 2016) |
| **CPI-AL** | Agricultural Labourers | Labour Bureau (Base: 1986-87) |
| **CPI-RL** | Rural Labourers | Labour Bureau |

**CPI-Combined = the most important variant:**
- Used by RBI's MPC as the **benchmark for inflation targeting** (target: 4% ± 2%)
- When news says "India's retail inflation hit X%", it means CPI-C year-on-year

**CPI-IW** is also important:
- Used to calculate **Dearness Allowance (DA)** revisions for government employees
- Used for revising **minimum wages** for workers
- Base year 2016

**Composition of CPI-Combined (approximate weights):**

| Group | Weight (%) |
|---|---|
| Food & Beverages | ~45.86 |
| Pan, Tobacco & Intoxicants | ~2.38 |
| Clothing & Footwear | ~6.53 |
| Housing | ~10.07 |
| Fuel & Light | ~6.84 |
| Miscellaneous (health, transport, education) | ~28.32 |

**Key insight:** Food has ~46% weight in India's CPI — the highest of any category. This means a bad monsoon or vegetable price spike causes a large CPI jump even if all other prices are stable. That's India's structural inflation challenge.

---

### Wholesale Price Index (WPI)

WPI measures price changes at the **wholesale/producer level** — prices at which goods trade between firms, before reaching the retail consumer.

**Base Year: 2011-12**

**Published by:** Office of the Economic Adviser (OEA), Ministry of Commerce and Industry — monthly

**Composition:**

| Group | Weight (%) |
|---|---|
| Primary Articles (food, non-food crops, minerals) | ~22.62 |
| Fuel & Power (petrol, diesel, coal, electricity) | ~13.15 |
| Manufactured Products (steel, chemicals, textiles, etc.) | ~64.23 |

**Critically, WPI does NOT include services** — no education, healthcare, transport, retail. It only covers tradable goods.

---

### CPI vs WPI — The Most-Tested Comparison

| Feature | CPI | WPI |
|---|---|---|
| **Price level measured** | **Retail** (consumer pays) | **Wholesale** (producer/trader) |
| **Includes services?** | **Yes** (healthcare, education, transport) | **No** (goods only) |
| **Base year** | **2012** | **2011-12** |
| **Published by** | **MoSPI** | **OEA, Ministry of Commerce** |
| **Used for monetary policy?** | **Yes (CPI-C)** | No (used in trade contracts) |
| **Food weight** | ~46% (very high) | ~22% (lower) |
| **Released** | Monthly | Monthly |

---

### GDP Deflator

The most comprehensive price measure — covers **all** goods and services produced in the economy.

**Formula:**
```
GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100
```

- **Nominal GDP** = output measured at current year prices
- **Real GDP** = output measured at constant (base year) prices
- The ratio removes the "price effect" to show true output growth

**Key feature:** No fixed basket. Unlike CPI or WPI, the GDP Deflator automatically adjusts its coverage to whatever is being produced in the economy in that year.

**Not directly released** — derived from National Accounts Statistics published by MoSPI.

## Effects of Inflation

| Inflation → | Winners | Losers |
|---|---|---|
| **Debtors vs Creditors** | **Debtors win** — real value of their debt falls | Creditors lose — real value of what they're owed falls |
| **Fixed income** | Asset holders (land, gold, equity) | Pensioners, salaried with fixed pay |
| **Government** | Government gains — tax revenues rise; real value of debt falls | Citizens — purchasing power falls |
| **Savers** | Real estate holders | Cash savers — real value of savings erodes |

**Classic SSC fact:** Inflation benefits debtors and hurts creditors. This is because the borrower repays in "cheaper" rupees.

## Measures to Control Inflation

### Monetary Measures (RBI)
- **Raise Repo Rate** → credit becomes costlier → borrowing and spending fall → demand-pull inflation controlled
- **Raise CRR/SLR** → less money available to lend → money supply contracts
- **OMO — sell G-secs** → absorbs liquidity from banks → money supply falls

### Fiscal Measures (Government)
- **Reduce government expenditure** → less government demand-pull
- **Raise taxes** → reduce disposable income → demand falls
- **Reduce fiscal deficit** → less money creation through government borrowing

### Supply-Side Measures
- **Import duty reduction** on essential commodities (e.g., edible oil, pulses) during supply crises
- **Release from buffer stocks** (food grain from FCI warehouses) into markets
- **Improve agricultural infrastructure** — cold chains, irrigation, road connectivity

## Phillips Curve

The **Phillips Curve** shows an inverse relationship between **inflation and unemployment**:
- Low unemployment → workers demand higher wages → costs rise → inflation goes up
- High unemployment → wage pressure falls → inflation falls

**In short run** this trade-off holds. But the **1970s stagflation** — high inflation AND high unemployment simultaneously — broke the curve. Economists then understood that the long-run Phillips Curve is vertical (no trade-off; monetary policy can't permanently lower unemployment).

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Deflation ≠ Disinflation** — deflation = prices actually falling; disinflation = inflation rate falling but still positive.
2. **Stagflation = inflation + stagnation** (high inflation + high unemployment + low growth) — 1970s USA oil shock is the classic case.
3. **CPI base year = 2012; WPI base year = 2011-12** — both are "2012-ish" but technically different; WPI's base is stated as a financial year.
4. **CPI is published by MoSPI; WPI by Office of Economic Adviser (Ministry of Commerce)** — different ministries for different indices.
5. **CPI-Combined = RBI's monetary policy benchmark** — when the MPC targets 4% ± 2%, it means CPI-C, NOT WPI.
6. **CPI includes services; WPI does not** — this is the single biggest structural difference between them.
7. **Food weight in CPI ≈ 46%** — highest category; why food inflation dominates India's headline CPI.
8. **WPI: Manufactured products = 64.23% (highest weight)** — primary articles and fuel together make up only ~36%.
9. **GDP Deflator = Nominal GDP / Real GDP × 100** — most comprehensive price measure; covers entire economy.
10. **Demand-pull = excess demand; cost-push = rising input costs** — causes differ; cures differ.
11. **Inflation benefits debtors; hurts creditors** — real value of loans falls with inflation.
12. **CPI-IW base year = 2016** (not 2012) — used for DA calculation; published by Labour Bureau.
13. **Core inflation = headline inflation minus food and fuel** — removes volatile components for long-term trend.
14. **Hyperinflation** examples: Weimar Germany (1923), Zimbabwe (2008) — not India's experience but SSC GK asks for examples.
15. **RBI fights inflation via Repo Rate hike** (monetary route) while government fights via cutting expenditure (fiscal route) — tools match the authority.
16. **Reflation ≠ inflation** — reflation is a deliberate policy choice to boost prices/growth after deflation; inflation is unintended and sustained.
