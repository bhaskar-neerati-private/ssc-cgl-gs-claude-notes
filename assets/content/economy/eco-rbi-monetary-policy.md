# RBI — Functions, Structure & Monetary Policy Tools

## The Engine Room of India's Economy

The Reserve Bank of India is not just another bank. It is the institution that decides how much money exists in India, what it costs to borrow it, and whether banks are stable enough to hold your savings. Every interest rate you pay on a home loan, every rupee in circulation, every forex reserve protecting India's economy — all flow through RBI.

---

## History and Constitution

| Fact | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Established** | **1 April 1935** under the Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934 |
| **Initially** | A private shareholders' bank (not government-owned) |
| **Nationalised** | **1 January 1949** under the RBI (Transfer to Public Ownership) Act, 1948 |
| **Headquarters** | **Mumbai** (shifted from Kolkata in 1937) |
| **Motto** | *"विश्वस्य् कल्याण"* — "For the welfare of all" |

## Functions of RBI

### 1. Monopoly of Note Issue

RBI has the **sole right to issue currency notes** in India — with one important exception:

- **₹1 coin and ₹1 note** = issued by the **Ministry of Finance** (Government of India); signed by the **Finance Secretary**
- **All other denominations (₹2, ₹5, ₹10, ₹20, ₹50, ₹100, ₹200, ₹500, ₹2,000)** = issued by **RBI**; signed by the **RBI Governor**

**Minimum Reserve System (since 1956):**
RBI must maintain a minimum reserve of ₹200 crore (₹115 crore in gold + ₹85 crore in foreign currency) against which it can issue unlimited notes. Before 1956, India used the Proportional Reserve System (40% gold/forex required against notes issued) — changed because rigid reserves constrained currency supply.

### 2. Banker to the Government

- Maintains accounts of Central and State governments
- Provides **Ways and Means Advances (WMA)** — short-term overdrafts when government revenue falls short
- Manages **public debt** on government's behalf (issues and redeems G-secs)
- Acts as **fiscal agent** in international transactions

### 3. Banker's Bank and Lender of Last Resort

- Holds reserves (CRR) of all scheduled commercial banks
- Provides **emergency liquidity** to banks facing crises — the "Lender of Last Resort" function prevents bank runs from cascading into systemic collapse
- Clears and settles inter-bank transactions

### 4. Controller of Credit (Monetary Authority)

Uses tools (detailed below) to regulate the volume and cost of credit — the core of monetary policy.

### 5. Custodian of Foreign Exchange Reserves

- Manages India's **foreign exchange reserves** (forex)
- India's forex reserves (2024): ~$620–650 billion — among the world's **top 5**
- Intervenes in the forex market (buying/selling dollars) to prevent excessive volatility in the rupee exchange rate

### 6. Regulatory and Supervisory Authority

RBI regulates:
- Scheduled commercial banks (public, private, foreign)
- Regional Rural Banks (RRBs)
- Cooperative banks (partially)
- Non-Banking Financial Companies (NBFCs)
- Payment Banks, Small Finance Banks

RBI does **NOT** directly regulate:
- Insurance companies → **IRDAI** (Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India)
- Stock markets/mutual funds → **SEBI** (Securities and Exchange Board of India)
- Pension funds → **PFRDA** (Pension Fund Regulatory and Development Authority)

## Governance Structure

**Governor** — appointed by the Central Government; heads the Central Board of Directors

**Deputy Governors** — maximum 4 (usually 3–4 in office simultaneously); each oversees specific departments

**Monetary Policy Committee (MPC):**
- **6 members:** 3 from RBI (Governor chairs; Deputy Governor; one RBI officer) + 3 external members appointed by Central Government
- Decides the **Repo Rate** — India's key policy interest rate
- Meets every 2 months (6 times a year)
- Decisions by **majority vote**; Governor has **casting vote** in a tie
- Established under the amended RBI Act in **2016** — a landmark reform making monetary policy a committee decision rather than the Governor's alone

## Monetary Policy Tools

Monetary policy = RBI's actions to regulate money supply, credit, and interest rates to achieve **price stability (control inflation)** and **support economic growth**.

**Inflation Target (since 2016):** CPI inflation of **4%**, within a band of **±2%** (i.e., acceptable range: 2% to 6%). If CPI stays outside 2–6% for three consecutive quarters, RBI must submit a written explanation to the government.

### Quantitative Instruments — Control Overall Credit Volume

#### Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR)

Every scheduled commercial bank must maintain a certain percentage of its **Net Demand and Time Liabilities (NDTL)** as **cash with RBI**.

- **NDTL** = Current deposits (demand) + Fixed/savings deposits (time)
- Banks earn **zero interest** on CRR balances held with RBI
- RBI can use CRR to quickly control money supply

| CRR Direction | Effect |
|---|---|
| **↑ CRR (raised)** | Banks must keep more with RBI → less money to lend → credit contracts → inflation falls |
| **↓ CRR (lowered)** | Banks keep less with RBI → more money to lend → credit expands → growth stimulated |

**Current CRR:** ~4% (subject to change by MPC)

#### Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR)

Every scheduled bank must maintain a certain % of NDTL in **liquid assets** — cash, gold, or **approved government securities (G-secs)**.

- Banks hold SLR in their **own vaults/accounts** (not with RBI)
- Banks earn returns on G-secs (unlike CRR); G-secs pay the government's interest coupon
- SLR ensures banks always have liquid assets to meet sudden withdrawals

**CRR vs SLR — the most tested distinction:**

| Feature | CRR | SLR |
|---|---|---|
| Where held | **Cash with RBI** | **G-secs/gold/cash, by bank itself** |
| Interest earned? | **No (zero)** | **Yes (on G-secs)** |
| Asset type | Cash only | Cash, gold, or approved securities |

**Current SLR:** ~18% (subject to change)

#### Repo Rate

**"Repo" = Repurchase Agreement**

When a commercial bank needs short-term funds, it comes to RBI, gives G-secs as collateral, and receives rupees — with an agreement to buy back the G-secs the next day.

**Repo Rate = the interest rate RBI charges for this overnight lending.**

- **Higher Repo Rate** → borrowing from RBI costlier → banks raise their own lending rates → home loans, business loans get expensive → borrowing falls → spending falls → inflation cools
- **Lower Repo Rate** → cheaper borrowing from RBI → banks pass on lower rates to customers → borrowing rises → investment and spending increases → growth stimulated

**Repo Rate is the KEY benchmark policy rate** — it is the primary signal of RBI's monetary stance. All other rates are derived from it.

**Current Repo Rate:** ~6.5% (subject to MPC decisions)

#### Reverse Repo Rate

**RBI borrows from banks** (banks park excess funds with RBI overnight in exchange for G-secs).

**Reverse Repo Rate = interest RBI pays to banks for this deposit.**

- **Higher RRR** → parking with RBI earns more → banks prefer parking over lending → less money in market → contracts credit
- **Lower RRR** → parking with RBI earns less → banks prefer to lend commercially → more credit in market

**Relationship:** Reverse Repo Rate = **Repo Rate minus 25 basis points (0.25%)**

#### Standing Deposit Facility (SDF) ← New (April 2022)

Introduced in April 2022, **SDF replaced the Reverse Repo Rate** as the operational floor of the rate corridor.

- Banks deposit funds with RBI **without providing any collateral** (unlike reverse repo which involves G-secs)
- SDF Rate = Repo Rate − 25 bp (same as reverse repo rate numerically, but no collateral needed)
- This gives RBI more flexibility to absorb excess liquidity

#### Marginal Standing Facility (MSF) ← Introduced 2011

An emergency borrowing window for banks — they can borrow from RBI overnight even beyond their SLR requirements (by pledging G-secs that were part of SLR holdings, up to a limit of 1% of NDTL).

- **MSF Rate = Repo Rate + 25 basis points (0.25%)** — more expensive than repo
- Safety valve for banks in acute short-term liquidity emergencies

#### The Interest Rate Corridor

```
MSF Rate  (Repo + 0.25%)  ← ceiling — most expensive RBI window
      |
 Repo Rate                 ← benchmark — main policy rate
      |
SDF Rate  (Repo − 0.25%)  ← floor — cheapest; banks park with RBI
```

All three rates move together when MPC changes the Repo Rate.

#### Bank Rate

- RBI's **long-term lending rate** to banks (as opposed to overnight repo)
- Now primarily used as the **penal rate**: banks that fail to maintain CRR or SLR pay a penalty at the Bank Rate
- Currently aligned with **MSF Rate** (Repo + 25 bp)

#### Open Market Operations (OMO)

RBI buys or sells **government securities in the open market** to manage long-term liquidity:

| RBI Action | Effect on Liquidity | Effect on Economy |
|---|---|---|
| **Buys G-secs from banks** | Injects rupees into banking system | **Expansionary** — increases money supply |
| **Sells G-secs to banks** | Absorbs rupees from banking system | **Contractionary** — reduces money supply |

OMO is RBI's most powerful tool for structural (long-term) liquidity management.

### Qualitative Instruments — Direct Credit Towards or Away From Specific Sectors

#### Selective Credit Controls (SCCs)
Direct RBI instructions to banks — e.g., restrict lending to commodity speculators; increase agricultural credit.

#### Margin Requirements
The % of loan value the borrower must contribute from their own pocket.
- **Higher margin** → bank lends less against the same collateral → credit contracts for that asset class

#### Moral Suasion
RBI persuades banks (through speeches, circulars, meetings) without using legal force. Non-binding but influential.

#### Priority Sector Lending (PSL)

RBI mandates banks to direct a share of their credit to socially important sectors:

| Category | PSL Target |
|---|---|
| **Total PSL** | **40% of Adjusted Net Bank Credit (ANBC)** for domestic scheduled commercial banks |
| **Agriculture** | **18% of ANBC** (of which 10% must go to small/marginal farmers) |
| **Micro enterprises** | **7.5% of ANBC** |
| **Weaker sections** | **12% of ANBC** |

Banks failing PSL targets must deposit the shortfall into **RIDF (Rural Infrastructure Development Fund)** at NABARD — at below-market interest rates, effectively penalising them.

**Priority Sectors include:** Agriculture, MSMEs, Housing (affordable), Education, Social infrastructure (water, sanitation), Export credit, Weaker sections, Renewable energy

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **RBI established 1 April 1935; nationalised 1 January 1949** — both dates tested; the 1935 date is the founding, 1949 is nationalisation.
2. **₹1 note/coin = Ministry of Finance (Finance Secretary's signature)** — all other denominations = RBI (Governor's signature). The ₹1 distinction is SSC's most repeated currency fact.
3. **CRR = cash kept WITH RBI; earns ZERO interest** · **SLR = liquid assets kept BY BANK ITSELF; G-secs earn interest** — both are % of NDTL.
4. **Repo Rate = RBI lends TO banks** (banks borrow from RBI); **Reverse Repo = RBI borrows FROM banks** (banks lend to RBI). Direction matters — reversed in trick questions.
5. **Higher Repo Rate = anti-inflationary** (contracts credit by making borrowing expensive); Lower Repo Rate = pro-growth.
6. **MSF Rate = Repo + 25 bp** (ceiling, most expensive); **SDF Rate = Repo − 25 bp** (floor, cheapest for parking).
7. **Bank Rate = penal rate** for CRR/SLR defaults; currently aligned with MSF rate; not the same as Repo Rate.
8. **OMO: RBI BUYING G-secs = injects liquidity** (expansionary); RBI SELLING G-secs = absorbs liquidity (contractionary) — direction is counterintuitive to some.
9. **MPC = 6 members: 3 RBI + 3 external; Governor has casting vote** — established under amended RBI Act 2016.
10. **Inflation target = 4% CPI ± 2% (band: 2%–6%)** — this flexible inflation targeting was adopted in 2016.
11. **Repo Rate = KEY benchmark policy rate** — not Bank Rate, not CRR, not SLR. Repo is the primary lever.
12. **RBI HQ = Mumbai** (moved from Kolkata in 1937) — "central office" is Mumbai; regional offices in major cities.
13. **RBI regulates commercial banks and NBFCs** — does NOT regulate insurance (IRDAI), stock markets (SEBI), pensions (PFRDA).
14. **PSL target = 40% of ANBC** for domestic banks; agriculture sub-target = 18%; micro enterprises = 7.5%; weaker sections = 12%.
15. **Lender of Last Resort = RBI's function** — provides emergency funds to solvent but illiquid banks during crises.
16. **SDF (Standing Deposit Facility) introduced April 2022** — replaced Reverse Repo as operational floor; key difference: NO collateral required from banks when parking funds with RBI under SDF.
