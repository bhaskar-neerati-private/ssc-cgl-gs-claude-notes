# Commercial Banks — Types, Nationalisation, RRBs & New-Age Banks

## Banking's Long Journey: From Calcutta Merchants to Digital Wallets

In 1770, a group of Calcutta merchants set up the **Bank of Hindustan** — India's first bank. It failed. But the impulse it represented — pooling deposits and lending them out — has never failed. Two and a half centuries later, India has over 100,000 bank branches, 12 public sector banks, hundreds of private banks, 43 Regional Rural Banks, and a new generation of Payment Banks running on mobile phones.

The story of Indian banking is really the story of three great upheavals: the **British consolidation** of three Presidency Banks into one imperial institution; **Indira Gandhi's nationalisation** of 14 banks in 1969 in a political earthquake; and the **post-1991 privatisation wave** that let private banks and foreign banks flood back in. Understanding these three waves unlocks everything SSC asks about banking.

## A Brief Timeline: India's Banking History

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| **1770** | Bank of Hindustan — India's first bank (failed 1832) |
| **1806** | Bank of Bengal (Calcutta) — first Presidency Bank |
| **1840** | Bank of Bombay — second Presidency Bank |
| **1843** | Bank of Madras — third Presidency Bank |
| **1921** | **Imperial Bank of India** — merger of all three Presidency Banks |
| **1935** | RBI established; took over currency and central banking from Imperial Bank |
| **1955** | **State Bank of India** — Imperial Bank nationalised under SBI Act 1955; RBI held 60% stake |
| **1959** | 8 former princely state banks became SBI Associates |
| **1969** | **14 banks nationalised** (deposits > ₹50 crore) — Indira Gandhi's landmark act |
| **1975** | Narasimham Committee → Regional Rural Banks concept |
| **1976** | **Regional Rural Banks Act** — RRBs established |
| **1980** | **6 more banks nationalised** (deposits > ₹200 crore) |
| **1991** | Liberalisation: private banks and foreign banks allowed back |
| **1993** | New-generation private banks licensed: ICICI, HDFC, Axis |
| **2015** | Payment Banks and Small Finance Banks licensed |
| **2019–20** | Mega bank mergers → PSBs reduced from 27 to 12 |

## Nationalisation: The Political Earthquake of 1969

Indira Gandhi, facing opposition from the old Congress right wing (the "Syndicate"), needed a populist masterstroke. On **19 July 1969**, she nationalised **14 major commercial banks** in a single stroke under the **Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act, 1969**.

The criterion: **deposits exceeding ₹50 crore**.

The rationale she gave: banking must serve the "aam aadmi" — credit was flowing to industry and commerce but bypassing agriculture and small business. Nationalisation would redirect it.

The 14 banks included: Central Bank of India, Bank of India, Punjab National Bank, Canara Bank, Dena Bank, United Bank of India, Syndicate Bank, Allahabad Bank, Indian Bank, Bank of Baroda, Union Bank of India, Bank of Maharashtra, Indian Overseas Bank, UCO Bank.

**Then in 1980**, six more banks were nationalised — this time the criterion was **deposits > ₹200 crore**. These included Andhra Bank, Corporation Bank, New Bank of India, Oriental Bank of Commerce, Punjab & Sind Bank, Vijaya Bank.

**Mnemonic for 1969 nationalisation:**
- **Year:** 1969 → 14 banks → ₹50 crore threshold
- **Year:** 1980 → 6 banks → ₹200 crore threshold
- Pattern: the threshold quadrupled; the number of banks halved.

## Current Bank Structure: 2019–20 Mega Mergers

The 27 public sector banks of 2017 were reduced to **12 PSBs** through a series of mergers announced in 2019–20, effective April 2020:

| Merged Into | Banks Merged |
|---|---|
| **Punjab National Bank** | Oriental Bank of Commerce + United Bank of India |
| **Canara Bank** | Syndicate Bank |
| **Union Bank of India** | Andhra Bank + Corporation Bank |
| **Indian Bank** | Allahabad Bank |
| **Bank of Baroda** | Vijaya Bank + Dena Bank (earlier, 2019) |

**State Bank of India** earlier absorbed all 5 SBI Associate Banks (2017).

**Current 12 PSBs:** SBI, PNB, Bank of Baroda, Canara Bank, Union Bank, Indian Bank, Bank of India, Central Bank, UCO Bank, Bank of Maharashtra, Punjab & Sind Bank, Indian Overseas Bank.

## Types of Commercial Banks in India

### 1. Public Sector Banks (PSBs)
Government holds > 51% equity.
- 12 PSBs currently (post-merger)
- Largest: **State Bank of India** (also the largest bank in India overall)
- Regulated by: **RBI** (banking operations) + **Ministry of Finance** (ownership/governance)

### 2. Private Sector Banks
- **Old Private Banks:** Established before 1991 but survived — e.g., Federal Bank, Karur Vysya Bank, City Union Bank
- **New Private Banks:** Licensed after 1991 — ICICI Bank, HDFC Bank, Axis Bank, Kotak Mahindra Bank, Yes Bank, IDFC First Bank
- Largest private bank: **HDFC Bank** (now merged with HDFC Ltd)

### 3. Foreign Banks
- Operate through branches/subsidiaries in India
- Examples: Citibank, Standard Chartered, HSBC, Deutsche Bank
- Stricter RBI requirements for opening branches (must prioritise underbanked areas)

### 4. Regional Rural Banks (RRBs)
- Established under **RRB Act 1976**, based on **Narasimham Committee (1975)** recommendations
- Purpose: provide banking to rural/semi-urban areas neglected by mainstream banks
- **Ownership pattern: 50:15:35** = Central Govt : State Govt : Sponsor Bank
- Each RRB is "sponsored" by a PSB (e.g., Prathama UP Gramin Bank sponsored by Bank of Baroda)
- Serve their designated districts only; classified as scheduled commercial banks
- Currently: **~43 RRBs** (down from 196 in 2005 through amalgamation)
- Headquarters: district towns; operate in their state only

### 5. Small Finance Banks (SFBs)
Licensed by RBI 2015; began operations 2016–17.
- Purpose: serve unbanked/underbanked segments — small borrowers, micro enterprises, farmers
- Key requirements:
  - **75% of ANBC** must go to Priority Sector Lending (vs 40% for normal banks)
  - At least **25% of branches** in unbanked rural centres
  - Minimum **50% of loans** must be < ₹25 lakh
- Examples: AU Small Finance Bank, Equitas SFB, Ujjivan SFB, Jana SFB, ESAF SFB
- Can accept deposits AND give loans (unlike Payment Banks)

### 6. Payment Banks
Licensed by RBI 2015; revolutionary concept.
- Can **accept deposits up to ₹2 lakh** per customer (raised from ₹1 lakh)
- **CANNOT give loans or credit cards** — the defining restriction
- Can offer debit cards, net banking, mobile banking
- Must invest 75% of demand deposits in G-secs/T-bills (SLR requirement)
- Examples: **Airtel Payments Bank, India Post Payments Bank, Jio Payments Bank, NSDL Payments Bank, Fino Payments Bank**
- Note: **Paytm Payments Bank** — RBI cancelled its banking licence in 2024 for regulatory violations

## Deposit Insurance: DICGC

**Deposit Insurance and Credit Guarantee Corporation (DICGC)**
- A wholly-owned subsidiary of **RBI**
- Insures bank deposits against bank failure
- Coverage: **₹5 lakh per depositor per bank** (raised from ₹1 lakh in February 2020)
- Covers: savings, current, fixed, recurring deposits
- Does NOT cover: deposits of state/central government, inter-bank deposits, deposits abroad

**The ₹5 lakh limit:** If your bank fails, DICGC pays up to ₹5 lakh of your combined deposits at that bank. If you have ₹10 lakh in one bank, you lose ₹5 lakh. But if you have ₹5 lakh each in two different banks, both are fully insured.

## Non-Performing Assets (NPAs)

An asset (loan given by bank) becomes **Non-Performing** when interest/principal is overdue for **more than 90 days**.

**Classification:**
- **Substandard Asset:** NPA for < 12 months
- **Doubtful Asset:** NPA for > 12 months
- **Loss Asset:** uncollectable (must be written off)

**Key Law — SARFAESI Act 2002:**
Securitisation and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of Security Interest Act. Allows banks to seize and auction collateral of defaulting borrowers **without court intervention** — a powerful recovery tool.

**Asset Reconstruction Companies (ARCs):** Buy bad loans from banks at a discount; try to recover them. RBI-regulated.

## Lead Bank Scheme

Introduced in **1969** (coinciding with nationalisation) based on the **Gadgil Study Group** recommendations.

- Each district in India assigned to a **Lead Bank** — typically a PSB
- Lead bank coordinates credit planning for all banks in the district
- Acts as "leader" for district-level banking outreach and rural credit

## Credit Creation: How Banks Multiply Money

Banks create money through **fractional reserve banking**. When you deposit ₹100:
- Bank keeps ₹4 (CRR at 4%) with RBI + some as SLR
- Lends out ~₹76 to someone else
- That person deposits ₹76 in another bank → which lends ₹58 → and so on

**Money Multiplier = 1 / CRR** (simplified). Higher CRR = lower multiplier = less credit creation.

## Basel Norms: Capital Adequacy

**Basel norms** (from the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision, BIS, Switzerland) set minimum capital requirements for banks worldwide to absorb losses.

| Norm | Year | Capital Adequacy Ratio |
|---|---|---|
| **Basel I** | 1988 | 8% minimum CAR |
| **Basel II** | 2004 | Risk-based approach |
| **Basel III** | 2010 (post-2008 crisis) | More stringent; stricter liquidity/leverage ratios |

**RBI mandates Indian banks to maintain CAR of minimum 9%** — higher than Basel's 8% minimum.

**Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR) = Capital / Risk-Weighted Assets × 100**

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Bank of Hindustan (1770) ≠ still exists** — it was India's first bank but closed in 1832. Don't confuse with later banks.
2. **Three Presidency Banks (1806, 1840, 1843) → Imperial Bank (1921) → SBI (1955)** — the lineage chain is heavily tested.
3. **SBI Act 1955 nationalised Imperial Bank** — this is separate from the 1969 nationalisation. SBI predates the 1969 wave.
4. **1969: 14 banks nationalised; threshold = ₹50 crore deposits; date = 19 July 1969** — all three facts tested together.
5. **1980: 6 more banks; threshold = ₹200 crore deposits** — don't swap the 50/200 crore figures between 1969 and 1980.
6. **RRB ownership: 50:15:35 = Centre:State:Sponsor Bank** — this exact ratio is frequently tested; don't say 60:20:20 or other wrong ratios.
7. **RRB Act 1976; Narasimham Committee 1975** — committee preceded the act by one year.
8. **Payment Banks CANNOT give loans or issue credit cards** — this is their defining restriction. They can accept deposits (up to ₹2 lakh) but lending is prohibited.
9. **DICGC insures ₹5 lakh per depositor per bank** — raised from ₹1 lakh in 2020. "Per bank" means if you have accounts in two banks, both are separately insured.
10. **DICGC is a wholly-owned subsidiary of RBI** — not a government body, not SEBI's subsidiary.
11. **NPA = loan overdue > 90 days** — the 90-day criterion is the technical definition.
12. **SARFAESI Act 2002 = banks can seize collateral without court order** — purpose and year both tested.
13. **Lead Bank Scheme = 1969 (same year as nationalisation)** — each district has one lead bank for credit planning.
14. **Small Finance Banks: 75% PSL target** vs. normal banks' 40% — SFBs have almost double the PSL obligation.
15. **Current PSBs = 12 (post 2019–20 mergers)** — went from 27 PSBs in 2017 to 12 by 2020.
16. **Money Multiplier = 1/CRR** — higher CRR → less credit creation → less money multiplied through the system.
