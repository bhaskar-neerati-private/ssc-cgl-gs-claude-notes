# Basic Concepts — GDP, GNP, NDP, NNP, GVA & Sectors of Economy

## The Measurement Problem

Every government wants to answer one basic question: how much did our economy produce this year? The answer sounds simple — just add everything up. But "everything" is the problem. Add the value of wheat + the value of flour made from that wheat + the value of bread made from that flour, and you've counted the wheat three times.

Economists solved this with a set of precisely defined aggregates. Understanding what each one counts — and what it deliberately excludes — is the core of this lesson.

---

## The Kitchen Analogy

Think of the Indian economy as a vast kitchen with millions of cooks producing millions of dishes simultaneously.

- **GDP** = the total value of all finished dishes cooked **inside India's kitchen** in a year — regardless of whether the cook is Indian or a foreign visitor working here.
- **GNP** = the total value of all dishes cooked by **Indian cooks** — wherever they happen to be working, inside India or in another country's kitchen.

The distinction is **territory vs. nationality**.

---

## The Four Core Aggregates

| Aggregate | Formula | What it Counts | What it Excludes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **GDP** | (Measured directly) | All output within India's borders; Indian AND foreign producers in India | Output of Indians working abroad |
| **GNP** | GDP + NFIA | All output by Indian nationals; inside India AND abroad | Output of foreigners working in India |
| **NDP** | GDP − Depreciation | Domestic output, net of capital wear-and-tear | Depreciation; Indian production abroad |
| **NNP** | GNP − Depreciation | National output, net of capital wear-and-tear | Depreciation |

**NFIA = Net Factor Income from Abroad**
= Income earned by Indians working/investing abroad
**minus** Income earned by foreigners working/investing in India

- If NFIA > 0 (India earns more abroad than foreigners earn here): **GNP > GDP**
- If NFIA < 0 (foreigners earn more in India than Indians earn abroad): **GNP < GDP**

**Practical example:** An Indian software engineer working in Silicon Valley sends $3,000 home every month. This income counts in India's **GNP** (Indian national earning) but NOT in India's **GDP** (not produced inside India's territory).

---

## Depreciation — The Difference Between Gross and Net

A factory buys machinery worth ₹10 lakh. The machinery has a 10-year working life. Every year it "wears out" by ₹1 lakh — this is **depreciation** (also called Capital Consumption Allowance or Consumption of Fixed Capital).

**"Gross"** aggregates (GDP, GNP) **include** depreciation in their total.
**"Net"** aggregates (NDP, NNP) **subtract** depreciation.

Why do "Net" measures matter? Because the gross output partly just replaces what was used up — it doesn't represent a real addition to the nation's wealth. Net measures show true addition to productive capacity.

---

## The National Income Chain

This chain is the most important relationship to memorise:

```
GDP  (Gross Domestic Product — within India)
  + NFIA
= GNP  (Gross National Product — by Indian nationals)
  − Depreciation
= NNP at Market Price
  − Net Indirect Taxes  (Indirect Taxes − Subsidies)
= NNP at Factor Cost
= NATIONAL INCOME (NI)
```

**National Income (NI) = NNP at Factor Cost** — this exact equation appears directly or indirectly in SSC questions constantly.

---

## Factor Cost vs Market Price

When you pay ₹40 for a loaf of bread:
- The baker earns wages + profit = ₹35 (this is Factor Cost)
- The government collects GST = ₹5 (this is the indirect tax)

So: **Market Price = Factor Cost + Net Indirect Taxes**
And: **Factor Cost = Market Price − Net Indirect Taxes**

Where: **Net Indirect Taxes = Indirect Taxes − Subsidies**

| Perspective | Includes | Excludes |
|---|---|---|
| **Market Price (MP)** | Indirect taxes (GST, excise, customs) | Subsidies |
| **Factor Cost (FC)** | Subsidies (government support to producers) | Indirect taxes |

**When subsidies are large:** Factor cost can actually be *higher* than market price (because subsidies reduce what consumers pay below what producers receive — government makes up the difference).

---

## GVA — Gross Value Added

GVA solves the **double counting problem**.

Consider the bread chain:
- Farmer grows wheat → sells at ₹5/kg → GVA added: **₹5**
- Miller buys wheat at ₹5, grinds into flour → sells at ₹8 → GVA added: **₹3** (= 8 − 5)
- Baker buys flour at ₹8, bakes bread → sells at ₹15 → GVA added: **₹7** (= 15 − 8)
- **Total GVA = ₹5 + ₹3 + ₹7 = ₹15** (= final price of bread; no double counting)

**GVA = Value of Output − Intermediate Consumption**

At each stage, we only count the *new value added*, not the full output value.

**GDP and GVA relationship (India's current methodology):**

> **GDP at Market Price = GVA at Basic Prices + Net Taxes on Products**

India switched to GVA-based GDP estimation in **January 2015** when the new base year (2011-12) was adopted. The old method used the expenditure approach more directly.

---

## Nominal vs Real GDP

**Nominal GDP:** Measured at current year prices — rises automatically when prices rise, even if output doesn't.

**Real GDP:** Measured at constant base year prices — shows actual change in output after removing inflation.

**Example:** If India's Nominal GDP rose from ₹100 lakh crore to ₹110 lakh crore, but prices rose 8%, then Real GDP grew only by ~2%. The "GDP growth rate" you see in newspapers refers to **Real GDP growth**.

**GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100**

The GDP Deflator measures economy-wide price changes across ALL goods and services produced. This differs from the CPI, which tracks a fixed basket of consumer goods only.

---

## India's GDP Base Year: 2011-12

Every few years, India updates its **base year** — the reference year against which real GDP is measured.

| Base Year | Used From | Changed To |
|---|---|---|
| 1999-2000 | — | 2004-05 |
| **2004-05** | Earlier | Replaced in **January 2015** |
| **2011-12** | **January 2015** | **Currently in use** |

A base year revision recalibrates the economy to reflect its current structure — new industries (e.g., telecom, IT, organised retail) and new price levels. Base year revisions often significantly alter historical GDP estimates.

---

## Who Calculates India's GDP?

**MoSPI (Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation)**
- The apex statistical ministry
- Under it: **NSO (National Statistical Office)**

**NSO was formed in 2019** by merging:
- **CSO (Central Statistics Office)** — calculated GDP/national income
- **NSSO (National Sample Survey Organisation)** — conducted household surveys (consumption, poverty, employment)

Both old names still appear in SSC questions. CSO = GDP estimation; NSSO = surveys.

**RBI does NOT calculate GDP** — a common trap. RBI only uses GDP data published by MoSPI/NSO.

---

## Per Capita Income

**Per Capita Income = National Income / Total Population**

This divides the total national pie by the number of people — giving an average income per person, which is a much better indicator of living standards than the total GDP figure (a larger country will always have a higher GDP even if its citizens are poorer).

India's per capita income at current prices (FY 2022-23): approximately ₹1.97 lakh per year.

## Sectors of the Economy

Every economy organises its production into three sectors, and the balance between them changes as a country develops:

| Sector | Activities | India's Share of GDP (approx.) |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary** | Agriculture, forestry, fishing, mining, quarrying | **~15–18%** |
| **Secondary** | Manufacturing, construction, electricity, gas, water supply | **~25–28%** |
| **Tertiary (Services)** | Trade, transport, banking, IT, education, healthcare, government services | **~54–58%** |

**India is a services-led economy** — over half of GDP comes from services. This is unusual for a developing country. Most countries go through a **Primary → Secondary → Tertiary** progression (agriculture first, then manufacturing, then services). India largely "leapfrogged" heavy manufacturing and moved directly to services, driven by the post-1991 IT boom.

---

## Types of Economies

| Type | Who Owns Resources | Who Sets Prices | Global Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Capitalist / Market Economy** | Private individuals and firms | Market forces (demand and supply) | USA, UK, Germany |
| **Socialist / Command Economy** | State / government | Central planning committee | Former USSR, Cuba, North Korea |
| **Mixed Economy** | Both private and public sector | Combination of market + state regulation | India (traditional model), most countries today |

**India = Mixed Economy:** Both public sector enterprises (railways, defence, oil PSUs) and private enterprises coexist. The 1991 LPG (Liberalisation-Privatisation-Globalisation) reforms shifted India toward the market end of the mixed economy spectrum, but the state retains a major role in banking, defence, and infrastructure.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **GDP = within India's geographic boundary**; includes foreigners working in India; excludes Indians working abroad. **GNP = by Indian nationals**; includes Indians abroad; excludes foreigners in India. Territory vs. nationality.
2. **NNP at Factor Cost = National Income** — this precise equation. Not NDP at FC, not GNP. NNP_FC = NI.
3. **"Gross" = before depreciation; "Net" = after subtracting depreciation**. GDP > NDP; GNP > NNP (for the same economy, same year).
4. **Factor Cost = Market Price MINUS Net Indirect Taxes** (Taxes − Subsidies). If subsidies > taxes, FC > MP.
5. **National Income is SMALLER than GDP** — because NI = GDP + NFIA − Depreciation − Net Indirect Taxes (subtracting multiple items).
6. **India's current GDP base year = 2011-12** (adopted January 2015, replacing 2004-05).
7. **Real GDP = at constant prices; removes inflation**. GDP growth rate in the news = Real GDP growth, not nominal.
8. **GDP Deflator ≠ CPI**: deflator covers all goods and services in GDP; CPI covers a fixed consumer basket. Deflator = Nominal GDP / Real GDP × 100.
9. **GVA avoids double counting** by measuring only value added at each stage — not gross output.
10. **GDP = GVA at Basic Prices + Net Taxes on Products** — India's current methodology (since 2015).
11. **India's services sector = ~55% of GDP** — larger than primary (15-18%) and secondary (25-28%) combined; India is a service-led economy.
12. **Per Capita Income = National Income / Population** — better welfare measure than total GDP; a small rich country beats a large poor country on per capita.
13. **NFIA positive → GNP > GDP** (Indians earn more abroad than foreigners earn in India). For most developing countries, GNP < GDP (more foreigners earning within than nationals earning outside).
14. **MoSPI / NSO calculates GDP** — NOT the RBI. RBI is a monetary institution; GDP measurement is a statistical function.
15. **CSO and NSSO were merged into NSO in 2019** — questions may use old names; CSO = GDP; NSSO = surveys.
16. **India = Mixed Economy**; the "mixed" part is important — neither pure capitalism nor pure socialism. After 1991, more market-oriented but still mixed.
