# Poverty, Inequality, Unemployment & MGNREGS

## The Uncomfortable Arithmetic

In 2024, India is the world's fifth-largest economy by GDP. It is also home to more poor people than any other nation on Earth. This is the central contradiction of India's development story — aggregate growth coexisting with enormous deprivation at the base of the pyramid.

How you measure poverty determines what you see. Define the poverty line strictly (just enough calories to survive) and poverty looks lower. Define it broadly (calories plus healthcare, education, sanitation) and the numbers are much larger. India has wrestled with this methodological question for decades — through four major poverty line committees, each redefining who counts as poor.

For SSC, the key facts are: the poverty line committees (Alagh, Lakdawala, Tendulkar, Rangarajan), the types of unemployment (especially disguised unemployment), MGNREGS (the cornerstone rural employment law), and the inequality measures (Gini Coefficient, Lorenz Curve, HDI).

## Poverty: Measuring the Floor

### What Is a Poverty Line?

The **poverty line** is the minimum level of income or consumption expenditure considered necessary to meet basic needs. Households below the poverty line are "poor." Households above it are "non-poor."

India has used primarily **calorie-based** and **consumption expenditure-based** methods.

---

### Four Poverty Line Committees

| Committee | Year | Methodology | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Alagh Committee** | 1979 | Calorie norm: 2400 kcal/day (rural), 2100 kcal/day (urban) | India's first official poverty line |
| **Lakdawala Committee** | 1993 | State-specific poverty lines; linked to CPI | Used for most of 1990s–2000s |
| **Tendulkar Committee** | 2005 (report 2009) | Shifted from calories to broader consumption basket (food + education + health + clothing) | Poverty = 37.2% (2004-05); 21.9% (2011-12); set at ₹447/month (rural), ₹579/month (urban) at 2004-05 prices |
| **Rangarajan Committee** | 2012 (report 2014) | Higher consumption norms; found poverty higher than Tendulkar | Poverty = 29.5% (2011-12); ₹972/month rural, ₹1,407/month urban at 2011-12 prices |

**The trend:** Each successive committee found different poverty rates for the same period — the "right" poverty figure depends on which basket you use. No single number is universally accepted.

**Tendulkar approach** was used as the official methodology after 2009. NITI Aayog has since shifted toward the **Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI)**.

---

### Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI)

Going beyond income, the **MPI** measures deprivation across three dimensions and 10 indicators:

**3 Dimensions:**
1. **Health:** Nutrition; child and adult mortality
2. **Education:** Years of schooling; school attendance
3. **Standard of Living:** Cooking fuel, sanitation, drinking water, electricity, housing quality, assets

A person is **MPI poor** if deprived in **30% or more of the weighted indicators**.

- **UNDP Global MPI:** Published by UNDP/Oxford Poverty and Human Development Initiative (OPHI) — tracks 100+ countries
- **India National MPI:** Published by NITI Aayog (first in 2021; updated annually)

**NITI Aayog MPI 2023:** 11.28% of India's population is multidimensionally poor (2022–23) — a dramatic fall from 29.17% in 2013–14. About **24.82 crore people** lifted out of multidimensional poverty between 2013–14 and 2022–23.

---

### Poverty Concepts

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Absolute Poverty** | Below a fixed minimum standard (India's poverty line approach) |
| **Relative Poverty** | Bottom X% of income distribution (used in developed countries — always exists) |
| **Extreme Poverty** | World Bank threshold: < **$2.15/day** (PPP, 2017 prices); was $1.90/day earlier |
| **Poverty Trap** | Poor cannot invest to escape poverty — all resources consumed just surviving |

## Inequality Measures

### Gini Coefficient

The **Gini Coefficient** measures income/consumption inequality within a country.
- Range: **0 (perfect equality)** to **1 (perfect inequality)**
- A Gini of 0 = everyone has equal income; Gini of 1 = one person has everything
- **India's Gini:** approximately **0.35–0.37** (moderate inequality by global standards)
- High-inequality countries (South Africa, Brazil): Gini > 0.55
- Nordic countries: Gini ~0.25–0.30

### Lorenz Curve

The **Lorenz Curve** is the graphical basis for the Gini Coefficient.
- Plots cumulative % of income (y-axis) against cumulative % of population (x-axis)
- **Line of perfect equality:** 45-degree diagonal — bottom 40% earn exactly 40% of income
- **Actual Lorenz Curve:** bows below the diagonal — the farther it bows, the more unequal
- **Gini = Area between diagonal and Lorenz Curve / Total area below diagonal**

### Human Development Index (HDI)

Published by **UNDP** in the annual **Human Development Report**.

HDI = Composite index of three dimensions:
1. **Health:** Life expectancy at birth
2. **Education:** Mean years of schooling + Expected years of schooling
3. **Income:** GNI per capita (PPP, $)

**HDI Categories:**
- Very High HD: ≥ 0.800
- High HD: 0.700–0.799
- **Medium HD: 0.550–0.699** ← India is here
- Low HD: < 0.550

**India's HDI (2023 Report, 2022 data):**
- Value: **0.644** (Medium Human Development)
- Rank: **134th out of 193 countries**
- Top: Switzerland, Norway, Iceland (consistently high performers)
- India outperforms peers on income but lags on health and education indicators

## Types of Unemployment

### 1. Cyclical Unemployment
Caused by recessions — when economic activity contracts, businesses lay off workers. Cured by expansionary fiscal/monetary policy.

### 2. Frictional Unemployment
Workers between jobs — searching, relocating, or transitioning. Temporary and normal in a healthy economy. Every functioning economy has some frictional unemployment.

### 3. Structural Unemployment
**The most serious type.** Skills mismatch — when technology or economic structure changes and workers' skills become obsolete. A mill worker displaced by automation who doesn't have software skills is structurally unemployed. Long-term; requires retraining.

### 4. Seasonal Unemployment
Prevalent in **Indian agriculture** — workers are idle during off-season (between sowing and harvest). Millions of agricultural workers have no wage employment for months. MGNREGS was designed partly to address this.

### 5. Disguised Unemployment
**The most important type for SSC — specific to developing countries.**

When more people are employed on a task than are actually needed. Their **marginal productivity = zero** (removing them would not reduce total output).

**Classic Indian example:** A small farm that can productively use 4 workers may have 10 family members working on it. The extra 6 are "disguisedly unemployed" — they appear to be working but contribute nothing extra.

**Disguised unemployment is rampant in Indian agriculture** — it's why agriculture employs ~45% of the workforce but contributes only ~18% of GDP.

### 6. Educated Unemployment
Graduates who cannot find jobs matching their qualifications. A distinct Indian challenge — the education system produces graduates faster than the economy creates appropriate jobs.

### 7. Underemployment
Working below one's capacity — a graduate driving an auto; a doctor working part-time; someone working seasonally wanting full-time work.

## Measuring Unemployment: PLFS

**PLFS (Periodic Labour Force Survey):** Conducted by MoSPI (National Statistical Office); replaced the old NSSO Employment-Unemployment Survey from 2017–18.

**Key Indicators:**
| Indicator | Definition |
|---|---|
| **LFPR (Labour Force Participation Rate)** | % of working-age population that is either employed or seeking work |
| **WPR (Worker Population Ratio)** | % of working-age population that is employed |
| **UER (Unemployment Rate)** | % of the labour force that is unemployed and seeking work |

**PLFS 2022–23 Key Findings:**
- Unemployment Rate (Usual Status): **3.2%** (significant fall from 6% in 2017–18)
- Female LFPR has improved significantly — rising from ~23% to ~37% in rural areas
- However, much employment is in informal/self-employment (not salaried jobs)

## MGNREGS — The Right to Work Law

**Full Name:** Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Scheme
**Act:** National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (NREGA), 2005
**Renamed:** Mahatma Gandhi NREGA (MGNREGA/MGNREGS) in **2009** (birth centenary of Gandhi)

### The Core Guarantee

**100 days of unskilled manual wage employment per financial year per household** in rural areas — a **legal right** (the first time wage employment became a justiciable right in India; can be enforced in court if denied).

Extended to **150 days** in case of natural calamities (drought, flood).

### Key Provisions

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Work demand** | Adult member applies in writing to Gram Panchayat/Programme Officer |
| **Work provision deadline** | Within **15 days** of demand being registered |
| **If work not provided** | Unemployment allowance payable (1/4 of wage for first 30 days; 1/2 thereafter) |
| **Minimum wages** | State-specific agricultural wage rates; notified annually by Centre |
| **Women reservation** | At least **1/3 (33%) of beneficiaries must be women** |
| **Payment mode** | Bank/Post Office accounts (direct benefit transfer — eliminates middlemen) |
| **Social audit** | Mandatory Gram Sabha social audit of all works |
| **Administered by** | Ministry of Rural Development |

### Permissible Works Under MGNREGS

- Water conservation and harvesting (check dams, ponds)
- Drought proofing (plantation, afforestation)
- Irrigation canals
- Land development
- Rural connectivity (roads, bridges)
- Flood control
- Renovation of traditional water bodies
- Any other work notified by Centre/State

### Centre-State Cost Sharing

| Cost Component | Centre | State |
|---|---|---|
| Unskilled wage cost | **100%** | 0% |
| Material cost | **75%** | 25% |
| Administrative cost | **6% of total** | — |

### Impact of MGNREGS
- Over **15 crore households** work under MGNREGS annually
- Raised rural wage floors — even private employers have to pay competitive wages
- Enabled building of rural infrastructure (tens of thousands of water bodies, roads)
- Criticism: leakages, fake musters, asset quality concerns; works not linked to productivity

## Historical Poverty Alleviation Programmes

| Programme | Year | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **IRDP** (Integrated Rural Development Programme) | 1978 | Self-employment assets for rural poor |
| **TRYSEM** | 1979 | Training rural youth for self-employment |
| **JRY** (Jawahar Rozgar Yojana) | 1989 | Wage employment in rural areas |
| **PMRY** | 1993 | Self-employment for educated unemployed |
| **SGSY** (Swarnajayanti Gram Swarozgar Yojana) | 1999 | SHG-based self-employment (replaced IRDP) |
| **SGRY** (Sampoorna Grameen Rozgar Yojana) | 2001 | Wage + food employment in rural areas |
| **NREGA → MGNREGS** | 2005 → 2009 | Guaranteed wage employment; legal right |
| **DAY-NRLM** (Deendayal Antyodaya Yojana — National Rural Livelihoods Mission) | 2011 (relaunched) | SHG-based livelihoods (replaced SGSY) |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Alagh Committee 1979 = calorie-based** (2400 rural, 2100 urban); India's first official poverty line methodology.
2. **Tendulkar Committee 2005 (report 2009)** shifted from calories to consumption basket — official method 2009–2019; poverty 21.9% in 2011-12.
3. **Rangarajan Committee found HIGHER poverty than Tendulkar** — 29.5% vs 21.9% for 2011-12 — using higher consumption norms.
4. **MPI = 3 dimensions: Health, Education, Standard of Living** — 10 indicators; deprived in 30%+ = MPI poor.
5. **India's HDI rank = 134th; value = 0.644; category = Medium Human Development** — all three points tested.
6. **Gini Coefficient 0 = perfect equality; 1 = perfect inequality; India ≈ 0.35–0.37** — range and India's value both tested.
7. **Lorenz Curve: further from diagonal = more inequality; Gini = area between diagonal and curve** — graphical interpretation.
8. **Disguised unemployment: marginal productivity = zero; rampant in Indian agriculture** — definition and Indian context.
9. **Structural unemployment = skills mismatch due to technology/structural change** — most serious, long-term type.
10. **NREGA enacted 2005; renamed MGNREGS/MGNREGA in 2009** — both years tested; 2009 = Gandhi birth centenary.
11. **MGNREGS guarantees 100 days/year/household** — extended to 150 in natural calamity; per HOUSEHOLD, not per person.
12. **Work must be provided within 15 days of demand** — if not, unemployment allowance kicks in.
13. **MGNREGS: at least 1/3 beneficiaries must be women** — the 33% women reservation is a frequently tested number.
14. **MGNREGS wage cost: 100% borne by Centre; material cost: 75% Centre + 25% State** — cost-sharing ratio is tested.
15. **MGNREGS = legal right to work** — the first time wage employment became a justiciable right in India; can be enforced in court.
16. **PLFS = Periodic Labour Force Survey by MoSPI** — replaced old NSSO EUS from 2017-18; tracks LFPR, WPR, UER.
