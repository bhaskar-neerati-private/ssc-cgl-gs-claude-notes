# Development Finance — NABARD, SIDBI, NHB, Exim Bank & MUDRA

## The Banks Behind the Banks

A farmer in Vidarbha needs a crop loan. A garment workshop in Surat needs a machine. A young family in Jaipur needs a home loan. A software firm wants to export services to Europe. A tiny tea shop needs ₹30,000 to buy a refrigerator.

None of these will walk into ICICI Bank's corporate office and get a yes. They are too small, too risky, too far from formal financial circuits. This is the gap that **Development Finance Institutions (DFIs)** were built to fill.

DFIs are specialised financial institutions that channel long-term credit to priority sectors — agriculture, small industry, housing, exports, infrastructure — that commercial banks under-serve because of risk aversion, short-term funding constraints, or lack of specialised capacity. They don't compete with commercial banks; they complement and **refinance** them, creating pipelines from capital markets to the last mile.

India has five major DFIs, each with a domain: **NABARD** (agriculture and rural), **SIDBI** (MSMEs), **NHB** (housing), **Exim Bank** (exports and imports), and **MUDRA** (micro enterprises). Each is a statutory body with a specific Act, a specific mandate, and specific tools. For SSC, establishment years, HQs, parent Acts, and key schemes are all tested.

## NABARD — National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development

**Established:** 12 July 1982
**Act:** NABARD Act, 1981
**HQ:** Mumbai
**Based on recommendations of:** B. Sivaraman Committee (Committee to Review Arrangements for Institutional Credit for Agriculture and Rural Development — CRAFICARD)
**Ownership:** Government of India ~99.6%; RBI ~0.4% (RBI has progressively transferred its equity to GoI)

### What NABARD Does

**1. Apex Refinance Institution for Agriculture:**
NABARD provides refinance (lending to lenders) to:
- Commercial banks (for agricultural loans)
- Regional Rural Banks (RRBs)
- State Cooperative Banks / District Central Cooperative Banks

When a farmer borrows from a cooperative bank, that bank's loans are partly funded by cheap NABARD refinance. NABARD thus amplifies credit flow without directly lending to farmers.

**2. Supervises RRBs and Cooperative Banks:**
NABARD conducts inspections of all Regional Rural Banks and State Cooperative Banks — a regulatory function alongside its developmental role. RBI is the primary bank regulator, but NABARD handles these rural/cooperative institutions.

**3. Rural Infrastructure Development Fund (RIDF):**
Banks that fall short of Priority Sector Lending (PSL) targets must deposit the shortfall in RIDF, maintained with NABARD. NABARD uses this pool — at below-market interest rates — to fund rural infrastructure: village roads, minor irrigation, bridges, warehouses, rural health centres. RIDF = PSL shortfall → rural investment.

**4. Self Help Group — Bank Linkage Programme (SHG-BLP):**
World's largest microfinance programme (by outreach). NABARD promotes SHGs (groups of 10–20 rural women), links them to banks. Members save within the group; group takes a bank loan; individual members access credit at group rates. Over 1.4 crore SHGs linked to banks.

**5. Kisan Credit Card (KCC):**
Conceptualised by NABARD in 1998. A revolving credit facility — like a credit card for farmers — allowing them to withdraw crop loans up to a sanctioned limit. Flexible repayment matching harvest cycles. Covers crop cultivation, post-harvest, maintenance, allied activities.

**6. Watershed Development:**
NABARD funds Watershed Development programmes — conserving rainfall, recharging groundwater, preventing soil erosion in drought-prone areas.

## SIDBI — Small Industries Development Bank of India

**Established:** 2 April 1990
**Act:** SIDBI Act, 1989
**HQ:** Lucknow
**Originally:** A subsidiary of IDBI; now an independent statutory body
**Ownership:** Mix of public sector financial institutions (SBI, LIC, IDBI, etc.)

### What SIDBI Does

**Apex Institution for MSME Finance:**
SIDBI is the principal financial institution for promotion, financing, and development of Micro, Small, and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs).

**Refinance to Banks:**
SIDBI provides cheap refinance to banks and financial institutions that lend to MSMEs — making MSME loans more affordable.

**Direct Lending:**
For larger MSME projects needing equipment, technology, or expansion, SIDBI directly finances.

**Credit Guarantee Fund Trust for Micro and Small Enterprises (CGTMSE):**
Managed jointly by SIDBI and Ministry of MSME.
- Provides collateral-free guarantees for loans to micro and small enterprises up to **₹2 crore**
- Removes the biggest barrier for small entrepreneurs: lack of collateral
- Bank gives loan → CGTMSE guarantees up to 85% of the loan if borrower defaults → bank takes reduced risk → more credit to small enterprises

**Microfinance:**
SIDBI funds Microfinance Institutions (MFIs) that lend to Self Help Groups and individual borrowers in rural/urban informal sector. The chain: SIDBI → MFI → SHG/individual.

**MUDRA was set up as a SIDBI subsidiary** (in 2015) — the micro-enterprise-focused daughter institution.

## NHB — National Housing Bank

**Established:** 9 July 1988
**Act:** NHB Act, 1987
**HQ:** New Delhi
**Originally:** A wholly-owned subsidiary of RBI; in 2019, RBI transferred its stake to the Government of India

### What NHB Does

**Apex Institution for Housing Finance:**
NHB provides long-term refinance to Housing Finance Companies (HFCs) — HDFC (now merged with HDFC Bank), LIC Housing Finance, PNB Housing Finance, Can Fin Homes — enabling them to offer home loans.

**Regulation of Housing Finance Companies:**
Until 2019, NHB regulated HFCs. In 2019, regulation of HFCs was transferred to **RBI** (following NBFC/HFC regulatory issues). NHB retains its developmental and refinancing role.

**RESIDEX — National Housing Price Index:**
India's first and official residential property price index — tracks home prices across major cities. Used to monitor housing market trends.

**Affordable Housing:**
NHB supports PMAY (Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana — Housing for All) by channelling interest subsidy to banks/HFCs under the Credit Linked Subsidy Scheme (CLSS).

## Exim Bank — Export-Import Bank of India

**Established:** 1 January 1982
**Act:** Export-Import Bank of India Act, 1981
**HQ:** Mumbai
**Ownership:** 100% Government of India

### What Exim Bank Does

**Trade Finance:**
Exim Bank finances India's cross-border trade — exports AND imports:
- **Supplier's Credit:** Indian exporters get credit to supply goods to foreign buyers
- **Buyer's Credit:** Foreign buyers get credit from Exim Bank to buy Indian goods
- **Pre-shipment finance:** Helps exporters fund production before receiving export payment

**Lines of Credit (LoC) — India's Soft-Power Tool:**
Exim Bank's most distinctive function — extends concessional loans (Lines of Credit) to governments of developing countries to finance their purchase of Indian goods and services.

The logic: Country X (say, an African nation) needs to build a road. India's Exim Bank gives X a low-interest loan to buy Indian materials, hire Indian engineers, use Indian construction firms. X gets infrastructure; India gets business and influence.

Exim Bank manages the largest portfolio of Lines of Credit to Africa, Southeast Asia, and South Asia. This is India's foreign policy and development diplomacy instrument — distinct from commercial banking.

**Project Exports:**
Helps Indian companies execute turnkey projects abroad (dams, power plants, telecom networks).

**Forfaiting:**
Exim Bank buys medium-term export receivables from Indian exporters at a discount — the exporter gets immediate cash; Exim Bank waits to collect from the foreign buyer.

## MUDRA — Micro Units Development and Refinance Agency

**Established:** 8 April 2015 (launch of PM MUDRA Yojana)
**Act:** No separate Act — established as a company under the Companies Act
**HQ:** Mumbai
**Nature:** A subsidiary of SIDBI — not an independent apex bank
**Regulatory oversight:** RBI

### The Three Tiers: Shishu, Kishore, Tarun

MUDRA divides micro enterprise credit into three tiers based on enterprise stage:

| Product | Loan Amount | Stage |
|---|---|---|
| **Shishu** | Up to **₹50,000** | Startup / very early stage |
| **Kishore** | ₹50,001 to **₹5 lakh** | Growing / mid-stage |
| **Tarun** | ₹5 lakh to **₹10 lakh** | Established micro enterprise |
| **Tarun Plus** | ₹10 lakh to **₹20 lakh** | Expanded category (added later) |

**How MUDRA works:**
MUDRA doesn't directly lend to micro entrepreneurs. It provides **refinance** to banks, NBFCs, and Microfinance Institutions (MFIs) — which then lend under the PM MUDRA Yojana brand. **No collateral required** for MUDRA loans — this is the key feature removing the biggest barrier to micro-enterprise credit.

**Who benefits:** Street vendors, small shopkeepers, artisans, small food processors, tailors, repair shops, transport operators — the non-farm, non-corporate informal economy.

## NaBFID — The Newest DFI (2021)

**National Bank for Financing Infrastructure and Development**
**Established:** 2021 under NaBFID Act, 2021
**HQ:** Mumbai
**Purpose:** Fill the infrastructure financing gap left after IDBI's conversion into a commercial bank
**Focus:** Long-term debt financing for roads, railways, ports, airports, energy infrastructure
**Government holds majority stake**; can raise market borrowings at attractive rates (sovereign backing)

## Quick Reference: All Major DFIs

| DFI | Estd. | Act | HQ | Mandate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **NABARD** | 1982 | NABARD Act 1981 | Mumbai | Agriculture & rural development |
| **Exim Bank** | 1982 | Exim Bank Act 1981 | Mumbai | Export-import finance; Lines of Credit |
| **NHB** | 1988 | NHB Act 1987 | New Delhi | Housing finance |
| **SIDBI** | 1990 | SIDBI Act 1989 | Lucknow | MSME finance |
| **MUDRA** | 2015 | — (Companies Act) | Mumbai | Micro enterprise finance (SIDBI subsidiary) |
| **NaBFID** | 2021 | NaBFID Act 2021 | Mumbai | Infrastructure finance |

**Memory trick for order:** NABARD and Exim Bank born together (1982) → NHB (1988) → SIDBI (1990) → MUDRA (2015) → NaBFID (2021).

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **NABARD established 12 July 1982; Act: NABARD Act 1981; HQ: Mumbai** — the HQ-Mumbai is the trap (most assume it's Delhi or a rural area).
2. **NABARD set up on B. Sivaraman Committee recommendations** (CRAFICARD) — not Narasimham Committee (that was banking reforms).
3. **NABARD supervises RRBs and cooperative banks** — RBI is the ultimate regulator but NABARD does day-to-day supervision of these rural banking institutions.
4. **RIDF = Banks' PSL shortfall deposited with NABARD** → NABARD funds rural roads, irrigation, bridges — the circular loop of PSL compliance.
5. **Kisan Credit Card = NABARD's concept (1998)** — a revolving credit facility for farmers; not just a debit card or savings instrument.
6. **SIDBI HQ = Lucknow** — the only major DFI NOT in Mumbai or Delhi; Lucknow is the trap answer most miss.
7. **SIDBI Act 1989; established 2 April 1990** — act came one year before the institution's launch.
8. **CGTMSE = joint initiative of SIDBI + Ministry of MSME** — provides collateral-free guarantee up to ₹2 crore for micro/small enterprise loans.
9. **NHB HQ = New Delhi** (not Mumbai — common confusion since most finance institutions are in Mumbai).
10. **NHB regulation of HFCs transferred to RBI in 2019** — NHB still refinances HFCs but no longer regulates them; RBI took over HFC regulation.
11. **RESIDEX = NHB's residential property price index** — India's first housing price index.
12. **Exim Bank = 100% Government of India** (unlike NABARD where RBI also holds a small share) — fully government-owned.
13. **Lines of Credit = Exim Bank's foreign policy tool** — concessional loans to developing countries to buy Indian goods/services.
14. **MUDRA = subsidiary of SIDBI** — NOT an independent bank; NOT an apex institution; set up under Companies Act (no separate MUDRA Act).
15. **MUDRA Shishu = ≤ ₹50,000; Kishore = ₹50,001–₹5 lakh; Tarun = ₹5–₹10 lakh** — three tiers + amounts are all tested; don't swap Shishu and Kishore.
16. **No collateral for MUDRA loans** — the defining feature enabling micro enterprises to access formal credit.
