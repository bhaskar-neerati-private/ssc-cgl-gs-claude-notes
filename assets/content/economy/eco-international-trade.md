# International Trade, Balance of Payments, FDI & SEZ

## Why Nations Trade

In 1817, David Ricardo published a simple but powerful idea: even if England could produce both cloth and wine more efficiently than Portugal, both countries would be better off if England specialised in cloth and Portugal in wine, then traded. The key was not **absolute** advantage (who is better overall) but **comparative** advantage (who gives up less by specialising).

This insight — that trade creates mutual gains even between unequal economies — underpins the entire modern global trading system. India exports IT services to the USA and imports crude oil from Saudi Arabia, not because India cannot produce oil or the USA cannot write software, but because specialisation and trade make both sides richer.

Understanding India's trade — the balance of payments, the current account deficit that haunts policymakers, the FDI flows that fund growth, and the SEZ experiment — is essential for SSC. The numbers, the terminology, and the 1991 crisis backstory all appear in exams.

---

## Theories of International Trade

| Theory | Economist | Core Idea |
|---|---|---|
| **Absolute Advantage** | Adam Smith | Export what you produce most efficiently |
| **Comparative Advantage** | David Ricardo | Export where your relative cost advantage is highest — even if you're absolutely less efficient |
| **Heckscher-Ohlin** | Heckscher & Ohlin | Countries export goods that use their most abundant factor (labour-rich India exports labour-intensive goods) |

**The Indian implication:** India has abundant labour → comparative advantage in labour-intensive manufacturing (textiles, leather, gems) and services (IT outsourcing) → should specialise in these.

## Balance of Payments (BoP)

The **Balance of Payments** is the comprehensive account of all economic transactions between India's residents and the rest of the world in a given period (usually a year).

Every international transaction — an export, an import, a foreign investment, a loan repayment, a student's remittance home — appears in the BoP. It has two major accounts:

---

### 1. Current Account

Records flows of goods, services, income, and transfers.

| Component | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| **Merchandise (Goods) Trade** | Physical exports and imports — oil, gems, machinery, textiles (also called "visible" trade) |
| **Services Trade** | IT services, tourism, education, financial services ("invisible" trade) |
| **Primary Income** | Wages earned abroad + investment income (dividends, interest on FDI/FII) |
| **Secondary Income** | **Remittances** from Indians abroad; government grants and transfers |

**Current Account Deficit (CAD):**
When imports of goods + services > exports of goods + services (plus net income and transfers).

India typically runs a **current account deficit** — we import more than we export (especially crude oil). CAD of 1–3% of GDP is considered manageable.

**India's CAD drivers:**
- **Crude oil:** India imports ~85% of its oil needs — a single commodity that dominates the import bill
- **Gold:** India's insatiable appetite for gold creates a persistent import demand
- **Electronics:** smartphones, computers, components — India imports heavily

**India's CAD cushions:**
- **Services exports:** India's IT, BPO, and professional services generate large forex inflows
- **Remittances:** India is consistently the **world's largest recipient of remittances** (~$100–120 billion per year)
  - Sources: USA, UAE, Saudi Arabia, UK, Canada, Kuwait
  - Highest-receiving states: Kerala, Uttar Pradesh, Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, Bihar

---

### 2. Capital and Financial Account

Records flows of financial assets — investments, loans, reserve movements.

| Component | Examples |
|---|---|
| **FDI (Foreign Direct Investment)** | Foreign company builds a factory in India; acquires 10%+ equity |
| **FPI (Foreign Portfolio Investment)** | Foreign investor buys Indian stocks/bonds (no management control) |
| **External Commercial Borrowings (ECBs)** | Indian companies borrow in foreign currency from overseas lenders |
| **NRI Deposits** | FCNR-B, NRE, NRO accounts — forex brought in by diaspora |
| **Official Reserves** | RBI's forex reserve changes |

---

### BoP Identity

**Current Account + Capital Account + Errors & Omissions = 0** (in principle)

- **BoP Surplus:** More inflows than outflows → **Forex reserves rise**
- **BoP Deficit:** More outflows than inflows → **Forex reserves fall**

India's forex reserves (~$620–650 billion in 2024) are among the world's top 5 — a buffer against external shocks.

---

### India's 1991 BoP Crisis

By mid-1991, India's **foreign exchange reserves had fallen to just 2 weeks' worth of imports** — the country was on the brink of defaulting on international payments.

The rescue: India pledged **67 tonnes of gold** with the Bank of England and Bank of Japan as collateral to secure an IMF emergency loan. This national humiliation became the catalyst for the 1991 LPG reforms — liberalisation, privatisation, globalisation (covered in Topic 14).

**Why 1991 happened:** Fiscal deficit → government borrowing → inflation → uncompetitive exports → trade deficit → depleting reserves → crisis.

## Exchange Rate

The exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another.

| System | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Fixed Rate** | Government/central bank pegs currency to another | Saudi Riyal pegged to USD |
| **Floating Rate** | Market forces (supply-demand) determine rate | UK Pound, Euro |
| **Managed Float** | Market determines rate; central bank intervenes to prevent excessive swings | **India's system** |

India uses a **managed float** — RBI lets the rupee fluctuate with market forces but buys/sells dollars to prevent extreme volatility that could destabilise trade and investment.

**Rupee depreciation:** Rupee weakens → exports become cheaper for foreigners (good for exporters) → imports become more expensive (bad for oil importers, bad for CAD). India's crude import bill surges when rupee depreciates.

## FDI — Foreign Direct Investment

**FDI** = a foreign entity acquires **10% or more equity** in an Indian company, typically with strategic/management control or long-term stake.

**Two routes into India:**

| Route | Description |
|---|---|
| **Automatic Route** | No prior government approval needed; most sectors; invest and notify RBI after |
| **Government Route** | Prior approval from Ministry/DPIIT needed; applies to sensitive sectors |

**DPIIT** (Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade, under Ministry of Commerce) is the nodal authority for FDI policy. FIPB (Foreign Investment Promotion Board) was abolished in 2017 — DPIIT/relevant Ministry now handles approvals.

**FDI Sector Caps (Key examples):**

| Sector | Permitted FDI |
|---|---|
| Manufacturing | 100% (automatic) |
| IT/Software | 100% (automatic) |
| Defence | 74% (automatic); beyond 74% via Government route |
| Insurance | 74% (automatic) |
| Print Media (Newspapers) | 26% (Government route) |
| Broadcasting | 49% |
| Multi-brand Retail | 51% (Government route, with conditions) |
| Atomic Energy | **Prohibited** |
| Lottery, Gambling | **Prohibited** |

**Top FDI Source Countries for India:** Mauritius, Singapore, USA, Netherlands, Japan
- **Why Mauritius?** Not because Mauritius companies actually invest — it's **treaty-shopping**: investors from USA, Europe route investments via Mauritius to avail of the India-Mauritius Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA), which earlier exempted capital gains tax. India renegotiated this treaty in 2016.

## Special Economic Zones (SEZs)

**SEZ Act: 2005**

SEZs are geographically demarcated zones where special economic rules apply to attract investment and boost exports — simpler regulations, tax benefits, duty-free imports.

**History:**
- **1965:** India's first **Export Processing Zone (EPZ)** at **Kandla, Gujarat** — also the **first EPZ in Asia**
- EPZ model: export-only manufacturing in a duty-free enclave
- 2000: Government announced conversion of EPZs to SEZs
- **2005:** SEZ Act passed; comprehensive framework; explosive growth in SEZ approvals

**Key Benefits for SEZ Units:**
- **Duty-free import** of capital goods, raw materials, and components
- **Income tax exemption** (initially 100% for first 5 years, modified later; now partly under MAT)
- **Single-window clearance** — no multiple department permissions
- Flexibility in labour laws
- Self-certification for customs; faster processing

**Who Approves SEZs:**
- **Board of Approval (BoA):** chaired by Commerce Secretary; approves SEZ proposals
- **Development Commissioner:** administers an approved SEZ
- Ministry of Commerce and Industry is the nodal ministry

**Types of SEZs:**
- **Multi-product SEZs** — any sector
- **Sector-specific SEZs** — e.g., IT, gems, pharmaceuticals, electronics
- **Free Trade and Warehousing Zones (FTWZs)** — for trade/logistics

**Notable SEZs:**
- **Santacruz, Mumbai** — first SEZ post-2005 Act; gems and jewellery
- **Noida SEZ** — IT/electronics
- **Cochin SEZ** — electronics, engineering
- **Kandla Free Trade Zone** — now called Deendayal Port SEZ
- **GIFT City, Gandhinagar** — has IFSC status (a special financial SEZ)

**SEZ controversy:** Large SEZs acquired fertile agricultural land; displaced farmers; critics called them "land banks for developers" — led to scaling back of approvals post-2008.

## Export Promotion Framework

| Scheme | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **RoDTEP** (Remission of Duties and Taxes on Exported Products) | Refunds embedded domestic taxes/duties on exports; replaced MEIS (2021) |
| **EPCG** (Export Promotion Capital Goods) | Import capital goods at reduced/zero duty for use in export production |
| **SEIS** (Service Exports from India Scheme) | Incentivises service exporters |
| **EOU** (Export Oriented Units) | Units operating outside SEZs but with duty-free import benefits for export production |
| **Advance Authorisation** | Duty-free import of inputs used in export products |

**Foreign Trade Policy (FTP):** Issued by Ministry of Commerce every 5 years. Currently: **FTP 2023**.

## Twin Deficit Problem

India frequently suffers from **Twin Deficits**:
- **Fiscal Deficit** (government spending > revenue) AND
- **Current Account Deficit** (imports > exports)

They are linked: fiscal deficit → government borrows and spends more → more demand for imports → CAD worsens. Managing both simultaneously is the central challenge of Indian macroeconomic policy.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Comparative Advantage = David Ricardo; Absolute Advantage = Adam Smith** — both economists and their theories tested separately.
2. **Current Account = goods + services + income + remittances** — not just goods trade; services (IT) and remittances are major current account items for India.
3. **India is the world's largest recipient of remittances** — consistently exceeds $100 billion annually; above China, Mexico, Philippines.
4. **India's CAD main driver = crude oil imports** (India imports ~85% of oil) — gold is second; electronics is third.
5. **1991 BoP crisis: forex reserves fell to 2 weeks of imports** — India pledged 67 tonnes of gold with Bank of England/Japan to get IMF loan.
6. **India uses managed float exchange rate** — not fixed, not pure float; RBI intervenes to manage volatility.
7. **FDI = 10% or more equity + management/strategic involvement; FPI = below 10% or no management control** — the 10% threshold is tested.
8. **Automatic Route = no prior approval; Government Route = prior approval from DPIIT/Ministry** — route names matter.
9. **FDI prohibited in atomic energy, lottery/gambling** — completely banned sectors.
10. **Mauritius is top FDI source due to treaty-shopping (DTAA), not because Mauritian companies invest** — the real investor is often from USA/Europe routing via Mauritius.
11. **India's first EPZ = Kandla, Gujarat (1965) = first EPZ in Asia** — both facts tested together.
12. **SEZ Act 2005; Board of Approval chaired by Commerce Secretary** — Act year and BoA chair both tested.
13. **FIPB abolished in 2017; DPIIT now handles FDI approvals** — FIPB is often still mentioned in old questions; be aware it no longer exists.
14. **RoDTEP replaced MEIS in 2021** — current export incentive scheme; MEIS was WTO-incompatible.
15. **GIFT City = IFSC = financial SEZ** — special zone, not a regular SEZ; governed by IFSCA.
16. **Twin deficits = fiscal deficit + current account deficit simultaneously** — India's recurring macro problem; each worsens the other.
