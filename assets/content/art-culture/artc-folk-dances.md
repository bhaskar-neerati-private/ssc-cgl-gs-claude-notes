# Folk Dances of India

## The Dance That Confused the British Census

In 1871, British census officials in Punjab asked villagers about their "occupation." A group of men answered: *Bhangra*. The officials were baffled — they expected "farmer" or "artisan." But to those Punjabi men, the harvest dance was not a pastime; it was their identity. Bhangra was what you did when you survived another year of sowing and reaping in harsh land. The British filed them as "cultivators" and moved on, but the dance they couldn't categorise has since conquered wedding halls from London to Sydney. Folk dances are exactly this: they are not performed for an audience — they are performed *as* a community. That is the fundamental distinction SSC expects you to know.

While classical dances are codified by texts and academies, folk dances emerge organically from community life — harvests, weddings, festivals, seasons. Every Indian state has signature folk dances. SSC tests state-dance pairings relentlessly.

## High-Priority Folk Dances — Detailed Notes

### Bhangra and Giddha (Punjab)
- **Bhangra**: Harvest festival dance of male farmers; associated with Baisakhi (April 13/14); energetic, athletic leaps; **dhol** drum is central
- **Giddha**: Female counterpart of Bhangra; slower, expressive; includes **bolian** (witty couplets)
- SSC distinction: Bhangra = male, harvest. Giddha = female, expressive.

### Garba and Dandiya Raas (Gujarat)
- **Garba**: Performed around a clay pot (Garba) with a lamp inside or idol of Goddess Amba; circular movements; 9 nights of Navratri
- **Dandiya Raas**: Performed with coloured sticks; associated with Rasa Lila of Krishna; post-Garba; also called "Sword Dance" as sticks represent swords of Goddess Durga
- SSC trap: Both are from Gujarat. Garba = no sticks. Dandiya = sticks.

### Ghoomar (Rajasthan)
- Performed by Bhil tribe women; now associated with Rajput women; swirling gown (ghagra); circular movement; name comes from "ghoomna" (spinning)
- UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage discussions often mention it alongside Kalbeliya

### Kalbeliya (Rajasthan)
- Dance of the **Kalbeliya** (snake charmer community); performed by women; black swirling skirts imitating snake movements; **been** (pungi/flute) and **dufli** drum accompaniment
- UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage: **Kalbeliya** was inscribed in 2010

### Chhau (Tri-State Dance)
- Performed in **three styles** across three states:
  - **Purulia Chhau** (West Bengal) — masks mandatory; vigorous
  - **Seraikella Chhau** (Jharkhand) — half-masks; more refined
  - **Mayurbhanj Chhau** (Odisha) — no masks; closest to classical
- UNESCO Intangible Heritage: Listed in **2010** (same year as Kalbeliya)
- Based on Mahabharata and Ramayana stories; martial arts elements

### Bihu (Assam)
- Three types: **Rongali/Bohag Bihu** (spring, most important — April, agricultural New Year), **Kongali/Kati Bihu** (autumn, quiet lamps), **Bhogali/Magh Bihu** (winter, harvest feast)
- Rongali Bihu: Young men and women dance together; **dhol, pepa (buffalo horn flute), gogona (jaw harp)** instruments
- SSC asks: which Bihu is a harvest festival? → Bhogali/Magh Bihu

### Lavani (Maharashtra)
- Name from "Lavanya" (beauty); traditionally performed by women on stage (Tamasha stage)
- Semi-classical to folk spectrum; 9-yard saree (nauvari) costume; **dholki** drum accompaniment
- Romantic/social commentary themes; popular in Pune and Kolhapur

### Yakshagana (Karnataka)
- Night-long theatrical form blending dance, music, dialogue, costume, makeup
- Stories from Puranas; performed in open fields from dusk to dawn
- Pre-dates Kathakali; Karnataka's equivalent of night-long dance-drama

### Bamboo Dance / Cheraw (Mizoram)
- Internationally famous; performers jump between and across moving bamboo poles
- UNESCO appreciation; origin in warrior tradition of Mizo

### Theyyam (Kerala)
- Ritual dance of North Kerala (Malabar region); performer becomes a deity
- Elaborate makeup, towering costumes; considered divine possession
- Different from Kathakali (which is theatre/performance); Theyyam is a religious ritual

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Bhangra** = male Punjabi harvest dance; **Giddha** = female Punjabi expressive dance. Both Punjab, but gender distinction is the test.
2. **Garba** = no sticks, circular around a lamp/pot; **Dandiya Raas** = with sticks. Both Gujarat, Navratri.
3. **Kalbeliya** = snake charmer community dance, Rajasthan — UNESCO 2010.
4. **Chhau** = three states: WB (Purulia), Jharkhand (Seraikella), Odisha (Mayurbhanj) — UNESCO 2010.
5. **Bihu**: Rongali = spring/April new year, Bhogali = winter harvest feast. SSC asks which is the "harvest festival" — Bhogali/Magh Bihu.
6. **Yakshagana** = Karnataka, night-long dramatic form. NOT Kerala.
7. **Cheraw** (Bamboo Dance) = Mizoram (not Nagaland or Manipur).
8. **Theyyam** = North Kerala (Malabar) ritual where performer becomes a deity — distinct from Kathakali.
9. **Nati** = Himachal Pradesh (Kullu Valley); **Garhwali** = Uttarakhand. Both hilly North states.
10. **Koodiyattam** was **first** Indian element added to UNESCO ICH list in 2008.
11. **Garba of Gujarat** was added to UNESCO ICH in **2023** — one of the most recent.
12. **Kumbh Mela** was added to UNESCO ICH in **2017**.
13. **Ghoomar** = swirling dance from Rajasthan; Bhil tribe origin, associated with Rajput women.
14. **Pandavani** = Chhattisgarh; associated with Mahabharata storytelling; Teejan Bai = most famous exponent.
15. **Lai Haraoba** = Manipur's most important folk festival-dance; pre-dates Manipuri classical dance.
16. **Sangai Deer** dance of Manipur is different from regular Manipuri classical — don't confuse Manipuri folk and classical.
