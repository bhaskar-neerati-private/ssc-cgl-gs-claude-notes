# Indian Paintings — Caves, Schools & Styles

## The Paintings That Survived 1,500 Years

In 1819, a British cavalry officer named John Smith, on a tiger hunt in Maharashtra's Sahyadri hills, noticed a carved entrance in a rocky cliff. He had stumbled upon the Ajanta Caves — 30 rock-cut Buddhist caves containing some of the world's oldest and finest paintings, dated 2nd century BCE to 7th century CE. The cave walls were covered with scenes from Jataka tales, the life of the Buddha, and court scenes rendered with such anatomical precision and emotional depth that they astonished 19th-century European art historians. What struck them most was that these 1,500-year-old Indian painters had already solved problems that European artists struggled with during the Renaissance — foreshortening, three-dimensional rendering, emotional expression in faces. The story of Indian painting is the story of these sustained traditions from the caves to the courts.

## Cave Paintings

### Ajanta (Maharashtra)

- **Location**: Aurangabad district, Maharashtra; UNESCO World Heritage Site (1983)
- **Period**: 2nd century BCE – 6th century CE
- **Religion**: Buddhist
- **Content**: 30 caves; Jataka tales (stories of Buddha's previous lives), life of Buddha, court scenes, flora and fauna
- **Technique**: Fresco — wet lime plaster on walls, painted with natural pigments
- **Style**: Fluid lines, rich colours; emotional expressions; realistic animal painting
- **Famous paintings**: "Dying Princess" (Cave 17), "Padmapani Bodhisattva" (Cave 1 — considered masterpiece), "Flying Apsaras"
- **Key fact**: Paintings discovered in 1819 by British officer **John Smith**

### Ellora (Maharashtra)

- **Location**: Aurangabad district; UNESCO WHS (1983, same as Ajanta)
- **Period**: 5th–11th century CE
- **Religion**: Buddhist, Hindu, Jain (all three represented — unique!)
- **32 caves**: 12 Buddhist (Cave 1–12), 17 Hindu (Cave 13–29), 5 Jain (Cave 30–34)
- **Kailasa Temple** (Cave 16): Largest monolithic rock-cut structure in the world; carved from single rock; Rashtrakuta dynasty
- **Note**: Ellora has more sculpture than paintings

### Bagh Caves (Madhya Pradesh)

- **Location**: Dhar district, MP
- **Period**: 5th–6th century CE
- **Religion**: Buddhist
- **Content**: Similar style to Ajanta; secular scenes of royal life

### Sittanavasal (Tamil Nadu)

- **Period**: 7th–9th century CE
- **Religion**: Jain
- **Content**: Paintings in a Jain cave; Pallava period style

## Miniature Painting Schools

Miniature paintings are small, detailed paintings typically on paper (and earlier on palm leaves or cloth). They flourished under royal patronage from the 7th to 19th centuries.

### Mughal School

- **Period**: 16th–18th century
- **Founders**: **Mir Sayyid Ali** and **Abdus Samad** (Iranian painters brought by Humayun)
- **Under Akbar**: Mass production; workshops (Karkhana); narrative paintings of court life, battles, animals; Akbar-Nama, Babur-Nama illustrated
- **Under Jahangir**: Finest quality; portrait painting emerged; **Ustad Mansur** (animals and birds), **Abu'l Hasan** (portraits)
- **Features**: Persian style merged with Indian warmth; realistic portraits; rich gold borders; fine detail
- **Under Shah Jahan**: More formal and decorative; architectural accuracy
- **Decline**: Under Aurangzeb (hostile to art)

### Rajput School (Rajasthani School)

Distinct from Mughal; indigenous; bold colours; flat figures; Hindu religious themes (Radha-Krishna, Ramayana, Nayika paintings).

| Sub-School | Location | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Mewar School** | Udaipur | Most ancient; bold, rich colours; simple compositions; Chawand, Nathdwara style |
| **Marwar School** | Jodhpur | Strong Mughal influence; portraits of rulers |
| **Bundi-Kota School** | Bundi, Kota | Hunting scenes; lush foliage; dense compositions; vibrant greens |
| **Jaipur School** | Jaipur | Most Mughalised of Rajput schools; court scenes |
| **Kishangarh School** | Kishangarh (Ajmer) | Famous **Bani Thani** painting (18th c. Savant Singh); elongated features, stylised figures; considered "Mona Lisa of India" |

### Pahari School (Hill School)

- **Location**: Himalayan foothills — Himachal Pradesh (Kangra, Guler, Basohli, Chamba), Jammu

| Sub-School | Location | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Basohli School** | Jammu | Earliest Pahari; bold colours; stark lines; beetle wing-cases used for jewellery (glitter effect) |
| **Guler School** | HP (Guler) | Transitional; refined Basohli into gentle style |
| **Kangra School** | HP (Kangra) | Peak: 18th–19th century; under Sansar Chand; lyrical Radha-Krishna themes; soft colours, graceful figures; considered most beautiful Pahari style |

### Deccan School

- **Location**: Bidar, Golconda, Ahmednagar, Bijapur (Deccan Sultanates)
- **Period**: Late 16th–18th century
- **Features**: Blend of Mughal and Persian with South Indian elements; dark backgrounds; Persian crescent moon and sky imagery; rich jewel tones

## Regional/Folk Painting Traditions

| Style | State | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Madhubani (Mithila)** | Bihar (Mithila region) | Geometric patterns; natural pigments (plant/mineral); themes of Hindu gods, nature, wedding rituals; **Warli** geometric comparison |
| **Warli** | Maharashtra (Palghar tribal belt) | Simple white paintings on dark red/brown mud walls; geometric shapes — circles, triangles, squares; tribal figures; harvest, hunting, farming scenes |
| **Pattachitra** | Odisha (and Bengal) | Cloth/palm leaf paintings; Jagannath cult; ganjifa cards; bold outlines; natural colours |
| **Kalamkari** | Andhra Pradesh | Hand-painted/block-printed cloth; two types: **Srikalahasti** (hand-drawn, pen — kalam) and **Machilipatnam** (block-printed); religious themes |
| **Pichwai** | Rajasthan (Nathdwara) | Large temple cloth paintings; depict life of Lord Shrinathji (Krishna); deep colours on dark cloth |
| **Phad** | Rajasthan | Scroll painting; Devnarayan and Pabuji legends; Bhopa community performs with it |
| **Gond Art** | Madhya Pradesh (Gondwana tribal) | Dot-and-line filling; animal and nature motifs; Pardhan Gond artist: Jangarh Singh Shyam |
| **Saura** | Odisha (Saura tribe) | White figures on mud walls; similar to Warli but distinct |
| **Tanjore (Thanjavur) Painting** | Tamil Nadu | Rich gold foil, semi-precious stones, glass; religious themes (Ganesha, Krishna); thick gesso work |
| **Kerala Murals** | Kerala (temples) | Bright colours on wet plaster; Puranic themes; Mattanchery Palace, Krishnapuram Palace |
| **Bengal Patachitra** | West Bengal | Scroll paintings (Pata) by Chitrakar community; Kalighat style — simplified figures; Hindu deities |

## Key Facts for SSC

- **Ajanta**: Only cave paintings; Buddhist; UNESCO 1983; Fresco technique
- **Ellora**: Cave sculptures primarily; Buddhist + Hindu + Jain; Kailasa Temple = monolithic rock cut
- **Bani Thani**: Kishangarh School; "Mona Lisa of India"; by Nihal Chand for ruler Savant Singh
- **Mughal Founder**: Humayun brought Mir Sayyid Ali and Abdus Samad from Persia
- **Best Mughal**: Jahangir period (portrait mastery); Ustad Mansur for animals
- **Madhubani**: Bihar; also known as Mithila Painting; GI tag; UNESCO noted
- **Warli**: Maharashtra (Palghar/Nasik tribal); white geometric on dark background

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Ajanta** = Buddhist cave paintings; **Ellora** = sculptures + cave temples (Buddhist + Hindu + Jain). Never confuse.
2. **Ajanta discovered in 1819** by British officer **John Smith** during a tiger hunt.
3. **Kailasa Temple** (Cave 16) at Ellora = largest **monolithic** (single rock) structure in world; **Rashtrakuta** dynasty.
4. **Bani Thani** = Kishangarh School, Rajasthan = "Mona Lisa of India" — painted by **Nihal Chand**.
5. **Kangra School** = most refined Pahari school; **Basohli** = oldest, boldest, uses beetle wings.
6. **Madhubani** = Bihar (Mithila region), NOT Madhya Pradesh. Common geography trap.
7. **Warli** = Maharashtra (Palghar tribal) — white geometric shapes on earth-toned background.
8. **Pattachitra** = Odisha; cloth painting; Jagannath themes. Don't confuse with Kalamkari.
9. **Kalamkari**: Srikalahasti style = pen (hand-drawn); Machilipatnam style = block print. Both Andhra Pradesh.
10. **Tanjore Painting** = Tamil Nadu; uses **gold foil** — distinguishing feature from other schools.
11. **Mughal Akbar period** = narrative mass paintings; Jahangir = finest portrait painting; Shah Jahan = formal/decorative.
12. **Mewar** = oldest Rajput school; **Kishangarh** = most stylised (Bani Thani); **Bundi-Kota** = hunting scenes.
13. **Ustad Mansur** = Jahangir's court painter famous for nature (animals, birds); "Wonders of the World" paintings.
14. **Kerala Murals** = in temples; **Mattanchery Palace** (Dutch Palace) has finest examples.
15. **Phad** = Rajasthan scroll; **Bhopa** community performs with it; stories of **Devnarayan** and **Pabuji**.
16. **Gond Art** = Madhya Pradesh tribal art; dot-and-line fill; **Jangarh Singh Shyam** = famous Gond artist.
