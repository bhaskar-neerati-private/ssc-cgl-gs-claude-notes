# Human Digestive System — Organs, Enzymes, Absorption & Disorders

## The 9-Metre Journey

From the moment food enters your mouth, it embarks on a remarkable 9-metre journey through a winding tube — being mechanically crushed, chemically dismantled, and systematically absorbed. By the time the journey ends at the anus, a plate of rice and dal has been converted into glucose molecules in your blood, amino acids rebuilding your muscles, and fatty acids fuelling your brain.

The human digestive system is a production line of extraordinary precision — every organ plays a distinct role, every enzyme has one specific substrate, and the sequence cannot be reversed.

---

## The Alimentary Canal — The Route

**Mouth → Pharynx → Oesophagus → Stomach → Small Intestine (Duodenum → Jejunum → Ileum) → Large Intestine (Caecum → Colon → Rectum) → Anus**

**Accessory organs (not part of the canal but pour secretions into it):** Salivary glands, Liver, Gallbladder, Pancreas

Two types of digestion happen throughout:
- **Mechanical digestion:** Physical breakdown — chewing, churning, peristalsis
- **Chemical digestion:** Enzyme action — proteins/carbs/fats broken into small molecules

---

## 1. Mouth (Buccal Cavity)

**Teeth:**
- Adults have **32 teeth** (4 types: incisors, canines, premolars, molars)
- Children have **20 deciduous (milk) teeth**
- Dental formula in adults: **2-1-2-3 / 2-1-2-3** (incisors – canines – premolars – molars per half jaw)
- **Wisdom teeth:** 4 third molars (erupt in late teens/early twenties)

**Salivary Glands (3 pairs):**
| Gland | Location | Size |
|---|---|---|
| **Parotid** | In front of ears | Largest pair |
| **Submandibular** | Under jaw | Medium |
| **Sublingual** | Under tongue | Smallest pair |

*Note: Mumps virus infects the parotid glands — causes painful swelling in front of ears*

**Saliva:** pH ~6.8; ~1–1.5 litres secreted per day
- **Salivary amylase (Ptyalin):** Starch → Maltose (only starchy food digestion begins in mouth)
- **Mucin:** Lubricates food for swallowing
- **Lysozyme:** Antibacterial enzyme — destroys bacteria in food

The chewed, saliva-mixed ball of food is called a **bolus**.

---

## 2. Pharynx and Oesophagus

**Pharynx:** Common passage for food and air (throat)
- **Epiglottis:** Cartilage flap that covers the larynx (windpipe) during swallowing — prevents food from entering the respiratory tract. "The traffic cop at the crossroads."

**Oesophagus (Food Pipe):**
- ~25 cm long; muscular tube
- **No digestion occurs here**
- **Peristalsis:** Wave-like muscular contractions that push food downward — works against gravity (you can swallow upside down)
- Transit time: Food reaches stomach in ~8 seconds

---

## 3. Stomach

A **J-shaped muscular sac** holding ~1.5 litres.

**Three layers of muscle** allow churning (mechanical digestion) in all directions.

**Gastric juice** (secreted by gastric glands in the stomach wall):

| Component | Secreted by | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **HCl (Hydrochloric acid)** | Parietal (Oxyntic) cells | pH 1.5–3.5; kills bacteria; activates pepsinogen → pepsin; denatures proteins |
| **Pepsinogen → Pepsin** | Chief (Zymogenic) cells | Proteins → Peptides (endopeptidase) |
| **Rennin** | Chief cells (infants) | Coagulates milk protein (caseinogen → casein); active mainly in infants |
| **Gastric lipase** | Chief cells | Partial fat digestion |
| **Mucus** | Goblet cells | Protects stomach lining from HCl damage |

**Intrinsic factor:** Produced by parietal cells; essential for absorption of **Vitamin B12** in the ileum. Deficiency → pernicious anaemia.

The stomach converts bolus into a semi-liquid mass called **chyme**, which is released into the duodenum through the **pyloric sphincter**.

---

## 4. Small Intestine (~6–7 metres)

The major site of both digestion and absorption. Three sections:

| Section | Length | Key Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Duodenum** | ~25 cm (C-shaped) | Receives bile + pancreatic juice; most chemical digestion |
| **Jejunum** | ~2.5 m | Active absorption of nutrients |
| **Ileum** | ~3.5 m | Absorbs vitamin B12, bile salts; final absorption |

Three secretions do the chemical work here:

---

### A. Bile (from Liver, stored in Gallbladder)
- **Not an enzyme** — a physical agent
- Alkaline (pH 7.6–8.6); yellow-green fluid
- **Bile salts (sodium glycocholate, sodium taurocholate):** Emulsify fats — break large fat globules into tiny droplets (like detergent breaking grease), massively increasing surface area for lipase action
- **Bile pigments (bilirubin, biliverdin):** Breakdown products of haemoglobin from old RBCs — give bile its colour; give faeces their brown colour; excess in blood → jaundice

---

### B. Pancreatic Juice (from Pancreas)
Alkaline (pH ~8); neutralises acidic chyme from stomach.

| Enzyme | Substrate → Product |
|---|---|
| **Pancreatic amylase** | Starch → Maltose |
| **Pancreatic lipase (Steapsin)** | Fats (triglycerides) → Fatty acids + Glycerol |
| **Trypsinogen → Trypsin** (activated by enterokinase) | Proteins → Peptides |
| **Chymotrypsin** | Proteins → Peptides |
| **Carboxypeptidase** | Peptides → Amino acids (from C-terminal end) |
| **Nucleases (DNase, RNase)** | Nucleic acids → Nucleotides |

---

### C. Intestinal Juice — Succus Entericus
Secreted by intestinal glands (crypts of Lieberkühn):

| Enzyme | Substrate → Product |
|---|---|
| **Erepsin** (mix of peptidases) | Peptides → Amino acids |
| **Maltase** | Maltose → Glucose + Glucose |
| **Sucrase (Invertase)** | Sucrose → Glucose + Fructose |
| **Lactase** | Lactose → Glucose + Galactose |
| **Intestinal lipase** | Fats → Fatty acids + Glycerol |
| **Enterokinase** | Activates trypsinogen → Trypsin |

---

## Complete Digestion Summary

| Nutrient | Begins in | Key Enzymes | Final Products |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Carbohydrates** | Mouth | Salivary amylase, pancreatic amylase, maltase, sucrase, lactase | Glucose, Fructose, Galactose |
| **Proteins** | Stomach | Pepsin → Trypsin, Chymotrypsin, Erepsin | Amino acids |
| **Fats** | Small intestine | Bile (emulsification) + Pancreatic lipase + Intestinal lipase | Fatty acids + Glycerol |

**No digestion of carbohydrates in stomach** — pepsin only works on protein; HCl inactivates salivary amylase.

---

## Absorption — Getting Nutrients into Blood

The small intestine wall is structurally designed for maximum absorption:
- **Villi:** Finger-like projections from the inner wall (~1 mm tall)
- **Microvilli (brush border):** Tiny projections on each villus cell
- Together they increase surface area from ~33 m² (flat tube) to **~600 m²** — the size of a tennis court

**What goes where:**

| Nutrient | Absorbed into | Path to bloodstream |
|---|---|---|
| Glucose, amino acids, galactose, fructose | Blood capillaries in villi | Portal vein → Liver → Hepatic vein → Heart |
| Fatty acids + glycerol (as chylomicrons) | **Lacteals** (lymph capillaries in villi) | Lymph → Thoracic duct → Subclavian vein → Blood |
| Water, electrolytes | Blood (mainly in large intestine) | Directly into blood |
| Vitamin B12 | Ileum (requires intrinsic factor from stomach) | Blood |
| Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) | Absorbed with fats via lacteals | Lymph → Blood |
| Vitamin K and B12 | Synthesised by gut bacteria in colon | Absorbed into blood |

---

## 5. Large Intestine (~1.5 metres)

**Parts:** Caecum → Ascending Colon → Transverse Colon → Descending Colon → Sigmoid Colon → Rectum → Anal Canal → Anus

**Functions:**
- **Water absorption** (major function — converts liquid chyme into solid faeces)
- Electrolyte (sodium, chloride) absorption
- **No digestion** of food nutrients
- **Gut microbiome** (bacteria): Synthesise **Vitamin K** and **Vitamin B12**; ferment undigested fibre
- Storage of faeces until defecation

**Appendix:** Worm-shaped structure hanging from caecum; vestigial organ in humans; appendicitis = inflammation of appendix → severe right lower abdominal pain

---

## The Liver — The Largest Gland

**Weight:** ~1.5 kg (in adults)
**Position:** Upper right abdomen, under the diaphragm
**Classification:** Largest gland; largest internal organ (skin is the largest organ overall)

**Functions of the Liver:**

| Function | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Bile production** | Produces ~600–1000 ml bile/day; stored in gallbladder |
| **Glycogenesis** | Glucose → Glycogen (storage when blood sugar is high) |
| **Glycogenolysis** | Glycogen → Glucose (when blood sugar falls) |
| **Gluconeogenesis** | Makes glucose from proteins, fats, lactic acid |
| **Deamination** | Removes amino group (NH₂) from excess amino acids → Urea (excreted in urine) |
| **Detoxification** | Breaks down drugs, alcohol, toxins |
| **Plasma protein synthesis** | Albumin, fibrinogen (clotting), prothrombin, globulins |
| **Storage** | Glycogen; fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, K; Vitamin B12; iron |
| **Cholesterol metabolism** | Regulates synthesis and breakdown |
| **Heat production** | Major thermogenic organ |
| **Kupffer cells** | Liver macrophages; phagocytose old RBCs, bacteria |

**Jaundice:** Excess bilirubin in blood → yellow skin, eyes, urine
- Pre-hepatic: Excess haemolysis (RBC destruction)
- Hepatic: Liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis)
- Post-hepatic/Obstructive: Bile duct blockage (gallstones)

---

## The Pancreas — Double-Duty Gland

**Both exocrine and endocrine gland:**

| Role | Cells | Secretion |
|---|---|---|
| **Exocrine** | Acinar cells | Pancreatic juice (enzymes — into duodenum) |
| **Endocrine** | Islets of Langerhans | Hormones — into blood |

**Islets of Langerhans (hormone-secreting cells):**
| Cell type | Hormone | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Alpha (α) cells** | **Glucagon** | Raises blood glucose (glycogenolysis) |
| **Beta (β) cells** | **Insulin** | Lowers blood glucose (promotes uptake) |
| **Delta (δ) cells** | **Somatostatin** | Inhibits both insulin and glucagon secretion |

**Diabetes Mellitus:**
- Type 1: Auto-immune destruction of beta cells → no insulin → hyperglycaemia
- Type 2: Insulin resistance (cells don't respond to insulin) → most common type

## Common Digestive Disorders

| Disorder | Cause | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Jaundice** | Excess bilirubin (liver disease, haemolysis, bile duct blockage) | Yellow skin, eyes, urine |
| **Peptic ulcer** | *H. pylori* infection + excess HCl; NSAIDs | Pain in stomach/duodenum |
| **Constipation** | Low fibre/water intake; slow peristalsis | Hard, infrequent stools |
| **Diarrhoea** | Infection, food poisoning, irritable bowel | Watery frequent stools; dehydration |
| **Appendicitis** | Inflammation of appendix | Severe right lower abdominal pain |
| **Lactose intolerance** | Lactase enzyme deficiency | Bloating, cramps, diarrhoea after milk |
| **Cirrhosis** | Chronic liver damage (alcohol, hepatitis) | Liver scarring → liver failure |
| **Gallstones** | Cholesterol/bilirubin crystallisation in gallbladder | Severe right upper abdominal pain |
| **Celiac disease** | Autoimmune reaction to gluten → damages villi | Malabsorption, bloating |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Salivary amylase (ptyalin) digests starch → maltose in mouth** — the only digestion that begins in the mouth; no protein or fat digestion here.
2. **Pepsin digests protein — secreted as pepsinogen by chief (zymogenic) cells; activated by HCl** — the sequence: chief cells → pepsinogen → HCl activates → pepsin.
3. **HCl is secreted by parietal (oxyntic) cells** — not chief cells; not goblet cells.
4. **Bile is NOT an enzyme** — it emulsifies (physically breaks up) fats; produced by liver, stored in gallbladder.
5. **Enterokinase activates trypsinogen → trypsin** — trypsin does NOT activate itself; enterokinase is the trigger.
6. **Lactase digests lactose → glucose + galactose; sucrase digests sucrose → glucose + fructose** — exact products are testable.
7. **Intrinsic factor (from stomach parietal cells) is required for Vitamin B12 absorption in the ileum** — deficiency → pernicious anaemia (not regular anaemia).
8. **Fatty acids are absorbed into lacteals (lymph capillaries), not blood capillaries** — contrast with glucose and amino acids going into blood.
9. **Liver = largest gland; skin = largest organ** — confusing these two is a very common trap.
10. **Liver stores: glycogen, vitamins A, D, E, K, vitamin B12, iron** — the fat-soluble vitamins (not vitamin C) are stored in liver.
11. **Kupffer cells are macrophages in the liver** — they phagocytose old RBCs and bacteria; not to be confused with cells of islets of Langerhans.
12. **Pancreas: Beta cells → Insulin (lowers glucose); Alpha cells → Glucagon (raises glucose)** — the alpha/beta cell hormone assignment is frequently tested in reverse.
13. **Vitamin K and some B vitamins are synthesised by gut bacteria in the large intestine** — not produced by the body itself.
14. **Appendix is attached to the caecum (beginning of large intestine)** — appendicitis causes right lower abdominal pain.
15. **Mumps virus infects the parotid glands** — largest salivary glands, in front of/below the ears.
16. **Rennin coagulates milk (caseinogen → casein)** — active in infants; nearly absent in adults; rennet from calves' stomach is used in cheese-making.
