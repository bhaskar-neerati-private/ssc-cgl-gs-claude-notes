# Genetics — Mendel's Laws, DNA, RNA, Mutations & Heredity

## The Monk Who Changed Biology

In the 1850s, a quiet Austrian monk named Gregor Johann Mendel was growing peas in a monastery garden in Brno (now Czech Republic). He wasn't just gardening — he was running what would become the most important biological experiments in history.

Mendel chose the garden pea (*Pisum sativum*) for good reasons: it grows fast, it self-pollinates (so he could control crosses), and it has clear, contrasting traits. Over eight years (1856–1863), he crossed thousands of plants and counted millions of offspring.

What he found were patterns — ratios so clean and consistent they could only mean something fundamental about how traits are inherited. He published in 1866. The world ignored him for 34 years. Only in 1900, after Mendel's death, did three scientists independently rediscover his work and recognise what he had done: **founded the science of genetics**.

---

## The Seven Traits of Mendel's Pea Plants

Mendel chose 7 contrasting characters — each with two clearly distinct forms:

| Character | Dominant Form | Recessive Form |
|---|---|---|
| Seed shape | Round | Wrinkled |
| Seed colour | Yellow | Green |
| Pod shape | Inflated | Constricted |
| Pod colour | Green | Yellow |
| Flower colour | Purple | White |
| Flower position | Axial (along stem) | Terminal (at tip) |
| Stem height | Tall | Dwarf |

All seven happened to be on different chromosomes — which is why Mendel's law of independent assortment worked cleanly. (If he had chosen genes close together on the same chromosome, he would have seen linkage and been confused.)

## Mendel's Three Laws

### Law 1 — Law of Dominance
When two plants with contrasting traits are crossed, only **one trait (dominant) appears** in the F1 generation. The other trait (recessive) is hidden but not lost.

**P:** TT (Tall) × tt (Dwarf) → **F1:** All Tt (Tall)

### Law 2 — Law of Segregation (Monohybrid Cross)
The two alleles for a trait **separate during gamete formation** — each gamete carries only one allele. When F1 plants self-pollinate:

**F1 × F1:** Tt × Tt →

| | T | t |
|---|---|---|
| **T** | TT | Tt |
| **t** | Tt | tt |

- Genotype ratio F2: **1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt**
- Phenotype ratio F2: **3 Tall : 1 Dwarf** (3:1)

### Law 3 — Law of Independent Assortment (Dihybrid Cross)
Genes for different traits assort **independently** during gamete formation.

**P:** TTRR × ttrr → **F1:** TtRr (Tall Round)
**F1 × F1:** TtRr × TtRr → **F2 phenotype ratio: 9:3:3:1**
- 9 Tall Round : 3 Tall Wrinkled : 3 Dwarf Round : 1 Dwarf Wrinkled

## Key Genetic Terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Gene** | Unit of heredity; a segment of DNA coding for a trait |
| **Allele** | Alternative forms of a gene (e.g., T and t for height) |
| **Homozygous** | Both alleles identical (TT or tt) |
| **Heterozygous** | Different alleles (Tt) |
| **Dominant** | Allele expressed in both homozygous and heterozygous condition |
| **Recessive** | Allele expressed only when homozygous (aa) |
| **Genotype** | Genetic constitution (e.g., Tt) |
| **Phenotype** | Observable trait (e.g., Tall) |
| **Locus** | Fixed position of a gene on a chromosome |
| **Test cross** | Crossing unknown genotype with homozygous recessive to determine it |

## Beyond Mendel — Variations in Inheritance

### Incomplete Dominance
Neither allele is completely dominant — the heterozygote shows an **intermediate phenotype**.

**Example:** Snapdragon (*Antirrhinum*) flower colour
- RR = Red; rr = White; Rr = **Pink** (intermediate)
- F2 (Rr × Rr): **1 Red : 2 Pink : 1 White** (phenotype ratio = genotype ratio, 1:2:1, not 3:1)

### Codominance
Both alleles are **fully expressed** in the heterozygote — no blending, both appear simultaneously.

**Example:** ABO blood groups — alleles I^A and I^B are codominant; both antigens appear on RBCs in AB group.

### Multiple Allelism
More than **two alleles** exist for a single gene in the population (though each individual still carries only two).

**Example:** ABO blood groups — three alleles: I^A, I^B, i

## ABO Blood Groups

**Discovered by:** Karl Landsteiner, 1901 (Nobel Prize 1930)

| Blood Group | Genotype | Antigen on RBC | Antibody in Plasma |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A** | I^A I^A or I^A i | A antigen | Anti-B |
| **B** | I^B I^B or I^B i | B antigen | Anti-A |
| **AB** | I^A I^B | A and B antigens | None |
| **O** | ii | None | Anti-A and Anti-B |

**Universal Donor:** Blood group **O** (no antigens on RBCs — won't trigger immune reaction in recipient)
**Universal Recipient:** Blood group **AB** (no antibodies in plasma — won't attack donor cells)

### Rh Factor
- Discovered by: Landsteiner and Wiener (1940) — named after rhesus monkey
- **Rh+** (Rh positive): Have Rh (D) antigen on RBCs — ~85% of humans
- **Rh-** (Rh negative): Lack Rh antigen — ~15% of humans
- **Erythroblastosis Foetalis (Haemolytic Disease of the Newborn):** Rh- mother + Rh+ father → Rh+ foetus. During first delivery, some foetal RBCs enter mother's blood → she produces anti-Rh antibodies. In the **second Rh+ pregnancy**, these antibodies cross the placenta and destroy foetal RBCs. Prevented by injecting **Rho(D) immune globulin (RhoGAM)** to the mother.

## Sex Determination

| System | Females | Males | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **XY system** | XX (homogametic) | XY (heterogametic) | Humans, Drosophila |
| **ZW system** | ZW (heterogametic) | ZZ (homogametic) | Birds, some reptiles |
| **XO system** | XX | XO | Grasshoppers |

**In humans:** Sex is determined by the father's contribution — sperm carries either X (→ girl) or Y (→ boy). Egg always carries X.

## Sex-Linked Inheritance

Genes located on the X chromosome:

| Disorder | Type | Defect |
|---|---|---|
| **Colour blindness** | X-linked recessive | Defect in cone cells (red-green distinction) |
| **Haemophilia A** | X-linked recessive | Deficiency of clotting factor VIII |
| **Haemophilia B** | X-linked recessive | Deficiency of clotting factor IX (Christmas disease) |

**Why males are more affected:** Males are XY — they have only one X chromosome, so one recessive allele is sufficient to express the trait. Females (XX) need both alleles to be recessive (carrier female: X^H X^h — appears normal but passes to sons).

**Royal haemophilia:** Queen Victoria was a carrier; several of her descendants had haemophilia.

## DNA — The Molecule of Heredity

**Full form:** Deoxyribonucleic acid

**History of DNA Discovery:**
| Scientist | Year | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| Friedrich Miescher | 1869 | Discovered nucleic acid in pus cells (called it "nuclein") |
| Oswald Avery, MacLeod, McCarty | 1944 | Proved DNA is the genetic material (Avery's experiment with pneumococcus) |
| **Rosalind Franklin** | 1952 | X-ray diffraction image of DNA (**Photo 51**) — showed helical structure |
| **Watson and Crick** | 1953 | **Double helix model** of DNA (Nobel Prize 1962, with Wilkins) |
| Meselson and Stahl | 1958 | Proved **semi-conservative replication** using heavy nitrogen (^15N) |

### DNA Structure (Watson-Crick Model)
- **Double helix** — two antiparallel strands twisted around each other
- Each strand is a polynucleotide: phosphate–deoxyribose (sugar)–nitrogenous base
- **Nitrogenous bases:**
  - **Purines** (double ring): Adenine (A), Guanine (G)
  - **Pyrimidines** (single ring): Cytosine (C), Thymine (T)
- **Base pairing rules (Chargaff's Rules):**
  - A pairs with T (2 hydrogen bonds)
  - G pairs with C (3 hydrogen bonds)
  - Therefore A = T and G = C in any DNA molecule; A+G = C+T
- The two strands run **antiparallel** — one 5'→3', the other 3'→5'

## RNA — Structure and Types

| Feature | DNA | RNA |
|---|---|---|
| Sugar | Deoxyribose | Ribose |
| Strands | Double-stranded | Usually single-stranded |
| Bases | A, T, G, C | A, **U (Uracil)**, G, C |
| Location | Nucleus (mainly) | Nucleus + cytoplasm |

**Three types of RNA:**

| Type | Function |
|---|---|
| **mRNA** (messenger RNA) | Carries genetic code from DNA to ribosome for translation; longest-lived |
| **tRNA** (transfer RNA) | Brings amino acids to ribosome; cloverleaf shape; contains anti-codon |
| **rRNA** (ribosomal RNA) | Structural and catalytic component of ribosomes; most abundant RNA; made in nucleolus |

## Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

**Proposed by:** Francis Crick, 1958

```
DNA  →(Transcription)→  mRNA  →(Translation)→  Protein
 ↑_____(Replication)_↑
```

- **Replication:** DNA makes a copy of itself (semi-conservative; occurs in S phase)
- **Transcription:** DNA → mRNA (occurs in nucleus)
- **Translation:** mRNA → Protein (occurs on ribosomes)
- **Reverse transcription (exception):** RNA → DNA — done by **retroviruses** (like HIV) using **reverse transcriptase** enzyme

### Genetic Code
- **Codon:** Sequence of 3 nucleotides (triplet) that codes for one amino acid
- 4³ = 64 possible codons for 20 amino acids → code is **degenerate** (redundant — multiple codons for same amino acid)
- Code is **universal** — same in almost all organisms (minor exceptions in mitochondria)
- **Start codon:** AUG (codes for methionine; also called initiator codon)
- **Stop codons (nonsense codons):** UAA, UAG, UGA (terminate translation)

## Mutations

A mutation is a **heritable change in the DNA sequence**.

**Types:**
| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Point mutation (substitution)** | One nucleotide replaced by another | Sickle cell anaemia |
| **Insertion** | Extra nucleotide(s) added | Frameshift — all downstream amino acids change |
| **Deletion** | Nucleotide(s) removed | Frameshift |
| **Chromosomal mutation** | Change in chromosome number or structure | Down syndrome, Turner syndrome |

**Mutagens (agents causing mutations):**
- **Physical:** Ionising radiation (X-rays, gamma rays), UV light
- **Chemical:** Benzene, mustard gas, nitrous acid

### Sickle Cell Anaemia
- **Type:** Autosomal recessive
- **Mutation:** Single point mutation — Adenine replaced by Thymine in the β-globin gene → Glutamic acid replaced by Valine at position 6 of the β-globin chain
- **Effect:** RBCs become sickle-shaped under low oxygen conditions; blocked blood vessels, anaemia
- **Heterozygous advantage:** Carriers (HbA HbS) are resistant to malaria (Plasmodium cannot thrive in sickled cells) — explains why the mutation persists in malaria-endemic regions

## Human Genome Project (HGP)

- **Launched:** 1990 (USA, UK, France, Germany, Japan, China)
- **Completed:** April 2003
- **Key finding:** Human genome has ~20,000–25,000 protein-coding genes in ~3 billion base pairs of DNA
- **Surprise:** Only ~1.5% of human DNA codes for proteins; rest is non-coding ("junk DNA" — now known to have regulatory functions)
- **First genome sequenced:** Haemophilus influenzae (bacteria, 1995)

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Mendel = "Father of Genetics"; his experiments on *Pisum sativum* (pea plant); published 1866; rediscovered 1900** — all four facts together.
2. **Mendel's F2 ratios: Monohybrid = 3:1 (phenotype); Dihybrid = 9:3:3:1** — exact ratios, not approximations.
3. **Incomplete dominance: F2 phenotype is 1:2:1 (not 3:1)** — example is snapdragon, not pea.
4. **ABO blood groups: I^A and I^B are codominant (not incompletely dominant)** — both fully expressed in AB group.
5. **Universal Donor = O (no antigens); Universal Recipient = AB (no antibodies)** — confusing these two is very common.
6. **Rh factor discovered by Landsteiner and Wiener (1940), named after rhesus monkey** — Landsteiner also discovered ABO groups (1901, Nobel 1930).
7. **Watson and Crick (1953) = double helix model; Rosalind Franklin's Photo 51 = X-ray crystallography data** — Franklin's contribution is a common "who-did-what" question.
8. **Chargaff's rules: A=T (2 H bonds), G=C (3 H bonds)** — if %A=30%, then %T=30%, %G=%C=20% each.
9. **mRNA has Uracil (U), not Thymine (T); RNA has ribose, not deoxyribose** — DNA/RNA base comparison.
10. **Start codon = AUG; Stop codons = UAA, UAG, UGA** — only one start; three stops.
11. **Central dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein; reverse transcriptase in retroviruses reverses DNA→RNA direction** — HIV uses reverse transcriptase.
12. **Meselson-Stahl (1958) proved semi-conservative replication** — using heavy nitrogen (^15N) experiment.
13. **Down Syndrome = Trisomy 21 (47 chromosomes); Turner = 45,XO; Klinefelter = 47,XXY** — number and chromosome involved.
14. **Haemophilia A = factor VIII; Haemophilia B = factor IX (Christmas disease)** — confusing A and B is the exam trap.
15. **Sickle cell anaemia: autosomal recessive; Glu→Val substitution in β-globin; heterozygotes resistant to malaria** — all three facts.
16. **Human Genome Project completed 2003; ~20,000–25,000 genes; 3 billion base pairs** — HGP year is frequently tested.
