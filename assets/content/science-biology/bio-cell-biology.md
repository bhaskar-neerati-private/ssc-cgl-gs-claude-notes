# Cell — Structure, Organelles & Division (Mitosis & Meiosis)

## The Smallest Unit of Life

In 1665, Robert Hooke peered through a crude microscope at a thin slice of cork and saw tiny box-like compartments. He called them **cells** — after the Latin *cellula*, meaning small room, because they reminded him of monks' living quarters in a monastery.

What Hooke saw were actually dead cell walls. It took another two centuries before scientists understood that the cell is not just a structural unit but the fundamental unit of **life itself** — everything that happens in a living organism happens inside cells or because of them.

Today, cells are the lens through which SSC tests biology. Master cell structure and division, and you've built the foundation for every other biology topic.

---

## Cell Theory — Three Principles

| Scientist | Contribution |
|---|---|
| **Robert Hooke** (1665) | First observed and named cells (in cork) |
| **Anton van Leeuwenhoek** (1670s) | First observed living cells (bacteria, protozoa) — "Father of Microbiology" |
| **Matthias Schleiden** (1838) | All plants are made of cells |
| **Theodor Schwann** (1839) | All animals are made of cells |
| **Rudolf Virchow** (1855) | "Omnis cellula e cellula" — every cell comes from a pre-existing cell |

**Cell Theory (modern):**
1. All living organisms are composed of cells
2. The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life
3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells (Virchow's contribution — added later)

## Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

This is the first and most fundamental division in the living world:

| Feature | Prokaryotic | Eukaryotic |
|---|---|---|
| **Nucleus** | No membrane-bound nucleus (nucleoid region) | True membrane-bound nucleus |
| **DNA form** | Circular, no histones | Linear, wrapped around histones |
| **Organelles** | None (no membrane-bound organelles) | Many membrane-bound organelles |
| **Size** | Smaller (1–10 µm) | Larger (10–100 µm) |
| **Cell division** | Binary fission | Mitosis or meiosis |
| **Ribosomes** | 70S type | 80S type (cytoplasmic); 70S in mitochondria/chloroplast |
| **Examples** | Bacteria, Archaea | All animals, plants, fungi, protists |

**Mnemonic:** **PRO**karyotes are **PRO**-simple — no nucleus membrane, no organelles.

## Plant Cell vs Animal Cell

| Feature | Plant Cell | Animal Cell |
|---|---|---|
| **Cell wall** | Present (cellulose) | Absent |
| **Chloroplasts** | Present (in green parts) | Absent |
| **Vacuole** | Large central vacuole | Small or absent |
| **Centrioles** | Absent (except lower plants) | Present |
| **Lysosomes** | Rare | Common |
| **Shape** | Fixed, rectangular | Variable |
| **Plasmodesmata** | Present | Absent |

## Cell Organelles — Structure and Function

### 1. Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)
- Made of **phospholipid bilayer** with proteins embedded (Fluid Mosaic Model — Singer & Nicolson, 1972)
- Selectively permeable — controls what enters and exits
- **Functions:** Material transport, cell recognition, signal reception

### 2. Cell Wall
- Present only in plant cells, fungi, and bacteria
- **Plants:** Cellulose; **Fungi:** Chitin; **Bacteria:** Peptidoglycan (murein)
- Provides shape, rigidity, and protection; freely permeable

### 3. Nucleus
- "Control centre" of the cell — houses genetic material (DNA)
- Bounded by double membrane: **nuclear envelope** with nuclear pores
- Contains: **Nucleoplasm, Nucleolus (makes rRNA), Chromatin (DNA + histone proteins)**
- **Nucleolus:** Site of ribosome synthesis; disappears during cell division

### 4. Mitochondria
- **"Powerhouse of the cell"** — site of cellular respiration (ATP production)
- Has own DNA and ribosomes (semi-autonomous organelle) — **endosymbiotic origin**
- Double membrane: outer (smooth) + inner (highly folded — **cristae**)
- Matrix (inside) contains enzymes for Krebs cycle
- ATP made via **oxidative phosphorylation** on cristae

### 5. Chloroplasts
- **"Kitchen of the cell"** — site of photosynthesis (found in plant cells and algae)
- Has own DNA and ribosomes (semi-autonomous — endosymbiotic origin)
- Double membrane + internal thylakoid membranes stacked into **grana**
- Light reactions: occur in thylakoids (grana); Dark reactions (Calvin cycle): occur in **stroma**
- Contains: **Chlorophyll** (main pigment), carotenoids (accessory pigments)

### 6. Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)
- Network of membranes running through cytoplasm
- **Rough ER (RER):** Has ribosomes; synthesises and transports proteins
- **Smooth ER (SER):** No ribosomes; synthesises lipids and steroids; detoxification

### 7. Golgi Body (Golgi Apparatus)
- "Post office of the cell" / "Packaging and dispatch centre"
- Processes, packages, and dispatches proteins and lipids made in ER
- Produces **lysosomes**
- **Discovered by:** Camillo Golgi (1898)

### 8. Ribosomes
- Site of protein synthesis (translation)
- Not membrane-bound — found free in cytoplasm or on RER
- **Prokaryotes:** 70S (50S + 30S subunits)
- **Eukaryotes:** 80S (60S + 40S subunits) in cytoplasm; 70S in mitochondria and chloroplasts
- The antibiotic tetracycline targets bacterial 30S ribosome subunit

### 9. Lysosomes
- **"Suicide bags of the cell"** — contain digestive enzymes (hydrolases)
- Break down worn-out organelles (autophagy), cellular debris, foreign particles
- Bounded by single membrane; acidic interior
- Absent or rare in plant cells

### 10. Vacuoles
- Storage organelles bounded by single membrane (tonoplast in plants)
- **Plant cells:** Large central vacuole — stores water, ions, waste products; maintains turgor pressure
- **Animal cells:** Small, temporary food or contractile vacuoles

### 11. Centrosome and Centrioles
- Present in animal cells and lower plants; absent in higher plants
- **Centrioles:** Pair of cylindrical structures (9+0 arrangement of microtubules)
- Form the **spindle fibres** during cell division
- **Centrosome = 2 centrioles + surrounding pericentriolar material**

### 12. Cytoskeleton
- Network of protein filaments in cytoplasm
- Three types: Microfilaments (actin), Intermediate filaments, Microtubules (tubulin)
- Provides cell shape, enables movement, organelle transport

## Cell Division

Cells reproduce through division. Two types matter for the exam:

---

### Mitosis — For Growth and Repair

**Purpose:** Growth of organisms, repair/replacement of somatic (body) cells, asexual reproduction in unicellular organisms

**Where it occurs:** All somatic cells (body cells except gametes)

**Result:** 2 daughter cells, genetically identical to parent, same chromosome number (diploid → 2 diploid)

**Phases of Mitosis:**

| Phase | What Happens | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Interphase** (pre-mitosis) | DNA replication (S phase), cell growth | Chromosomes not visible; chromatin form |
| **Prophase** | Chromosomes condense and become visible; spindle fibres form; nuclear envelope breaks down | Chromatin → chromosomes |
| **Metaphase** | Chromosomes align at cell equator (metaphase plate); spindle attached to centromeres | Easiest phase to count chromosomes |
| **Anaphase** | Centromeres split; sister chromatids pulled to opposite poles by spindle fibres | Cell looks like it's being pulled apart |
| **Telophase** | Nuclear envelope reforms around each set; chromosomes de-condense | Two nuclei form |
| **Cytokinesis** | Cytoplasm divides — 2 daughter cells | Cleavage furrow (animals); cell plate (plants) |

**Mnemonic:** **IPMAT** — Interphase, Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase

---

### Meiosis — For Sexual Reproduction

**Purpose:** Produce gametes (sperm, eggs) in animals; spores in plants

**Where it occurs:** Gonads (testes, ovaries) in animals; pollen-producing structures in plants

**Result:** 4 daughter cells, genetically unique, half the chromosome number (diploid → 4 haploid)

**Two rounds of division:**
- **Meiosis I** (Reductive division): Separates homologous chromosome pairs → 2 cells, each with half the chromosomes
- **Meiosis II** (similar to mitosis): Separates sister chromatids → 4 haploid cells

**Key event in Meiosis I — Prophase I:**
- **Crossing over (Recombination):** Homologous chromosomes exchange segments at **chiasmata** — this creates **genetic variation** in offspring

**Meiosis I Substages (Prophase I):**
| Substage | What Happens |
|---|---|
| Leptotene | Chromosomes begin to condense |
| Zygotene | Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) |
| Pachytene | Crossing over occurs at chiasmata |
| Diplotene | Homologues begin to separate; chiasmata visible |
| Diakinesis | Chromosomes maximally condensed; nuclear envelope breaks down |

---

### Mitosis vs Meiosis — Comparison

| Feature | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---|---|---|
| Occurs in | Somatic cells | Germ cells (gonads) |
| Divisions | 1 | 2 (I + II) |
| Daughter cells | 2 | 4 |
| Chromosome number | Same as parent (2n → 2n) | Half of parent (2n → n) |
| Genetic identity | Identical to parent | Unique (due to crossing over) |
| Crossing over | None (no homologous pairing) | Yes (Pachytene of Prophase I) |
| Purpose | Growth, repair, asexual reproduction | Sexual reproduction, gamete formation |

## Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is the complete sequence of events from one cell division to the next:

```
G1 phase → S phase → G2 phase → M phase (Mitosis/Meiosis)
      ↑__________________________|
```

- **G1 (Gap 1):** Cell growth; preparation for DNA replication
- **S (Synthesis):** DNA replication — chromosome number doubles (2n → 4n DNA content)
- **G2 (Gap 2):** Cell continues to grow; prepares for division
- **M phase:** Actual cell division (mitosis or meiosis)
- **G0 phase:** Cells exit the cycle and remain in a non-dividing state (e.g., neurons)

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Robert Hooke named cells (1665, cork) but didn't discover living cells — Leeuwenhoek did** — two different "firsts."
2. **Prokaryotes have NO membrane-bound nucleus and NO membrane-bound organelles** — they do have ribosomes (70S) but not ER, Golgi, mitochondria, etc.
3. **Mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own DNA and 70S ribosomes** — evidence of endosymbiotic origin; this is why antibiotics (which target 70S) can affect these organelles.
4. **Cell wall composition: Plants = cellulose; Fungi = chitin; Bacteria = peptidoglycan** — three different materials, three different kingdoms; confusing any two is a trap.
5. **Lysosomes = "suicide bags"; Mitochondria = "powerhouse"; Golgi = "post office"** — these nicknames are directly asked.
6. **Ribosomes: Prokaryotes 70S (50S+30S); Eukaryotes 80S (60S+40S)** — the "S" is Svedberg unit (sedimentation coefficient), not size. 70 ≠ 50+30 and 80 ≠ 60+40 because S units don't add linearly.
7. **Centrioles are ABSENT in higher plant cells** — only present in animal cells and lower plants. Plants still divide (using spindle from other structures).
8. **Metaphase is the best phase to count chromosomes** — they are maximally condensed and aligned.
9. **Mitosis: 1 division → 2 identical diploid cells; Meiosis: 2 divisions → 4 unique haploid cells** — the 4 vs 2 distinction.
10. **Crossing over occurs during Pachytene of Prophase I of Meiosis I** — not Prophase II, not Metaphase.
11. **In Meiosis I, homologous chromosomes separate; in Meiosis II, sister chromatids separate** — the "what separates when" question.
12. **DNA replication occurs in S phase of Interphase, NOT during mitosis** — DNA is replicated before division begins.
13. **Neurons (nerve cells) are in G0 — they do NOT divide after maturity** — this is why brain injuries are permanent; neurons can't be replaced.
14. **Light reactions of photosynthesis occur in thylakoids (grana); dark reactions (Calvin cycle) in stroma** — two separate compartments within chloroplast.
15. **Fluid Mosaic Model of cell membrane was proposed by Singer and Nicolson (1972)** — phospholipid bilayer with floating proteins.
16. **Nucleolus disappears during cell division and reappears at the end** — it is the site of rRNA synthesis and is active only in non-dividing cells.
