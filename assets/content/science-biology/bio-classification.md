# Classification of Living Organisms — Five Kingdoms, Phyla & Plant Kingdom

## The Problem of Naming Everything

Imagine you are a scientist in 1750. You've just sailed back from South America with specimens of 200 species never seen before in Europe. How do you record, describe, and communicate them to other scientists? Every country uses a different name for the same creature — a "cougar" in America is a "puma" in South America and a "mountain lion" elsewhere.

The Swedish botanist **Carl Linnaeus** solved this problem. His system, introduced in *Systema Naturae* (1758), gave every living organism a universal two-part Latin name — **binomial nomenclature**. A wolf is *Canis lupus* whether you're reading it in Swedish, Hindi, or Japanese. This single idea made modern biology possible.

Linnaeus is the **"Father of Taxonomy"** — the science of naming, describing, and classifying living organisms.

## Binomial Nomenclature — The Rules

- Two names: **Genus** (capitalised) + **species** (lowercase)
- Always written in **italics** (or underlined when handwritten)
- Both names are in **Latin** or Latinised form

**Examples:**
| Organism | Scientific Name |
|---|---|
| Human | *Homo sapiens* |
| Lion | *Panthera leo* |

## Taxonomic Hierarchy

From broadest to most specific:

**Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species**

**Mnemonic:** **K**ing **P**hilip **C**ame **O**ver **F**or **G**ood **S**oup

**Example: Human classification:**
- Kingdom: Animalia
- Phylum: Chordata
- Class: Mammalia
- Order: Primates
- Family: Hominidae
- Genus: *Homo*
- Species: *sapiens*

## Systems of Classification

| System | Proposed by | Year | Kingdoms |
|---|---|---|---|
| Two-kingdom | Linnaeus | 1758 | Plantae, Animalia |
| Five-kingdom | **R.H. Whittaker** | **1969** | Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia |
| Three-domain | Carl Woese | 1990 | Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya |

**The Five-Kingdom System (Whittaker, 1969)** is the standard for Indian competitive exams.

## Five Kingdoms — At a Glance

### Kingdom 1: Monera
- **Prokaryotes** — no membrane-bound nucleus
- Cell wall present (peptidoglycan in bacteria)
- Examples: Bacteria, Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae)
- **Cyanobacteria:** Photosynthetic; nitrogen-fixers (*Nostoc*, *Anabaena*); cause algal blooms
- **Mycoplasma:** Smallest living organisms; no cell wall; can survive without oxygen

**Bacterial shapes:**
| Shape | Name | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Spherical | Coccus | *Staphylococcus*, *Streptococcus* |
| Rod | Bacillus | *E. coli*, *Bacillus anthracis* |
| Spiral | Spirillum | *Spirillum*, *Treponema* |
| Comma | Vibrio | *Vibrio cholerae* |

### Kingdom 2: Protista
- **Unicellular eukaryotes** — single cell with membrane-bound nucleus
- Examples: *Amoeba*, *Paramecium*, *Euglena*, Diatoms, *Plasmodium* (malaria parasite)
- *Euglena*: plant-animal crossover — has chloroplasts (autotrophic in light) but also ingests food (heterotrophic in dark)
- Diatoms: have silica cell walls; form diatomaceous earth (used in filters, abrasives)

### Kingdom 3: Fungi
- **Heterotrophic** (cannot make own food)
- Mostly multicellular (except yeast — unicellular)
- Cell wall: **Chitin** (not cellulose)
- Nutrition: Saprophytic (decompose dead matter), parasitic, or mutualistic
- Reproduce by spores

**Important fungi:**
| Fungus | Type | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| *Penicillium* | Mold (blue-green) | Source of **penicillin** antibiotic; also used in cheese-making |
| *Aspergillus* | Mold | Citric acid production; causes aspergillosis |
| *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* | Yeast (unicellular) | Bread-making, beer/wine fermentation |
| *Agaricus* | Mushroom | Common edible mushroom |
| *Rhizopus* | Bread mold | Spoils bread |
| *Puccinia* | Rust | Wheat rust disease |
| *Ustilago* | Smut | Maize smut disease |

**Lichen:** Mutualistic association of **fungi + algae**
- Fungi provides water, shelter, and minerals; algae provides food (photosynthesis)
- Pioneer organisms in barren rock
- **Bio-indicator of air pollution** — lichens die in polluted air (sensitive to SO₂)

**Mycorrhiza:** Mutualistic association of **fungi + plant roots**
- Fungi increases water and mineral absorption for the plant
- Plant provides carbohydrates to the fungi

## About Viruses (Not a Kingdom)

Viruses are **acellular** — not made of cells. They are **non-living outside a host** but show living characteristics (replication, mutation) inside a host cell.

| Scientist | Year | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **D.J. Ivanowski** | 1892 | Discovered viruses (tobacco mosaic disease) — first to show disease agent passed through bacterial filter |
| **M.W. Beijerinck** | 1898 | Called the agent "contagium vivum fluidum" (living fluid) |
| W.M. Stanley | 1935 | Crystallised TMV virus (proved virus is protein-nucleic acid complex) |

**Structure:** Protein coat (capsid) + nucleic acid (DNA or RNA — never both)

## The Plant Kingdom

Plants are grouped into five major categories based on vascular tissue, seed production, and flower/fruit:

| Group | Vascular Tissue | Seeds | Flowers | Enclosed Seed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Algae** | No | No | No | — |
| **Bryophyta** | No | No | No | — |
| **Pteridophyta** | Yes (first) | No | No | — |
| **Gymnosperms** | Yes | Yes (first) | No | No (naked) |
| **Angiosperms** | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |

---

### 1. Algae (Thallophyta)
- No roots, stems, or leaves (thallus body)
- Aquatic; photosynthetic
- **Produce ~50% of the world's oxygen**

| Colour | Division | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Green | Chlorophyta | *Spirogyra*, *Chara*, *Ulva* (sea lettuce), *Chlorella* |
| Brown | Phaeophyta | *Fucus*, *Sargassum*, *Laminaria* (kelp) |
| Red | Rhodophyta | *Porphyra*, *Gelidium* (source of **agar**), *Gracilaria* |

### 2. Bryophyta — "Amphibians of the Plant Kingdom"
- First true land plants — but still need water for fertilisation
- No vascular tissue (no xylem or phloem)
- Examples: *Funaria* (moss), *Riccia* (liverwort), *Marchantia* (liverwort)

### 3. Pteridophyta — First Vascular Plants
- First plants with vascular tissue (xylem and phloem)
- No seeds — reproduce by spores
- Examples: Ferns (*Pteris*, *Adiantum*, *Dryopteris*), *Selaginella*, *Equisetum* (horsetail)
- **Carboniferous forests** were dominated by pteridophytes — their fossils formed today's coal deposits

### 4. Gymnosperms — "Naked Seeds"
- First seed-bearing plants; seeds not enclosed in fruit
- No flowers; no true fruits; seeds borne on cone scales
- Examples: *Pinus* (pine), *Cycas*, *Ginkgo*, *Cedrus* (deodar), *Abies* (fir)
- **Cycas:** "Living fossil"; oldest gymnosperm; separate male and female plants (dioecious)
- **Ginkgo biloba:** "Maidenhair tree"; living fossil; medicinally important

### 5. Angiosperms — "Enclosed Seeds"
- Most diverse plant group; seeds enclosed in fruit (inside ovary)
- Have flowers and fruits
- Two major classes:

**Monocot vs Dicot:**
| Feature | Monocotyledonae | Dicotyledonae |
|---|---|---|
| Seed leaves (cotyledons) | 1 | 2 |
| Venation | Parallel | Reticulate (net-like) |
| Root system | Fibrous (adventitious) | Tap root |
| Floral parts | 3 or multiples of 3 | 4, 5 or multiples |
| Vascular bundles | Scattered in stem | Arranged in a ring |
| Examples | Rice, wheat, maize, grass, onion, bamboo, coconut, lily, orchid | Mango, pea, sunflower, rose, bean, tomato, cotton, mustard |

## The Animal Kingdom — Major Phyla

### Phylum 1: Porifera (Sponges)
- Simplest animals; **aquatic (mostly marine)**
- No true tissues; body has pores (*ostia*) and canals
- Examples: *Sycon* (Scypha), *Spongilla* (freshwater sponge), *Euspongia* (bath sponge)

### Phylum 2: Cnidaria (Coelenterata)
- Aquatic; **radial symmetry**; **cnidoblasts** (stinging cells) for defence and prey capture
- Two body forms: **polyp** (sessile, e.g., *Hydra*, coral) and **medusa** (free-swimming, e.g., jellyfish)
- Examples: *Hydra*, jellyfish (*Aurelia*), sea anemone, coral (*Meandrina*)
- Coral reefs: calcium carbonate skeletons; "rainforests of the sea"

### Phylum 3: Platyhelminthes (Flatworms)
- Flat, bilaterally symmetrical; **acoelomate** (no body cavity)
- Mostly parasitic; some free-living
- Examples: *Taenia* (tapeworm — intestinal parasite), *Fasciola* (liver fluke), *Planaria* (free-living; famous for regeneration)

### Phylum 4: Nematoda (Roundworms / Aschelminthes)
- Round, cylindrical; **pseudocoelom**; mostly parasitic
- Examples: *Ascaris lumbricoides* (intestinal roundworm), *Wuchereria bancrofti* (filariasis/elephantiasis), *Ancylostoma* (hookworm), *Enterobius* (pinworm)

### Phylum 5: Annelida (Segmented Worms)
- Body clearly **segmented**; **true coelom**; closed circulatory system
- Examples: Earthworm (*Pheretima*), leech (*Hirudinaria*), *Nereis*
- Earthworm: "**Friend of the farmer**" — improves soil aeration and fertility; hermaphrodite (both male and female organs)

### Phylum 6: Arthropoda — Largest Animal Phylum
- **Jointed appendages (legs)**; **exoskeleton of chitin**; open circulatory system
- Largest phylum in animal kingdom (more species than all others combined)
- Major classes:
  - **Insecta:** 6 legs; wings; 3 body parts (head, thorax, abdomen); e.g., butterfly, mosquito, housefly, honeybee
  - **Arachnida:** 8 legs; no wings; e.g., spider, scorpion, tick, mite
  - **Crustacea:** 10 legs; aquatic; e.g., prawn, crab, lobster
  - **Myriapoda:** Many legs; e.g., centipede (*Scolopendra*), millipede

### Phylum 7: Mollusca
- Soft body; **mantle** (body wall); most have calcium carbonate shell
- Open circulatory system (except cephalopods)
- Examples: *Pila* (apple snail), *Octopus* (no shell — most intelligent mollusc), *Sepia* (cuttlefish), mussel, pearl oyster (*Pinctada*)
- **Pearl formation:** Irritant particle → mantle secretes nacre (calcium carbonate layers) around it → pearl

### Phylum 8: Echinodermata
- "**Spiny skin**"; exclusively **marine**; **water vascular system** (hydraulic movement via tube feet)
- Radial symmetry in adults; bilateral in larvae
- Examples: Starfish (*Asterias*), sea urchin, sea cucumber (*Holothuria*), brittle star
- Starfish can regenerate severed arms

### Phylum 9: Chordata
- Have **notochord** (skeletal rod), **dorsal hollow nerve cord**, pharyngeal gill slits at some stage
- Sub-phylum **Vertebrata:** Have backbone (vertebral column)

**Five Classes of Vertebrates:**

| Class | Blood | Habitat | Skin | Key Feature | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Pisces** (Fish) | Cold-blooded | Aquatic | Scales | Gills; no four limbs | *Labeo* (rohu), *Catla*, shark (*Scoliodon*), flying fish (*Exocoetus*) |
| **Amphibia** | Cold-blooded | Land+Water | Moist, glandular | Metamorphosis; both gills & lungs | Frog (*Rana*), toad (*Bufo*), salamander |
| **Reptilia** | Cold-blooded | Mostly land | Dry, scaly | Amniotic egg; no metamorphosis | Lizard, snake, crocodile, turtle, gecko (*Hemidactylus*) |
| **Aves** (Birds) | Warm-blooded | Land/Air | Feathers | Hollow bones; four-chambered heart | Pigeon, crow, ostrich (flightless), penguin (flightless) |
| **Mammalia** | Warm-blooded | Land/Water | Hair/fur | Mammary glands; most viviparous | Human, whale, bat, dolphin |

**Special mammals to remember:**
- **Bat:** Only flying mammal (not a bird)
- **Whale / Dolphin / Dugong:** Aquatic mammals (not fish); breathe air
- **Duck-billed Platypus (*Ornithorhynchus*):** Egg-laying mammal (monotreme); also has venom spurs; found in Australia
- **Echidna:** Also egg-laying mammal (monotreme)
- **Kangaroo:** Marsupial — gives birth to undeveloped young; raised in pouch

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Carl Linnaeus = "Father of Taxonomy"; introduced binomial nomenclature in *Systema Naturae* (1758)** — not "Father of Biology" (Aristotle).
2. **Five-Kingdom classification = R.H. Whittaker, 1969** — not Linnaeus (two kingdoms) or Woese (three domains).
3. **Fungi cell wall = Chitin; Plants = Cellulose; Bacteria = Peptidoglycan** — SSC tests all three materials.
4. **Viruses are acellular — discovered by D.J. Ivanowski (1892), tobacco mosaic disease** — Ivanowski showed infectious agent passed through bacterial filter.
5. **Bryophyta = "Amphibians of plant kingdom"** — need water for fertilisation; first land plants but no vascular tissue.
6. **Pteridophyta = first plants with vascular tissue** — but still no seeds.
7. **Gymnosperms = naked seeds (not enclosed); Angiosperms = seeds inside fruit (enclosed)** — the "naked vs enclosed" distinction.
8. **Monocot: 1 cotyledon, parallel venation, fibrous roots; Dicot: 2 cotyledons, reticulate venation, tap root** — all three features together.
9. **Rice, wheat, maize, onion, bamboo = Monocots; Mango, pea, tomato, bean = Dicots** — commonly confused examples.
10. **Arthropoda = largest phylum in animal kingdom** — more species than all other phyla combined.
11. **Insecta = 6 legs; Arachnida = 8 legs** — a very direct and frequent question.
12. **Earthworm = "friend of farmer"; hermaphrodite; phylum Annelida** — all three facts.
13. **Platypus and Echidna = egg-laying mammals (monotremes)** — mammals that lay eggs; common confusion.
14. **Bat = only flying mammal; Whale and Dolphin = aquatic mammals (not fish)** — classification by trait confusion.
15. **Lichen = fungi + algae symbiosis; bio-indicator of air pollution** — lichen die when SO₂ levels rise.
16. **Penicillin comes from *Penicillium*; Agar comes from red algae (*Gelidium*)** — source organisms for these substances are frequently tested.
