# Excretory, Skeletal & Muscular Systems — Kidneys, Bones & Muscles

## The Body's Three Great Infrastructure Systems

A city cannot function without sewage treatment, a structural skeleton, and a workforce that moves. The human body has exactly these three: the **excretory system** disposes of metabolic waste; the **skeletal system** provides the framework; the **muscular system** provides the movement. Understanding them together reveals how they are interdependent — bones anchor muscles, muscles pump blood through kidneys, kidneys clean the blood that nourishes bone marrow.

---

## Part I — The Excretory System

### Why We Must Excrete

Every chemical reaction in your cells produces waste. Protein metabolism generates **ammonia** — a compound so toxic that even small concentrations damage neurons. The liver converts ammonia into the less toxic **urea** (via the ornithine cycle, also called the urea cycle). Urea must then be removed from the blood by the kidneys. If kidney function fails, urea accumulates in blood — a life-threatening condition called **uraemia**.

**The four organs of excretion:**

| Organ | What it excretes |
|---|---|
| **Kidneys** (primary) | Urea, uric acid, creatinine, salts, excess water |
| **Lungs** | CO₂, water vapour |
| **Skin** | Water, NaCl, lactic acid (via sweat) |
| **Liver** | Bile pigments — bilirubin (from haemoglobin breakdown) → excreted in bile → faeces |

---

### The Kidneys

**Basic facts:**
- 2 kidneys; bean-shaped; each ~11 cm long, ~150g
- Located retroperitoneally (behind the peritoneum) on either side of the vertebral column
- The **right kidney** sits slightly lower than the left (displaced by the liver)
- Each kidney contains approximately **1 million nephrons**

**Internal structure:**
- **Cortex** (outer): contains glomeruli and convoluted tubules
- **Medulla** (inner): contains Loop of Henle and collecting ducts; organised into pyramid-shaped structures
- **Renal pelvis**: funnel that collects urine → drains to ureter

---

### The Nephron — Functional Unit of the Kidney

The nephron is the kidney's filtration + reabsorption + secretion unit. Think of it as a factory line with three stages: first pour everything in, then take back what you need, then add a few more things to discard.

**Structure of a nephron:**

| Component | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Bowman's capsule** | Cortex | Cup-like structure; surrounds the glomerulus |
| **Glomerulus** | Inside Bowman's capsule | Tuft of capillaries; high-pressure filtration |
| **Proximal Convoluted Tubule (PCT)** | Cortex | Reabsorbs 60-70% of filtrate: glucose, amino acids, Na⁺, water |
| **Loop of Henle** | Medulla | Creates concentration gradient; descending = water permeable; ascending = Na⁺ pumped out |
| **Distal Convoluted Tubule (DCT)** | Cortex | Selective reabsorption/secretion; regulated by ADH and aldosterone |
| **Collecting duct** | Medulla | Final water reabsorption under ADH control; empties into renal pelvis |

---

### Three Processes of Urine Formation

**1. Ultrafiltration (Glomerular filtration):**
- Blood pressure forces plasma (minus large proteins and blood cells) into Bowman's capsule → **glomerular filtrate**
- Volume filtered per day: **~180 litres** (Glomerular Filtration Rate = ~125 mL/min)
- Only 1.5 litres becomes urine — the rest is reabsorbed

**2. Tubular Reabsorption:**
- PCT reabsorbs all glucose, all amino acids, ~80% Na⁺ and water, all Vitamin C
- Loop of Henle concentrates urine by the counter-current mechanism
- DCT reabsorbs remaining Na⁺ and water (controlled by aldosterone and ADH)
- Normal urine = no glucose, no protein (their presence signals disease)

**3. Tubular Secretion:**
- Substances actively added FROM blood INTO tubule to be excreted
- Examples: excess H⁺ (acid-base balance), K⁺, creatinine, some drugs

---

### Composition of Normal Urine

- ~95% water
- Urea (main nitrogenous waste in humans)
- Uric acid (from purine breakdown)
- Creatinine (from muscle metabolism)
- Salts (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻)
- Urochrome pigment (gives yellow colour — from bilirubin breakdown)

**Species comparison of nitrogenous waste:**
| Group | Waste form | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Humans + mammals | **Urea** (ureotelic) | Water available; urea less toxic |
| Birds, reptiles, insects | **Uric acid** (uricotelic) | Water conservation (very low solubility; solid/paste) |
| Fish, aquatic animals | **Ammonia** (ammonotelic) | Water abundant; excreted directly |

---

### Hormonal Regulation of Kidney Function

- **ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone):** from posterior pituitary; acts on collecting duct → increases water reabsorption → less, more concentrated urine. Deficiency → diabetes insipidus (very dilute, large volume urine)
- **Aldosterone:** from adrenal cortex; acts on DCT → Na⁺ and water reabsorption → blood pressure
- **RAAS (Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System):** Low BP → kidneys release **Renin** → converts Angiotensinogen to Angiotensin I → ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme in lungs) → Angiotensin II → stimulates aldosterone secretion → Na⁺ retention → BP rises
- **PTH (Parathyroid hormone):** increases Ca²⁺ reabsorption in kidney tubules

---

### Kidney Disorders

| Disorder | Description |
|---|---|
| **Renal calculi (Kidney stones)** | Crystals of calcium oxalate (most common), uric acid, or struvite; form in renal pelvis/ureter → severe colicky pain |
| **Nephritis (Bright's disease)** | Inflammation of nephrons (Glomerulonephritis); autoimmune post-streptococcal infection; proteinuria/haematuria |
| **Renal failure** | Acute (reversible) or Chronic (irreversible); uraemia; treated by dialysis or transplant |
| **Gout** | Uric acid crystals deposited in joints (especially big toe — podagra); intensely painful; not a kidney disease per se but linked to impaired uric acid excretion |

**Dialysis:**
- Artificial kidney; cleanses blood when kidneys fail
- **Haemodialysis:** patient's blood passed through dialyser (artificial semi-permeable membrane); waste diffuses out; clean blood returned
- **Peritoneal dialysis:** dialysis fluid injected into abdominal cavity; peritoneum acts as filter

## Part II — The Skeletal System

### The Living Framework

A skeleton is not inert scaffolding — it is living, dynamic tissue constantly remodelling, storing calcium (99% of body's calcium is in bones), and manufacturing blood cells in red marrow. Without bone, you would collapse under gravity; without marrow, you would have no blood.

**Functions of the skeletal system:**
1. Support — body shape and posture
2. Protection — skull protects brain; rib cage protects heart and lungs; vertebral column protects spinal cord
3. Movement — bones are levers; muscles attach to produce movement
4. Haematopoiesis — blood cell production in red bone marrow
5. Mineral storage — calcium and phosphorus reservoir
6. Fat storage — yellow bone marrow stores fat

---

### How Many Bones?

- **Adult: 206 bones** (this is the answer SSC wants)
- At birth: ~270–300 bones (many are cartilage; they gradually ossify and fuse)
- Most fusion happens during childhood and adolescence; largely complete by age 25

**Bone composition:**
- **Ossein (collagen)** — the organic protein matrix; gives flexibility and tensile strength
- **Hydroxyapatite (calcium phosphate)** — the mineral component; gives hardness and compressive strength

---

### Types of Bones

| Type | Example | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Long bones | Femur, humerus, tibia | Longer than wide; shaft + two ends (epiphyses); red marrow at ends in adults |
| Short bones | Carpals, tarsals | Roughly equal dimensions; mostly spongy bone |
| Flat bones | Skull, sternum, scapula, ribs | Thin, flat; large surface area; contain red marrow (primary hematopoiesis in adults) |
| Irregular bones | Vertebrae, facial bones | Complex shapes that don't fit other categories |
| Sesamoid bones | **Patella** (kneecap) | Embedded in tendons; reduce friction |

---

### Division of the Skeleton

**Axial Skeleton — 80 bones (central axis of body):**

| Component | Count | Key details |
|---|---|---|
| **Skull** | 22 | 8 cranial bones (protect brain) + 14 facial bones; mandible = only movable skull bone |
| **Vertebral column** | 33 | Cervical 7 + Thoracic 12 + Lumbar 5 + Sacral 5 (fused) + Coccygeal 4 (fused) |
| **Sternum** | 1 | Breastbone; 3 parts: manubrium + body + xiphoid process |
| **Ribs** | 24 (12 pairs) | 7 true + 3 false + 2 floating |

**Rib classification:**
- **True ribs (1–7):** attached directly to sternum by costal cartilage
- **False ribs (8–10):** connect to sternum indirectly via the 7th rib's costal cartilage
- **Floating ribs (11–12):** free ends; not attached to sternum at all

**Vertebral column mnemonic — cervical 7:**
> Every mammal — giraffe, human, whale, elephant — has exactly **7 cervical vertebrae**. Only sloths (6 or 9) are exceptions.

**Appendicular Skeleton — 126 bones (limbs and girdles):**
- Pectoral girdle (clavicle + scapula) × 2 = 4
- Upper limb × 2: Humerus + Radius + Ulna + 8 carpals + 5 metacarpals + 14 phalanges = 30 × 2 = 60
- Pelvic girdle (hip bone = ilium + ischium + pubis fused) × 2 = 2
- Lower limb × 2: Femur + Patella + Tibia + Fibula + 7 tarsals + 5 metatarsals + 14 phalanges = 30 × 2 = 60

---

### Bone Marrow

| Type | Location | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Red marrow** | Flat bones in adults (sternum, ribs, pelvis, skull, vertebrae); spongy ends of long bones in children | Haematopoiesis (makes RBCs, WBCs, platelets) |
| **Yellow marrow** | Shaft (diaphysis) of long bones in adults | Fat storage; can revert to red marrow under stress (blood loss, severe anaemia) |

---

### Joints

A joint is where two or more bones meet. The type of joint determines the type of movement possible.

| Joint Type | Examples | Movement |
|---|---|---|
| **Ball-and-socket** | Hip, shoulder | Maximum range — flexion, extension, rotation, circumduction (all directions) |
| **Hinge** | Elbow (humerus-ulna), knee (femur-tibia), interphalangeal | Flexion + extension only (one axis, like a door hinge) |
| **Pivot** | Atlas-axis (top of vertebral column) | Rotation only (allows you to shake your head "no") |
| **Gliding/Plane** | Between carpals/tarsals, between vertebrae | Sliding movement (limited) |
| **Saddle** | Thumb base (1st carpometacarpal) | Biaxial movement; unique to thumb; enables thumb's grip |
| **Condyloid/Ellipsoid** | Wrist (radiocarpal) | Two axes (flexion/extension + abduction/adduction) |
| **Fixed (Fibrous/Suture)** | Skull bones | No movement; bones bound by fibrous tissue |

**Cartilage** (avascular — no blood supply):
- Hyaline cartilage: articular surfaces of joints, tracheal rings, costal cartilage (most common)
- Fibrocartilage: intervertebral discs, pubic symphysis (shock absorption)
- Elastic cartilage: outer ear (pinna), epiglottis (flexibility)

**Skeletal disorders:**
| Disorder | Cause | Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Rickets | Vitamin D deficiency in children | Soft bones → bowed legs, delayed teething |
| Osteomalacia | Vitamin D deficiency in adults | Soft bones, bone pain, fractures |
| Osteoporosis | Low bone density (ageing, low Ca²⁺/Vit D, oestrogen drop in menopause) | Fragile bones, fracture risk (vertebrae, hip, wrist) |
| Arthritis (Osteoarthritis) | Cartilage wear and tear | Joint pain, stiffness, especially weight-bearing joints |
| Rheumatoid arthritis | Autoimmune — immune attacks synovial membrane | Inflammation, deformity of multiple joints |

## Part III — The Muscular System

### Three Kinds of Muscle

The body has three types of muscle tissue, each with a different location, control, and microscopic appearance:

| Type | Control | Striated? | Nuclei | Location | Key feature |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Skeletal** (Striated, Voluntary) | Voluntary | Yes | Multi-nucleate | Attached to bones | Responsible for all conscious movement |
| **Smooth** (Visceral, Involuntary) | Involuntary | No | Single nucleus | Walls of hollow organs (stomach, intestine, blood vessels, bladder) | Slow, sustained contraction |
| **Cardiac** | Involuntary | Yes | Single nucleus (with intercalated discs) | Heart wall only | Never fatigues; contracts rhythmically without external nerve signal |

**Cardiac muscle's unique feature:** Intercalated discs (specialised junctions) allow electrical signals to spread directly between cardiac muscle cells, so the entire heart contracts as one coordinated unit.

---

### The Record-Holders in Muscles

| Record | Muscle |
|---|---|
| **Largest muscle** | Gluteus maximus (buttock) |
| **Longest muscle** | Sartorius (crosses both hip and knee) |
| **Smallest muscle** | Stapedius (in middle ear; controls stapes bone) |
| **Strongest muscle (relative to size)** | Masseter (jaw muscle) |
| **Hardest-working** | Heart (cardiac muscle — never rests) |

---

### How Muscles Contract — Sliding Filament Theory

Proposed by Huxley and Hanson (1954). Every muscle fibre contains parallel **sarcomeres** — the basic contractile units:

- Each sarcomere has thick **myosin filaments** and thin **actin filaments**
- During contraction: myosin heads attach to actin → pull actin filaments inward → sarcomere shortens
- Result: muscle fibre shortens (contracts)

**Energy source:** ATP (from cellular respiration). Calcium ions (Ca²⁺) — released from sarcoplasmic reticulum when a nerve signal arrives — trigger the actin-myosin interaction.

**Rigor mortis:** After death, ATP production stops → Ca²⁺ remains high → actin-myosin stay locked → muscles become rigid (onset 2-4 hours, maximum 12 hours, resolves after ~48-60 hours as proteins break down).

---

### Connective Tissue Links

| Structure | Connects | Composition |
|---|---|---|
| **Tendons** | Muscle → Bone | Fibrous collagen; inelastic; transmit force precisely |
| **Ligaments** | Bone → Bone | Collagen + elastin; slightly elastic; stabilise joints |

> **Mnemonic:** "Tender Muscles Bite Bones, Let Both Be" → Tendons = Muscle to Bone; Ligaments = Bone to Bone

Sprains = ligament injury (overstretched/torn at joint); Strains = muscle/tendon injury.

---

### Muscular Disorders

| Disorder | Cause | Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Muscular Dystrophy** (DMD) | Genetic; X-linked recessive; mutation in dystrophin gene | Progressive muscle weakness and wasting; primarily affects boys |
| **Myasthenia Gravis** | Autoimmune; antibodies attack ACh receptors at neuromuscular junction | Muscle weakness worsening with activity; drooping eyelids |
| **Tetanus** | Clostridium tetani toxin (tetanospasmin) blocks inhibitory signals | Spastic paralysis; lockjaw (trismus); vaccine-preventable |
| **Botulism** | Clostridium botulinum toxin blocks ACh release | Flaccid paralysis (muscles cannot contract); can cause respiratory failure |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Adult human body has 206 bones** — not 208, not 212. Infants have ~270–300 (cartilage fuses over time).
2. **Nephron = functional unit of the kidney** — not neuron (the nerve cell).
3. **Urea = main nitrogenous waste in humans (ureotelic); birds/reptiles = uric acid; fish = ammonia** — the form of waste reveals the organism's lifestyle.
4. **180 litres of blood filtrate is produced per day; only 1.5 litres becomes urine** — extraordinary 99% reabsorption efficiency.
5. **Tendons connect muscle to bone; Ligaments connect bone to bone** — tendons transmit force; ligaments stabilise joints.
6. **Gluteus maximus = largest muscle; Stapedius = smallest muscle; Sartorius = longest muscle** — all three facts are exam favorites.
7. **Ball-and-socket joint (hip, shoulder) allows the greatest range of movement** — can move in all planes (circumduction).
8. **Cardiac muscle is involuntary but striated** — this uniqueness catches many off guard (skeletal = voluntary + striated; smooth = involuntary + unstriated; cardiac = involuntary + striated).
9. **12 pairs of ribs: 7 true + 3 false + 2 floating** — total 24 ribs. True ribs attach DIRECTLY to sternum.
10. **All mammals have 7 cervical vertebrae** — including the giraffe (very long neck, still only 7 very large vertebrae).
11. **Red bone marrow in flat bones (in adults) produces RBCs, WBCs, and platelets** — long bone shafts have yellow marrow (fat storage).
12. **Gout = uric acid crystals depositing in joints** (especially big toe) — it is linked to excess uric acid, NOT excess urea.
13. **ADH from posterior pituitary acts on collecting duct** — increases water reabsorption; its absence causes diabetes insipidus.
14. **Dialysis = artificial kidney** — haemodialysis is the most common form; the dialysis membrane mimics the glomerulus.
15. **Skin excretes sweat (NaCl + lactic acid + water); Lungs excrete CO₂ and water vapour; Liver excretes bile pigments** — kidneys are not the only excretory organs.
16. **Rickets = Vitamin D deficiency in children (bowed legs); Osteomalacia = same deficiency in adults (soft bones); Osteoporosis = low bone density (fragile bones)** — three different conditions, all linked to calcium/Vitamin D.
