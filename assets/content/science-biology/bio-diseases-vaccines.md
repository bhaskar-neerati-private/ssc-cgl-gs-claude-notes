# Human Diseases — Bacterial, Viral, Protozoan, Fungal & Vaccines

## The Invisible War

In 1854, Dr. John Snow traced a cholera epidemic in London to a single water pump on Broad Street — not to "bad air" (the miasma theory that had ruled medicine for centuries), but to contaminated water. By removing the pump handle, he stopped the outbreak. It was perhaps the first time epidemiology saved lives, decades before anyone could see the pathogen responsible.

Understanding disease — what causes it, how it spreads, and how the body and medicine fight back — is one of the highest-yield topics in SSC biology. The examiners return to it again and again because it sits at the intersection of science, public health, and current events.

---

## Classification of Human Diseases

**Communicable (Infectious):** Caused by pathogens; spread from person to person or through vectors.
**Non-communicable:** Not infectious; caused by lifestyle factors, genetic mutations, environmental exposure, or nutrient deficiency.

**Types of pathogens:**
- Bacteria (prokaryotes; treated by antibiotics)
- Viruses (non-cellular; treated by antivirals; prevented by vaccines)
- Protozoa (unicellular eukaryotes; treated by antiprotozoals)
- Fungi (eukaryotes; treated by antifungals)
- Helminths/Worms (multicellular parasites; treated by anthelmintics)
- Prions (misfolded proteins; no treatment — extremely rare)

## Bacterial Diseases

### Tuberculosis (TB)
- **Causative agent:** *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (discovered by Robert Koch, 1882 — "Koch's bacillus")
- **Transmission:** Airborne (droplet nuclei; coughing, sneezing, speaking)
- **Primary site:** Lungs (pulmonary TB), but can affect any organ (extra-pulmonary: TB meningitis, skeletal, renal)
- **Key features:** Chronic cough (>2 weeks), blood-stained sputum, weight loss, night sweats, fever
- **Diagnosis:** Sputum smear (AFB stain), Mantoux (tuberculin) skin test, Chest X-ray, GeneXpert (rapid molecular test)
- **Treatment:** DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course) — 6-month regimen: Isoniazid + Rifampicin + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol (first 2 months), then Isoniazid + Rifampicin (4 months)
- **Vaccine:** BCG (Bacille Calmette-Guérin) — live attenuated *Mycobacterium bovis*; given at birth in India; prevents severe TB in children (TB meningitis, miliary TB)
- **India:** Highest TB burden in the world; National TB Elimination Programme (NTEP) targets elimination by 2025
- **Multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB):** resistant to Isoniazid and Rifampicin — major global health crisis

---

### Typhoid (Enteric Fever)
- **Causative agent:** *Salmonella typhi*
- **Transmission:** Fecal-oral route — contaminated food and water ("disease of dirty water")
- **Key features:** Sustained high fever (stepladder pattern — rises daily), rose spots (pale pink spots on abdomen), bradycardia (slow heart rate relative to fever — paradoxical), hepatosplenomegaly, constipation then diarrhoea
- **Diagnosis:** Widal test (detects antibodies); blood culture (gold standard in first week)
- **Treatment:** Ciprofloxacin, ceftriaxone, azithromycin (chloramphenicol was classic but resistance emerged)
- **Carrier:** Mary Mallon ("Typhoid Mary") — asymptomatic carrier who infected 51 people as a cook in early 20th century USA

---

### Cholera
- **Causative agent:** *Vibrio cholerae* (El Tor biotype = current pandemic strain, 7th pandemic since 1961)
- **Transmission:** Fecal-oral route — contaminated water; "comma-shaped" bacterium
- **Key features:** Profuse, painless "rice-water" stools (clear, watery — appearance resembles rice grains); rapid dehydration and electrolyte loss → hypovolaemic shock; vomiting
- **Treatment:** ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution — water + glucose + NaCl + KCl) is the cornerstone; antibiotics (tetracycline, doxycycline) shorten duration
- **Prevention:** Safe water, sanitation (WASH interventions)

---

### Plague
- **Causative agent:** *Yersinia pestis*
- **Transmission:** Bite of infected **rat fleas** (*Xenopsylla cheopis*); infected rats are the primary reservoir; pneumonic plague spreads person-to-person via aerosols
- **Forms:** Bubonic plague (swollen lymph nodes = "buboes"); Pneumonic plague (lung infection); Septicaemic plague (blood infection)
- **History:** "Black Death" — 14th century pandemic killed ~25 million Europeans (1/3 of Europe's population); India outbreaks: Surat 1994
- **Treatment:** Streptomycin, doxycycline, ciprofloxacin

---

### Leprosy (Hansen's Disease)
- **Causative agent:** *Mycobacterium leprae* (Hansen, 1873 — first bacterium proven to cause human disease)
- **Transmission:** Prolonged close contact with an untreated patient; droplets from nose/mouth; slowest-growing bacterium known (~13 days per generation)
- **Affects:** Peripheral nerves → numbness → injuries go unnoticed → deformities; skin (pale patches with reduced sensation); eyes
- **Treatment:** MDT (Multi-Drug Therapy) — Rifampicin + Dapsone + Clofazimine (6 months for PB, 12 months for MB)
- **India:** India eliminated leprosy (as a public health problem: <1 case per 10,000 population) in **December 2005** — but still reports highest absolute number of new cases globally

---

### Tetanus
- **Causative agent:** *Clostridium tetani* (anaerobic, Gram-positive rod; forms spores in soil)
- **Entry:** Deep puncture wounds, unclean cuts, animal bites, unsterile delivery in neonates
- **Toxin:** Tetanospasmin — travels to spinal cord/brain; blocks release of inhibitory neurotransmitters (glycine, GABA) → motor neurons fire uncontrollably → **spastic paralysis**
- **Symptoms:** Lockjaw (trismus — jaw muscles spasm), risus sardonicus (grinning expression from facial muscle spasm), opisthotonos (backward arching of body), respiratory failure
- **Treatment:** Tetanus immunoglobulin (neutralise toxin), wound cleaning, benzodiazepines (muscle relaxants), mechanical ventilation
- **Vaccine:** **Tetanus Toxoid (TT)** — part of DPT in childhood; booster every 10 years; pregnant women vaccinated (TT to prevent neonatal tetanus)

---

### Diphtheria and Whooping Cough (Pertussis)
| Aspect | Diphtheria | Pertussis (Whooping Cough) |
|---|---|---|
| Agent | *Corynebacterium diphtheriae* | *Bordetella pertussis* |
| Transmission | Airborne | Airborne (highly contagious) |
| Key feature | **Pseudomembrane** in throat/larynx (grey-white membrane that bleeds when removed) → airway obstruction; diphtheria toxin → heart and nerve damage | Paroxysmal cough ("whoop" sound on inspiration), post-tussive vomiting, subconjunctival haemorrhages; infants most severely affected |
| Vaccine | DPT (combined with Pertussis, Tetanus) | DPT |

## Viral Diseases

### Influenza
- **Causative agent:** Influenza viruses A, B, C (RNA viruses, Orthomyxoviridae)
- **Transmission:** Airborne droplets; highly contagious
- **Subtypes of Influenza A** (named by H and N surface proteins): H1N1 (Swine flu — 2009 pandemic), H3N2 (seasonal), H5N1 (Bird flu — highly lethal in humans), H7N9
- **Antigenic drift:** Minor mutations in surface antigens → seasonal flu vaccines must be updated annually
- **Antigenic shift:** Major reassortment of gene segments → pandemic potential (H1N1 1918 Spanish flu = 50 million deaths)
- **Treatment:** Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) within 48 hours of symptoms; annual flu vaccine

---

### HIV/AIDS
- **Causative agent:** Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) — **Retrovirus** (RNA virus with reverse transcriptase enzyme)
- **Transmission:** Unprotected sexual contact, sharing contaminated needles, blood transfusion, mother-to-child (vertical) during pregnancy/delivery/breastfeeding
- **Target cell:** **CD4⁺ T lymphocytes** (helper T cells) — essential coordinators of immune response
- **Pathogenesis:** HIV integrates into CD4⁺ T cell DNA → slow destruction of CD4⁺ cells → immune system collapses → AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome) defined when CD4 count <200 cells/μL or AIDS-defining illness occurs
- **AIDS-defining infections:** *Pneumocystis jirovecii* pneumonia (PCP), Cryptococcal meningitis, CMV retinitis, Toxoplasmosis, Kaposi's sarcoma
- **Diagnosis:** ELISA (screening → detects antibodies); Western Blot (confirmatory)
- **Treatment:** ART (Antiretroviral Therapy) — HAART (Highly Active ART) — suppresses viral replication to undetectable levels; not a cure but allows near-normal life expectancy
- **India:** Second highest HIV burden globally; NACO coordinates response

---

### Dengue
- **Causative agent:** Dengue virus (DENV 1-4, Flavivirus, 4 serotypes)
- **Vector:** **Female *Aedes aegypti* mosquito** (breeds in clean, stagnant water — flower pots, tyres, coolers; bites during the day — unlike malaria mosquito which bites at night)
- **Key features:** High fever, severe headache, retro-orbital (behind eyes) pain, myalgia (muscle pain), arthralgia (joint pain — "breakbone fever"), skin rash
- **Danger signs:** **Dengue Haemorrhagic Fever (DHF)** — thrombocytopenia (low platelets), bleeding; **Dengue Shock Syndrome** — plasma leakage → shock
- **Diagnosis:** NS1 antigen (early), IgM/IgG antibodies; platelet count
- **Treatment:** Supportive only (no specific antiviral); fluid management; avoid aspirin (increases bleeding)
- **Second infection:** More severe because of antibody-dependent enhancement (ADE) — antibodies from first serotype infection can actually enhance infection by a different serotype

### Polio (Poliomyelitis)
- **Causative agent:** Poliovirus (RNA virus, Picornaviridae, 3 serotypes: 1, 2, 3)
- **Transmission:** **Fecal-oral route** (contaminated water and food)
- **Pathogenesis:** Most infections asymptomatic; in <1%: enters CNS → destroys motor neurons in anterior horn of spinal cord → **flaccid (limp) paralysis** (asymmetric; lower limbs more affected) — muscle wasting, deformity
- **Vaccine:** **OPV** (Oral Polio Vaccine — Sabin — live attenuated; 3 drops orally); **IPV** (Inactivated Polio Vaccine — Salk — injection); India uses both in immunisation schedule
- **India:** India was declared **Polio-free in March 2014** (last wild poliovirus case in Howrah, West Bengal, January 2011)
- **Global:** Near eradication — only Afghanistan and Pakistan still have endemic transmission of wild poliovirus

---

### Hepatitis
Five hepatitis viruses (A, B, C, D, E) all cause liver inflammation but differ crucially:

| Virus | Transmission | Vaccine | Chronic infection? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hepatitis A (HAV)** | Fecal-oral (contaminated water/food) | Yes | No (self-limiting) |
| **Hepatitis B (HBV)** | Blood, sexual, vertical (mother→child) | **Yes (recombinant vaccine)** | Yes — 5-10% adults; 90% of infected infants → cirrhosis, liver cancer |
| **Hepatitis C (HCV)** | Blood (sharing needles), transfusion | **No vaccine** | Yes — 70-85% → chronic; leading cause of liver transplant; curable with DAAs (Direct-Acting Antivirals) |
| **Hepatitis D (HDV)** | Blood; only with HBV co-infection | HBV vaccine prevents D | Worsens HBV |
| **Hepatitis E (HEV)** | Fecal-oral | Yes (China only) | No (except in pregnant women — can be severe) |

### Rabies
- **Causative agent:** Rabies virus (Rhabdovirus, RNA virus)
- **Transmission:** Saliva of infected animal — usually through bite (dogs 99% in India; also bats, foxes, jackals)
- **Pathogenesis:** Travels along nerves to brain → fatal encephalitis; 100% fatal once symptoms appear
- **Symptoms:** Hydrophobia (fear of water — due to painful throat spasms when swallowing), aerophobia, agitation, seizures, coma
- **Prevention:** Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) = wound cleaning + rabies immunoglobulin + rabies vaccine series (if bitten by suspected rabid animal)
- **Pre-exposure:** Vaccination recommended for veterinarians, wildlife workers, travellers to endemic areas

---

### Smallpox (Historical)
- **Causative agent:** Variola major virus
- **History:** Edward Jenner used cowpox (vaccinia) to create the first vaccine (1796); mass vaccination campaigns throughout 20th century
- **Eradication:** WHO declared smallpox **eradicated on 8 May 1980** — the **only human infectious disease completely eradicated** from the world (last natural case: Ali Maow Maalin, Somalia, 1977)
- Only two laboratories now hold Variola virus: CDC (Atlanta, USA) and VECTOR Institute (Novosibirsk, Russia)

---

### COVID-19
- **Causative agent:** SARS-CoV-2 (Betacoronavirus, RNA virus)
- **Pandemic:** WHO declared pandemic on 11 March 2020
- **Transmission:** Airborne/respiratory droplets; fomites
- **Vaccines in India:** Covishield (Oxford-AstraZeneca, manufactured by Serum Institute of India); Covaxin (Bharat Biotech, India's first indigenous COVID vaccine); Sputnik V (Russian); later — Corbevax, ZyCoV-D (world's first DNA vaccine for COVID)

## Protozoan Diseases

### Malaria
- **Causative agent:** *Plasmodium* (4 main species)

| Species | Fever cycle | Severity | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| *P. falciparum* | Every 36-48 hours | **Most dangerous** — cerebral malaria, blackwater fever | Most drug-resistant; Africa, India |
| *P. vivax* | Every 48 hours (Benign tertian) | Less severe; dormant liver forms (hypnozoites) cause relapses | Most common in India |
| *P. malariae* | Every 72 hours (Quartan) | Mild | — |
| *P. ovale* | Every 48 hours | Mild; rare | Mainly West Africa |

- **Vector:** **Female *Anopheles* mosquito** (bites at night; breeds in stagnant/slow-moving water)
- **Discovery:** Charles Laveran discovered *Plasmodium* in RBCs (1880, Nobel Prize 1907); **Ronald Ross** discovered the mosquito vector (1897, Nobel Prize 1902) — working in Secunderabad, India
- **Life cycle:** Sporozoites in mosquito saliva → injected into human → liver cells (exoerythrocytic stage) → RBCs (erythrocytic stage) → merozoites → fever when RBCs rupture → gametocytes → taken up by mosquito
- **Symptoms:** Cyclical fever with rigors, chills, sweating; anaemia (RBCs destroyed); splenomegaly; jaundice
- **Treatment:** Chloroquine (for sensitive strains); ACT — Artemisinin-based Combination Therapy (for resistant falciparum); Primaquine (clears liver stage of P. vivax)
- **Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT):** Detects malaria antigens

---

### Amoebic Dysentery
- **Causative agent:** *Entamoeba histolytica*
- **Transmission:** Fecal-oral (contaminated water/food/hands)
- **Key features:** Bloody, mucoid diarrhoea; abdominal cramps; can cause liver abscess (amoebiasis)
- **Treatment:** Metronidazole (Flagyl)

---

### Kala-azar (Visceral Leishmaniasis)
- **Causative agent:** *Leishmania donovani*
- **Vector:** **Sandfly (*Phlebotomus*)** — bites at dusk and night
- **Key features:** Prolonged fever, massive splenomegaly (enlarged spleen), hepatomegaly, extreme wasting, darkening of skin (Kala-azar = "black fever" in Hindi/Urdu — after the skin pigmentation)
- **Endemic in India:** Bihar, Jharkhand, UP, West Bengal
- **Treatment:** Miltefosine (oral, first-line in India); Amphotericin B

---

### Sleeping Sickness (African Trypanosomiasis)
- **Causative agent:** *Trypanosoma brucei*
- **Vector:** **Tsetse fly** (*Glossina*)
- **Key features:** Fever, lymphadenopathy → CNS invasion → confusion, sleep disturbances (sleeping sickness), coma, death
- **Distribution:** Sub-Saharan Africa only (tsetse fly habitat)

## Fungal Diseases

### Ringworm (Tinea)
- **Causative agent:** Dermatophytes (fungi: *Trichophyton*, *Microsporum*, *Epidermophyton*) — NOT a worm!
- **Forms:** Tinea corporis (body), Tinea capitis (scalp), Tinea pedis (athlete's foot — between toes), Tinea cruris (groin — "jock itch"), Tinea unguium (nails — onychomycosis)
- **Treatment:** Antifungal creams (clotrimazole, miconazole); oral terbinafine or griseofulvin for scalp/nail

### Candidiasis (Thrush)
- **Causative agent:** *Candida albicans* (normally a commensal)
- **Occurs:** When immune system is compromised (HIV/AIDS, diabetes, prolonged antibiotics, steroids); also vaginal candidiasis in pregnancy
- **Forms:** Oral thrush (white plaques), vaginal thrush, invasive candidiasis (bloodstream — serious in ICU patients)

### Aspergillosis
- **Causative agent:** *Aspergillus fumigatus* (mould spores inhaled; ubiquitous in environment)
- **Occurs:** In immunocompromised patients (chemotherapy, organ transplant, HIV); can form pulmonary aspergilloma (fungal ball in pre-existing lung cavity)

## Helminth (Worm) Diseases

| Disease | Causative Worm | Vector/Route | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Filariasis (Elephantiasis)** | *Wuchereria bancrofti* | **Culex mosquito** | Lymph vessel blockage → massive limb swelling (elephantiasis) |
| **Ascariasis (Roundworm)** | *Ascaris lumbricoides* | Fecal-oral (eggs) | Largest intestinal roundworm; abdominal pain; pulmonary migration causes cough |
| **Taeniasis (Tapeworm)** | *Taenia solium* (pork tapeworm); *T. saginata* (beef tapeworm) | Eating undercooked pork/beef | *T. solium* cysts (cysticerci) in brain → neurocysticercosis (seizures) |
| **Hookworm** | *Ancylostoma duodenale*, *Necator americanus* | Skin penetration (walking barefoot) | Iron-deficiency anaemia (worms suck blood) |

## Vaccines — The Immune System's Memory

**Immunisation** works by presenting the immune system with an antigen (part of or a killed/weakened pathogen) → B cells produce specific antibodies + memory cells → future infection encounters primed memory → rapid, protective response.

**Types of vaccines:**
| Type | Mechanism | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Live attenuated | Weakened pathogen; strongest immunity | BCG, OPV, MMR, Yellow fever |
| Inactivated (killed) | Killed pathogen; safer; multiple doses needed | IPV (Salk polio), Hepatitis A, Influenza (injectable) |
| Toxoid | Inactivated toxin | Tetanus toxoid, Diphtheria toxoid (part of DPT) |
| Subunit/Recombinant | Purified protein antigens | Hepatitis B (recombinant HBsAg), HPV vaccine |
| mRNA vaccine | mRNA instructs cells to make antigen | COVID-19 Pfizer-BioNTech, Moderna |
| DNA vaccine | DNA plasmid | ZyCoV-D (COVID-19) — world's first approved DNA vaccine |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Malaria is caused by Plasmodium — a PROTOZOAN, NOT a bacterium or virus** — a classic mislabelling trap.
2. **Female *Anopheles* mosquito transmits malaria; *Aedes aegypti* (female) transmits dengue** — both female, different mosquito genera.
3. ***P. falciparum* is the most dangerous malaria species** — causes cerebral malaria; most drug-resistant; highest mortality.
4. **Ringworm is caused by a FUNGUS (Dermatophyte), NOT a worm** — the name is completely misleading.
5. **Smallpox was eradicated in 1980 — the only human infectious disease completely eradicated** — the last natural case was in Somalia in 1977.
6. **Edward Jenner (1796) used cowpox (vaccinia) to prevent smallpox — the world's first vaccine** — not Pasteur (who came later).
7. **BCG vaccine is for Tuberculosis** — does NOT prevent TB infection in adults but prevents severe childhood forms (TB meningitis, miliary TB).
8. **HIV is a retrovirus** (RNA virus with reverse transcriptase); ELISA screens for antibodies; Western blot confirms. CD4⁺ T cells are destroyed.
9. **Kala-azar is transmitted by sandfly (Phlebotomus); sleeping sickness by tsetse fly (Glossina)** — often confused in exams.
10. **India was declared polio-free in 2014** (last case 2011, WHO certification March 2014).
11. **Tetanus = spastic paralysis** (blocks inhibitory signals); **Botulism = flaccid paralysis** (blocks ACh release) — same genus (*Clostridium*), opposite effects.
12. **Hepatitis B has a vaccine; Hepatitis C does NOT** (but HCV is now curable with direct-acting antivirals like Sofosbuvir). HCV is blood-borne; most common cause of liver transplant.
13. **DPT vaccine**: D = Diphtheria (toxoid), P = Pertussis (whooping cough), T = Tetanus (toxoid).
14. **Filariasis (elephantiasis) is caused by *Wuchereria bancrofti* and transmitted by *Culex* mosquito** — NOT Aedes or Anopheles.
15. **Ronald Ross discovered the role of Anopheles mosquito in malaria transmission in 1897 while working in Secunderabad, India** — Nobel Prize 1902.
16. **Dengue has 4 serotypes; second infection with a different serotype is more dangerous** (antibody-dependent enhancement). Aedes mosquito bites during the DAY (unlike malaria mosquito which bites at night).
