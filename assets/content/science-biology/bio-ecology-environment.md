# Ecology — Food Chains, Pyramids, Biomes & Environmental Issues

## Life in Web

In 1944, a small island off Alaska was chosen for an experiment. 29 reindeer were introduced to St. Matthew Island — a pristine wilderness with no predators and abundant lichen. By 1963, the population had exploded to 6,000 animals. By 1966, it had crashed to 42 — and the island's vegetation had been stripped bare.

No predator killed them. They ate themselves out of existence.

This is ecology's most sobering lesson: every population exists within a web of relationships — with its food, its predators, its competitors, and its environment. Remove any thread from that web, and the entire fabric can unravel.

---

## What Is Ecology?

**Ecology** = the scientific study of interactions between organisms and their environment.
- Coined by German biologist **Ernst Haeckel** (1866)
- "Oikos" (Greek) = household; "logos" = study of

**Levels of ecological organisation (ascending scale):**

Individual → **Population** (all members of one species in one area) → **Community** (all populations in an area) → **Ecosystem** (community + abiotic environment) → **Biome** (large geographic region with characteristic climate and vegetation) → **Biosphere** (all ecosystems on Earth — the global life zone)

---

## The Ecosystem

An ecosystem is a functional unit of nature — a community of living organisms interacting with each other and with their non-living (abiotic) environment.

| Component | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Biotic** (living) | Plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, decomposers |
| **Abiotic** (non-living) | Sunlight, temperature, rainfall, soil, water, minerals, CO₂, O₂ |

**Types of ecosystems:**
- **Natural:** Forests, grasslands, oceans, rivers, wetlands, deserts
- **Artificial (Man-made):** Crop fields (agroecosystems), aquaria, gardens, urban greenery

## Food Chains and Food Webs

### Food Chain
A linear sequence of organisms where each is eaten by the next — transfer of energy and matter from one trophic level to the next.

**Typical terrestrial food chain:**
> **Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Hawk**
> (Producer → Primary consumer → Secondary consumer → Tertiary consumer → Quaternary consumer)

**Two main types:**
| Type | Starting point | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Grazing Food Chain (GFC)** | Living green plants (producers) | Grass → Deer → Tiger |
| **Detritus Food Chain (DFC)** | Dead organic matter (detritus) | Leaf litter → Earthworm → Robin → Hawk |

> The Detritus Food Chain is the dominant energy pathway in most terrestrial ecosystems — the energy locked in dead plant material is far greater than that consumed by herbivores.

---

### Food Web
A network of multiple interconnected food chains. More realistic than a single food chain — most organisms eat more than one thing and are eaten by more than one predator.

**Why food webs are more stable than food chains:**
- More alternative energy pathways
- If one species declines, others can compensate
- Longer food chains are more vulnerable to disruption at any level

---

## Trophic Levels

| Trophic Level | Name | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| T1 | **Producers (Autotrophs)** | Green plants, algae, phytoplankton |
| T2 | **Primary consumers (Herbivores)** | Rabbit, grasshopper, deer, cow, caterpillar |
| T3 | **Secondary consumers (Carnivores)** | Frog, snake, small fish, fox |
| T4 | **Tertiary consumers (Top predators)** | Eagle, shark, tiger, lion |
| — | **Decomposers (Saprotrophs)** | Bacteria, fungi — break down dead organic matter |

**Decomposers** are essential but are NOT usually shown in food chains/pyramids — they operate at every level, breaking down dead matter from all trophic levels and returning minerals to the soil.

---

## Ecological Pyramids

Ecological pyramids represent the quantitative relationship between trophic levels. Three types:

### 1. Pyramid of Numbers
- Represents the **number of individual organisms** at each trophic level
- Usually upright in grassland ecosystems (many grasses → many insects → fewer frogs → fewer snakes)
- **Can be INVERTED:** e.g., a single large tree (T1) supports thousands of insects (T2) — the base (T1) has fewer individuals than T2

### 2. Pyramid of Biomass
- Represents **total living mass (dry weight)** at each trophic level
- Usually upright in terrestrial ecosystems (lots of plant biomass → less herbivore biomass → even less carnivore)
- **Can be INVERTED in aquatic (marine) ecosystems:** Phytoplankton (T1) have very low total biomass at any given moment because they reproduce extremely rapidly (high turnover rate), but they support a much larger standing biomass of zooplankton and fish

### 3. Pyramid of Energy
- Represents **total energy flow** at each trophic level
- **ALWAYS upright — NEVER inverted**
- This is because energy is always lost at each trophic level (as heat, for movement, in undigested matter) — energy at a higher level can NEVER exceed that at the level below

---

## The 10% Rule (Lindeman's Law, 1942)

**Only approximately 10% of energy at any trophic level is transferred to the next level.** The remaining ~90% is:
- Lost as heat during respiration
- Used for movement, reproduction, and growth of the organism
- Passed in undigested material (faeces)
- Lost to decomposers

**Implication:** Food chains are typically limited to 3–5 trophic levels. By the 5th level, so little energy remains that populations cannot be sustained.

**Example:**
- 10,000 kcal of plant energy → 1,000 kcal to herbivores → 100 kcal to first carnivore → 10 kcal to second carnivore

**Why eating lower on the food chain is more energy-efficient:** A person eating grain directly receives 10% of the plant's energy; if the grain feeds cattle, and then we eat the cattle, we receive only 1% of the original plant energy.

## Nutrient Cycling (Biogeochemical Cycles)

### Carbon Cycle
- **Photosynthesis:** CO₂ → organic carbon (in plants)
- **Respiration:** Organic carbon → CO₂ (by all living things)
- **Combustion:** Fossil fuels and biomass → CO₂ (human activity — increasing atmospheric CO₂)
- **Decomposition:** Dead organic matter → CO₂ (by bacteria/fungi)
- **Ocean sink:** CO₂ dissolves in oceans (forms carbonic acid → acidification of oceans)

### Nitrogen Cycle
| Process | Organisms | Transformation |
|---|---|---|
| **Nitrogen fixation** | Rhizobium (legumes), Azotobacter, Anabaena | N₂ → NH₃ (usable form) |
| **Nitrification** | Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter | NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (absorbed by plants) |
| **Assimilation** | Plants, then animals | NO₃⁻ → amino acids, proteins |
| **Ammonification** | Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) | Dead organic matter → NH₃ |
| **Denitrification** | Pseudomonas, other bacteria | NO₃⁻ → N₂ (returns to atmosphere) |

### Phosphorus Cycle
- **No gaseous phase** (unlike C and N cycles) — P cycles between rock/soil and living organisms through weathering and biological uptake
- Essential for DNA, RNA, ATP, and bone/teeth

### Water Cycle (Hydrological Cycle)
- Evaporation (from water bodies) + Transpiration (from plants) = Evapotranspiration
- Condensation → precipitation (rain, snow)
- Runoff → rivers → oceans; Infiltration → groundwater

## Biomes — Major Terrestrial Biomes

A biome is a large area of the Earth with a characteristic climate, vegetation, and associated wildlife.

| Biome | Climate | Key Vegetation | Key Animals | Location |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tropical Rainforest** | Hot, wet year-round (>200cm rain) | Tallest trees, 4 layers (emergent/canopy/understory/floor), highest biodiversity | Jaguars, toucans, tree frogs, gorillas | Amazon, Congo, SE Asia |
| **Tropical Savanna (Grassland)** | Hot; distinct wet + dry seasons | Grassland with scattered trees (acacia, baobab) | Elephants, lions, zebras, wildebeest, giraffes | Africa, India (Deccan) |
| **Desert (Hot)** | Hot days, cold nights; <250mm rain/year | Cacti, succulents, thorny shrubs (CAM plants) | Camels, sidewinder snakes, fennec fox, scorpions | Sahara, Thar, Arabian |
| **Temperate Deciduous Forest** | Moderate; 4 distinct seasons | Oaks, maples, beech, birch — lose leaves in winter | Bears, deer, foxes, squirrels | Eastern USA, Europe, Japan |
| **Boreal Forest (Taiga)** | Cold; long harsh winter; short summer | Conifers: pine, spruce, fir — needle-like leaves (cold/drought adapted) | Wolves, lynx, moose, elk | Russia, Canada, Scandinavia |
| **Tundra** | Extreme cold; permafrost (permanently frozen subsoil) | Mosses, lichens, sedges, low shrubs — no trees | Polar bears, Arctic fox, caribou, snowy owl | Arctic, high mountain tops |
| **Temperate Grassland** | Moderate; seasonal; low rainfall | Grasses; few trees | Bison, prairie dogs (N. America); saiga antelope (Eurasia) | Prairies (N. America), Steppes (Eurasia), Pampas (S. America) |
| **Marine (Ocean)** | Covers 71% of Earth's surface | Phytoplankton (base of ocean food chain); kelp forests; coral reefs | Whales, sharks, tuna, coral reef fish |  |

**Boreal Forest (Taiga) = Largest terrestrial biome on Earth** — stretching across northern Russia, Canada, and Scandinavia.

## Environmental Issues

### Greenhouse Effect and Global Warming

The **natural greenhouse effect** is beneficial — certain gases in the atmosphere (Greenhouse Gases, GHGs) trap heat radiated from Earth's surface, keeping the planet warm enough to support life.

**Greenhouse gases (in order of abundance in atmosphere):**
1. Water vapour (H₂O) — most abundant GHG; natural; largest warming effect
2. Carbon dioxide (CO₂) — primary driver of human-induced warming; from burning fossil fuels
3. Methane (CH₄) — from rice paddies, livestock (enteric fermentation), landfills, natural gas leaks; 25× more potent than CO₂ over 100 years
4. Nitrous oxide (N₂O) — from fertilisers, vehicles; 298× more potent than CO₂
5. Ozone (O₃) — tropospheric ozone is a GHG
6. CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons) — synthetic; extremely potent (thousands× CO₂); also destroy ozone layer

**Enhanced greenhouse effect:** Human activities have increased CO₂, CH₄, N₂O → more heat trapped → **global warming** → climate change (sea level rise, glacier melting, extreme weather events, coral bleaching).

---

### Ozone Layer Depletion

- **Ozone layer (O₃):** Located in the **stratosphere** (~15–35 km altitude)
- **Function:** Absorbs **UV-B and UV-C radiation** from the sun — protecting life from radiation damage (DNA mutations → skin cancer, cataracts, immune suppression; plant damage)
- **Depletion agent:** **CFCs** (chlorofluorocarbons — from refrigerants like Freon, aerosol propellants, foam blowing agents); also halons (fire extinguishers), methyl bromide (fumigant)
- **Mechanism:** CFCs are inert in troposphere → rise to stratosphere → UV breaks them down → releases Cl atoms → Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂; each Cl atom can destroy 100,000 O₃ molecules (catalytic cycle)
- **Antarctic ozone hole:** First reported 1985; most severe (springtime depletion over Antarctica)
- **Solution:** **Montreal Protocol (1987)** — the most successful international environmental treaty; phased out CFCs and other ozone-depleting substances. Ozone layer is slowly recovering.

---

### Acid Rain

- **Formation:** Burning fossil fuels releases SO₂ (sulphur dioxide) and NO₂ (nitrogen dioxide) into the atmosphere → react with water vapour and oxygen → **H₂SO₄** (sulphuric acid) and **HNO₃** (nitric acid) → fall as acid rain
- **pH:** Normal rain is slightly acidic (~5.6, due to dissolved CO₂ forming carbonic acid). Acid rain has pH **< 5.6**
- **Effects:**
  - Kills fish in lakes and rivers (pH drops below 4.5 → most aquatic life dies)
  - Damages/kills forests (soil acidification → loss of nutrients; direct leaf damage)
  - Corrodes buildings, statues, and monuments — including marble (CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + CO₂ + H₂O)
  - **Taj Mahal** (Agra): marble yellowing and pitting from acid rain/SO₂ — court-ordered emission controls around Agra

---

### Eutrophication

**Definition:** Nutrient enrichment (primarily nitrates and phosphates from agricultural runoff and sewage) of water bodies.

**Chain of events:**
1. Excess nutrients → rapid growth of algae and cyanobacteria (**algal bloom**)
2. Dense algal layer blocks sunlight from reaching deeper water
3. When algae die → decomposed by bacteria → bacteria use up dissolved oxygen (**BOD rises**)
4. Oxygen depletion (**hypoxia**) → fish and other aquatic life die → **"dead zones"**

Famous example: Baltic Sea, Gulf of Mexico "dead zone" (from Mississippi River runoff).

---

### Biomagnification (Bioaccumulation)

**Definition:** The increasing concentration of a persistent pollutant (that resists biodegradation) at successively higher trophic levels.

**Classic example — DDT (Dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane):**
| Trophic level | DDT concentration |
|---|---|
| Water | 0.000003 ppm |
| Phytoplankton | 0.04 ppm |
| Small fish | 2 ppm |
| Larger fish | 25 ppm |
| Osprey/Eagle | 25 ppm (causes eggshell thinning → reproductive failure) |

**Other biomagnifying pollutants:** Mercury (Hg) — especially methylmercury; PCBs; dioxins; lead (Pb).

**Why it occurs:** Organisms at lower trophic levels absorb pollutant but excrete it slowly; predators eat many prey animals, each containing pollutant → pollutant accumulates faster than it is excreted → concentration multiplies up the food chain.

**India:** Banned DDT for agricultural use; still permitted for malaria control (indoor residual spraying). Rachel Carson's "Silent Spring" (1962) first alerted the public to DDT's wildlife impacts.

---

### Ozone in the Troposphere vs Stratosphere

| Location | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Stratospheric ozone (15–35 km)** | **Good ozone** — shields Earth from UV radiation; depleted by CFCs |
| **Tropospheric ozone (ground level)** | **Bad ozone** — air pollutant; formed from vehicle and industrial emissions (NOₓ + VOCs + sunlight); causes respiratory problems, crop damage; component of smog |

> Mnemonic: "Good up high, bad nearby."

## Biodiversity

**Three levels of biodiversity:**
1. **Genetic diversity** — variation in genes within a species
2. **Species diversity** — variety of species in a given area (richness + evenness)
3. **Ecosystem diversity** — variety of habitats, communities, and ecological processes

**Biodiversity Hotspots:** Regions with exceptionally high species richness AND under significant threat of destruction. Defined by Norman Myers; currently **36 hotspots** globally.

**India's Biodiversity:**
- India = 2.4% of world's land area but hosts ~8% of recorded species
- **2 major biodiversity hotspots in India:** **(1) Western Ghats (with Sri Lanka)** and **(2) Eastern Himalaya (Indo-Burma region)**

**IUCN Red List Categories (most threatened to least):**
- **Extinct (EX)** → Extinct in Wild (EW) → **Critically Endangered (CR)** → **Endangered (EN)** → **Vulnerable (VU)** → Near Threatened (NT) → Least Concern (LC)

## Conservation

### Protected Areas in India (5 types)

| Type | Strict? | Human activity allowed? |
|---|---|---|
| **National Park** | Strictest | No; not even grazing |
| **Wildlife Sanctuary** | Less strict | Some human activities (grazing, limited use of resources) |
| **Biosphere Reserve** | Least strict | Core zone = strict; buffer + transition zones allow human use |
| **Tiger Reserve** | — | Part of Project Tiger; often overlap with NPs and WLSs |
| **Conservation Reserve / Community Reserve** | — | New category; community involvement in conservation |

### Key Conservation Projects

| Project | Year | Target species | Key fact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Project Tiger** | 1973 | Bengal Tiger (*Panthera tigris tigris*) | First launched in Corbett NP; India has **53+ Tiger Reserves** (as of 2023); India holds ~70-75% of world's wild tigers (~3,700 tigers) |
| **Project Elephant** | 1992 | Asian Elephant | Elephant corridors; India has ~60% of world's Asian elephants |
| **Project Hangul** | 1970 | Kashmir Stag (Hangul, *Cervus hanglu*) | Critically endangered; Dachigam NP, J&K |
| **Crocodile Conservation** | 1975 | Mugger, Gharial, Saltwater Crocodile | Gharial (*Gavialis gangeticus*) critically endangered; Chambal river |

### Important Environmental Conventions

| Convention | Year | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **CITES** | 1973 | Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species; prevents overexploitation through wildlife trade |
| **Ramsar Convention** | 1971 | Conservation of wetlands; Ramsar (Iran) site of signing; India has 75+ Ramsar sites |
| **Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD)** | 1992 | Rio Earth Summit; 3 goals: conservation + sustainable use + equitable benefit sharing |
| **Kyoto Protocol** | 1997 | Binding GHG reduction targets for developed nations |
| **Paris Agreement** | 2015 | All countries commit to limit global warming to well below 2°C, preferably 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels |
| **Montreal Protocol** | 1987 | Phase-out of CFCs and ozone-depleting substances; most successful environmental treaty |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Pyramid of Energy is ALWAYS upright** — energy ALWAYS decreases at each higher trophic level; it can NEVER be inverted. Only pyramids of numbers and biomass can be inverted.
2. **Lindeman's 10% Law (1942):** Only ~10% of energy transfers to the next trophic level; 90% is lost as heat, movement, and undigested matter.
3. **Decomposers are NOT part of the food chain or ecological pyramids** — they break down dead matter from ALL trophic levels and return nutrients to the soil; they operate separately.
4. **Boreal forest (Taiga) is the LARGEST terrestrial biome on Earth** — not the tropical rainforest (which has the highest biodiversity but doesn't cover as much land as taiga).
5. **Tropical rainforests have the HIGHEST biodiversity** but cover only ~7% of Earth's surface.
6. **Ozone depletion: CFCs destroy ozone; Montreal Protocol (1987) banned CFCs** — ozone hole is over Antarctica; stratospheric ozone absorbs UV-B.
7. **"Good ozone" (stratospheric) vs "bad ozone" (tropospheric)** — stratospheric ozone protects life; ground-level ozone is a pollutant causing respiratory disease.
8. **Acid rain pH < 5.6** (normal rain ~5.6 from dissolved CO₂); acid rain from SO₂ and NOₓ from fossil fuel combustion. Damages Taj Mahal's marble.
9. **Eutrophication = nutrient enrichment → algal bloom → oxygen depletion → fish death** — driven by phosphate (sewage) and nitrate (agricultural fertiliser) runoff.
10. **Biomagnification: DDT (and mercury) INCREASE in concentration at HIGHER trophic levels** — top predators get the most — opposite of what many expect.
11. **Project Tiger launched in 1973** (not 1970 or 1975); first reserve = Corbett National Park; India has ~53+ tiger reserves and ~70% of world's wild tigers.
12. **Ramsar Convention (1971) protects WETLANDS** — not forests, not coral reefs, not grasslands. India has 75+ Ramsar sites.
13. **CITES (1973) regulates international wildlife TRADE** — not habitat protection.
14. **CBD signed at Rio Earth Summit 1992** — 3 goals: conservation, sustainable use, equitable benefit sharing. NOT the same as CITES.
15. **Water vapour is the most abundant greenhouse gas** — CO₂ is the primary driver of human-caused warming, but water vapour accounts for more total warming. Many candidates incorrectly say CO₂ is the most abundant GHG.
16. **India's 2 biodiversity hotspots: Western Ghats (+ Sri Lanka) and Eastern Himalaya (Indo-Burma)** — some sources include the Himalayas as a third; for SSC purposes, focus on Western Ghats and Indo-Burma/Eastern Himalaya.
