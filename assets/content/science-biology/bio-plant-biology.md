# Plant Biology — Photosynthesis, Respiration, Transpiration & Plant Hormones

## The Quiet Revolution

For the first two billion years of Earth's existence, the atmosphere contained almost no oxygen. Then, approximately 2.4 billion years ago, photosynthetic cyanobacteria began releasing oxygen as a byproduct of splitting water molecules. This "Great Oxygenation Event" transformed the atmosphere — and made complex animal life possible.

Every time you breathe, you are inhaling the waste product of ancient photosynthesis. Every molecule of food you eat traces its energy back to a plant that captured sunlight. Understanding how plants work is understanding the foundation of all life on Earth.

---

## Photosynthesis — The Core Equation

**6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂**

*(In the presence of chlorophyll)*

- **Reactants:** Carbon dioxide (from air via stomata) + Water (from soil via roots) + Light energy
- **Products:** Glucose (stored chemical energy) + Oxygen (released into atmosphere)
- **Location:** Chloroplasts — specifically the thylakoid membranes (light reactions) and stroma (Calvin cycle)
- **Carried out by:** All green parts of the plant (cells containing chloroplasts)

**Crucial fact:** The oxygen released in photosynthesis comes from WATER (H₂O), NOT from CO₂. This was established by experiments using isotopically labelled oxygen (¹⁸O). Water is split (photolysed) by light energy, releasing O₂.

---

## Inside the Chloroplast

The chloroplast has two functionally distinct compartments:

| Compartment | Reactions | Products |
|---|---|---|
| **Thylakoid membranes** (stacked into grana) | Light-dependent reactions | ATP, NADPH, O₂ (from water photolysis) |
| **Stroma** (liquid surrounding thylakoids) | Light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle) | Glucose (G3P → sucrose, starch) |

---

## Stage 1 — Light-Dependent Reactions (Thylakoid)

Two **photosystems** (protein complexes with chlorophyll and accessory pigments) work in sequence:

### Photosystem II (PS II — P680)
- Absorbs light at **680 nm** wavelength
- **Splits water** (photolysis): 2H₂O → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ + **O₂** ↑
- The oxygen released here is the source of atmospheric O₂
- Electrons replace those lost by excited chlorophyll → electron transport chain begins

### Photosystem I (PS I — P700)
- Absorbs light at **700 nm** wavelength
- Re-energises electrons from PS II
- Reduces NADP⁺ → **NADPH** (electron carrier, needed for Calvin cycle)
- **ATP** is synthesised via photophosphorylation (as electrons move through the ETC — "chemiosmosis")

**Outputs of light reactions:** ATP + NADPH + O₂ (released)
These power the Calvin cycle.

---

## Stage 2 — Calvin Cycle / Light-Independent Reactions (Stroma)

Also called the Carbon Fixation Cycle or C3 Cycle:
- CO₂ is "fixed" (incorporated into organic molecules) by the enzyme **RuBisCO** (Ribulose bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase — the most abundant protein on Earth)
- First stable product: **3-PGA (3-phosphoglycerate)** — a 3-carbon compound (hence "C3 plants")
- ATP and NADPH (from light reactions) drive the reduction of 3-PGA → **G3P (Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate)**
- G3P is used to build glucose, sucrose, starch, and other organic molecules
- RuBP (ribulose bisphosphate) is regenerated to accept more CO₂

---

## C3, C4, and CAM Plants — Adaptive Strategies

Different photosynthetic pathways have evolved for different climatic conditions:

### C3 Plants (Most common)
- First product of CO₂ fixation = 3-carbon compound (3-PGA)
- Use only the Calvin cycle
- Less efficient in hot/dry conditions (RuBisCO performs **photorespiration** at high temperatures — wastes fixed carbon)
- Examples: Rice, wheat, barley, soybeans, most trees

### C4 Plants (Hot climate adaptation)
- First product = **4-carbon compound** (oxaloacetate) — fixed initially in mesophyll cells
- CO₂ is first captured by PEP carboxylase (high affinity for CO₂, no photorespiration) in mesophyll cells → concentrated and delivered to bundle sheath cells where the Calvin cycle runs
- Much more efficient in hot, bright, dry conditions
- Examples: **Maize (corn), Sugarcane, Sorghum, Bajra (pearl millet), Amaranth**
- Significance: Maize and sugarcane are among the world's most productive crops

### CAM Plants (Desert adaptation — Crassulacean Acid Metabolism)
- Stomata open at **NIGHT** to absorb CO₂ (stored as malic acid) — avoid water loss during the day
- During the day, stomata are **closed** (water conservation); CO₂ is released from malic acid and fixed by Calvin cycle
- Extremely water-efficient; can survive in very dry environments
- Examples: Cacti, succulents, pineapple (Ananas comosus), agave, jade plant

## Photosynthetic Pigments

| Pigment | Colour | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Chlorophyll a** | Blue-green | Main reaction-centre pigment; absorbs red (660-700nm) and blue (430nm) light; present in ALL photosynthetic organisms |
| **Chlorophyll b** | Yellow-green | Accessory pigment; broadens wavelength absorption range |
| **Carotenoids** | Yellow-orange | Accessory pigments; protect against photo-oxidation; cause autumn leaf colours when chlorophyll breaks down |
| **Phycoerythrin** | Red | In red algae (Rhodophyta) |
| **Phycocyanin** | Blue | In blue-green algae (cyanobacteria) |

**Paper chromatography** separates these pigments; they travel different distances (Rf values) based on solubility.

## Transpiration — Water Loss from Leaves

**Definition:** Evaporation of water from plant aerial surfaces, primarily through stomata.

**Types:**
| Type | Route | % of total |
|---|---|---|
| **Stomatal** | Through stomata (pores in leaf epidermis) | ~90% |
| **Cuticular** | Through the waxy cuticle covering leaf surface | ~10% |
| **Lenticular** | Through lenticels (pores in woody stems/bark) | <1% |

---

### Stomata — The Gatekeepers

Each stoma is surrounded by two **guard cells** — specialised, bean-shaped epidermal cells containing chloroplasts.

**Mechanism of stomatal opening:**
1. Light → K⁺ ions (potassium) actively pumped INTO guard cells
2. K⁺ influx → water enters by osmosis → guard cells become turgid (swell)
3. Due to their unequal wall thickness, the inner wall is thicker → swelling causes the cells to bow outward → **stoma opens**

**Closing:** CO₂ accumulation, darkness, water stress → K⁺ pumped out → guard cells lose water → become flaccid → **stoma closes**. ABA (Abscisic Acid — the stress hormone) triggers stomatal closure during drought.

**Transpiration pull (Cohesion-Tension Theory):**
Water evaporates from leaf cells → creates negative pressure (tension) in leaf → water molecules, which are cohesive (stick to each other via hydrogen bonds) and adhesive (stick to xylem vessel walls), are pulled upward through xylem from roots. This explains how a 100-metre tall Sequoia tree gets water to its crown — no pump is needed; physics does the work.

---

### Factors Affecting Transpiration
| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| High temperature | Increases transpiration (more evaporation) |
| High humidity | Decreases transpiration (less vapour pressure gradient) |
| Wind | Increases transpiration (removes moist air layer from leaf surface) |
| Light | Increases transpiration (opens stomata) |
| Water deficit | Decreases transpiration (ABA closes stomata) |

## Plant Respiration

Plants respire (break down glucose for energy) in ALL cells, ALL the time — including at night. Photosynthesis occurs only in light and only in green (chlorophyll-containing) cells.

| Process | Location in cell | Conditions | ATP yield | End products |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Aerobic respiration** | Mitochondria (and cytoplasm for glycolysis) | Requires O₂ | ~38 ATP | CO₂ + H₂O |
| **Anaerobic (fermentation)** | Cytoplasm only | No O₂ | 2 ATP | Ethanol + CO₂ (plants/yeast); Lactic acid (animals) |

**Yeast fermentation** (anaerobic): Glucose → Ethanol + CO₂ — exploited in bread making (CO₂ causes dough to rise) and alcohol production.

## Plant Hormones (Phytohormones)

Plant hormones are chemical messengers produced in one part of the plant and transported to another where they regulate growth, development, and responses to the environment.

---

### Auxins (IAA — Indole-3-Acetic Acid)

**Produced by:** Shoot apical meristem (growing tip), young leaves, developing seeds
**Key functions:**
- **Cell elongation** — auxin loosens cell walls, allowing cells to absorb water and expand
- **Phototropism** — light causes unequal auxin distribution: auxin moves to the shaded side → cells on shaded side elongate more → shoot bends toward light
- **Apical dominance** — auxin from the shoot apex suppresses growth of lateral (side) buds; removing the tip ("pinching") removes auxin → lateral buds grow → bushier plant
- **Root initiation** — promotes adventitious root formation (cuttings)
- **Fruit development** — prevents premature fruit drop; promotes fruit set

**2,4-D (2,4-Dichlorophenoxyacetic acid):** Synthetic auxin used as a **selective herbicide** — kills broad-leaved (dicot) weeds in cereal crops (monocots) at concentrations that do not harm grasses.

---

### Gibberellins (GA — Gibberellic Acid)

**Discovered from:** *Gibberella fujikuroi* — a fungus that caused "foolish seedling disease" in rice (bakanae disease — plants grew unusually tall and thin)
**Produced by:** Young leaves, roots, developing seeds, fruits

**Key functions:**
- **Stem elongation (bolting):** Dramatic elongation of internodes; enables biennial plants to "bolt" (rapidly elongate and flower) when given the right cue
- **Seed germination:** Breaks seed dormancy; promotes germination (stimulates amylase in endosperm to release stored sugars)
- **Fruit size:** Applied to grapes → increases berry size (Thompson seedless grapes treated commercially)
- **Male flower production** in some species

---

### Cytokinins

**Discovered from:** Yeast extract and coconut milk (showed cell division activity)
**Produced by:** Root tips, developing fruits and seeds

**Key functions:**
- **Cell division** (cytokinesis) — primary role; promotes cell division in presence of auxin
- **Delay of senescence** — inhibit leaf yellowing and ageing (used commercially to keep cut flowers and vegetables fresh longer)
- **Lateral bud growth** — promote growth of lateral buds (works against auxin's apical dominance)
- **Chloroplast development** in leaves

**Kinetin** was the first synthetic cytokinin discovered.

---

### Abscisic Acid (ABA)

**Known as:** The "stress hormone" or "dormin"
**Produced by:** Leaves (especially under stress), roots, fruits

**Key functions:**
- **Stomatal closure** during water stress (drought) — most important exam fact
- **Seed dormancy** — inhibits germination; keeps seeds dormant until conditions are right
- **Leaf and fruit abscission** — promotes leaf fall in autumn (along with ethylene)
- **Responses to cold, heat, salinity, drought** — general stress response hormone

**ABA and guard cells:** Water deficit → ABA levels rise rapidly → ABA binds guard cell receptors → triggers K⁺ efflux → guard cells lose turgor → stomata close → reduces water loss from leaves

---

### Ethylene (C₂H₄ — a gas!)

**Unique among plant hormones** — it is a gas, not dissolved in water; produced and acts at the site of production
**Produced by:** Ripening fruits, root tips, wounded/stressed tissues, germinating seeds

**Key functions:**
- **Fruit ripening** — most commercially important function; promotes colour change (chlorophyll breakdown), softening (cell wall enzyme activation), sweetening, and aroma development. Fruits are stored under reduced oxygen / with CO₂ to delay ripening; exposed to ethylene to trigger ripening before sale
- **Leaf, fruit, and flower abscission** — promotes drop
- **Promotes flowering** in pineapple (Ananas comosus) — exploited commercially by spraying Ethephon (ethylene-releasing compound) on pineapple plantations
- **Senescence** acceleration — "one rotten apple spoils the barrel" is literally ethylene chemistry

**Calcium carbide (CaC₂):** Releases acetylene gas (similar to ethylene) when wet — used illegally in India to artificially ripen fruits (mangoes, bananas). Health authorities warn it may contain arsenic and phosphorus impurities that are toxic.

## Nitrogen Fixation

Plants need nitrogen for amino acids, DNA, and chlorophyll — but they cannot use atmospheric N₂ directly.

| Organism | Type | Process | Key Fact |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Rhizobium* | Symbiotic (in legume root nodules) | N₂ → NH₃ | Most important; provides nitrogen to pulse/legume crops (lentils, chickpeas, soybeans) — foundation of crop rotation |
| *Azotobacter*, *Azospirillum* | Free-living in soil | N₂ → NH₃ | Aerobic; general soil enrichment |
| *Anabaena* | Free-living (cyanobacterium) | N₂ → NH₃ | Found in paddy fields; important for rice cultivation |
| *Clostridium* | Free-living, anaerobic | N₂ → NH₃ | Anaerobic conditions |

**Nitrogenase enzyme:** Catalyses N₂ → NH₃; requires strict anaerobic conditions (O₂ destroys it) and large amounts of ATP.

**Why legumes fix nitrogen:** Rhizobium infects root hair cells of legumes → forms nodules (visible pink/red bumps on legume roots) → bacteria inside nodules fix N₂; in return, plant provides sugars. Nodule colour: pink/red = active (due to leghaemoglobin — similar to mammalian haemoglobin, binds O₂ to protect nitrogenase).

**Nitrification:**
- *Nitrosomonas* bacteria: NH₃ → NO₂⁻ (nitrite)
- *Nitrobacter* bacteria: NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (nitrate) — the form plants can absorb
- Together: nitrification — makes nitrogen available to plants from ammonium compounds

**Denitrification:** *Pseudomonas* and other soil bacteria convert NO₃⁻ → N₂ (back to atmosphere) — completes the nitrogen cycle.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Oxygen in photosynthesis comes from WATER (H₂O), NOT from CO₂** — established by isotope studies. The O₂ is produced when water is split (photolysis) at Photosystem II.
2. **Photosystem II (P680) splits water and produces O₂; Photosystem I (P700) produces NADPH** — the numbering is counterintuitive: PS II comes BEFORE PS I in the electron transport chain.
3. **C4 plants: Maize, Sugarcane, Sorghum, Bajra** — more efficient in hot, sunny conditions; first CO₂ fixation product is 4-carbon oxaloacetate, not 3-carbon 3-PGA.
4. **CAM plants open stomata at NIGHT** to absorb CO₂ and close them during the day — reverse of normal plants; maximises water conservation in deserts.
5. **RuBisCO is the enzyme that fixes CO₂ in the Calvin cycle — it is the most abundant protein on Earth.**
6. **Transpiration pull (cohesion-tension theory)** explains water transport in tall trees — no pump; water is pulled up by negative pressure created by evaporation from leaves.
7. **ABA (Abscisic Acid) is the stress hormone** — closes stomata during drought; promotes seed dormancy. The acronym ABA = "Abscisic Acid = Abscission (leaf drop) / Avoidance of water loss / stops growth."
8. **Ethylene is the only GASEOUS plant hormone** — ripens fruits; promotes pineapple flowering; one rotten fruit produces ethylene that accelerates ripening of nearby fruits.
9. **Auxins accumulate on the SHADED side of a shoot → cells elongate more → shoot bends toward the light (phototropism).**
10. **Apical dominance = auxin from shoot tip suppresses lateral bud growth** — removing the growing tip removes auxin → lateral buds grow → bushy plant. Used in plant pruning.
11. **2,4-D is a synthetic auxin used as a selective herbicide** — kills broad-leaved weeds (dicots) in cereal (monocot) crops.
12. **Gibberellins promote stem elongation (bolting) and break seed dormancy** — originally isolated from a rice disease-causing fungus (*Gibberella fujikuroi*).
13. **Cytokinins delay senescence (leaf ageing)** and promote cell division — used commercially to extend the shelf life of cut flowers and green vegetables.
14. **Rhizobium in root nodules of LEGUMES fixes atmospheric nitrogen** — symbiotic relationship. Pink nodules = active fixation (leghaemoglobin protects nitrogenase from O₂).
15. **Nitrification: Nitrosomonas (NH₃ → NO₂) + Nitrobacter (NO₂ → NO₃)** — makes nitrogen available to plants. Denitrification (Pseudomonas) returns N₂ to atmosphere.
16. **Insectivorous plants (Pitcher plant, Venus flytrap, Sundew)** live in nitrogen-poor soils — they obtain nitrogen by trapping and digesting insects. This does NOT affect their ability to photosynthesise normally.
