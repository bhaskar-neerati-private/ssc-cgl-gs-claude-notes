# Quit India Movement, Independence & Partition (1942–1947)

## The Final Act — Five Years That Changed Everything

Between August 1942 and August 1947, India went from being a colony with no clear independence date to two free nations in the space of five years. The sequence is fast, complex, and full of decisions that shaped the subcontinent for generations. Every event in this period is SSC-tested. Every date matters. Every name has a trap attached to it.

Start with a map of forces in 1942: Gandhi's Congress wanting immediate independence; Jinnah's Muslim League demanding Pakistan; the British fighting a war they might lose (Japan had just captured Singapore in February 1942, the largest British military surrender in history); and the Japanese army at India's door in Burma.

---

## The Cripps Mission — March 1942

With Japan threatening from the east, Britain desperately needed Indian cooperation in the war effort. Prime Minister Churchill sent **Sir Stafford Cripps** to India in March 1942 with a set of proposals:

- After the war: **Dominion Status** for India (full self-government within the British Commonwealth, like Canada and Australia)
- A **Constituent Assembly** to draft a new constitution, with Indian elected members and representatives of the Princely States
- Any province that did not wish to join the new Indian Union could opt out — and could form a separate union

That last clause — the right of provinces to opt out — was a significant concession to Jinnah. It implicitly acknowledged the possibility of Pakistan.

Congress rejected the offer. Gandhi called it a **"post-dated cheque on a crashing bank"** — a cheque issued on a bank (Britain) that was failing, for a date in the future that might never arrive. Why accept a wartime promise from a country that might lose the war, in exchange for cooperating in that war now?

The Muslim League also rejected it — feeling the provisions didn't adequately guarantee a separate Muslim state. The Cripps Mission failed.

---

## The Quit India Movement — 8–9 August 1942

With diplomacy exhausted, Gandhi made the most radical call of his career. At the **Bombay Session (Gowalia Tank, August Kranti Maidan)** on **8 August 1942**, the Congress passed the **Quit India Resolution** — demanding an immediate end to British rule.

Gandhi's speech that evening: **"Do or Die"** — *"We shall either free India or die in the attempt."*

The British response was instantaneous. At **dawn on 9 August 1942**, the government arrested Gandhi, Nehru, Azad, Patel, and virtually the entire Congress leadership — before they could organise anything. Congress was declared illegal. The press was censored.

What followed was something the British had not planned for: a **leaderless mass uprising**. With no central command, thousands of local leaders and ordinary people took to the streets. Government buildings were attacked, railway lines cut, telegraph wires severed. The movement was more violent than Gandhi had intended — but it was also more spontaneous and widespread.

**Key figures of the underground movement:**

- **Aruna Asaf Ali** — On 9 August, with all major leaders arrested, she went to Gowalia Tank and hoisted the Congress flag over the grounds — a symbolic act of defiance that made her the face of the August Revolution. She worked underground for years.
- **Usha Mehta** — ran a **secret Congress Radio** from a hidden location in Bombay, broadcasting nationalist news for several months before the British found and shut it down.
- **Jayaprakash Narayan (JP)** — escaped from **Hazaribagh Jail** in November 1942 by scaling the wall, and organised underground resistance networks across Bihar and UP.
- **Ram Manohar Lohia** — underground nationalist work; later a major socialist politician.
- **Satara (Maharashtra) and Midnapore (Bengal)** — "parallel governments" (prati sarkar) were set up by local activists after driving out British authority in some areas.

The British response was brutal: over **60,000 arrested**, more than **1,000 killed**, villages bombed from the air in Bihar, mass floggings. By early 1943 the movement had been suppressed — but it had demonstrated that India's passive acquiescence in British rule was over.

Gandhi was held at the **Aga Khan Palace in Pune**. His secretary **Mahadev Desai** died there (15 August 1942 — just six days after arrest). His wife **Kasturba Gandhi** died there too, on **22 February 1944**. Gandhi himself was released on **6 May 1944** on health grounds.

---

## The Rajagopalachari Formula and Gandhi-Jinnah Talks (1944)

Released from prison, Gandhi tried one more time to reach Jinnah. **C. Rajagopalachari** ("Rajaji") proposed a formula:
- The Muslim League would support the independence demand
- After independence, contiguous Muslim-majority districts in the northwest and northeast could hold a plebiscite to decide whether to join India or form a separate state

Gandhi accepted this as a basis for talks. The **Gandhi-Jinnah talks** took place in **September 1944**, meeting 14 times. They failed. Jinnah wanted a clear pre-independence commitment to Pakistan based on the two-nation theory; Gandhi was unwilling to concede Pakistan before independence and could not accept the two-nation theory (that Hindus and Muslims were fundamentally two separate nations requiring two separate states).

---

## The Simla Conference (June–July 1945)

Viceroy Wavell proposed forming an Executive Council composed entirely of Indians (except the Commander-in-Chief). The **Wavell Plan**: all portfolios to go to Indian ministers, with equal Hindu-Muslim representation in the Council.

The conference failed on a single point: Jinnah insisted that the Muslim League must have the **exclusive right** to nominate all Muslim members of the council. Congress (which had Muslim members — including Maulana Azad, the Congress president) refused to accept the League's monopoly over Muslim representation. Jinnah would not back down. The conference collapsed.

---

## The 1945 Turning Point — Labour Victory and New Elections

**May 1945**: Germany surrenders. **August 1945**: Japan surrenders (atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, 6 and 9 August). World War II ends.

**July 1945**: British elections. Churchill's Conservatives were defeated in a landslide by Clement **Attlee's Labour Party**. Labour had historically been sympathetic to Indian independence. The new government was genuinely committed to transferring power.

New provincial and central elections were called. The **1945-46 elections** produced a reversal of 1937:
- Congress swept the general constituencies as before
- The Muslim League won **86 of 90 Muslim seats in the Central legislature** — decisively demonstrating that it now spoke for Muslim India in a way it had not in 1937

The elections made two things clear: Congress represented non-Muslim India; the League represented Muslim India. The question was whether they could share a single state.

---

## The Cabinet Mission — March–June 1946

The most serious attempt to find a solution short of partition came from the **Cabinet Mission** — three British Cabinet ministers sent to India in March 1946:
- **Lord Pethick-Lawrence** (Secretary of State for India)
- **Sir Stafford Cripps** (Board of Trade)
- **A.V. Alexander** (First Lord of the Admiralty)

The Cabinet Mission's proposal (May 1946) rejected partition but tried to give the Muslim League the substance of what it wanted through a **three-tier grouping scheme**:

- **Tier 1 — Centre**: Handled only defence, foreign affairs, and communications
- **Tier 2 — Groups of provinces**: Provinces would be grouped; Muslim-majority provinces in the northwest (Punjab, Sindh, NWFP, Balochistan) would form Group B; Bengal and Assam would form Group C; the rest (Hindu-majority) would form Group A. Groups could have their own executives and legislatures — effectively giving Muslims regional autonomy
- **Tier 3 — Individual provinces**: Retained powers not assigned above

Both Congress and the League initially accepted. Then **Nehru** (who had become Congress president) publicly stated in July 1946 that the Constituent Assembly, once convened, would be sovereign and was not bound by the grouping scheme — effectively saying Congress could undo the Group arrangements that were the League's whole reason for acceptance.

Jinnah called this a "breach of faith" and withdrew the League's acceptance. The Cabinet Mission plan died.

---

## Direct Action Day — 16 August 1946

Jinnah called for **"Direct Action"** — if the British and Congress would not give Pakistan, the League would use force. **16 August 1946** was declared Direct Action Day.

What followed in Calcutta was catastrophic. The **Great Calcutta Killings** (16–20 August 1946) left between **4,000 and 5,000 people dead** in four days of communal massacre. Violence then spread: **Noakhali** (Bengal, October 1946) saw attacks on Hindus; **Bihar** saw retaliatory attacks on Muslims. **Punjab** burned through early 1947.

Gandhi walked through **Noakhali for 49 days** (November 1946 – January 1947) — village by village, often alone, trying to stop the violence. Mountbatten later called him "a one-man boundary force." Gandhi then moved to Bihar, then to Calcutta and Delhi, fasting and walking. His personal courage in the last year of his life was extraordinary.

---

## The Interim Government (September 1946)

Wavell invited Congress to form an **Interim Government** in September 1946. **Jawaharlal Nehru** became Vice-President of the Executive Council (effectively Prime Minister — the Viceroy was formally the President). The Muslim League joined in October 1946 — not to make it work, but to obstruct. **Liaquat Ali Khan** as Finance Minister used his position to block Congress proposals and demonstrate the impossibility of a united government.

The Constituent Assembly met in December 1946 — the League boycotted it.

---

## Mountbatten and the 3 June Plan

Wavell, who was increasingly pessimistic and talking about a "breakdown plan" (evacuating British and Indian civil servants to a line in the north), was replaced. **Lord Louis Mountbatten** arrived as Viceroy on **22 March 1947** with instructions to transfer power by **June 1948**.

Mountbatten was charming, decisive, and in a hurry. He met Congress and League leaders, assessed the situation, and concluded that partition was unavoidable. He moved the date forward by nearly a year.

The **3 June Plan** (also called the **Mountbatten Plan**), announced on 3 June 1947:

- India would be divided into **two independent dominions: India and Pakistan**
- The **Punjab Legislative Assembly** would vote — if it voted for partition, Punjab would be divided between the two dominions
- The **Bengal Legislative Assembly** would vote — same process; Bengal was divided
- **NWFP**: a referendum. The population voted for Pakistan; Khan Abdul Ghaffar Khan ("Frontier Gandhi") was heartbroken and opposed the result
- **Sindh** and **Balochistan**: separate arrangements
- Both new states would be **Dominions** (within the British Commonwealth) initially — full republic status would come later (India became a Republic in 1950)
- **Date of independence: 15 August 1947** — moved forward by Mountbatten (originally June 1948)
- **Mountbatten** would be the first Governor-General of India (Nehru's request). **Jinnah** became the first Governor-General of Pakistan (and its first Head of State — Liaquat Ali Khan was the first Prime Minister)

---

## Indian Independence Act 1947

The British Parliament passed the **Indian Independence Act on 18 July 1947**. It provided for:
- Two independent Dominions of India and Pakistan from 15 August 1947
- The lapse of British paramountcy over Princely States (they were technically free to accede to either dominion or remain independent — in practice Sardar Patel, assisted by V.P. Menon, integrated 562 of 565 states into India by persuasion and pressure)
- The end of the office of Secretary of State for India

**15 August 1947**: India became independent. Nehru's famous speech — *"At the stroke of the midnight hour, when the world sleeps, India will awake to life and freedom."* — was delivered in the Constituent Assembly. Gandhi was not in Delhi; he was in Calcutta trying to stop communal violence.

Pakistan celebrates independence on **14 August** (its transfer of power ceremonies occurred a day earlier).

---

## The Radcliffe Line — 17 August 1947

**Sir Cyril Radcliffe** — a British lawyer who had never previously been to India — was given the task of drawing the boundaries between India and Pakistan in both Punjab and Bengal. He had five weeks. He did it by examining maps and census data in a room in Delhi, without visiting the areas being divided.

The Radcliffe Award was announced on **17 August 1947 — two days after independence**. This was deliberate: announcing it before independence would have caused riots while British troops were still responsible for order.

The Punjab division triggered the largest forced migration in human history: **10–14 million people** crossed the new border in both directions. Muslims moved to Pakistan; Hindus and Sikhs moved to India. The violence was staggering — estimates of deaths range from **200,000 to 500,000** or more.

---

## Kashmir — The Unfinished Business

Every major Princely State was integrated into India or Pakistan. **Kashmir** was the exception.

Maharaja **Hari Singh** — a Hindu ruler of a Muslim-majority state, bordering both India and Pakistan — wanted independence and delayed signing any Instrument of Accession. In **October 1947**, Pakistan-backed Pashtun tribal raiders (with Pakistani regular soldiers embedded) invaded Kashmir.

Hari Singh panicked and requested Indian military help. India's condition: sign the Instrument of Accession first. Hari Singh signed on **26 October 1947**. Indian troops were airlifted to Srinagar. The **First Kashmir War** (1947-48) began. A UN ceasefire in January 1949 left India holding approximately two-thirds of Kashmir (including the Kashmir Valley) and Pakistan holding the rest (now called Azad Kashmir and Gilgit-Baltistan). The ceasefire line became the Line of Control — which remains disputed to this day.

## Exam Traps — 18 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Cripps Mission = March 1942** — BEFORE Quit India (August 1942). The mission failed AND THEN Quit India was launched.
2. Gandhi's phrase for the Cripps offer: **"post-dated cheque on a crashing bank"** — not "hollow promise" or any other paraphrase.
3. **Quit India Resolution = 8 August 1942** (NOT 9 August). Gandhi's "Do or Die" speech was also 8 August.
4. **9 August 1942** = day of arrests AND day Aruna Asaf Ali hoisted the Congress flag at Gowalia Tank.
5. **Gowalia Tank = August Kranti Maidan** — same location; either name may appear.
6. **Usha Mehta** ran the **secret Congress Radio** (NOT Aruna Asaf Ali — Aruna hoisted the flag; Usha ran the radio).
7. **JP (Jayaprakash Narayan)** escaped **Hazaribagh Jail** (NOT a different jail).
8. **Cabinet Mission = three members**: Pethick-Lawrence + Cripps + A.V. Alexander. Attlee was NOT in the Cabinet Mission (he was the PM who sent it).
9. **Direct Action Day = 16 August 1946** — led to Great Calcutta Killings.
10. **Mountbatten arrived 22 March 1947** — NOT January or February.
11. **3 June Plan = Mountbatten Plan** — NOT the Cabinet Mission Plan (which was May 1946, different).
12. **Indian Independence Act passed 18 July 1947** — NOT 15 August.
13. **Pakistan: 14 August; India: 15 August** — Pakistan celebrates one day earlier.
14. **Mountbatten = first Governor-General of India**; **Jinnah = first Governor-General of Pakistan** (NOT Prime Minister). **Liaquat Ali Khan** was Pakistan's first Prime Minister. **Nehru** was India's first PM.
15. **Radcliffe Line announced 17 August 1947** — two days AFTER independence, deliberately delayed to prevent pre-independence riots.
16. **Sir Cyril Radcliffe** had never previously visited India before being given the job of drawing the partition boundary.
17. **Hari Singh signed Instrument of Accession on 26 October 1947** — AFTER Pakistani tribal raiders invaded; BEFORE Indian troops landed in Srinagar.
18. **Gandhi was assassinated on 30 January 1948** by **Nathuram Godse** in the garden of Birla House, New Delhi — while conducting an evening prayer meeting.
