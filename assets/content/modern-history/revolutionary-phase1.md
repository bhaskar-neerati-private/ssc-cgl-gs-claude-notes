## When the Petition Failed, the Bomb Spoke

Not everyone agreed with Gandhi's non-violence or the Moderates' letters. Alongside the constitutional movement ran a darker, fiercer current — men and women who believed that Britain would never surrender India through persuasion alone, that violence in the service of freedom was not just permissible but sacred.

Phase 1 of revolutionary nationalism (roughly 1897–1918) has three distinct theaters: **Bengal** (bomb-making, conspiracy, the Anushilan Samiti), **London** (India House, assassinations in the heart of empire), and **North America** (the Ghadar Party — Indian immigrants planning revolution from San Francisco).

These movements failed to overthrow British rule. But they shaped the imagination of a generation — including a certain 15-year-old in Lahore named Bhagat Singh, who idolized Kartar Singh Sarabha.

## Bengal: The Bomb Factory

### The Origins — Anushilan Samiti

"Anushilan" means practice or cultivation. The **Anushilan Samiti** began in 1902 in Calcutta — outwardly a fitness and physical culture organization, something like a wrestling club and gymnasium. But its founders saw physical training as preparation for armed resistance.

- Founded in Calcutta (1902) by Promotha Mitter and others
- Later split into: **Calcutta Anushilan Samiti** (more political) and **Dhaka Anushilan Samiti** (more militant)
- Trained young men in physical fitness, weapons use, and revolutionary ideology
- **Yugantar** (1906) — revolutionary newspaper associated with the Samiti's ideology; founded by **Barindra Kumar Ghosh** (Aurobindo's younger brother) and **Bhupendranath Dutta** (Vivekananda's younger brother); openly advocated armed resistance against the British
- The **Maniktala Garden** near Calcutta — where Barindra Kumar Ghosh operated a secret bomb-making facility

### The Chapekar Brothers — First Political Assassination (1897)

The first major act of revolutionary violence in modern India:
- **Damodar Hari Chapekar** and **Balkrishna Hari Chapekar**
- Shot and killed **Plague Commissioner Walter Rand** (and Lt. Ayerst) in Pune on **22 June 1897**
- Context: Rand's plague operations involved forcible entry into homes, stripping patients, destroying property — considered deeply humiliating; news of the shootings was celebrated by Tilak in Kesari (which got Tilak his first sedition charge)
- Chapekar brothers were caught and **hanged in 1898**
- Significance: First instance of political assassination in modern India; established the pattern of targeting British officials

### The Muzaffarpur Bombing and Khudiram Bose (1908)

The most dramatic revolutionary act of the Bengal phase:

- **30 April 1908**: **Khudiram Bose** (age ~18) and **Prafulla Chaki** threw a bomb at a carriage at Muzaffarpur, Bihar — intending to kill District Judge D.H. Kingsford, known for handing out severe sentences to political activists
- The bomb hit the wrong carriage — killed two British women: **Mrs. Kennedy and her daughter**
- Khudiram was caught trying to flee; Prafulla Chaki shot himself to avoid arrest
- Khudiram Bose was tried and **hanged on 11 August 1908** — aged approximately 18-19. He went to the gallows reportedly with a smile, holding his copy of the Gita. He became an instant martyr symbol
- **Significance**: His youth and courage made him a romantic revolutionary icon. Songs were written about him. The phrase "Khudiram ke fan" (fan of Khudiram) entered common speech

### The Alipore Bomb Case (1908)

The Muzaffarpur bombing led police to the wider revolutionary network:
- Police raided the **Maniktala Garden** — found bomb-making materials, weapons, revolutionary literature
- **Barindra Kumar Ghosh** (Aurobindo's brother) and 36 others were arrested
- **Aurobindo Ghosh** was also arrested — accused of conspiracy (his writings in Yugantar were cited)
- **Defence lawyer**: C.R. Das — who gave a brilliant defense
- **Bipin Chandra Pal** refused to testify against Aurobindo → imprisoned for contempt of court
- Verdict: Aurobindo was **acquitted** (1909). Barindra Kumar Ghosh and others were transported to the **Andaman Islands Cellular Jail**
- **Aurobindo in 1910**: After a spiritual experience, he left Calcutta for Pondicherry (French territory — outside British jurisdiction) and renounced politics entirely, founding his ashram

### The Delhi Durbar Bomb Attempt (1912)

- **23 December 1912**: When Viceroy Lord Hardinge II was riding an elephant during the ceremonial entry into Delhi (shifting the capital from Calcutta to Delhi), a bomb was thrown at the howdah (elephant seat)
- Hardinge was wounded but survived; his aide was killed
- Organizer: **Rash Behari Bose** (1886–1945) — NOT to be confused with Subhas Chandra Bose
- The actual bomb-thrower: **Basanta Kumar Biswas** (hanged 1915)
- Rash Behari Bose escaped; eventually fled to **Japan** (1915) disguised as a student; spent the rest of his life in Japan; later connected with the INA (handed leadership to Subhas Chandra Bose)

## The Cellular Jail — "Kala Pani"

The **Andaman Islands Cellular Jail** (built 1906) was the British empire's ultimate punishment for Indian revolutionaries:
- Called **"Kala Pani"** (Black Waters) — crossing the sea was considered ritually polluting for upper-caste Hindus; it also meant exile from everything familiar
- 693 individual cells, each isolated — designed to prevent prisoners communicating
- Prisoners were subjected to hard labour: oil pressing, rope-making, breaking rocks
- **Notable prisoners**: V.D. Savarkar (1911–1921), Barindra Kumar Ghosh, Yogendra Shukla, many Ghadar Party members
- The jail is now a national memorial; the "sound and light" show is famous

## London: Revolution at the Heart of Empire

### India House (1905) — Shyamji Krishna Varma

**Shyamji Krishna Varma** (1857–1930) — a Sanskrit scholar and lawyer who had been a student of Herbert Spencer and Dayananda Saraswati — founded **India House** in London in 1905:
- A hostel for Indian students at Highgate, London
- Published ***The Indian Sociologist*** — openly advocated Indian independence; one of the first revolutionary nationalist publications
- Offered scholarships ("India House fellowships") to Indian students for study in England, selecting those with nationalist inclinations
- India House became a gathering point for radical Indian nationalists in London

Shyamji Krishna Varma left London in 1907 (sensing he might be arrested), went to Paris, then Geneva. He died in Geneva in 1930; his ashes were eventually brought back to India in 2003.

### V.D. Savarkar at India House

**Vinayak Damodar Savarkar (1883–1966)** — "Veer Savarkar" — came to London on a Shyamji scholarship:
- Wrote ***The Indian War of Independence 1857*** (1909) — while in London; British banned it before publication; it was published in the Netherlands and smuggled into India
- Connected with French and Russian revolutionary networks
- Helped organize smuggling of revolvers into India (linked to the Nashik Conspiracy Case)
- **Arrested in 1910** in London for conspiracy
- **The Marseilles Escape Attempt (1910)**: While being transported by ship back to India under arrest, Savarkar jumped into the sea at Marseilles harbour (France) and swam to shore, appealing to French authorities for asylum. The British got him back through diplomatic pressure. This incident became internationally known — the Hague Permanent Court of Arbitration was asked to rule on whether France should have handed him back (it ruled that France had acted correctly but irregularly)
- Sent to Andaman Cellular Jail (1911); released 1921; later confined to Ratnagiri
- Savarkar later became a prominent Hindu nationalist; his 1923 book defined "Hindutva"

### Madanlal Dhingra (1883–1909) — Assassination in London

**Madanlal Dhingra** was an India House member, deeply influenced by Savarkar:
- **1 July 1909**: At a reception of the National Indian Association in London, Dhingra shot and killed **Sir William Hutt Curzon Wyllie** — Political ADC to the Secretary of State for India
- Also shot and killed a Parsi doctor, Cawas Lalcaca, who tried to intervene
- Arrested on the spot; refused to show remorse; gave a defiant statement calling Curzon Wyllie an agent of British oppression
- **Hanged on 17 August 1909** at Pentonville Prison, London
- His act shocked Britain and led to the effective closure of India House
- In India, he was celebrated as a martyr by nationalists; condemned by Moderates (including Gandhi, who was living in South Africa at the time — he wrote that Dhingra's act was "an act of dastardly violence")

## The Ghadar Party (1913) — Revolution from San Francisco

### Who Were the Ghadaris?

By the early 20th century, tens of thousands of Indian immigrants — mostly Punjabi Sikhs — worked on railroad construction and farms in the western United States and Canada. They faced intense racial discrimination: barred from becoming citizens, denied property rights, excluded from many professions.

These men were far from India but deeply connected to its fate. When nationalist publications reached them, they burned. The Ghadar Party ("Ghadar" = revolt, mutiny — same word used for 1857) was their revolutionary organization.

### Founding (1913)

- **Founded**: San Francisco, USA, **1913**
- **Key organizer**: **Lala Har Dayal** (1884–1939) — a brilliant intellectual who had won a scholarship to Oxford, renounced it for revolution, taught briefly at Stanford, and organized Indian immigrants
- **First President**: **Sohan Singh Bhakna** — a Punjabi Sikh farmer from Amritsar district
- **Headquarters**: San Francisco (initially the "Yugantar Ashram")
- **Newspaper**: ***Ghadar*** — published in multiple Indian languages: **Urdu, Punjabi, Hindi, Gujarati, Marathi, Tamil** — to reach all Indian immigrants regardless of region or literacy; the newspaper gave the party its name
- **Goal**: Armed revolution in India; overthrow British rule immediately

### The Komagata Maru Incident (1914)

One of the most dramatic events connected to the Ghadar network:
- A Japanese steamship, **Komagata Maru**, sailed from Hong Kong in 1914 carrying **376 passengers** — mostly Punjabi Sikh Canadians trying to return from India (or migrate to Canada)
- Canadian immigration authorities refused to allow the passengers to land (racial exclusion policies)
- The ship sat in Vancouver harbour for **two months** — passengers facing starvation conditions
- Finally forced to return to India
- On **29 September 1914**, when the ship docked at **Budge Budge** near Calcutta, British authorities tried to arrest several passengers; passengers resisted; British opened fire — **19 passengers were killed**
- The rest were detained or dispersed
- The Komagata Maru incident inflamed Ghadar sentiment and accelerated their plans

### The Ghadar Plan (1914–1915)

When WWI broke out in August 1914, Har Dayal and the Ghadar leadership saw a perfect moment:
- "The British are distracted. India's soldiers are being sent to fight Britain's war. Now is the time!"
- Thousands of Ghadar members from the USA and Canada sailed back to India — an extraordinary wave of return migration driven by revolutionary zeal
- The plan: Infiltrate the Indian Army; organise a simultaneous mutiny of Indian soldiers; launch a revolution

**What went wrong**:
- The plan was betrayed to British intelligence (by informers within the movement)
- British authorities cracked down before the planned date (February 1915)
- Mass arrests; leaders hanged or transported

**Key martyrs of the Ghadar Phase:**
- **Kartar Singh Sarabha** (1896–1915): 19-year-old Ghadar activist; returned from the USA to lead the revolution; hanged at Lahore on 16 November 1915. He became the most romantic figure of Ghadar — the teenager who gave his life for freedom. **Bhagat Singh kept his photo as a constant companion** — calling him his guru
- **Vishnu Ganesh Pingle**: Hanged 1915
- **Mewa Singh**: A Ghadar member who shot and killed Canadian immigration officer Malcolm Reid in Vancouver (retaliation for Komagata Maru); hanged in 1915

**Har Dayal**: Arrested briefly in the USA (1914) but released on bail; escaped to Europe; eventually moved to Britain; his later years were more academic than revolutionary.

**Sohan Singh Bhakna**: Sentenced and transported to Andaman Cellular Jail.

## The Revolutionary Ideology — What They Believed

Revolutionary nationalists drew from several sources:

- **Russian nihilists and Narodnaya Volya**: The Russian revolutionary movement that assassinated Czar Alexander II (1881) was a model — proving that violence could strike at the heart of autocratic power
- **Irish Republican Brotherhood**: Irish revolutionary tradition of armed resistance to British rule
- **Giuseppe Mazzini**: Italian nationalist — his biography was widely read by Indian revolutionaries; his concept of "Young Italy" and sacrificial nationalism inspired "Young India" movements
- **Bhagavad Gita**: Many revolutionaries (Khudiram Bose, later Bhagat Singh) drew from the Gita's concept that action for a just cause is dharma — Tilak's Gita Rahasya had given philosophical backing to this

The **Bande Mataram / Yugantar** newspapers articulated a vision: the British had no right to rule India; India's freedom must be seized, not begged; death in the service of the motherland is the highest calling.

## Exam Traps

1. **Anushilan Samiti (1902) = Bengal** — NOT Punjab or Maharashtra; initially a fitness group turned revolutionary
2. **Yugantar newspaper = Barindra Kumar Ghosh + Bhupendranath Dutta** — Bhupendranath Dutta was Vivekananda's brother (NOT Aurobindo's)
3. **Khudiram Bose = Muzaffarpur bombing (30 April 1908)** — hanged 11 August 1908; age ~18–19
4. **Muzaffarpur target = Judge Kingsford** — bomb killed Mrs. Kennedy and daughter (WRONG carriage)
5. **Alipore Bomb Case = Barindra Kumar Ghosh arrested; Aurobindo acquitted** — NOT Aurobindo convicted
6. **Bipin Chandra Pal** refused to testify in Alipore case → imprisoned for contempt (NOT for revolutionary activities)
7. **Delhi Durbar Bomb (1912) = Rash Behari Bose organized; Basanta Kumar Biswas threw it** — Hardinge wounded, not killed
8. **Rash Behari Bose ≠ Subhas Chandra Bose** — different people; Rash Behari escaped to Japan; connected INA later
9. **India House, London = Shyamji Krishna Varma** (founded); Savarkar was a prominent member, NOT founder
10. **Savarkar's 1857 book** = *The Indian War of Independence 1857* (1909) — written in London; banned before publication
11. **Madanlal Dhingra killed Curzon Wyllie (1909)** in London — hanged in London, NOT India
12. **Ghadar Party = 1913, San Francisco** — NOT India; NOT 1905 or 1915
13. **Ghadar Party first president = Sohan Singh Bhakna** — NOT Har Dayal (he was the key organizer/intellectual but Bhakna was president)
14. **Kartar Singh Sarabha = 19 years old; hanged 1915** — Bhagat Singh's idol
15. **Komagata Maru = 1914; Budge Budge (near Calcutta); 19 killed** — the ship, not the movement, was called Komagata Maru
16. **Cellular Jail = Andaman Islands** — "Kala Pani"; notable prisoners: Savarkar, Barindra Ghosh
17. **Ghadar newspaper published in multiple languages** — Urdu, Punjabi, Hindi, Gujarati, Marathi, Tamil (to reach all Indian immigrants)
