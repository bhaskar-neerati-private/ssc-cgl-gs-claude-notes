## Before the Mutiny, a Different War Was Being Fought

The early 19th century in India was not only about battles and treaties. Quietly, in the drawing rooms of Calcutta and the streets of Pune, a different revolution was underway — one against the caste system, against child marriage and Sati, against idol worship and superstition, against the thousand years of social practices that reformers believed had weakened Indian society from within.

Two cities led this reformation: **Calcutta** (Bengal) and **Bombay/Pune** (Maharashtra). The leaders who emerged from these cities — Ram Mohan Roy, Derozio, Vidyasagar, Phule, Ranade — were as important to India's transformation as any general or Viceroy. Some worked within Hindu tradition; others broke from it entirely. All believed India had to reform itself before it could be free.

---

## Bengal: The Center of the Indian Renaissance

Bengal was the first region deeply touched by British education, Western ideas, and contact with European rationalism. Out of this collision between the ancient and the modern emerged the **Bengal Renaissance** — a flowering of critical thought, literature, and social reform.

---

### Raja Ram Mohan Roy (1772–1833) — The Father of Modern India

Ram Mohan Roy is the towering figure of 19th-century India. He was not a product of the colonial system — he was a polymath who knew Sanskrit, Persian, Arabic, Bengali, and English, had read the Vedas and the Quran and the Bible, and drew from all of them to argue for a rational, humane religion.

**His core beliefs**:
- One God — no idols, no polytheism (he found this supported in the Upanishads themselves)
- No caste discrimination — all humans equal before God
- Women are equal — Sati is murder; widow remarriage must be allowed
- Education in English and science — India must modernize

**Key actions and organizations**:

| Year | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1815 | Founded **Atmiya Sabha** — first organization; forerunner to Brahmo Samaj |
| 1820 | Published **Precepts of Jesus** — extracted moral teachings of Jesus, separating them from miracles; angered both orthodox Hindus AND Christian missionaries |
| 1821 | Founded **Sambad Kaumudi** — Bengali newspaper; advocated press freedom, widow remarriage, end of Sati |
| 1822 | Founded **Mirat-ul-Akhbar** — Persian newspaper; first Indian-owned Persian paper; stopped when press regulations tightened |
| 1828 | Founded **Brahmo Sabha** (later called **Brahmo Samaj**) — "Society of God"; monotheistic; opposed idolatry and caste; became the most important reform organization of 19th-century India |
| 1829 | **Sati abolished** — Regulation XVII under Lord Bentinck; Roy's years of campaigning (writing, petitioning, public persuasion) were decisive in building the case |
| 1830 | Went to England as ambassador of Mughal Emperor Akbar II — to appeal against reduction of royal pension; also to monitor implementation of Sati abolition |
| 1833 | Died in **Bristol, England** — never returned to India |

**Titles and epithets**:
- **"Father of Modern India"** — for initiating India's social and intellectual modernization
- **"Father of Indian Renaissance"** — for starting Bengal's cultural and intellectual rebirth
- **"Father of Indian Journalism"** — for pioneering Indian-owned newspapers
- **"Raja"** — title given by Mughal Emperor Akbar II, not a hereditary title

**Remember**: Ram Mohan Roy fought against Sati but Sati was abolished by **Lord Bentinck** (1829). Roy's work made the abolition politically possible — but the credit for the Act goes to Bentinck.

### Henry Louis Vivian Derozio (1809–1831) — The Firestarter

Derozio was an Anglo-Indian (part Indian, part Portuguese/British) who became a teacher at **Hindu College, Calcutta** at the age of just 17. In a remarkably short career (he died at 22), he created the **Young Bengal Movement**.

His approach was radical for the time:
- Encouraged students to question everything — tradition, religion, authority
- Promoted rationalism, free thinking, scepticism of received tradition
- His followers (called **Derozians**) attacked Hindu customs, ate beef, drank wine, questioned Brahmin authority — deliberately provocative

**Impact**: The Young Bengal movement was short-lived and its excesses (deliberately transgressing social norms) alienated mainstream society. But it planted seeds of rational inquiry and free thought in Bengal's educated class. Derozio was dismissed from Hindu College in 1831 for his "dangerous" influence; he died later that year.

**Key distinction**: Derozio/Young Bengal = rationalism and social transgression; not directly connected to Brahmo Samaj.

### Brahmo Samaj — The Three Phases

The Brahmo Samaj had a complex history with two major splits:

**Phase 1: Ram Mohan Roy (1828–1833)**
- Founded Brahmo Sabha (1828) — monotheistic, based on Upanishads
- No image worship; no caste; no priestly authority
- After Roy's death (1833), organization went into decline

**Phase 2: Debendranath Tagore (1817–1905)**
- Rabindranath Tagore's father; called **"Maharshi"** (Great Sage)
- Revived Brahmo Samaj in 1843; gave it organizational structure
- Created the **Brahmo Dharma** — a scripture for the movement
- More conservative than Roy — focused on spiritual reform, less on social agitation

**Phase 3: Keshab Chandra Sen (1838–1884)**
- Young, charismatic; breathed new energy into the Samaj
- Promoted inter-caste dining and marriage; introduced women into the movement
- Used vernacular languages rather than Sanskrit
- **First split (1866)**: Keshab's progressive faction (Brahmo Samaj of India) vs. Debendranath's conservative faction (Adi Brahmo Samaj — "original" Brahmo Samaj)
- **Second split (1878)**: Keshab's daughter married the underage Maharaja of Cooch Behar — violating the Brahmo Samaj's own anti-child-marriage principles. Disgusted reformers split off to form **Sadharan Brahmo Samaj** (1878); Keshab continued with **Nava Vidhan** (New Dispensation) — blending Hinduism, Islam, Christianity

**Mnemonic for Brahmo Samaj splits**: RAM founded it → DEBEN revived it → KESHAB split it TWICE (1866 and 1878)

### Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar (1820–1891) — The Campaigner for Widows

Vidyasagar ("Ocean of Learning") was a Sanskrit scholar, educator, and relentless social campaigner. He was not a member of Brahmo Samaj — he was a more traditional Hindu — but his contributions to Bengal's reform were enormous.

**Key contributions**:
- **Widow remarriage**: Argued from Hindu scriptures (Parasara Smriti) that widow remarriage was permissible — won the legal battle with the **Hindu Widows' Remarriage Act (1856)** under Lord Dalhousie
- **Child marriage and polygamy**: Campaigned strongly against both; polygamy particularly rampant among Bengali Kulin Brahmins
- **Women's education**: Founded multiple girls' schools in Bengal; collaborated with Bethune (John Elliot Drinkwater Bethune) on the Bethune School (1849)
- **Bengali language**: Simplified the Bengali alphabet and script; wrote foundational Bengali textbooks
- **Simplicity**: Was known for wearing simple clothes and wooden sandals (choti), refusing to be intimidated by orthodox opposition

**Critical exam distinction**: 
- Ram Mohan Roy **campaigned against Sati** and called for widow remarriage
- Vidyasagar **legally secured widow remarriage** (1856) — different people, different acts, different decades

## Bombay/Maharashtra: Reform on Different Terms

The reform movement in Bombay Presidency operated in a different social context. Maharashtra had a strong Brahmin scholarly tradition (Peshwa legacy) and also a powerful counter-tradition of Bhakti saints who had challenged caste — Dnyaneshwar, Namdev, Eknath, Tukaram. The 19th-century reformers drew from both.

---

### Prarthana Samaj (1867) — The Moderate Reformers

"Prarthana" = Prayer. The **Prarthana Samaj** (Prayer Society) was founded in **Bombay in 1867** by **Atmaram Pandurang**, inspired by Keshab Chandra Sen's visit to Bombay.

**Key features**:
- Monotheistic; against idol worship — but far more moderate than Brahmo Samaj
- Worked within Hindu tradition — did not demand outright rejection of Hindu practices
- Focused on: abolition of caste restrictions, widow remarriage, female education, uplift of depressed classes
- Drew inspiration from Maharashtra's Bhakti tradition (Tukaram, Ramdas)

**Leading figures after Pandurang**:
- **Mahadev Govind Ranade (1842–1901)**: Judge, economist, social reformer — the same Ranade we met in the economic nationalism discussion. Believed social reform and political freedom were inseparable. Co-founded Indian National Social Conference (1887).
- **R.G. Bhandarkar (1837–1925)**: Renowned Sanskrit scholar; advocated widow remarriage and women's education

**Prarthana Samaj vs Brahmo Samaj**:
| Feature | Brahmo Samaj | Prarthana Samaj |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Founded | 1828 (Bengal) | 1867 (Bombay) |
| Approach | More radical — rejected Hindu orthodox practices outright | More moderate — reformed within Hindu tradition |
| Inspiration | Upanishads, Roy's rationalism | Bhakti saints + Western rationalism |
| Social focus | Broad (press, education, Sati, widows) | Caste, widows, depressed classes |

---

### Jyotirao (Jotiba) Phule (1827–1890) — The Anti-Caste Revolutionary

Phule was from the Mali (gardener) caste — considered low caste in the Brahmin-dominated Maharashtra of his time. His education at a Scottish mission school exposed him to egalitarian ideas. His conclusion: the caste system was the root of India's oppression, and Brahmins were its primary enforcers.

**Key contributions**:

- **1848**: Opened the **first school for girls in Pune** — at a time when educating girls (especially from all castes) was considered transgressive
- **1851**: Opened a school for untouchables
- **Wife Savitribai Phule (1831–1897)**: Taught in the schools; became India's **first woman teacher**. When walking to school, Savitribai was pelted with stones and dung by orthodox opponents — she carried a spare sari to change into. Her courage was as significant as Phule's.
- **1873**: Founded **Satyashodhak Samaj** ("Truth-Seekers' Society") — challenged Brahmin authority over religion and ritual; promoted rationalism and social equality; performed weddings without Brahmin priests
- **1873**: Published **Gulamgiri** ("Slavery") — dedicated to African-Americans who had fought against slavery; drew explicit parallel between caste oppression and race-based slavery
- Called **"Mahatma"** by his followers (before Gandhi received the same title)

**Key idea**: Phule rejected the entire Brahminic tradition — the Vedas, the Manusmriti, Brahmin priests. This set him apart from reformers like Ram Mohan Roy who sought to purify Hinduism from within. Phule questioned whether Hinduism itself (as structured by Brahmins) was the source of oppression.

---

### Parsi Reforms — Rahnumai Mazdayasnan Sabha (1851)

The Parsi community (Zoroastrian refugees from Iran, settled in Bombay) had its own reform movement:
- **Rahnumai Mazdayasnan Sabha** (Religious Reform Association) — founded 1851
- Founders: **Naoroji Furdonji**, S.S. Bengalee, and **Dadabhai Naoroji** (the economic nationalist)
- Promoted: rationalism in Zoroastrian practice, women's rights, English education
- Published *Rast Goftar* (Truth-Teller) newspaper — edited by Dadabhai Naoroji

## Exam Traps

1. **Brahmo Samaj founded in 1828 = Ram Mohan Roy** — NOT Debendranath or Keshab
2. **Sati abolished 1829 = Bentinck** — Roy campaigned but Bentinck legislated
3. **Widow Remarriage Act 1856 = Vidyasagar** — NOT Ram Mohan Roy (he advocated it but died 1833)
4. **Hindu College Calcutta = Derozio/Young Bengal** — NOT Brahmo Samaj
5. **Prarthana Samaj (1867) = Bombay** — NOT Bengal (Brahmo Samaj = Bengal)
6. **Prarthana Samaj founded by Atmaram Pandurang** — NOT Ranade (he was a leader but not founder)
7. **Satyashodhak Samaj (1873) = Jyotirao Phule** — NOT Ranade or Prarthana Samaj
8. **Savitribai Phule = first woman teacher in India** — Phule's wife, NOT a Brahmo Samaj figure
9. **Derozio died at 22** — extremely young; dismissed from Hindu College
10. **Maharshi = Debendranath Tagore** — NOT Ram Mohan Roy or Rabindranath
11. **Brahmo Samaj first split = 1866** — Adi Brahmo Samaj vs Brahmo Samaj of India; second split = 1878 — Sadharan Brahmo Samaj splits off
12. **"Gulamgiri" (1873) = Jyotirao Phule** — NOT Ambedkar (a later figure; similar themes)
13. **Ram Mohan Roy died in Bristol, England (1833)** — while on a trip to advocate for India
14. **"Raja" in Ram Mohan Roy's name** = title from Mughal Emperor Akbar II — NOT a hereditary caste title
15. **Rahnumai Mazdayasnan Sabha = Parsi reforms** — Dadabhai Naoroji was a co-founder
