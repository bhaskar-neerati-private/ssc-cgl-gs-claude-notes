## A Continent Waking Up

If Bengal lit the first candle of reform, North India lit a bonfire. The second wave of 19th-century socio-religious reform was louder, more combative, and often explicitly nationalist. It also reached across religions — Hindu, Muslim, Christian-influenced, and universalist movements all emerged in this period, each with a different vision of what India should become.

Four movements define this era: **Arya Samaj** (aggressive Hindu revivalism), **Aligarh Movement** (Muslim modernism), **Ramakrishna Mission** (universalist spirituality with social service), and **Theosophical Society** (Western-origin movement that paradoxically revived Eastern traditions). Around them swirled smaller but significant Islamic reform movements and women's liberation efforts.

## 1. Arya Samaj (1875) — "Back to the Vedas"

### The Founder

**Swami Dayananda Saraswati (1824–1883)** was born Moolshankar in Gujarat. At 14, he saw mice eating the offerings at a Shiva temple — a sight that shattered his faith in idol worship. He spent years wandering, studying Sanskrit, and eventually found his intellectual foundation in the Vedas. His conclusion: **true Hinduism** (as found in the Vedas) is monotheistic, rational, and egalitarian — everything that had accumulated since (idols, caste by birth, child marriage, pilgrimage) was corruption.

### Key Facts

- **Founded**: 1875, **Bombay** (later headquarters shifted to Lahore, but founded in Bombay — a common exam trap)
- **Motto**: "Back to the Vedas" — Vedas are infallible; post-Vedic texts (Puranas, smritis) are not binding
- **Major work**: ***Satyarth Prakash*** ("Light of Truth", 1875) — his definitive book; criticized idol worship, caste by birth, child marriage; also criticised Christianity and Islam
- **Language**: Promoted Hindi and Sanskrit (not English); wrote in Hindi to reach ordinary people

### What Arya Samaj Stood FOR

- One God (no idols, no polytheism)
- Vedas as the supreme authority
- Caste by **merit** (not birth) — a radical position for the 19th century
- Widow remarriage; opposition to child marriage
- Women's education
- Hindi as a national language

### What Arya Samaj Stood AGAINST

- Idol worship, temple rituals, priestly exploitation
- Untouchability (but in practice, Arya Samaj remained largely upper-caste dominated)
- Child marriage, polygamy, Sati
- Christianity and Islam (Dayananda was openly critical — unlike Ram Mohan Roy who admired Jesus)

### Two Distinct Legacies

**Dayananda Anglo-Vedic (DAV) Schools and Colleges**:
- Founded after Dayananda's death (1886) by his followers, including Lala Hansraj
- Combined modern (English) and Vedic education
- Now one of the largest educational networks in India (DAV schools)
- Linked to the "anglicised" wing of Arya Samaj — more accepting of Western learning

**Gurukul Kangri** (1902):
- Founded by **Swami Shraddhananda** (a disciple) near Haridwar
- Purely Vedic education in traditional gurukul style; no English
- The "orthodox" wing of Arya Samaj

### Shuddhi and Sangathan

Two social programmes that gave Arya Samaj its distinctive political character:

- **Shuddhi**: Reconverting Hindus who had converted to Islam or Christianity back to Hinduism through a purification ceremony. Ram Mohan Roy's Brahmo Samaj would never have attempted this — Dayananda saw it as reclaiming lost Hindus
- **Sangathan**: Hindu unity and social organisation — building a politically aware Hindu community

These programmes made Arya Samaj more confrontational than Bengal's reform movements and contributed to Hindu-Muslim tensions in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

### Dayananda's Death

Dayananda was poisoned in 1883 — allegedly by the cook of Jodhpur's Maharaja, at the instigation of a courtesan named Nanhi Jan, whom Dayananda had publicly rebuked for her influence over the Maharaja. He died at 59.

**Epithet**: Sometimes called the **"Luther of India"** — like Martin Luther who challenged the Catholic Church's corruption and called Christians back to scripture, Dayananda challenged the corruption of Brahminic Hinduism and called Hindus back to the Vedas.

## 2. Aligarh Movement — Muslim Modernism

### Sir Syed Ahmad Khan (1817–1898)

Sir Syed Ahmad Khan was a senior official in the Company's judicial service who witnessed the 1857 Revolt at Bijnor and emerged from it with a precise diagnosis: Muslims were falling behind because they rejected English education and modern knowledge. Without modernisation, Muslim participation in British administration would shrink, their political voice would weaken, and they would be left permanently disadvantaged relative to Hindus who had earlier adopted English education.

**His prescription**:
- Reconcile Islam with modern science and rationalism — they are not in conflict
- Muslims must embrace English education without abandoning their faith
- Work with, not against, the British — confrontation would only harm Muslims further

### Key Institutions and Works

| Year | Institution/Work | Significance |
|------|-----------------|-------------|
| 1864 | **Scientific Society**, Aligarh | Translated Western scientific works into Urdu; promoted scientific thinking |
| 1870 | ***Tahzib-ul-Akhlaq*** ("Reforms of Morals") | Journal advocating Muslim modernism; modelled on Addison and Steele's English journals |
| 1875 | **Muhammadan Anglo-Oriental (MAO) College, Aligarh** | Modelled on Oxford/Cambridge; English + Islamic education combined |
| 1920 | Becomes **Aligarh Muslim University (AMU)** | Central university; Sir Syed's legacy institutionalised |

### Sir Syed and the INC

Sir Syed was **opposed to the Indian National Congress** (founded 1885). His reasons:
- Believed INC was dominated by Hindus and would not serve Muslim interests in democratic governance
- Feared that if India became democratic (majority rule), Muslims — being a minority — would be permanently outvoted
- Advised Muslims not to join the Congress

This stance made him controversial: nationalists like Badruddin Tyabji (a Muslim INC president) disagreed with him. But Sir Syed's suspicion of Hindu-majority representative institutions influenced the Muslim League's later separatist politics.

**Key distinction**: Sir Syed was NOT anti-Hindu personally — he worked with Hindus all his life. He was anti-Congress on strategic grounds.

## 3. Ramakrishna Mission (1897) — God in Man

### Ramakrishna Paramahamsa (1836–1886)

Ramakrishna was a priest at the **Dakshineswar Kali Temple** near Calcutta. He had no Western education — he was barely literate in formal terms. But his spiritual intensity attracted both common devotees and intellectuals. His core teaching:

- All religions lead to the same God — he practiced Islam, Christianity, and various Hindu traditions and found the same divine truth in each
- Serve God by serving human beings — "Jiva is Shiva" (the living person is God)
- Mystical devotion (bhakti) is the highest path

### Swami Vivekananda (1863–1902)

Born **Narendranath Datta** in Calcutta, Vivekananda was a rationalist who questioned Ramakrishna's mysticism when they first met. Ramakrishna touched him — and Narendra's rational questioning transformed into committed discipleship.

**The Chicago Speech (11 September 1893)**:
- Vivekananda attended the **Parliament of World's Religions** in Chicago
- Opening words: **"Sisters and Brothers of America"** — the audience gave a two-minute standing ovation before he had said anything more
- He presented Hinduism as a universal, tolerant religion that had sheltered the world's persecuted — Jews, Zoroastrians, even early Christians
- He argued that no religion has a monopoly on truth; religious exclusivism is the cause of the world's suffering
- Effect: India and Hinduism went from being seen as backward and primitive to being respected in Western intellectual circles; Vivekananda became a global figure

**Ramakrishna Mission (1897)**:
- Founded by Vivekananda one year after returning from America
- Named after his guru Ramakrishna Paramahamsa
- Headquarters: **Belur Math**, near Calcutta (on the Ganga)
- Mission: Combine spiritual teaching with practical social service — education, healthcare, disaster relief
- Still active: one of India's largest NGOs; runs schools, hospitals, relief operations

**Vivekananda's philosophy** — Neo-Vedanta:
- Vedanta (the Upanishads) is the universal religion of humanity
- Service to man is service to God (Seva = Puja)
- India's spiritual heritage is its greatest contribution to the world; but India must also be materially strong
- Famous quote: *"Arise, awake, and stop not till the goal is reached"* (from Katha Upanishad)
- Died at 39 in 1902 — legendarily, he predicted his own death

## 4. Theosophical Society — The Western Admirers

### Founding

- Founded in **New York in 1875** by **Helena Petrovna Blavatsky** (Russian mystic) and **Colonel Henry Steel Olcott** (American)
- Core beliefs: Universal brotherhood of humanity; ancient wisdom traditions (Hindu, Buddhist, Gnostic) contain higher truths; karma, reincarnation, occult forces exist
- Headquarters moved to **Adyar, Madras (Chennai) in 1882**

### Annie Besant (1847–1933)

The most important Theosophical Society figure for Indian history:
- British socialist and activist; joined the Theosophical Society in 1889; moved to India
- Became **President of the Theosophical Society (1907)**
- Embraced Indian nationalism; believed India's ancient traditions were superior to materialist Western civilization
- Published the newspaper ***New India***
- Founded the **Home Rule League** in September 1916 — demanding self-government for India (parallel to Bal Gangadhar Tilak's Home Rule League, April 1916)
- Elected **President of the Indian National Congress (1917)** — the **first woman president** of the INC
- Interned by the British during WWI for her activities — which actually boosted support for the Home Rule Movement

### Significance of Theosophical Society for India

A paradox: Western intellectuals came to India and told educated Indians that their spiritual traditions (Vedanta, Buddhism) were profound — at a time when the British rulers were dismissing Indian culture as primitive. This external validation helped rebuild Indian cultural confidence and contributed to the Hindu revivalism of the late 19th century.

## 5. Islamic Reform Movements

### Wahabi Movement

- Rooted in **Shah Waliullah (1703–1762)** — Delhi-based Islamic scholar who sought to purify Islam of syncretic practices (mixing with Hindu customs); translate Quran into Persian to make it accessible
- His son **Shah Abdul Aziz** declared India a **Darul Harb** (land of enemies/war) — implying Muslims had a religious duty to resist British rule
- **Syed Ahmad Barelvi (1786–1831)**: The active leader; launched armed jihad against the Sikh kingdom in Punjab (which he saw as oppressing Muslims in the Northwest)
  - Fought at **Balakot (1831)** — died in battle against Sikhs; became a martyr figure
  - Movement continued after his death; became anti-British as British replaced Sikhs
- Called "Wahabi" by British (linking to Arabian Wahabism) — the movement itself did not use this name; it called itself **Tariqah-i-Muhammadia**

### Faraizi Movement

- Founded in **Bengal** by **Haji Shariat Allah (1781–1840)**
- "Faraiz" = obligatory duties of Islam; movement demanded return to Islamic essentials
- Declared Bengal a Darul Harb — so Friday prayers should not be conducted (a technical Islamic ruling for land under non-Muslim rule)
- His son **Mohsin-ud-din (Dudu Miyan, 1819–1862)** expanded it; organised peasants against zamindars and British planters
- Had a more explicitly anti-British, anti-zamindar character than the Wahabi movement
- More socially oriented — peasant movement wrapped in Islamic reform

### Deoband Movement (1867)

- **Darul Uloom Deoband** — Islamic seminary founded in **Deoband, UP, in 1867**
- Founders: **Muhammad Qasim Nanautawi** and **Rashid Ahmad Gangohi**
- Conservative Islamic theology; emphasis on classical Islamic education (Hadith, Fiqh)
- Anti-British in orientation but through religious education and revival, not armed struggle
- Opposed the Aligarh Movement's Westernisation — saw English education as a threat to Islamic identity
- **Key SSC fact**: Deoband ulemas (scholars) were largely **against Partition in 1947** — they supported a united India and opposed the Muslim League's demand for Pakistan (Jamiat Ulema-e-Hind, the political wing, joined Congress)

## Exam Traps

1. **Arya Samaj founded in Bombay (1875)** — NOT Punjab (Punjab came to be its stronghold later, but it was founded in Bombay)
2. **Dayananda Saraswati's book = Satyarth Prakash** — NOT Vivekananda
3. **Luther of India = Dayananda** — NOT Ram Mohan Roy
4. **MAO College founded 1875; became AMU in 1920** — Sir Syed Ahmad Khan
5. **Ramakrishna Mission founded 1897 by Vivekananda** — named after his guru (Ramakrishna himself never founded it)
6. **Chicago Parliament of World's Religions = 1893** — opened with "Sisters and Brothers of America"
7. **Belur Math = HQ of Ramakrishna Mission** — near Calcutta, on the Ganga
8. **Vivekananda died at 39 (1902)** — very short life; born 1863
9. **Theosophical Society: NY 1875 → Adyar (Madras) 1882**
10. **Annie Besant = first woman INC president (1917)** — NOT Sarojini Naidu (she was second, 1925)
11. **Annie Besant's Home Rule League = 1916** — Tilak also founded one in 1916; both exist
12. **Syed Ahmad Barelvi died at Balakot (1831)** — fighting Sikhs, NOT British
13. **Deoband (1867) founders = Nanautawi + Gangohi** — opposed Aligarh's Western approach; opposed Partition
14. **Faraizi = Bengal; Wahabi = mainly NW India** — don't mix their regions
15. **Tahzib-ul-Akhlaq = Sir Syed's journal** — NOT a book; a periodical publication
