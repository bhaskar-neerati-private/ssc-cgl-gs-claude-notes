## Polite Rebels: The Men Who Petitioned an Empire

In December 1885, seventy-two educated Indians gathered in Bombay and held the first session of the Indian National Congress. They passed polite resolutions, wrote careful memorials to the Viceroy, and sent copies to Parliament in London. They asked for more Indians in the civil service. They asked for expansion of the Legislative Councils. They asked the British, in measured, reasonable, English prose, to please be a bit fairer.

For twenty years, this was Indian nationalism.

It seems timid in hindsight. And the Extremists who came after them (Tilak, Lal, Pal) said exactly that. But the Moderates built something essential — a national platform, a political vocabulary, an economic critique, and a class of Indians who thought of themselves as citizens of a nation, not just subjects of an empire. Without the Moderates, there is no Gandhi.

## Before the Congress: The Precursors

The INC did not appear from nowhere. A series of earlier organisations built up civic and political consciousness:

| Organisation | Year | Who Founded | Where |
|-------------|------|-------------|-------|
| Zamindari Association | 1837 | — | Calcutta |
| British India Association | 1851 | — | Calcutta |
| Madras Native Association | 1852 | — | Madras |
| Poona Sarvajanik Sabha | 1870 | M.G. Ranade | Pune |
| **Indian Association** | **1876** | **Surendranath Banerjee** | Calcutta |
| National Conference | 1883, 1885 | Surendranath Banerjee | Calcutta |

The **Indian Association (1876)** is the most important precursor. Surendranath Banerjee had been unfairly dismissed from the ICS (a rare Indian who had passed the exam was dismissed on technical grounds seen as discriminatory) and became a professional nationalist. His Indian Association was the first organisation that consciously tried to build a national — not regional — political base. He organised two National Conferences (1883 and 1885) that drew delegates from across India.

## The Founding of the INC (28 December 1885)

### The Key Facts

- **Date**: 28 December 1885
- **Place**: Gokuldas Tejpal Sanskrit College, **Bombay**
- **Founder**: **Allan Octavian Hume** (A.O. Hume) — a retired British ICS officer, ornithologist, and theosophist
- **First President**: **Womesh Chandra Bonnerjee** (W.C. Bonnerji) — a Calcutta barrister
- **Delegates**: 72 at the first session; grew rapidly
- **Original plan**: First session was supposed to be in Pune, but a plague outbreak shifted it to Bombay

### Why Did a British Officer Found the INC?

A.O. Hume had served in India for decades and was genuinely alarmed by the potential for violent revolution — a repeat of 1857. His idea: create a constitutional platform where educated Indians could articulate grievances peacefully. Better a talking shop than a bomb factory.

This gave rise to the famous **"Safety Valve Theory"** — the idea that the INC was created by the British to act as a safety valve for Indian discontent, letting pressure escape through constitutional debate before it exploded into revolution.

The term "safety valve" was first used critically by **Lala Lajpat Rai** — arguing that if the British helped found the INC, it was to serve British interests, not Indian ones. Whether the INC was founded with or against British interests is debated — but the organization quickly outgrew any such design.

Viceroy Lord Dufferin was initially sympathetic to the Congress (the INC was founded during his tenure); he later became hostile when it started making real political demands.

## The Moderate Phase (1885–1905): Philosophy and Methods

### Who Were the Moderates?

The first generation of Congress leaders are called the "Moderates" — not because they were timid as people, but because their political method was moderate: work within the system, persuade rather than confront.

**Key Moderate leaders**:
- **Dadabhai Naoroji** — Grand Old Man of India; Drain of Wealth theorist; first Indian MP
- **Gopal Krishna Gokhale** — Gandhi's political guru; founded Servants of India Society (1905)
- **Pherozeshah Mehta** — Lion of Bombay; dominated Bombay politics
- **Surendranath Banerjee** — Father of Indian Nationalism; founded Indian Association
- **W.C. Bonnerjee** — First INC President
- **Badruddin Tyabji** — First Muslim INC President
- **Madan Mohan Malaviya** — founded Banaras Hindu University (BHU, 1916)

### The 3 P's of the Moderate Method

The Moderates operated on three methods (easy to remember as **3 P's**):

1. **Petition**: Written memorials to the Viceroy, Secretary of State, and Parliament
2. **Prayer**: Formal requests and resolutions; respectful appeals
3. **Protest**: Public meetings, resolutions, newspaper articles — but all peaceful and constitutional

No mass agitation. No civil disobedience. No confrontation. The Moderates had complete faith in British constitutionalism — they believed that if they made a rational, well-documented case, the British would respond fairly.

---

## What Did the Moderates Demand?

Their demands fell into three broad categories:

### Constitutional Demands
- Expansion of Legislative Councils — more Indian members, more powers
- Separation of executive and judicial functions (collector should not be both judge and administrator)
- ICS examinations to be held in India (not only in London); age limit to be raised

### Economic Demands
- Reduction of military expenditure
- Reduction of Home Charges
- More investment in India's industrial and agricultural development
- Protection for Indian industries from British competition

### Civil Rights Demands
- Repeal of the Arms Act (1878) — racial discrimination in arms possession
- Free press
- Trial by jury

---

## The ICS Age Limit — A Specific Fight

The Indian Civil Service examinations were held **only in London** — a massive barrier for Indians who had to travel overseas, fund the trip, and compete in an unfamiliar environment. Even worse: the age limit for ICS examinations was **lowered from 21 to 19 years in 1877** — specifically to disadvantage Indians who needed more preparation time.

The Moderates fought for years to have the exam held in India simultaneously and to raise the age limit. This campaign was largely unsuccessful during the Moderate era — but it crystallized the argument that British India was systematically excluding Indians from governing their own country.

## Key Moderate Leaders: Who Did What

### Gopal Krishna Gokhale (1866–1915) — Gandhi's Political Guru

Gokhale was the most important Moderate leader of the early 20th century. A mathematics professor who became a politician, he was remarkable for the depth of his research into India's economic condition.

**Key contributions**:
- **Servants of India Society (1905)**: Founded to train Indians as professional social workers and political activists — full-time dedication to public service, like a secular monastery for reformers
- Served in the **Imperial Legislative Council** — used it as a platform to expose the budget's injustice; detailed analyses of famine expenditure, military costs, Home Charges
- **Gandhi's mentor**: When Gandhi returned from South Africa in 1915, Gokhale told him to spend a year travelling India before forming opinions. Gandhi followed this advice literally — his year of travel opened his eyes to rural India's poverty
- Gokhale died in 1915, before Gandhi took over the movement; the Moderate era ended with him

**Epithet**: "Prince of Workers" — for his hardworking, research-based approach to public service.

### Dadabhai Naoroji (1825–1917) — Grand Old Man of India

We've met him before (Drain of Wealth, Parsi reform). In the INC context:
- INC President **three times** (1886, 1893, 1906)
- His 1906 Calcutta session is historic: he introduced "**Swaraj**" (self-rule) as Congress's official goal for the first time — a major shift from "better government under British rule" to "self-government"
- Bridged the Moderate-Extremist divide at the crucial 1906 session

### Surendranath Banerjee (1848–1925) — "Surrender Not Banerjee"

His nickname — "Surrender Not Banerjee" — tells you everything. He was nicknamed by opponents trying to mock his obstinacy. He became known for it proudly.

- Unfairly dismissed from the ICS (after becoming one of the rare Indians to pass it); became a nationalist
- Founded **Indian Association (1876)** — first organization with truly national ambitions
- Published ***Bengalee*** newspaper
- INC President 1895, 1902
- Controversially accepted the Montagu-Chelmsford reforms (Government of India Act 1919) when Congress rejected them; became a minister under the new system; seen by many as too accommodating in his later years

## What Did the Moderates Actually Achieve?

### Indian Councils Act 1892 (Lord Lansdowne)

The one significant constitutional reform during the Moderate era:
- Increased membership in Legislative Councils (Central and Provincial)
- Introduced a limited form of election (some non-official members nominated through bodies like universities, municipalities, trade associations)
- NOT fully elected; British majority maintained; powers still very limited
- But it acknowledged the principle of Indian representation

### Other Achievements (Less Tangible)

- Built a national platform where Indians from all provinces could discuss shared concerns
- Created a political vocabulary for Indian grievances
- Made the Drain of Wealth argument mainstream — every educated Indian knew about it
- Established press and public meetings as political tools
- Trained a generation of political leaders
- Internationalized India's case (Naoroji in Parliament; lobbying in England)

## Why Were the Moderates Criticized?

The Extremists (Tilak, Bipin Chandra Pal, Lala Lajpat Rai — "Lal-Bal-Pal") argued:

1. **Elite bubble**: The Moderates represented only the educated urban elite; ordinary Indians (farmers, workers, women) were entirely unrepresented
2. **20 years of begging**: Two decades of petitions yielded only minor reforms (Indian Councils Act 1892); the method had clearly failed
3. **Wrong premise**: Assuming British would reform out of goodness was naive — the British were in India for economic benefit; why would they give it up voluntarily?
4. **No mass base**: Without mobilising the masses, you have no real political power; polite resolutions without the backing of popular pressure are meaningless
5. **Language barrier**: Writing in English, publishing in English — they were talking to the British, not to India

Tilak's counter-philosophy: **"Swaraj is my birthright and I shall have it"** — political freedom first; social reform second; and get it through mass agitation, not petitions.

---

## The Gathering Storm: 1905

By 1905, two forces were converging to make the Moderate era obsolete:

1. **Curzon's Partition of Bengal (October 1905)**: The government's blatant "divide and rule" — carried out despite massive peaceful protests — proved the Moderates right about nothing: peaceful protest had accomplished nothing against a determined government
2. **Japan's victory over Russia (1905)**: An Asian nation had defeated a European power — shattering the myth of European invincibility; if Japan could do it militarily, India could do it politically

The Extremist phase was about to begin. The Moderate era ended not with a defeat but with a demonstration of its own inadequacy.

## Exam Traps

1. **INC founded 28 December 1885 in Bombay** — NOT Delhi, NOT Calcutta, NOT Pune (original plan was Pune; shifted to Bombay due to plague)
2. **Founder = A.O. Hume** — a British officer, NOT Naoroji or Gokhale
3. **First President = W.C. Bonnerjee** — NOT Hume (Hume organized it but didn't preside)
4. **First Muslim President = Badruddin Tyabji (1887)** — NOT Sir Syed Ahmad Khan (he opposed INC)
5. **First woman President = Annie Besant (1917)** — first INDIAN woman president = Sarojini Naidu (1925)
6. **Safety Valve theory coined by Lala Lajpat Rai** — NOT Hume; it was Rai's critique of the Congress
7. **Gokhale founded Servants of India Society (1905)** — NOT the INC; NOT Indian Association
8. **Indian Association (1876) = Surendranath Banerjee** — NOT Gokhale; NOT Naoroji
9. **Indian Councils Act 1892 = under Lansdowne** — NOT Dufferin; NOT Curzon
10. **Naoroji was INC President 3 times** (1886, 1893, 1906) — the 1906 session adopted "Swaraj" as INC goal
11. **3 P's = Petition, Prayer, Protest** — the Moderate method
12. **Moderates believed in constitutional methods** — NO mass agitation, NO civil disobedience
13. **Poona Sarvajanik Sabha (1870) = M.G. Ranade** — important precursor; NOT Gokhale
14. **"Prince of Workers" = Gokhale** — "Grand Old Man of India" = Naoroji; "Lion of Bombay" = Pherozeshah Mehta
15. **ICS exam lowered from 21 to 19** in 1877 — to disadvantage Indians; Moderate demand was to raise it AND hold exam in India

1. Why is A.O. Hume's founding of the INC paradoxical, and what is the "Safety Valve" theory?
2. Name three demands of the Moderate phase Congress. Why were these demands considered "moderate"?
3. What did the Indian Councils Act 1892 achieve, and why was it considered insufficient by the Extremists?
4. What was Gopal Krishna Gokhale's Servants of India Society, and why did Gandhi consider Gokhale his political guru?
5. What specific events of 1905 made the Moderate method seem irrelevant?

*(Answers: 1. Paradox: a British officer, loyal to the Crown, founded the primary organization that eventually drove the British from India. Whether true or not, the Congress outgrew any such design. 2. Three demands: (a) Expansion of Legislative Councils with more Indian members; (b) Simultaneous ICS examinations in India; (c) Repeal of Arms Act 1878. "Moderate" because they worked within the colonial framework — asking for reforms, not independence; constitutional not confrontational. 3. Indian Councils Act 1892: increased Council membership; introduced limited nomination/election of some non-official members through universities, municipalities etc. Insufficient: British majority maintained; powers still advisory (no real legislation power); no direct elections; only minor expansion. 4. Servants of India Society (1905): trained Indians for professional public service — members pledged lifelong service, lived simply, researched social/political conditions. Gandhi's guru: Gokhale's patient, research-based, respectful but firm approach — engaging deeply with the system to change it — shaped Gandhi's early political philosophy. Gokhale told Gandhi to travel India before forming opinions; Gandhi obeyed. 5. (a) Curzon's Partition of Bengal (October 1905): massive peaceful protests had failed to stop it — proof that petitions achieved nothing against determined British policy; (b) Japan's defeat of Russia (1905): proved an Asian nation could take on a European power; destroyed the myth of European invincibility and inspired a more assertive Indian nationalism.)*
