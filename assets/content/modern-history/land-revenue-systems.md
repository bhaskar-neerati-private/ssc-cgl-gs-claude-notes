## Who Pays the King? — The Question That Shaped Colonial India

Land revenue was the backbone of British India's finances. Before the British, Mughal emperors had extracted a portion of the harvest through local officials (zamindars, mansabdars). The British had to decide: would they continue through these middlemen, or deal directly with cultivators? Their answer varied by region — and each experiment created a different social structure, a different class of exploited people, and ultimately a different pattern of poverty.

Three systems emerged. Understanding them is not just about memorizing three names — it's about understanding three different relationships between the state, the landlord, and the farmer.

---

## System 1: Permanent Settlement (Zamindari System)

**Year:** 1793  
**Architect:** Lord Cornwallis (recommended by Philip Francis and finalized by John Shore)  
**Region:** Bengal, Bihar, Orissa — later extended to parts of northern Madras and Benaras division

### The Deal

The British were frustrated. Revenue collection from Bengal was chaotic — different zamindars paid different amounts at different times. Cornwallis wanted certainty. So he made a permanent bargain with the zamindars:

> "Pay us a fixed annual revenue — forever. In return, you own the land."

The annual demand was **permanently fixed** — it would never be raised, regardless of how productive the land became. The zamindar became the landlord; the British got a reliable, predictable income.

### The "Sunrise Rule"

If a zamindar failed to deposit revenue **before sunrise** on the payment date, his entire estate would be auctioned to the highest bidder. This "Sunrise Rule" created constant anxiety — zamindars had to be absolutely punctual or lose everything accumulated over generations.

### Who Won, Who Lost

**Winners:** Zamindars (became hereditary landowners) and the Company (stable, predictable revenue without administrative effort)

**Losers:** Ryots (cultivators). They had no legal protection — zamindars could rack-rent (charge excessive rents), evict without notice, and demand illegal fees. The ryot had gone from being a cultivator with traditional rights to a tenant-at-will who could be expelled any moment.

**The Long-Term Problem:** The revenue was permanently fixed in 1793. As agricultural productivity improved and land values soared over the next century, **the Company got the same fixed amount while zamindars pocketed the difference**. The British acknowledged this mistake themselves — they estimated they had permanently given away about 2/3 of the potential revenue of Bengal.

### What It Created

The Permanent Settlement created a new class of **absentee landlords** — zamindars who lived in cities, collected rent through agents, never visited their land, and had no interest in agricultural improvement. The miserable condition of Bengal's peasantry in the 19th and early 20th centuries is directly traceable to this system.

## System 2: Ryotwari System

**Year:** 1820 (Madras); extended to Bombay by Mountstuart Elphinstone  
**Architect:** Sir Thomas Munro (Governor of Madras)  
**Region:** Madras Presidency, Bombay Presidency, Assam, Coorg (most of South and West India)

### The Logic

Thomas Munro looked at the Permanent Settlement and saw a disaster. He believed the zamindars were an artificial creation — Bengal had zamindars because Mughal administration had created them, but Madras did not have the same tradition. Why invent a landlord class where none existed?

Munro's alternative: **settle directly with the cultivator (ryot)**.

Under Ryotwari:
- Each cultivating ryot held his land directly from the government
- Revenue was **assessed on each plot** based on its quality and productivity
- The assessment was **not permanent** — it was revised periodically (every 20-30 years)
- As long as the ryot paid, he could not be evicted; if he failed to pay, the land reverted to government

### The Advantage (in theory)

No middlemen. The government dealt directly with those who actually worked the land. There was no zamindar pocketing the difference between government revenue and actual farm income. The government could also periodically revise assessments upward — unlike the Permanent Settlement where the British were locked into 1793 figures forever.

### The Reality (in practice)

Ryotwari assessments were often **brutally high** — sometimes 50–55% of gross produce. The British revenue officials set rates that maximized short-term collection, not agricultural sustainability. When the monsoon failed, ryots couldn't pay — they borrowed from moneylenders at crushing interest, and soon lost their land to the moneylender instead of the zamindar.

The system replaced the zamindar as oppressor with the moneylender. Either way, the cultivator suffered.

## System 3: Mahalwari System

**Year:** 1822 (first introduced by Holt Mackenzie); 1833 (revised and stabilized by R. Merttins Bird)  
**Region:** North-Western Provinces (present-day UP), Punjab, Agra, parts of Central India (Madhya Pradesh)  
**Also called:** Village Settlement

### The Concept

In the Gangetic plains and Punjab, land was traditionally held not by individual zamindars or individual ryots — but by **village communities** collectively. The village (mahal) was the unit.

Holt Mackenzie devised a settlement with the **whole village** rather than individual zamindars or individual cultivators. Revenue was assessed for the village as a whole, and the headman (lambardar or muqaddam) was responsible for collecting from village members and depositing with the government.

- If one farmer had a bad year, others in the village could cover — collective responsibility
- Revenue was **periodically revised** (not permanent like Cornwallis's system)
- Recognized traditional village community rights

### The Problem

In practice, "collective responsibility" often meant the headman bore the brunt — or exploited the system. And like Ryotwari, periodic revisions could spike the demand. The system also varied enormously by subregion — what worked in Punjab didn't necessarily work in UP.

## The Big Picture Comparison

| Feature | Permanent Settlement | Ryotwari | Mahalwari |
|---------|---------------------|----------|-----------|
| **Introduced by** | Cornwallis (1793) | Thomas Munro (1820) | Holt Mackenzie (1822); Bird (1833) |
| **Region** | Bengal, Bihar, Orissa | Madras, Bombay, Assam | NW Provinces, Punjab, MP |
| **Settlement with** | Zamindars (landlords) | Individual cultivator (ryot) | Village community (mahal) |
| **Revenue fixed?** | Permanently (never revised) | Periodically revised | Periodically revised |
| **Proportion of India** | ~19% of land | ~51% of land | ~30% of land |
| **Main problem** | Zamindar exploitation; fixed revenue loss | High assessment; moneylender debt | Headman exploitation; complex administration |
| **Who benefited** | Zamindars | Moneylenders | Village headmen |
| **Who suffered** | Cultivators/ryots | Cultivators/ryots | Cultivators/ryots |

Note the consistency in the last row — regardless of system, the cultivator suffered.

## Why Did Revenue Matter So Much?

The land revenue wasn't just income — it was the **foundation of the colonial economy**. Consider:

1. **It funded the army** — the Bengal Army (which mutinied in 1857) was paid from land revenue
2. **It funded "Home Charges"** — the money Britain charged India for "administrative expenses" (salaries of London staff, pensions of returned officers, etc.) — a form of drain
3. **It drove commercialization** — to pay cash revenue, farmers had to sell crops → forced to grow cash crops (cotton, indigo, opium) instead of food → when prices fell or monsoon failed, famine followed

This is why economic nationalists like Dadabhai Naoroji and R.C. Dutt connected land revenue directly to India's poverty and drain of wealth.

## Indigo Cultivation — A Footnote That Became a Movement

Under the Permanent Settlement in Bengal, zamindars (and European planters who leased land from them) forced cultivators to grow indigo on a portion of their land — the **"tinkathia" system** (3 out of 20 kathas per bigha compulsorily under indigo). Indigo was profitable for planters but barely covered cultivation costs for farmers.

The **Indigo Revolt (1859-60)** in Bengal — when farmers collectively refused to grow indigo — was one of the first organized peasant movements in India. Dinabandhu Mitra's play *Nil Darpan* (1860) dramatized the planters' brutality. This was a direct consequence of the Permanent Settlement creating an exploitative landlord class.

## Exam Traps

1. **Permanent Settlement ≠ Dalhousie or Wellesley** — it was **Cornwallis (1793)**
2. **Ryotwari ≠ Cornwallis** — it was **Thomas Munro** (Madras, 1820)
3. **Mahalwari ≠ Munro** — it was **Holt Mackenzie / R.M. Bird** (NW Provinces)
4. **Region traps:**
   - Bengal = Permanent Settlement (Zamindari)
   - Madras/Bombay = Ryotwari
   - NW Provinces/Punjab = Mahalwari
5. **Permanent Settlement was permanently fixed; Ryotwari and Mahalwari were periodically revised**
6. **Ryotwari covered the largest area** (~51%) of British India
7. **The "Sunrise Rule"** belongs to Permanent Settlement (not Ryotwari)
8. **Zamindars became landlords** under Permanent Settlement — not under Ryotwari
9. **Mahalwari** = village settlement; not "zamindari" though it also had headmen
10. **Indigo Revolt (1859-60)** = consequence of Permanent Settlement in Bengal; connected to Nil Darpan
