# Constitutional & Legislative Acts (1858–1919)

## The Logic of Reluctant Concession

British India's constitutional history is not a story of generous reform — it is a story of pressure applied, concession extracted, and promises made as slowly as possible. Each major act followed the same pattern: Indian political agitation → British commission/committee → a new law that gave a little and withheld a lot → Indian nationalists criticise it as inadequate → cycle repeats. Understanding this pattern makes the individual acts far easier to memorise.

Before diving into 1858, a brief baseline. The **Pitt's India Act 1784** had established a dual system: the East India Company continued to administer India commercially, but a **Board of Control** in London (six members: two ministers + four Privy Councillors) supervised its political functions. The Company could no longer make war, peace, or treaties without the Board's approval. This dual structure — Company + Crown oversight — persisted for 74 years, until a revolt made it untenable.

---

## Government of India Act 1858 — The Crown Takes Over

The **Government of India Act 1858** was the direct legislative consequence of the 1857 Revolt. Parliament's conclusion: the East India Company had failed catastrophically, and India was too important to be left to a private trading company.

**Key provisions:**
- The East India Company was **abolished** — India passed from Company to Crown
- A new office: **Secretary of State for India** — a Cabinet minister in London, accountable to Parliament, assisted by a **15-member Council of India** (advisers, mostly former Company officials)
- The Secretary of State had ultimate authority over Indian affairs from London
- The **Governor-General became Viceroy** — the Crown's personal representative in India. Lord Canning became the first Viceroy (previously he had been Governor-General)
- The Board of Control and the Court of Directors (Company bodies) were both abolished
- The **Queen's Proclamation of 1858** accompanied the act — promising non-interference in Indian religions, respect for existing rights of Indian princes, and equal treatment of Indians in government service (a promise largely unfulfilled)

The 1858 Act is essentially an administrative housekeeping measure — it changed who was in charge (Crown instead of Company) without giving Indians any political role whatsoever.

## Indian Councils Act 1861 — The First Step (Very Small)

After the trauma of 1857, British administrators debated how to prevent another revolt. One school argued: include Indians in the legislative process so they feel some ownership. The result was the **Indian Councils Act 1861**.

**Key provisions:**
- The **Viceroy's Executive Council** was enlarged: three to five additional members could be added for legislative purposes (making it a legislative council when needed)
- Indians could be **nominated** (not elected) to these expanded legislative councils — purely advisory, no power
- **Portfolio system introduced**: each member of the Executive Council became responsible for a specific department (Home, Finance, Military, etc.) — the beginning of ministerial responsibility in structure (though not in accountability)
- Viceroy given power to issue **Ordinances** — emergency laws, valid for 6 months
- **Decentralisation**: Bombay and Madras Presidencies, which had lost their legislative councils in 1833 (under Charter Act), had them restored. Bengal, North-West Provinces, and Punjab also got legislative councils.
- The act is notable for what it did NOT do: no elections, no Indian ministers, no budget discussion rights

## Indian Councils Act 1892 — "Almost an Election"

By the 1880s, the Indian National Congress was making consistent demands through its "3 Ps" method (Petition, Prayer, Protest). The Moderate leadership — Naoroji, Gokhale, Mehta — lobbied for greater Indian representation. The British response was the **Indian Councils Act 1892**.

**Key provisions:**
- Legislative councils enlarged — more members added at both central and provincial levels
- **Indirect election introduced** — but the word "election" was deliberately avoided; the act used "recommendation" (e.g., municipal councils and local bodies "recommend" names to be nominated). This was, functionally, a limited indirect election, but legally still nomination
- Members could now **discuss the annual budget** — but not vote on it; they could ask questions — but not supplementary questions
- In effect: some voice, no power

The 1892 Act is considered the first tiny step toward representative government, but it fell so far short of Congress demands that it only accelerated the rise of the Extremist faction within Congress.

## Indian Councils Act 1909 — Morley-Minto Reforms

**Secretary of State John Morley + Viceroy Lord Minto II = Morley-Minto Reforms = Indian Councils Act 1909.** This is one of the most consequential (and infamous) acts in Indian constitutional history, because of what it embedded.

**Key provisions:**

**Enlarged Legislative Councils:** Central Legislative Council expanded from 16 to 60 members. Provincial councils also enlarged significantly. Non-official elected members could form a majority in provincial councils (though the governor still had reserve powers).

**New powers for members:** Indians in legislative councils could now ask supplementary questions, move resolutions, and discuss the budget (previously only permitted in limited ways).

**First Indian in the Executive Council:** **Satyendra Prasanna Sinha** was appointed as the Law Member of the Viceroy's Executive Council — the first Indian to reach the highest advisory body of Indian governance. He later became Lord Sinha of Raipur (elevated to British peerage).

**The fatal provision — Separate Muslim Electorates:**
This is the act's most consequential and most debated legacy. The reforms introduced **separate communal electorates for Muslims**: Muslims would elect their own separate representatives from Muslim-only constituencies. Non-Muslims could not vote in these constituencies, and the seats were exclusively for Muslim candidates.

The demand had come from the Muslim League delegation (the **Shimla Deputation of 1906**, led by the Aga Khan) which had met Viceroy Minto and argued that Muslims needed separate representation to prevent being swamped by Hindu majorities. Minto agreed. Morley had reservations but yielded.

Congress fiercely opposed it — arguing that separate electorates would divide Indians along religious lines. The Muslim League supported it. Jinnah at the time was in Congress and opposed separate electorates (he would later become their champion). The debate over whether this provision "planted the seed of partition" has continued among historians.

**What Morley explicitly said:** Introducing the bill, Morley told Parliament that these reforms did NOT mean parliamentary government for India: *"If it could be said that this chapter of reforms led directly or necessarily to the establishment of a parliamentary system in India, I, for one, would have nothing to do with it."* This is a critical exam quote.

## Government of India Act 1919 — Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms

**Secretary of State Edwin Montagu + Viceroy Lord Chelmsford = Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms = Government of India Act 1919.** This is the most complex act in this series and generates the most exam questions.

The backdrop: Indians had contributed enormously to World War I (1914-18). Montagu issued a famous declaration in August 1917 — the **Montagu Declaration** — stating that British policy was *"the increasing association of Indians in every branch of the administration, and the gradual development of self-governing institutions with a view to the progressive realisation of responsible government in India."* This was the first time "responsible government" appeared as an official British goal. It was also vague enough to mean almost anything.

**Key provisions:**

**1. Dyarchy (Diarchy) in Provinces — THE KEY FEATURE:**
Provincial subjects were divided into two categories:
- **Reserved subjects** — retained by the Governor and his Executive Council, NOT accountable to the provincial legislature. These included: land revenue, justice, police, finance, irrigation. The Governor governed these; the legislature could not remove him.
- **Transferred subjects** — handed to Indian ministers who were accountable to the elected provincial legislature. These included: education, public health, agriculture, local self-government, public works (of local nature). Indians controlled these subjects, could be removed by the legislature.

This "dual government" in each province — part elected-accountable, part unelected-unaccountable — was called **Dyarchy** (from Greek: two rulers). It was inherently contradictory: ministers responsible to the legislature needed funds and cooperation from the reserved side (controlled by the governor) to actually govern their departments, but they had no power over that side. The system was designed to be limited — it did not work well in practice, which is exactly why it was abandoned in the 1935 Act.

**2. Bicameral Central Legislature:**
The old Imperial Legislative Council was replaced by a two-house Parliament:
- **Council of State** (upper house): 60 members, mostly nominated, some elected
- **Legislative Assembly** (lower house): 145 members, majority elected

But the central executive was NOT responsible to this legislature — the Viceroy could certify bills overriding the legislature, restore budgets cut by the legislature, and issue ordinances.

**3. Separate electorates extended:**
Building on 1909, separate communal electorates were now extended to **Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians**, and **Europeans** in addition to Muslims.

**4. Public Service Commission:**
The Act provided for the establishment of a **Public Service Commission** — a body to oversee civil service recruitment in India. This was the forerunner of the modern Union Public Service Commission (UPSC).

**5. Statutory Commission:**
The Act required that, ten years after its passage, a **statutory commission** would be appointed to review the reforms. That commission, appointed in 1927, was the **Simon Commission** — leading directly to the political crisis covered in the CDM lesson.

**6. Franchise expanded (but limited):**
About 10% of the adult population received the vote — property and tax-based qualifications meant most Indians were excluded.

## The Progression — A Summary Table

| Act | Year | Key Feature | What Was New |
|---|---|---|---|
| Government of India Act | 1858 | Crown takes over from Company | Secretary of State + India Council; Governor-General becomes Viceroy |
| Indian Councils Act | 1861 | Portfolio system; Ordinances | Indians nominated (not elected) to expanded councils; Bombay/Madras councils restored |
| Indian Councils Act | 1892 | Indirect "recommendation" (de facto election) | Budget discussion allowed; limited questions permitted |
| Indian Councils Act | 1909 | Morley-Minto Reforms | Separate Muslim electorates; Satyendra Prasanna Sinha in Executive Council |
| Government of India Act | 1919 | Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms | Dyarchy in provinces; bicameral central legislature; separate electorates extended; PSC established |

## Pre-1858 Reference Acts (For Context)

Exams occasionally test these alongside the main series:

| Act | Year | Key Provision |
|---|---|---|
| Regulating Act | 1773 | First British law to regulate EIC; Governor of Bengal became Governor-General (Warren Hastings first); Supreme Court established in Calcutta |
| Pitt's India Act | 1784 | Board of Control in London; Company kept commercial, Crown got political supervision |
| Charter Act | 1813 | Company's trade monopoly ended (except China and tea); Christian missionaries allowed entry |
| Charter Act | 1833 | Company became purely administrative (no more trade); Governor-General of Bengal became Governor-General of India (Bentinck first); competitive exams for civil service first proposed (but not implemented) |
| Charter Act | 1853 | Last Charter Act; competitive exams for ICS actually introduced; Legislative Council separated from Executive Council (legislative became more like a mini-Parliament) |

## Exam Traps — 17 Things SSC Will Test

1. **GoI Act 1858 ≠ Indian Councils Act** — GoI Act 1858 abolished the Company and created the Secretary of State; "Indian Councils Acts" are 1861, 1892, 1909 (separate series).
2. **Morley-Minto Reforms = Indian Councils Act 1909** — NOT 1907, NOT 1911. Morley (Secretary of State) + Minto (Viceroy).
3. **Separate Muslim electorates were introduced by the Indian Councils Act 1909** — NOT the 1919 Act (which extended them). The 1909 act is the origin.
4. **Satyendra Prasanna Sinha** — first Indian in Viceroy's Executive Council (1909 reforms); he became Law Member; later became Lord Sinha (first Indian elevated to British peerage).
5. **Dyarchy was introduced in PROVINCES** by the 1919 Act — NOT at the Centre. The Centre had a bicameral legislature but no dyarchy.
6. **Reserved subjects** (land revenue, police, justice, finance) were NOT accountable to the elected legislature — the Governor controlled them.
7. **Transferred subjects** (education, health, agriculture, local self-government) were accountable to the elected provincial legislature.
8. **Montagu-Chelmsford = GoI Act 1919**. Montagu = Secretary of State; Chelmsford = Viceroy. (Not "Chelmsford-Montagu" — Montagu is named first because he issued the 1917 Declaration.)
9. **"Responsible government" was first officially stated as a goal** in the 1917 Montagu Declaration (ahead of the 1919 Act).
10. **Morley explicitly denied** that the 1909 reforms meant parliamentary government — his statement is testable.
11. **Bicameral central legislature** introduced by **GoI Act 1919**: Council of State (upper) + Legislative Assembly (lower). The term "Parliament of India" was NOT used — it became that only after independence.
12. **Indian Councils Act 1861** introduced: Portfolio system (each Executive Councillor heads a department) + Viceroy's Ordinance power.
13. **Indian Councils Act 1892** introduced indirect "recommendation"/election (first time) + budget discussion rights (no voting).
14. **Simon Commission was mandated by the GoI Act 1919** — the 10-year review clause. Appointed 1927; arrived India 1928.
15. **Public Service Commission** was first provided for in GoI Act 1919 — the ancestor of the UPSC.
16. **Regulating Act 1773** — Warren Hastings became the first Governor-General (of Bengal). Charter Act 1833 — Bentinck became first Governor-General of India (not just Bengal).
17. **Charter Act 1853** — competitive examinations for ICS actually implemented (1833 only proposed it). Also: last Charter Act before the 1857 Revolt ended the Company era.
