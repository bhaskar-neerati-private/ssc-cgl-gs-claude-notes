# Civil Disobedience Movement, Round Table Conferences & Poona Pact (1930–1934)

## The Gap Years — A Movement Rebuilding Itself

When Gandhi withdrew the Non-Cooperation Movement in February 1922, there was a widespread sense of deflation. But the years between 1922 and 1929 were not empty — they were years of reorganisation, internal debates, and gathering pressure that would eventually produce the most dramatic single act of civil defiance in Indian history: the walk to Dandi.

Three events in the late 1920s pushed India back toward confrontation with the British:

**The Simon Commission (1927):** The Government of India Act 1919 had included a clause requiring a review of constitutional progress after ten years. In November 1927 — three years early — the British Conservative government appointed the **Indian Statutory Commission** under Sir John Simon to review the constitutional situation. The commission had seven members. Every single one of them was British. Not a single Indian was included.

The insult was calculated and the fury was genuine. The INC, the Muslim League, and virtually every Indian political organisation called for a boycott. When the Simon Commission arrived in India in February 1928, it was met in every city with black flags and the slogan: **"Simon Go Back."**

The most consequential protest came in Lahore. **Lala Lajpat Rai** — "Punjab Kesari," the Lion of Punjab, the man who had been deported to Mandalay in 1907, the man who had written *Unhappy India* — led a protest march against the Simon Commission in Lahore on 30 October 1928. The police superintendent, James Scott, ordered a lathi charge. Rai was struck directly on the chest. He gave a speech from his sickbed: "Every blow aimed at me is a nail in the coffin of the British Empire in India." On 17 November 1928, Lala Lajpat Rai died — officially of heart failure, but in nationalist memory, murdered by the British baton. Bhagat Singh, who idolised Rai, vowed revenge.

**The Nehru Report (1928):** As a counter to the all-British Simon Commission, the INC appointed an all-Indian committee to draft India's own constitutional proposals. Motilal Nehru (Jawaharlal's father) chaired it — the report thus became the **Nehru Report (1928)**. Its key recommendation: Dominion Status for India (within the British Empire, like Canada and Australia), with a unitary government and joint electorates (no separate communal electorates). Jinnah, who was still in Congress at this point and genuinely wanted Hindu-Muslim cooperation, offered amendments — his **"Fourteen Points" (1929)** — proposing modified separate electorates and greater provincial autonomy. Congress rejected them. This rejection pushed Jinnah toward separatism. The Nehru Report / Fourteen Points divide is often seen as a turning point in Jinnah's trajectory.

**Lahore Session — Purna Swaraj:** In December 1929, the INC met at Lahore. **Jawaharlal Nehru** presided — at 39, the youngest Congress president to that point. Two historic decisions were made: (1) The goal was changed from **Dominion Status** to **Purna Swaraj** (Complete Independence — a full break from the British Empire, not just autonomy within it). (2) **26 January 1930** was declared India's first Independence Day — a day for mass pledge-taking ceremonies across the country. **This is why we celebrate Republic Day on 26 January** — the date was chosen in 1950 for the Constitution coming into force precisely to honour the 1930 pledge. Every year from 1930 to 1947, nationalists gathered on 26 January to renew the independence pledge.

Gandhi was then asked to launch a civil disobedience campaign. He wrote to Viceroy Lord Irwin (30 January 1930) with eleven demands — the most prominent being abolition of the salt tax. Irwin did not respond substantively. Gandhi announced his campaign.

## The Dandi March — 12 March to 6 April 1930

On 12 March 1930, Gandhi set out from his Sabarmati Ashram in Ahmedabad with **78 chosen volunteers** to walk to **Dandi**, a coastal village in Gujarat — a distance of **241 miles (387 km)**. He was 60 years old.

Why salt? Salt is the one commodity every Indian — Hindu, Muslim, peasant, merchant, man, woman — uses every single day. The British had made the manufacture and sale of salt a government monopoly; it was illegal for Indians to make their own salt even from the sea they lived on. The salt tax was not large in revenue terms, but its symbolic quality was perfect: it was a law so petty, so universally applicable, so obviously unjust that breaking it would make the case for independence without a single speech.

The 24-day march was covered by foreign press — international journalists walked alongside Gandhi, cabling stories back to Europe and America. Each night, Gandhi addressed crowds who came from villages along the route. By the time the column reached Dandi, it had swelled to thousands.

On **6 April 1930**, Gandhi waded into the sea, picked up a handful of salt-crusted mud, and made salt. He had broken the law. The Civil Disobedience Movement had begun.

The response was extraordinary. Across India:
- On the Malabar coast and in Tamil Nadu, people made salt on beaches by the hundreds of thousands
- In the forests of Maharashtra, the **no-forest-law** campaign began — people cut trees and collected forest produce that British law had restricted
- In Gujarat's Bardoli, peasants refused to pay land revenue
- **C. Rajagopalachari** (Rajaji) led the **Vedaranyam Salt March** in Tamil Nadu — the South Indian parallel to Dandi
- In the North-West Frontier Province, **Khan Abdul Ghaffar Khan** — the "**Frontier Gandhi**" — organised the **Khudai Khidmatgar** (Servants of God), also called the "Red Shirts," a non-violent movement of Pashtuns. This was remarkable: Pashtuns, stereotyped as inherently martial, embracing Gandhian non-violence.
- On **18 April 1930**, **Surya Sen** ("Masterda") led the **Chittagong Armoury Raid** — revolutionary nationalists captured the Chittagong armoury and telegraph office, declared a "provisional government," and fought police and soldiers for several days before dispersing into the forest. Surya Sen was captured in 1933, hanged in January 1934.

Gandhi was arrested on **5 May 1930**. Then came the **Dharasana Salt Works raid** (21 May 1930) — led by Sarojini Naidu (Gandhi having been arrested), followed by Abbas Tyabji. Volunteers marched in columns toward the salt works; police beat them with steel-tipped lathis; the volunteers did not fight back, did not run. American journalist Webb Miller's eyewitness account was cabled to 1,350 newspapers worldwide. It was one of the most devastating pieces of publicity the British ever received.

## The Three Round Table Conferences

**First Round Table Conference (November 1930 – January 1931):**
The British invited Indian representatives to London to discuss constitutional reform. The INC **boycotted** it — Gandhi and most Congress leaders were in prison or leading the movement. The conference was attended by the Muslim League (Mohammed Ali Jinnah), the Hindu Mahasabha (M.M. Malaviya), the Princes of the native states, liberals, and other minority representatives. Without Congress, the conference was hollow — the British knew it. Its key outcome: agreement in principle on a federal structure for India. But nothing could be decided without Congress.

**Gandhi-Irwin Pact — 5 March 1931:**
Viceroy Lord Irwin opened direct negotiations with Gandhi. After weeks of talks, the **Gandhi-Irwin Pact** was signed on 5 March 1931. Its terms: (1) Congress would suspend the Civil Disobedience Movement; (2) Congress would attend the Second Round Table Conference; (3) The government would release political prisoners (except those convicted of violence); (4) Salt manufacturing on the coastline would be permitted to local people; (5) Peaceful picketing of foreign cloth and liquor shops would be allowed.

Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev, and Rajguru were hanged on **23 March 1931** — eighteen days after the pact. Many nationalists bitterly criticised Gandhi for not making their release a condition of the pact. Gandhi did appeal to Irwin for commutation; Irwin refused. The hangings cast a shadow over the pact and turned many revolutionary-minded Indians against Gandhi's method.

**Second Round Table Conference (September – December 1931):**
Gandhi attended in London as the sole representative of the INC. The conference broke down on the communal representation question — how to apportion legislative seats among Hindus, Muslims, Sikhs, Depressed Classes (Dalits), Christians, and others. Gandhi opposed separate electorates for Depressed Classes on the grounds that it would permanently divide Hindu society; Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, representing the Depressed Classes, demanded it. The conference ended without agreement.

Gandhi returned to India in December 1931 to find that Irwin had been replaced by **Lord Willingdon** — a much harder man. The political situation had deteriorated: NWFP and UP were under emergency ordinances. Gandhi met Willingdon and demanded these be lifted; Willingdon refused. Gandhi renewed Civil Disobedience in January 1932 and was immediately arrested.

**Third Round Table Conference (November – December 1932):**
Congress boycotted again; Gandhi was in jail. The conference was sparsely attended and largely irrelevant. Its deliberations eventually fed into the **Government of India Act 1935** — the longest piece of legislation in British parliamentary history to that point.

## Communal Award and the Poona Pact

**Communal Award (16 August 1932):**
British Prime Minister Ramsay MacDonald announced the **Communal Award** — the government's solution to the communal representation deadlock from the Second RTC. The Award gave separate electorates to Muslims, Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians, Europeans — and crucially — to **Depressed Classes (Dalits)**, who would have their own separate electorate to elect their own representatives.

Gandhi, in Yerwada Jail in Pune, announced a **fast unto death** against the separate electorate for Depressed Classes — set to begin 20 September 1932. His argument: a separate electorate would permanently segregate Depressed Class Hindus from the Hindu community, entrenching untouchability rather than reforming it. Dr. Ambedkar's counter: without a separate electorate, upper-caste Hindus would always control which "Dalit" candidates got elected — the reserved seats would be meaningless tokens.

Gandhi's fast created a political crisis. Nationalist leaders, Congress leaders, Hindu religious figures, and social reformers descended on Yerwada for emergency negotiations with Ambedkar. On **26 September 1932**, Gandhi (now on the verge of death) and Ambedkar reached the **Poona Pact**:

- Separate electorates for Depressed Classes **were dropped**
- Instead: **148 reserved seats** in Provincial (state) legislative assemblies (increased from the 71 proposed in the Communal Award)
- **18% of Central (national) legislature seats** reserved for Depressed Classes
- Elections to these seats: joint electorate, but primary elections within the Depressed Class community would produce a panel of candidates for the general electorate to vote on
- Educational grants and government jobs to be proportional

Gandhi broke his fast. The Communal Award provision for Depressed Classes was replaced by the Poona Pact framework.

**The Ambedkar-Gandhi divide:** The Poona Pact was a compromise under extreme duress. Ambedkar later wrote that he felt coerced — Gandhi's fast had effectively used moral blackmail; Ambedkar had to choose between letting Gandhi die (which would have triggered Hindu backlash against Dalits) or surrendering the separate electorate. The Poona Pact was the beginning of a profound mutual critique between Ambedkar and Gandhi that lasted the rest of their lives — Gandhi called Ambedkar's separate electorate demand "suicidal for Hinduism"; Ambedkar said Gandhi's model would keep Dalits perpetually subordinate.

## Exam Traps — 18 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Simon Commission was appointed in 1927** (not 1928 — it arrived in India in 1928, appointed 1927).
2. Simon Commission had **7 members** — ALL British. Zero Indians.
3. **Lajpat Rai died November 17, 1928** — NOT at the scene of the lathi charge on 30 October; he died weeks later, of heart failure attributed to the beating.
4. James Scott ordered the Lahore lathi charge — NOT Dyer (common confusion; Dyer was Jallianwala Bagh 1919, Scott was Lahore 1928).
5. **Nehru Report = Motilal Nehru** (the father), NOT Jawaharlal Nehru. Jawaharlal presided at Lahore Session 1929.
6. **Jinnah's 14 Points (1929)** were a response to the Nehru Report (1928) — two separate documents by two different Nehrus.
7. **Purna Swaraj adopted at Lahore Session December 1929** — NOT Calcutta, NOT 1928.
8. **Jawaharlal Nehru presided at Lahore 1929** — at age 39, making him the youngest Congress president to that point.
9. 26 January is Republic Day because the **1930 Purna Swaraj pledge was taken on 26 January 1930** — the date was chosen in 1950 to honour that pledge.
10. Dandi March: **12 March to 6 April 1930** (24 days), **241 miles**, **78 volunteers**, from **Sabarmati Ashram, Ahmedabad** to **Dandi, Gujarat**.
11. **Chittagong Armoury Raid = 18 April 1930** — Surya Sen ("Masterda"). Do NOT confuse with Dandi (different date, different location, different leader, different method).
12. **First RTC = Congress boycotted**. Second RTC = Gandhi attended alone as Congress rep. Third RTC = Congress boycotted again.
13. **Gandhi-Irwin Pact = 5 March 1931** — it suspended CDM, NOT ended it (CDM was renewed in January 1932 and formally withdrawn only in 1934).
14. **Bhagat Singh hanged 23 March 1931** — AFTER Gandhi-Irwin Pact (5 March); Gandhi could NOT secure commutation.
15. **Communal Award = 16 August 1932** by British PM **Ramsay MacDonald** — NOT Willingdon, NOT Churchill.
16. Poona Pact gave Depressed Classes **148 reserved seats** in Provincial assemblies (UP from 71 in Communal Award) — numbers are tested.
17. **Poona Pact = 26 September 1932** — Gandhi fasted in **Yerwada Jail, Pune** (NOT Sabarmati, NOT Delhi). Ambedkar signed the pact, not any other Dalit leader.
18. **Khan Abdul Ghaffar Khan = "Frontier Gandhi"** — his volunteers were called **Khudai Khidmatgar** (also "Red Shirts"). He led them in NWFP (North-West Frontier Province, now KPK in Pakistan).
