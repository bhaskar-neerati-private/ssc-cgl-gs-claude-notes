## The Men Who Built an Empire

Each Governor-General of the Company era left a specific imprint on India — some through conquest, some through law, some through infrastructure that still exists. Together, across eighty years, they transformed a trading company into an empire.

The office itself was created by the **Regulating Act of 1773**, which appointed a Governor-General of Bengal to supervise the other two Presidencies (Bombay and Madras). Under the **Charter Act of 1833**, the office was upgraded to Governor-General of India — with direct authority over all territories.

This lesson covers the twelve most important Governor-Generals before 1857, with their key actions, the policies that defined their tenure, and the exact facts SSC tests.

## 1. Warren Hastings (1772–1785)

### The First Governor-General

Warren Hastings was appointed **Governor of Bengal in 1772** — before the Regulating Act created the Governor-General title. The **Regulating Act of 1773** made him the first **Governor-General of Bengal**.

**What he did**:
- **Abolished Dual Government (1772)**: Took direct administrative control of Bengal; ended the fiction of the Nawab having any real power
- **Revenue reforms**: Direct collection by Company officials; replaced the corrupt practice of tax-farming
- **Judicial reforms**: Established civil and criminal courts at district level; Calcutta had a Supreme Court (created by Regulating Act)
- **Wars**: First Anglo-Maratha War (1775–82); Second Anglo-Mysore War (1780–84)
- **Rohilla War (1773–74)**: Helped Nawab of Awadh crush the Rohillas — controversial; used against him in later impeachment
- Supported Asiatic Society of Bengal (founded by William Jones, 1784)

**The Impeachment**: Edmund Burke (British parliamentarian) impeached Warren Hastings in 1788 for corruption and misuse of power. The trial lasted seven years (1788–1795). Hastings was **acquitted** — but the trial established the principle that Governors-General were accountable to Parliament.

## 2. Lord Cornwallis (1786–1793)

### The Reformer and the Permanent Settlement

Cornwallis arrived in India determined to clean up Company administration, which he found corrupt and inefficient. His two lasting contributions are the Permanent Settlement and the Cornwallis Code.

**Permanent Settlement (1793)**:
- Fixed the land revenue demand permanently — zamindars would pay a fixed sum to the Company forever
- Zamindars became hereditary owners of land (private property rights)
- Applied to: **Bengal, Bihar, Orissa** (not all of India — Madras and Bombay got different systems later)
- **Benefit**: Zamindars had incentive to invest in land (revenue was fixed, so any surplus was theirs)
- **Problem**: Peasants (ryots) were left at the mercy of zamindars; Company lost out on revenue increases as agriculture grew
- If zamindars failed to pay, their land was auctioned — many traditional zamindars were displaced; merchants bought their land

**Cornwallis Code (1793)**:
- Separated the roles of **Collector** (revenue) and **Magistrate** (law/order) — reducing corruption through separation of powers
- **Indianisation reversed**: Cornwallis believed Indians were inherently corrupt; he barred Indians from all posts with salaries above ₹500/year — effectively Europeanized the senior civil service
- This discrimination became a major grievance of educated Indians

**Third Anglo-Mysore War (1790–92)**: Led by Cornwallis himself; captured Seringapatnam; Treaty of Seringapatnam — Tipu gave up half territory and sons as hostages.

## 3. Lord Wellesley (1798–1805)

### The Empire-Builder

Already covered in the British Expansion lesson — key points recap:
- **Subsidiary Alliance (1798)**: First used with Hyderabad; spread across India
- **Fourth Anglo-Mysore War (1799)**: Tipu Sultan killed at Seringapatnam
- **Second Anglo-Maratha War (1803–05)**: British took Delhi-Agra; Mughal Emperor under British protection
- Wellesley described himself as "a Bengal tiger"; his aggressive expansionism alarmed the Company's directors in London (worried about costs); he was recalled

---

## 4. Lord Hastings / Marquess of Hastings (1813–1823)

### End of Maratha Power; Hills Opened

Not to be confused with Warren Hastings. Lord Hastings (Francis Rawdon-Hastings) was the most expansionist GG between Wellesley and Dalhousie.

**Third Anglo-Maratha War (1817–18)**: As covered; Peshwa Baji Rao II exiled; Maratha Confederacy dissolved; British became paramount power in India.

**Pindari War (1817–18)**: The Pindaris were armed bands (freebooters) who accompanied Maratha armies and raided villages. Hastings launched a massive operation to eliminate them — conducted simultaneously with the Third Maratha War.

**Anglo-Gurkha War (1814–16)**:
- Nepal's Gurkhas had been expanding into British-protected territories
- Hard-fought war; British suffered initial reverses
- **Treaty of Sagauli (1816)**: Nepal ceded Sikkim, Kumaon, Garhwal; gave British the right to station a Resident in Kathmandu; also opened up the hill towns — **Shimla, Mussoorie, Nainital** became British hill stations from this point
- Gurkhas allowed to join British Army — a practice that continues to this day

**Abandoned non-intervention policy** — asserted British paramountcy openly.

## 5. Lord William Bentinck (1828–1835)

### The Great Reformer

William Bentinck is the most reform-minded Governor-General of the Company era. His tenure transformed Indian society and administration more than any military conquest.

### Abolition of Sati (1829)

**Regulation XVII (1829)** declared the practice of Sati — the burning of a widow on her husband's funeral pyre — illegal and punishable as **culpable homicide**.

The credit belongs to both Bentinck and **Raja Ram Mohan Roy**, who had campaigned against Sati for years and provided Bentinck with data, public support, and the moral argument. Without Roy's groundwork, Bentinck might not have acted.

### Suppression of Thuggee

The Thugs were a criminal fraternity that ritually murdered and robbed travelers as an offering to the goddess Kali. **Colonel William Henry Sleeman** was the officer responsible for systematically identifying and capturing Thugs across India. Thousands were arrested; the practice was effectively eliminated by the 1840s.

### Macaulay's Minute on Education (1835)

Lord Macaulay (Law Member of the Governor-General's Council) submitted his famous Minute arguing that:
- **English should be the medium of instruction** (not Sanskrit or Persian)
- The goal was to create a class of people "Indian in blood and colour, but English in tastes, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect"
- The limited education funds should go toward English-medium education

Bentinck accepted Macaulay's recommendation. The **English Education Act of 1835** made English the official language of higher education in India — a decision with consequences for Indian nationalism, literature, and governance that persist today.

### First Governor-General of India

Under the **Charter Act of 1833**, the Governor-General of Bengal's title was changed to **Governor-General of India** — giving the position direct authority over all Indian territories. **William Bentinck was the first person to hold this title** (previously, they were Governor-General of Bengal).

### Other reforms
- Abolished provincial courts of appeal; reformed judicial system
- Cut military expenditure; reduced officers' allowances
- Allowed Indian judges (Sadar Amin) to try European defendants — revolutionary for the time

## 6. Lord Metcalfe (1835–1836)

### The Liberator of the Press

Sir Charles Metcalfe served as acting Governor-General for only one year — but his act earned him a permanent title: **"Liberator of the Press."**

The press in India operated under strict licensing regulations introduced by earlier Governors. Metcalfe **removed all restrictions on the Indian press** in 1835, allowing newspapers to be published without government permission. Indian journalism took off in the years following.

---

## 7. Lord Auckland (1836–1842)

### The Afghan Disaster

Auckland launched the **First Anglo-Afghan War (1839–42)** — arguably the worst British military disaster before 1857.

**Background**: Russia was expanding southward (the "Great Game"). Auckland feared Russian influence in Afghanistan and launched a military intervention to replace Dost Mohammad (the Afghan ruler) with the pro-British Shah Shuja.

**The disaster**: The British installed Shah Shuja, but Afghan resistance was overwhelming. In **January 1842**, the entire British force of ~16,000 soldiers and camp followers was massacred during the retreat from Kabul — only one person, Dr. William Brydon, reached Jalalabad alive. This catastrophic defeat shocked British India. A retaliatory force was sent later that year, but British prestige had been severely damaged.

Auckland was recalled following the disaster.

---

## 8. Lord Hardinge I (1844–1848)

### The First Anglo-Sikh War

After Ranjit Singh's death (1839), the Sikh Empire in Punjab weakened through internal succession disputes. The **First Anglo-Sikh War (1845–46)** followed.

**Treaty of Lahore (1846)**:
- British took territories between Sutlej and Beas
- Kashmir ceded to the British
- British sold Kashmir to **Gulab Singh** (Dogra chief who had helped them) for **75 lakh rupees** — the founding of the princely state of Jammu and Kashmir
- Sikh army reduced; British Resident stationed at Lahore

Hardinge also banned **female infanticide** — an early social reform.

## 9. Lord Dalhousie (1848–1856)

### The Last and Greatest Expansionist

Lord Dalhousie was the most transformative and aggressive Governor-General of the Company era. His tenure planted the seeds of the 1857 Revolt while simultaneously modernizing India's infrastructure.

### Doctrine of Lapse

Dalhousie's most controversial policy: if an Indian ruler died without a **natural (biological) heir**, the state would **lapse** to the British — adopted sons had no right of succession.

**States annexed under Doctrine of Lapse**:

| State | Year | Reason |
|-------|------|--------|
| Satara | 1848 | No natural heir |
| Jaitpur, Sambalpur | 1849 | No natural heir |
| Baghat | 1850 | No natural heir |
| Udaipur | 1852 | No natural heir |
| **Jhansi** | 1853 | No natural heir (Rani Lakshmibai's adopted son rejected) |
| **Nagpur** | 1854 | No natural heir |

The annexation of **Jhansi** infuriated **Rani Lakshmibai** — who became one of the central figures of the 1857 Revolt. The annexation of **Nagpur** ended the Bhonsle family's power.

Dalhousie also annexed **Awadh (1856)** — not under Doctrine of Lapse, but on grounds of "misgovernance." This enraged both the Nawab and his soldiers (most of the Bengal Army's sepoys were from Awadh and had strong loyalty to the Nawab's court).

### Military Changes — Seeds of 1857

- Moved the European Artillery from Bengal (where Indian soldiers could access it) to Calcutta (away from sepoy brigades)
- **General Services Enlistment Act (1856)**: Soldiers must serve overseas if required — violated caste taboos about crossing the sea

These changes alarmed and angered the Bengal Army sepoys.

### Infrastructure Revolution

Dalhousie's modernizing legacy was extraordinary:

| Innovation | Year | Details |
|-----------|------|---------|
| **Railways** | 1853 | First railway: Bombay to Thane, **16 April 1853** (21 miles) |
| **Electric Telegraph** | 1851 | First line: Calcutta to Agra |
| **Post Office** | 1854 | Uniform postage system (like the UK's Penny Post); cheaper, standardized |
| **Public Works Department** | — | Created to manage roads, bridges, canals |
| **Wood's Despatch (1854)** | 1854 | "Magna Carta of English Education in India" — framework for universities, high schools, primary schools |

**Roorkee Engineering College (1847)**: First engineering college in Asia — also Dalhousie's period.

Dalhousie also created the **modern telegraph network** connecting Calcutta to Bombay, Madras, Peshawar — enabling rapid communication that the British used to suppress the 1857 Revolt.

## Exam Traps

1. **Warren Hastings = first GG of Bengal** (1773); **Bentinck = first GG of India** (1833) — do not confuse.
2. **Permanent Settlement = Cornwallis (1793)**, not Bentinck or Dalhousie.
3. **Cornwallis barred Indians from senior posts** — NOT Dalhousie; Cornwallis was the one who Europeanized the civil service.
4. **Sati abolished = 1829, Regulation XVII** — Raja Ram Mohan Roy provided crucial moral/political support; Bentinck enacted it.
5. **Macaulay's Minute = 1835** — English as medium of instruction; Bentinck accepted it.
6. **Metcalfe = Liberator of the Press (1835)** — only served one year but freed the press.
7. **First railway = 16 April 1853, Bombay to Thane** — Dalhousie; NOT Bentinck.
8. **First telegraph = 1851, Calcutta to Agra** — Dalhousie; separate from railways.
9. **Doctrine of Lapse = Dalhousie** — Jhansi 1853, Nagpur 1854 are the most tested states.
10. **Awadh annexed 1856 — not under Doctrine of Lapse** — "misgovernance" was the stated reason.
11. **Wood's Despatch (1854) = Charles Wood** — education framework; Dalhousie era.
12. **Treaty of Sagauli (1816) = Anglo-Gurkha War** — Nepal gave hills; Gurkhas could join British Army.
13. **Kashmir sold to Gulab Singh for 75 lakh rupees (Treaty of Lahore 1846)** — foundation of J&K state.
14. **Lord Hastings ≠ Warren Hastings** — completely different people; Marquess of Hastings = Third Anglo-Maratha War.
