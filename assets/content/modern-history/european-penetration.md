## The Five Flags

Five European powers planted their flags on Indian soil between 1498 and 1700: Portugal, the Netherlands, Denmark, France, and Britain. Each came for trade, each built forts and factories, and each tried to elbow out the others. By 1763, four of them had been reduced to coastal footnotes. One remained standing — and was already building an empire.

But to understand how the British ended up ruling India, you must understand the competition they won. The Carnatic Wars between Britain and France (1746–1763) were the decisive contest. Everything after Plassey (1757) flows from the outcome of that struggle.

---

## Portugal: The Pioneers (1498–c.1740)

### Vasco da Gama and the Sea Route

On 20 May **1498**, Vasco da Gama dropped anchor near **Calicut (Kozhikode)** on the Malabar Coast — the first European to reach India by sea. This discovery of the sea route around the Cape of Good Hope broke the Arab monopoly on Indian Ocean trade and opened a new era.

The Portuguese weren't interested in territorial empire at first — they wanted to control **spice trade**. Their method: control the sea lanes, not the land.

### Alfonso de Albuquerque — The Architect

The real builder of Portuguese power in India was **Alfonso de Albuquerque** (Governor 1509–1515):

- Captured **Goa** (1510) — became the permanent headquarters of Portuguese India ("Estado da India")
- Captured **Malacca** (1511) — controlled the spice route to the East
- Attempted to capture **Hormuz** (Persian Gulf) — controlled the western trade route
- Moved the headquarters from Cochin to **Goa (1530)** under Nino da Cunha

### The Cartaz System — Control Without Conquest

Portuguese innovation: the **Cartaz** — a naval pass issued by the Portuguese to ships trading in the Indian Ocean. Ships caught without a cartaz could be seized. This turned the entire Indian Ocean into a Portuguese toll road without occupying a square mile of territory. Indian, Arab, and Chinese merchants had to pay for the right to sail.

### What the Portuguese Brought

Beyond trade, the Portuguese left a cultural footprint:
- Introduced **tobacco, potato, tomato, chilli, cashew** — all New World crops — to India
- Spread Christianity; built churches in Goa (still standing)
- Introduced **printing press** to India

### Portuguese Decline

By the mid-1600s, Portuguese power had been eroded by the Dutch and English — both better financed and more aggressive. They retained Goa, Daman, and Diu until **Indian military action in 1961** (Operation Vijay) finally ended Portuguese rule — the last European power to leave India.

## The Dutch: Masters of Asia (1602–c.1759)

### VOC — The First Multinational Corporation

The **Dutch East India Company** (VOC — *Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie*) was founded in **1602** — the first joint-stock company with a stock exchange listing. It had more power than most governments: it could wage war, negotiate treaties, and establish colonies.

**Dutch settlements in India**:

| Settlement | Year | Location |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Masulipatnam | 1605 | Andhra coast |
| Pulicat | 1610 | Tamil Nadu (main base; had a fort) |
| Surat | 1616 | Gujarat |
| Chinsurah | 1653 | Bengal |
| Negapatam | — | Tamil Nadu |
| Cochin | — | Kerala |

### Dutch Defeats — End of Dutch India

**Battle of Colachel (1741)**: The Dutch attempted to establish naval supremacy on the Malabar Coast but were **defeated by Marthanda Varma**, the king of Travancore. The Dutch admiral **Eustachius De Lannoy** surrendered and entered Travancore service — helping modernize the Travancore navy. This is called the **first decisive European defeat in India** by an Indian power.

**Battle of Bedara / Biderra (1759)**: The Dutch tried to reassert dominance in Bengal, sending a fleet and troops. The English under **Colonel Clive** defeated them decisively. After Bedara, the Dutch gave up on India entirely and focused on their East Indies empire (modern Indonesia).

## The Danish: The Forgotten Europeans

The Danes established settlements at:
- **Tranquebar (Tharangambadi)** — Tamil Nadu coast, their primary base (1620)
- **Serampore (Srirampur)** — Bengal

Danish power in India was always minor. They sold Tranquebar and Serampore to the British in **1845**. The Serampore Mission (established by Baptist missionaries under William Carey, 1799) was the more lasting Danish legacy — it translated the Bible into Bengali and other languages.

## France: The Rivals Who Almost Won (1664–1763)

### The French East India Company

The **French East India Company** was founded in **1664** under Louis XIV (Jean-Baptiste Colbert organized it). Their main settlement: **Pondicherry** (1674) on the Coromandel Coast.

Other French settlements: Chandannagar (Bengal), Mahe (Kerala), Karikal (Tamil Nadu), Yanam (Andhra).

### Dupleix — The Man Who Almost Changed History

**Joseph François Dupleix** (Governor-General of French India, 1742–1754) was a strategic genius. He realized that the key to European dominance in India was not just trade but **political intervention** — backing Indian princes in their succession disputes and using Indian soldiers (sepoys) trained in European discipline.

Dupleix introduced two concepts that the English later mastered and used against the French:
1. **Using sepoys** (Indian soldiers trained in European methods) — cheaper than European troops, effective in Indian conditions
2. **Subsidiary Alliance** — support an Indian ruler, station troops at his court, make him financially dependent

His political maneuvering in the Carnatic (modern Tamil Nadu) and Hyderabad brought French influence to its peak — for a brief period, France was the dominant European power in the Deccan.

## The Three Carnatic Wars (1746–1763)

These three wars between the French and English, fought on Indian soil, decided which European power would dominate India. They are among the most tested topics in Modern History.

### First Carnatic War (1746–1748)

**Background**: European echo of the **War of Austrian Succession** (1740–1748).

**Key events**:
- French under **General La Bourdonnais** captured **Madras** (1746) from the English — a shocking blow
- **Battle of St. Thome (1746)**: A small French force defeated the Nawab of Carnatic's army of 10,000 — demonstrating that European-trained forces could defeat Indian armies
- Treaty of **Aix-la-Chapelle (1748)**: Austria-Succession war ended in Europe; Madras was returned to the English

**Result**: French won militarily but gained nothing permanently.

---

### Second Carnatic War (1749–1754)

**Background**: Succession disputes in both Hyderabad and Carnatic simultaneously.

| State | French-backed | English-backed |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Hyderabad | Muzaffar Jung | Nasir Jung |
| Carnatic | Chanda Sahib | Muhammad Ali |

**Key event — Siege of Arcot (1751)**:

Robert Clive (just 26 years old, a clerk turned soldier) proposed a bold plan: attack Arcot, the capital of the Carnatic, to draw Chanda Sahib's forces away from Trichinopoly. With just **200 English soldiers**, Clive captured Arcot and held it for **53 days** against a besieging force of 10,000. This audacious action broke French prestige and made Clive famous.

Muhammad Ali became Nawab of Carnatic with English support. **Dupleix was recalled to France (1754)** — the French government was unwilling to fund the expensive Indian adventure. His recall ended French political ambitions in India. 

**Result**: English gained political dominance in Carnatic. French retreated.

---

### Third Carnatic War (1756–1763)

**Background**: European echo of the **Seven Years' War** (1756–1763) — Britain vs France globally.

**Key event — Battle of Wandiwash (1760)**:

English forces under **Sir Eyre Coote** decisively defeated the French under **Count de Lally** at the **Battle of Wandiwash** (22 January 1760). This is the battle that ended French power in India.

- The English captured **Pondicherry** (1761) and destroyed its fortifications
- **Treaty of Paris (1763)**: Pondicherry returned to France — but as an **open town** (no fortifications allowed). France was allowed to trade but could never be a military power again.

**Result**: British became undisputed masters of peninsular India. French retained Pondicherry only as a commercial settlement (until they voluntarily handed it to India in 1954).

## The English East India Company

### Foundation and Early Foothold

The **English East India Company** was founded on **31 December 1600** by Queen Elizabeth I's royal charter. It was a joint-stock company with a monopoly on English trade with Asia.

**Key factories established**:

| Settlement | Year | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Surat | 1608/1613 | First factory; firman from Mughal Emperor Jahangir |
| Madras (Fort St. George) | 1639 | Obtained from Nayak of Chandragiri |
| Bombay | 1661 | Received as dowry when Catherine of Braganza married Charles II |
| Calcutta (Fort William) | 1690 | Established by Job Charnock |

**Thomas Best's victory (1612)**: English ships defeated Portuguese fleet at **Battle of Swally** (near Surat) — proved English naval superiority over Portuguese, earned goodwill of Mughal Emperor Jahangir, who granted them trading rights.

**Three Presidencies** emerged: Madras (1653), Bombay (1687), Calcutta (1700) — each with its own Council and President.

---

### The Events Leading to Plassey

By 1756, the Company was firmly established in Bengal — the richest province of the Mughal Empire. But relations with the new Nawab of Bengal, **Siraj-ud-Daulah** (who succeeded Alivardi Khan in 1756), had soured:
- Siraj resented English fortification of Calcutta without permission
- English were sheltering his political enemies

**Black Hole of Calcutta (June 1756)**: Siraj captured Calcutta. According to a disputed account by J.Z. Holwell, **146 English prisoners** were locked in a small cell overnight; only 23 survived. Modern historians dispute the numbers, but the incident became British propaganda justifying their subsequent actions.

Robert Clive and Admiral Watson retook Calcutta in **January 1757**. The next act — the **Battle of Plassey** — would change India forever. That is the story of the next lesson.

## Why the English Won — The Four Advantages

The English succeeded where the Portuguese, Dutch, and French failed for four structural reasons:

1. **Naval strength**: England's Royal Navy was the world's best; protected Company supply lines and cut off rivals
2. **Financial muscle**: London's financial markets (Bank of England, 1694) gave the Company access to capital that French and Dutch companies couldn't match
3. **Political will**: English Parliament and government backed the Company consistently; France recalled Dupleix when things got expensive
4. **Commercial orientation**: English Company was purely profit-driven; focused on trade revenues to fund expansion. French Company was partly a government vanity project.

## Exam Traps

1. **Vasco da Gama landed at Calicut (Kozhikode), NOT Goa** — in 1498.
2. **Albuquerque captured Goa in 1510** — not Vasco da Gama.
3. **Battle of Colachel (1741): Dutch defeated by Travancore (not by English)** — Marthanda Varma won.
4. **Battle of Bedara (1759): Dutch defeated by English** — this ended Dutch presence in India.
5. **Dupleix introduced using sepoys** — English copied this from him.
6. **Siege of Arcot (1751)**: Clive's force = 200; besieging force = 10,000; held for 53 days.
7. **Battle of Wandiwash (1760)**: Coote (English) defeated Lally (French) — ended French power.
8. **First Carnatic War = Austrian Succession War; Third Carnatic War = Seven Years' War** — know the European echoes.
9. **Treaty of Paris (1763)** — French got Pondicherry back but WITHOUT fortifications.
10. **Bombay came to England as a dowry** (1661) — Catherine of Braganza + Charles II.
11. **Calcutta founded by Job Charnock** (1690) — not Clive.
12. **English East India Company founded 1600** — same year as, or just before, the Dutch VOC (1602).
