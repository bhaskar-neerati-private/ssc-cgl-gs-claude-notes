# Gandhi's Entry, Rowlatt Satyagraha & Non-Cooperation Movement (1915–1922)

## The Man Who Came Back Different

When Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi stepped off the ship at Bombay in January 1915, he was 45 years old and already famous — not in India, but in South Africa. He had spent 21 years there (1893–1914), fighting racial laws, founding his Phoenix Settlement and Tolstoy Farm, going to prison, developing the method he called *Satyagraha* (Truth-Force), and leading the Indian indentured labourers' resistance. He returned to India not as a revolutionary, not as a lawyer, but as something entirely new: a moral force in a dhoti.

His mentor Gopal Krishna Gokhale — the "Prince of Workers," who had urged Gandhi to return and serve India — gave him one instruction before dying in February 1915: **travel through India for one year, observe, listen, say nothing.** Gandhi obeyed. He spent 1915 touring the country by third-class train, talking to peasants, understanding poverty from inside it. This year of listening made him the kind of leader India had never had.

The three episodes that made Gandhi's name inside India came in quick succession:

**Champaran (1917):** Indigo farmers in Bihar were trapped in the *tinkathia* system — forced to cultivate indigo on 3/20ths of their land for British planters at exploitative fixed prices. Gandhi arrived, was served a notice to leave the district, refused, and calmly prepared to go to jail. The local magistrate backed down. Gandhi then conducted a systematic inquiry — travelling village to village, recording testimonies — and presented overwhelming evidence to the government. The Champaran Agrarian Act 1917 freed farmers from the tinkathia system. This was Gandhi's first political victory in India, and it established a template: go to the people, investigate, refuse to leave, force the government to engage. **Champaran is the first civil disobedience movement in India under Gandhi.**

**Ahmedabad Mill Strike (1918):** Mill workers in Ahmedabad — Gandhi's own city — demanded a 50% wage increase during a plague epidemic. The mill owner was Ambalal Sarabhai, whose sister Anasuya Sarabhai was Gandhi's close associate. Gandhi mediated, asked workers not to give up, and when the strike faltered, announced a *fast unto death* — his first fast in Indian politics. The mill owners, rattled, agreed to arbitration. Workers got a 35% increase. The episode is notable because Gandhi fasted against people he was trying to *help*, not against the British — demonstrating that satyagraha's pressure could be self-directed. He also had a personal connection (Anasuya) to both sides, making his position delicate and his fast even more remarkable.

**Kheda Satyagraha (1918):** Crops in Kheda district (Gujarat) failed; under revenue rules, if crops fall below 25% of normal, revenue should be remitted. The government refused to acknowledge the failure. Gandhi asked peasants to withhold revenue. The government eventually issued a secret circular saying revenue collection from poorer farmers would be suspended — a quiet climb-down. Vallabhbhai Patel emerged as Gandhi's lieutenant during this campaign. **The Kheda Satyagraha is significant as the first time the peasant class became directly involved in the freedom struggle under Gandhi.**

## The Rowlatt Act — The Black Law

World War I had ended in November 1918. Indians had contributed enormously — 1.5 million soldiers, massive financial contributions, 74,000 soldiers killed. There was widespread expectation of political reward: more self-government, more rights. Instead, in March 1919, the British passed the **Rowlatt Act** — officially the Anarchical and Revolutionary Crimes Act, based on the recommendations of the Rowlatt Committee chaired by Justice Sydney Rowlatt.

The Act allowed the government to imprison any person suspected of sedition *without trial* for up to two years. The famous phrase: *"No appeal, no daleel, no wakeel"* — no appeal, no argument, no lawyer. It was preventive detention taken to its extreme.

Indians were furious. Every single elected Indian member of the Imperial Legislative Council voted against it — the British still passed it. Gandhi called it the "Black Act" and called for a nationwide *hartal* (total shutdown) on **6 April 1919** — shops closed, transport stopped, people fasted. The response was overwhelming. For the first time, Gandhi had called a national protest and the whole nation had answered.

The trouble was that in Punjab, the protests turned violent. The British arrested two popular leaders — Dr. Satyapal and Dr. Kitchlew — in Amritsar on 10 April. Crowds marching to protest the arrests were fired upon. A British woman, Miss Marcella Sherwood, was attacked. Brigadier-General Reginald Dyer was called to restore order.

## Jallianwala Bagh — 13 April 1919

13 April 1919 was Baisakhi — the Punjabi new year festival. People from across the region had come to Amritsar to celebrate and to attend a public meeting at **Jallianwala Bagh**, a walled garden near the Golden Temple.

General Dyer arrived with 90 troops and 2 armoured cars (the cars couldn't enter the narrow lane leading into the Bagh). He blocked the main exit and, without any warning, ordered his men to fire into the crowd. They fired for approximately 10-15 minutes — 1,650 rounds. People fled to the wells inside the garden; many jumped in. The official figure was 379 dead, 1,200 wounded. Actual estimates are much higher — over 1,000 dead by most Indian accounts. Dyer imposed martial law, made Indians crawl on the street where Miss Sherwood had been attacked (the infamous "crawling order"), and flogged people publicly.

The Hunter Commission was formed to investigate. Its report (1920) censured Dyer but did not prosecute him. Dyer was forced to resign from the army — but the **British House of Lords** passed a motion praising him, and the British public raised £26,000 (the "Morning Post Fund") for him as a testimonial. Rabindranath Tagore renounced his knighthood in protest. The massacre made Gandhi conclude that the British government was "satanic" and could not be reformed from within.

**Exam trap:** Udham Singh avenged Jallianwala Bagh by shooting Michael O'Dwyer (Lieutenant-Governor of Punjab at the time) in London in 1940 — *not* General Dyer. O'Dwyer had approved Dyer's actions. Dyer himself had died of natural causes in 1927.

## The Khilafat Movement — An Alliance Opportunity

While Jallianwala Bagh was burning into Indian memory, a parallel issue was galvanising Indian Muslims: the fate of the Ottoman Caliphate after World War I.

The Caliph (Sultan of the Ottoman Empire) was the symbolic religious head of Sunni Muslims worldwide. Britain and its allies had defeated the Ottomans; the post-war peace settlement was expected to dismember the Ottoman Empire and abolish or severely weaken the Caliphate. Indian Muslims — including many who had never been particularly religious — saw this as a religious insult and an act of bad faith by Britain, to which Indian Muslims had been loyal throughout the war.

The **Khilafat Movement** began in 1919, led by Mohammed Ali and Shaukat Ali (the **Ali Brothers**). They demanded that the Sultan of Turkey retain his position as Caliph and that the Ottoman territories in Arab lands and Turkey be left intact.

Gandhi saw this as a golden opportunity for Hindu-Muslim unity — something every nationalist had dreamed of. He supported the Khilafat Movement enthusiastically, arguing that if Hindus supported Muslims' religious grievance, Muslims would join Hindus in the struggle for *Swaraj*. The Khilafat Committee and the Indian National Congress thus moved in parallel, and in many places merged their activities.

**Exam trap:** Mustafa Kemal Atatürk — a Turk, not a Briton — abolished the Caliphate himself in 1924. The Khilafat Movement ended not because the British agreed but because a Turkish nationalist leader abolished the very institution the movement was defending. This abrupt end caused a collapse of the Hindu-Muslim unity Gandhi had so carefully built.

## The Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–1922)

By mid-1920, Gandhi had made a decisive break. The Rowlatt Act, Jallianwala Bagh, the Hunter Commission's inadequate response, and the unsatisfactory Treaty of Sèvres (imposing harsh terms on the Ottomans) all convinced him that cooperative engagement with the British government was impossible. He returned his medals from South Africa to the Viceroy.

He launched the **Non-Cooperation Movement** — the first mass political movement under his leadership.

**The two-stage adoption:**
- **Calcutta Special Session, September 1920** — Gandhi proposed Non-Cooperation; it was debated fiercely. Lala Lajpat Rai presided. The motion passed. This was the special session.
- **Nagpur Session, December 1920** — The regular annual INC session formally and overwhelmingly adopted the Non-Cooperation programme with a broader mandate. C. Vijayaraghavachariar presided. The Congress also altered its constitution at Nagpur: reorganised on linguistic lines, dropped "welfare of the Indian people within the framework of the British Empire" and adopted *Swaraj* as the goal.

**The programme — four-phased withdrawal:**
1. **Surrender of titles and honorary offices** — lawyers, educators, and local government members resign honours
2. **Boycott of government educational institutions, courts, legislative councils, and foreign goods** — students leave government schools; lawyers quit courts; politicians don't contest elections; people stop buying British cloth and burn it publicly
3. **Non-payment of taxes** (if phase 2 went well)
4. **Rejection of government service** (the final phase)

The response was extraordinary. Lawyers like C.R. Das, Motilal Nehru, Saifuddin Kitchlew gave up their lucrative legal practices. Students left government schools — national schools multiplied. The Tilak Swaraj Fund raised one crore rupees in six months. INC membership, earlier in the thousands, reached lakhs. The Ali Brothers' Khilafat campaign and Gandhi's Non-Cooperation ran as twin engines of the same vehicle.

**Significant events within the movement:**
- **Boycott of the Duke of Connaught's visit** and the Prince of Wales's visit (November 1921) — when the Prince arrived in Bombay, there was a complete *hartal*; shops were shut; streets were empty. Violence broke out in Bombay between those who boycotted and those who celebrated — Gandhi fasted in response.
- **Moplah Rebellion (1921)** — Muslim peasants (Moplahs) in the Malabar region of Kerala rose against Hindu landlords and British officials. The uprising mixed Khilafat grievances with agrarian resentment. It turned violent and communal, with some Hindus forcibly converted. It created tension in the Non-Cooperation/Khilafat alliance.

By early 1922, the movement was at its peak — but Gandhi was preparing to escalate to mass civil disobedience. He planned a campaign starting from Bardoli taluka (Gujarat), beginning with non-payment of taxes.

## Chauri Chaura — 4-5 February 1922

Then came **Chauri Chaura.**

On 4-5 February 1922 in Chauri Chaura, Gorakhpur district (United Provinces), a procession of Non-Cooperation volunteers marched past a police station. The police fired on the crowd. The enraged crowd retaliated — chased the policemen into the station, set the station on fire, and killed **22 policemen** (some accounts say 23) who burned to death inside.

Gandhi heard the news and was devastated. He announced the **withdrawal of the Non-Cooperation Movement** on 12 February 1922 — unilaterally, without consulting the Congress Working Committee. His reasoning: the masses were not yet ready for non-violent satyagraha; if violence had broken out in Chauri Chaura, it could erupt anywhere; he would rather call off the movement at the moment of its apparent strength than allow it to become a violent revolution that would corrupt India's soul.

The reaction among nationalists was one of shock and outrage. Jawaharlal Nehru, in prison when he heard the news, later wrote that he wept with rage and helplessness. C.R. Das was furious. Subhas Chandra Bose called it "an unmitigated disaster." They had sacrificed their careers, suffered imprisonment, and then Gandhi pulled back seemingly on the eve of victory.

Gandhi accepted imprisonment without complaint. He was arrested in March 1922, tried for sedition, and sentenced to 6 years by Judge Broomfield — who famously said: "if the course of events in India should make it possible for the Government to reduce the period and release you, no one will be better pleased than I." Gandhi served 2 years; he was released in February 1924 for an appendix operation.

## Exam Traps — 17 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Champaran Satyagraha year = 1917**, NOT 1919 or 1920. It predates the Rowlatt Act.
2. **Champaran = First civil disobedience** under Gandhi in India (NOT Non-Cooperation, which was non-violent non-cooperation, not disobedience).
3. **Kheda = Vallabhbhai Patel** emerged as Gandhi's key lieutenant here (NOT Champaran).
4. Rowlatt Act was based on **Sydney Rowlatt Committee** recommendations. The Act was officially the **Anarchical and Revolutionary Crimes Act 1919**.
5. **Hartal on 6 April 1919** — this was Gandhi's Rowlatt Satyagraha day, NOT the Jallianwala Bagh date.
6. **Jallianwala Bagh = 13 April 1919** (Baisakhi). General Dyer, NOT O'Dwyer, gave the order to fire.
7. **Michael O'Dwyer** (Lieutenant-Governor of Punjab) approved Dyer's actions; **Udham Singh** shot O'Dwyer in London in **1940**, NOT Dyer himself.
8. **Hunter Commission** was set up to investigate Jallianwala Bagh (NOT the Rowlatt Act).
9. Tagore renounced his **knighthood** (NOT Nobel Prize) in protest against Jallianwala Bagh.
10. **Khilafat ended because Atatürk abolished the Caliphate in 1924** — NOT because Britain agreed to Muslim demands.
11. **Ali Brothers = Mohammed Ali + Shaukat Ali** (NOT Saukat Ali — common spelling error).
12. NCM first proposed at **Calcutta Special Session (September 1920)** chaired by **Lala Lajpat Rai** — NOT Nagpur.
13. NCM formally/fully adopted at **Nagpur Session (December 1920)** — this is the one usually tested as "adoption" of Non-Cooperation.
14. **C.R. Das and Motilal Nehru** were prominent lawyers who surrendered their practices during NCM — both later formed the **Swaraj Party** in 1923 after NCM was withdrawn (to enter councils).
15. **Chauri Chaura = Gorakhpur district, UP**, NOT Bihar, NOT Gujarat.
16. **22 police killed** at Chauri Chaura (some sources say 23 — if the question says 22 or 23, both refer to the same event).
17. Gandhi was sentenced to **6 years** but served only **2 years** (released February 1924 for appendix surgery — after Lord Reading, the Viceroy, ordered his release).

## The Aftermath — What Came Next

Gandhi's withdrawal of the NCM in 1922 created two divergent responses in the nationalist movement:

**Swarajists:** C.R. Das and Motilal Nehru formed the **Swaraj Party in 1923**. Their argument: since Gandhi has withdrawn mass movement, we should enter the legislatures and obstruct British governance from within. They did — and were remarkably effective, blocking budgets and making councils ungovernable for the British.

**No-Changers:** Rajagopalachari (Rajaji), Vallabhbhai Patel, and other Gandhians opposed council entry, arguing it would compromise the purity of the non-cooperation stance. This debate — swarajists vs. no-changers — dominated Congress politics from 1922 to 1924.

Meanwhile, in Bengal, a new generation was watching. A young man named Subhas Chandra Bose had given up his ICS posting to join the freedom struggle — he became C.R. Das's protégé in Bengal. Another young man, Jawaharlal Nehru, had discovered socialism while in prison. And in Punjab, Bhagat Singh — 15 years old in 1922 — had visited Chauri Chaura on foot to understand what had happened there. The next chapter of Indian nationalism was forming even as Gandhi prepared for his next great act.
