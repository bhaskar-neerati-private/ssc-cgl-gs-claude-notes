## From Traders to Rulers

In 1757, the English East India Company was a trading company with three coastal towns. By 1818, it was the paramount power in India, directly or indirectly controlling most of the subcontinent. The transformation happened in sixty years through a combination of military force, political conspiracy, and a tool called the Subsidiary Alliance that turned Indian rulers into financially dependent clients.

Two battles — **Plassey (1757)** and **Buxar (1764)** — are the hinges on which this story turns. Together they gave the Company Bengal's revenues and reduced the Mughal Emperor to a pensioner. Everything after followed from those two moments.

---

## Battle of Plassey (23 June 1757)

### Was It Even a Battle?

The Battle of Plassey is one of the most famous battles in Indian history — and one of the most misnamed. It was not a fair fight. It was a **conspiracy** in which the outcome had been decided before the first shot was fired.

**Background**: Siraj-ud-Daulah, Nawab of Bengal, distrusted the growing English power. After the Black Hole incident and English fortification of Calcutta, Robert Clive returned to retake Calcutta (January 1757). But instead of accepting peace, Clive plotted to replace Siraj with a pliant Nawab.

**The conspiracy**: Clive made a secret deal with **Mir Jafar** — Siraj's own commander-in-chief. Mir Jafar would command the largest part of Siraj's army but would not engage when battle came. In return, he would be made Nawab.

**23 June 1757 — the battle that wasn't**:

| Side | Strength |
|------|---------|
| Siraj-ud-Daulah | ~50,000 troops |
| English (Clive) | ~3,000 (English + Indian) |

Mir Jafar's vast force stood aside and watched. Siraj's remaining troops were routed. Siraj fled, was caught, and was **killed by Mir Jafar's son Miran** the next day.

**Aftermath**:
- **Mir Jafar** became Nawab — a puppet of the Company
- The Company received the **zamindari of 24 Parganas** (huge revenue source)
- Mir Jafar paid Clive a personal fortune (the origin of the phrase "nabob" for Englishmen who got rich in India)
- Bengal was now effectively a British protectorate

**Significance**: Plassey was not a military triumph — it was a political coup. But it gave the Company control of Bengal, the richest province in India, with revenues that funded all subsequent expansion.

## Battle of Buxar (22 October 1764)

### The Real Battle

If Plassey was a conspiracy dressed as a battle, **Buxar** was the genuine military test — and it mattered more.

**Background**: Mir Kasim (who replaced Mir Jafar as Nawab) proved less cooperative than the Company wanted. He abolished internal duties on trade — restoring equality between Indian and English merchants, denying the Company its illegal commercial advantage. Conflict followed. Mir Kasim was defeated and fled to Awadh.

**The coalition**: Mir Kasim formed an unprecedented alliance:
- **Mir Kasim** — Nawab of Bengal
- **Shuja-ud-Daulah** — Nawab of Awadh
- **Shah Alam II** — Mughal Emperor

Three major powers combined to stop the British. English forces under **Hector Munro** met this combined army at **Buxar** (in Bihar) on 22 October 1764.

**Result**: The English won decisively. The Mughal Emperor himself was defeated on the battlefield.

**Why Buxar matters more than Plassey**:
- At Plassey, the English won through treachery against one Nawab
- At Buxar, the English defeated three powers including the Mughal Emperor in open battle
- Buxar proved that English military organization genuinely outmatched Mughal-era Indian armies
- After Buxar, no Indian power east of the Vindhyas could challenge the English

## Treaty of Allahabad (1765) — The Diwani Grant

Robert Clive returned to India as Governor of Bengal in 1765 and negotiated the **Treaty of Allahabad** with Shah Alam II (Mughal Emperor):

**The Emperor granted the Company the Diwani (right to collect revenue) of Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa** in exchange for an annual payment and military protection.

This was the defining moment:
- **Diwani** (revenue) → Company
- **Nizamat** (law and order, criminal justice) → Nawab (nominally)

This created the **"Dual Government"** — the Company collected taxes but didn't directly govern. The Nawab governed but had no money because the Company collected all revenue. Both functions deteriorated: the Company squeezed maximum revenue; the Nawab couldn't maintain order without funds.

**Warren Hastings abolished Dual Government in 1772**, taking direct administrative control — marking the beginning of actual British administration of Bengal.

## The Anglo-Mysore Wars (1767–1799)

Mysore under **Hyder Ali** and later **Tipu Sultan** was the most formidable military opponent the English faced in 18th-century India. Four wars were fought:

### First Anglo-Mysore War (1767–1769)

**Who**: Hyder Ali vs British (with Marathas and Nizam initially, who switched sides mid-war)  
**Result**: Hyder Ali **marched to the gates of Madras** — the British were terrified. He forced the **Treaty of Madras (1769)**: mutual restoration of territories and a mutual defense pact.

This was a **British defeat** — they were forced to sign on Hyder Ali's terms. The mutual defense pact was also ignored when Mysore needed it.

---

### Second Anglo-Mysore War (1780–1784)

**Who**: Hyder Ali (then Tipu Sultan after 1782) vs British (Governor-General Warren Hastings)

Hyder Ali formed a grand alliance: Mysore + Nizam + Marathas. The English were caught off-guard; **Colonel Baillie's force was destroyed** (1780) — one of the worst British military defeats in India.

**Hyder Ali died of cancer (December 1782)** while the war continued. His son **Tipu Sultan** (the "Tiger of Mysore") continued the fight with equal determination.

**Treaty of Mangalore (1784)**: Both sides restored captured territories. This was the **last treaty in which terms were dictated to the English by an Indian power** — a remarkable achievement. After this, the tide gradually turned.

---

### Third Anglo-Mysore War (1790–1792)

**Who**: Tipu Sultan vs British (Lord Cornwallis, with Marathas and Nizam as British allies)

Cornwallis himself led the march to Tipu's capital **Seringapatnam (Srirangapatna)**.

**Treaty of Seringapatnam (1792)**:
- Tipu surrendered **half his territory** to the British alliance
- Paid a massive **war indemnity** (3.3 crore rupees)
- Gave **two of his sons as hostages** to Cornwallis

Tipu was humiliated but not destroyed. He spent the next seven years rebuilding — and seeking alliances with France and the Ottoman Empire.

---

### Fourth Anglo-Mysore War (1799)

**Who**: Tipu Sultan vs British (Lord Wellesley)

**Context**: Lord Wellesley arrived as Governor-General with a clear agenda — no independent Indian power should survive. He demanded Tipu accept a Subsidiary Alliance. Tipu refused — "Better to die like a soldier than live a miserable dependent on the infidels."

Wellesley attacked. The British stormed **Seringapatnam on 4 May 1799**. **Tipu Sultan was killed** defending his capital — sword in hand.

**Aftermath**: Mysore was handed back to the **old Hindu Wadiyar dynasty** as a protected state. The British took Coorg (Kodagu), South Kanara, and the Malabar districts. Mysore under Wadiyars accepted a Subsidiary Alliance.

**Tipu Sultan's legacy**: Tipu was the last Indian ruler to die fighting the British on the battlefield. His death removed the most formidable obstacle to British dominance in peninsular India.

## The Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775–1818)

The Marathas were the other major Indian power and took three wars to subdue.

### First Anglo-Maratha War (1775–1782)

**Background**: Dispute over the Peshwaship (effective head of Maratha Confederacy). Raghunathrao (Raghoba) was deposed; he sought English help against Madhavrao Narayan.

The English backed Raghoba — then backed down under the **Treaty of Purandar (1776)**, agreeing not to support him. But the Bombay Presidency continued supporting him, leading to war.

**Treaty of Salbai (1782)**: Status quo restored; English gave up Raghoba; peace for 20 years. This was effectively a British climbdown — the Maratha Confederacy remained intact.

---

### Second Anglo-Maratha War (1803–1805)

**Background**: Internal Maratha conflict. Peshwa **Baji Rao II** was defeated by Holkar and fled. He signed the **Treaty of Bassein (1802)** — accepting British Subsidiary Alliance in exchange for restoration to his position. This was a fatal step: it gave the British a pretext to intervene in all Maratha affairs.

**Lord Wellesley** declared war on Scindia and Bhonsle, who rejected the treaty.

**Results**:
- **Treaty of Deogaon (1803)**: Bhonsle gave up Orissa + part of Berar
- **Treaty of Surji-Arjungaon (1803)**: Scindia gave up Delhi, Agra, parts of Bundelkhand, Broach

**Significance**: British now controlled the **Delhi-Agra heartland** — the seat of Mughal power. Shah Alam II came under British protection. Lord Wellesley wanted to finish the Marathas entirely but was recalled before he could.

---

### Third Anglo-Maratha War (1817–1818)

**Background**: **Lord Hastings** (Marquess of Hastings) — the most aggressive British expansionist after Wellesley. Peshwa Baji Rao II attempted to shake off British control; Maratha chiefs (Scindia, Holkar, Bhonsle) also chafed.

**Key battles**:
- **Battle of Kirkee (1817)**: British defeated Peshwa near Pune
- **Battle of Sitabaldi (1817)**: British defeated Bhonsle at Nagpur
- **Battle of Nagpur (1817)**: Bhonsle submitted
- **Battle of Koregaon (1818)**: A force of 500 (mostly Mahar soldiers) held off Peshwa's 25,000-strong army — remembered today as the Koregaon victory
- **Battle of Ashti (1818)**: Peshwa finally defeated

**Aftermath**: Peshwa Baji Rao II was **exiled to Bithur** (near Kanpur) with a pension. His adopted son Nana Sahib would later play a role in the 1857 Revolt. The Maratha Confederacy was dissolved — territories distributed among the British and Maratha princes who submitted to Subsidiary Alliance.

**By 1818**: The British controlled virtually all of India except Punjab, Sindh, and parts of Rajputana. The conquest of India was effectively complete.

## Subsidiary Alliance — The Master Tool (Lord Wellesley, 1798)

The **Subsidiary Alliance** was Lord Wellesley's masterstroke — a system that made conquest unnecessary by making Indian rulers financially and militarily dependent.

### How It Worked

An Indian ruler who accepted the Subsidiary Alliance agreed to:
1. **Accept British troops** stationed at his court
2. **Pay for those troops** (the "subsidy") — either in cash or by ceding territory
3. **Dismiss other European officers** in his service
4. **Not conduct foreign affairs** without British approval
5. **Not make war or peace** with other powers without British approval

In return, the British:
1. **Protected** the ruler against external enemies
2. **Guaranteed** non-interference in internal affairs (in theory)

### The Trap

The subsidy payments were set high — deliberately so. Rulers who couldn't pay in cash had to cede territory. Once territory was ceded, the British presence grew. Once presence grew, "interference in internal affairs" was inevitable. The system turned Indian rulers into British dependants within a generation.

**First state to accept**: **Hyderabad (1798)**  
**Others**: Mysore (after Tipu's death), Tanjore, Surat, Awadh, Jodhpur, Jaipur, Machilipatnam, Berar, Scindia, Holkar

The Subsidiary Alliance was brilliantly designed — it used Indian rulers' own resources to pay for the troops that kept them subjugated.

## Exam Traps

1. **Battle of Plassey = 23 June 1757**; **Battle of Buxar = 22 October 1764** — both dates tested.
2. **Mir Jafar was Siraj's commander-in-chief who betrayed him** — not just any conspirator.
3. **Mir Kasim was more capable than Mir Jafar** — he actually tried to rule; the Company removed him precisely because he was not compliant enough.
4. **Buxar was more significant than Plassey** — Buxar defeated the Mughal Emperor + two Nawabs in open battle.
5. **Treaty of Allahabad (1765)**: Shah Alam II granted Diwani of Bengal, Bihar, Orissa to the Company.
6. **"Dual Government"** = Company had Diwani (revenue); Nawab had Nizamat (criminal justice) → chaos; abolished by Warren Hastings in 1772.
7. **Treaty of Mangalore (1784) = last treaty imposed ON the English** by an Indian power (Tipu Sultan).
8. **Tipu Sultan was killed at Seringapatnam on 4 May 1799** — during the 4th Anglo-Mysore War.
9. **Battle of Koregaon (1818)**: 500 (mostly Mahar soldiers) vs 25,000 Peshwa troops — significance in Dalit history (Ambedkar visited the Koregaon pillar annually).
10. **Subsidiary Alliance = Lord Wellesley (1798)** — first used with Hyderabad; NOT Dalhousie or Cornwallis.
11. **After Third Anglo-Maratha War (1818)**: Peshwa exiled to Bithur; Baji Rao II's adopted son = Nana Sahib (1857 connection).
12. **By 1818**: Punjab (Sikh Empire under Ranjit Singh), Sindh, and parts of Rajputana still outside British control.
