# HSRA, Bhagat Singh, INA & Subhas Chandra Bose

## The HRA and Its First Act — Kakori

Before Bhagat Singh, there was an earlier generation of revolutionary nationalists who laid the foundation. In 1924, a group of revolutionaries formed the **Hindustan Republican Association (HRA)** — the key figures being **Sachindra Nath Sanyal** and **Ram Prasad Bismil**. Their most dramatic act:

**The Kakori Train Robbery — 9 August 1925.** A group of HRA members stopped a train at Kakori (near Lucknow, United Provinces) and looted the government treasury being transported in it. The aim was to fund revolutionary activities. The British response was swift and severe: **Ram Prasad Bismil, Ashfaqullah Khan, Roshan Singh**, and **Rajendra Lahiri** were arrested, tried, and hanged in December 1927. Ashfaqullah Khan was Muslim; Ram Prasad Bismil was Hindu — their friendship, and their execution together, became an iconic symbol of Hindu-Muslim revolutionary solidarity. Bismil's famous verse: *"Sarfaroshi ki tamanna ab hamare dil mein hai."*

In **1928**, at a meeting at Ferozeshah Kotla, Delhi, the organisation was renamed: **Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA)** — "Socialist" added to signal a commitment to class-based revolution, not just political independence. The key figures of the new HSRA were **Bhagat Singh, Chandrashekhar Azad**, and **Sukhdev**.

## Bhagat Singh — The Boy Who Became a Myth

**Born: 28 September 1907**, Lyallpur (now Faisalabad, Pakistan). His family was deeply involved in the nationalist movement. As a young teenager he had walked to Chauri Chaura after Gandhi withdrew the Non-Cooperation Movement, trying to understand what had happened there. He carried Kartar Singh Sarabha's photograph in his pocket — Sarabha, who had been hanged at 19 in 1915, was his personal idol.

Bhagat Singh was not just a revolutionary — he was a thinker. He read Marx, Lenin, Bakunin, and Trotsky in prison. He wrote the essay **"Why I Am an Atheist"** (1931) in Lahore Central Jail — a sustained intellectual argument for rationalism and against the consolations of religion, remarkable in its clarity for a 23-year-old facing death. He founded the **Naujawan Bharat Sabha (1926)** — Young India Society — to organise working-class and peasant youth outside the Congress framework.

**Act 1 — The Saunders Killing (17 December 1928):**
When Lala Lajpat Rai died on 17 November 1928 after the Simon Commission lathi charge, Bhagat Singh and the HSRA vowed revenge on **James Scott** — the police superintendent who had ordered the beating. On 17 December 1928, Bhagat Singh and **Rajguru** shot and killed a police officer outside the Lahore police headquarters — but the officer they shot was **John P. Saunders**, a Deputy Superintendent of Police, not Scott. They had mistaken Saunders for Scott. Chandrashekhar Azad provided cover. All three escaped from Lahore.

**Act 2 — The Assembly Bomb (8 April 1929):**
The British government was pushing the **Public Safety Bill** and the **Trade Disputes Bill** through the Central Legislative Assembly — laws designed to suppress trade union activity and enable detention without trial. On 8 April 1929, Bhagat Singh and **Batukeshwar Dutt** walked into the visitor's gallery of the Central Legislative Assembly in Delhi and threw two small bombs onto the floor (designed to make noise, not kill — the bombs were deliberately weak). They threw handfuls of leaflets reading: *"It takes a loud noise to make the deaf hear."* Then they stood and shouted **"Inquilab Zindabad!"** — and waited to be arrested. They could have escaped; they chose not to. The arrest was part of the statement.

The phrase **Inquilab Zindabad** (Long Live the Revolution) is inextricably associated with Bhagat Singh, though it had been used earlier — Bhagat Singh popularised it as the revolutionary slogan.

**Trial and Death:**
Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev, and Rajguru were tried in the **Lahore Conspiracy Case** for the Saunders murder. The trial became a political theatre: Bhagat Singh used it to make revolutionary arguments, refused to participate in the proceedings at several stages, and became a national celebrity while in prison. The three were sentenced to death.

They were hanged on **23 March 1931** — one day earlier than the scheduled date of 24 March. The British advanced the hanging, apparently to prevent any last-minute political intervention or public gathering. They were hanged at sunset (around 7:30 PM) — also unusual — and their bodies were secretly cremated at Hussainiwala on the banks of the Sutlej River near Firozpur.

Bhagat Singh was **23 years old** when he died. Within months he was more famous than most living Congress leaders. The question of whether Gandhi could have saved them by making their release a condition of the Gandhi-Irwin Pact (signed just 18 days before the hanging) became a contested and bitter debate.

## Chandrashekhar Azad — The Name Says It All

**Chandrashekhar Azad** (born 1906) had famously told a magistrate who asked his name at an early trial: *Name: Azad (Free); Father's name: Swatantrata (Independence); Home address: Jail.* He was thrashed for it — and his name, Azad, stuck.

Azad was the military organiser of the HSRA — where Bhagat Singh was the face and thinker, Azad was the man who trained members and kept the network alive. He had a personal vow: he would never be taken alive by the British.

On **27 February 1931**, acting on a tip, British police surrounded **Alfred Park, Allahabad** (now renamed Chandrashekhar Azad Park in his honour), where Azad had gone to meet a colleague. He fought the police single-handedly for some time, reportedly shooting with both hands. When his last bullet remained, he shot himself — keeping his vow. He was **24 years old**.

## Subhas Chandra Bose — The Road Not Taken by Congress

**Born: 23 January 1897**, Cuttack, Odisha. Subhas Chandra Bose passed the Indian Civil Service examination in London in 1920 — in the fourth position. He then resigned without taking up the posting, unwilling to serve the British government. He came under the influence of **C.R. Das** in Bengal and became the Bengal Congress secretary. His mentor Das died in 1925.

Bose was imprisoned multiple times. He was deported to Mandalay (like Lajpat Rai and Tilak before him). He was elected **President of the INC twice**:
- **Haripura Session, 1938** — elected unopposed; presided over a productive session.
- **Tripuri Session, 1939** — re-elected against **Pattabhi Sitaramayya**, who was Gandhi's preferred candidate. Bose won 1,580 votes to Sitaramayya's 1,377. Gandhi reportedly said: *"Sitaramayya's defeat is my own defeat."* Under sustained pressure from Gandhi's allies in the Congress Working Committee — all of whom refused to cooperate with him — Bose resigned as president in April 1939.

He then formed the **Forward Bloc** (May 1939) — to unite all left-leaning nationalist forces.

**The Great Escape:**
Under house arrest in his Calcutta residence (Elgin Road) in January 1941, Bose disguised himself as a Pashtun insurance agent named **Mohammed Ziauddin**, slipped away, and began a journey that would take him from Calcutta to Peshawar (with his nephew Sisir Kumar Bose's help), across the Afghan border, through Kabul, to Moscow by rail, and finally to Berlin — arriving in April 1941.

In Germany he raised the **Indian Legion** (Azad Hind Fauj, German chapter) from Indian POWs captured in North Africa. He met Hitler. He broadcast on German radio. But Germany was a dead end for India's independence — Hitler showed no genuine interest in Indian freedom. Bose needed to be closer to the action.

## The INA — "Give Me Blood and I Will Give You Freedom"

**Getting to Southeast Asia:**
In February 1943, Bose left Germany in a German submarine, transferred to a Japanese submarine in the Indian Ocean, and arrived in Tokyo, then Singapore. It was Rash Behari Bose (the veteran revolutionary who had organised the 1912 Delhi Durbar bomb and escaped to Japan in 1915) who had built the **Indian Independence League (IIL)** and the first **Indian National Army** from Indian POWs captured when Singapore fell to Japan in February 1942. That first INA had been organised by **Captain Mohan Singh** — but it collapsed amid disputes over Japanese intentions.

Rash Behari Bose, recognising his own limitations as a leader, handed control to Subhas Chandra Bose. Subhas arrived in Singapore in July 1943.

**Reorganising the INA:**
Subhas restructured everything. He renamed the enterprise **Azad Hind Fauj** (Indian National Army). He established the **Azad Hind Government** (Provisional Government of Free India) in Singapore on **21 October 1943** — with himself as Head of State, Prime Minister, and Minister of War. Several Southeast Asian countries and the Axis powers recognised it. The government controlled the Andaman and Nicobar Islands (nominally — the Japanese held actual control).

The INA had three infantry brigades, named after **Gandhi, Nehru**, and **Azad** — a deliberate statement connecting revolutionary nationalism to mainstream Congress. A women's regiment, the **Rani of Jhansi Regiment**, was created — commanded by **Captain Lakshmi Sahgal** (Dr. Lakshmi Sahgal), a doctor who had joined the INA in Singapore.

His most famous addresses:
- *"Tum mujhe khoon do, main tumhe azadi dunga"* — "Give me blood and I will give you freedom"
- *"Chalo Delhi"* — "March to Delhi"
- *"Jai Hind"* — which became independent India's national salute

**The March to Imphal:**
In early 1944, the INA marched alongside the Japanese Army into Manipur and Nagaland. They reached **Imphal and Kohima** — on Indian soil. The INA planted its flag on Indian territory. For the British, this was deeply alarming: Indian soldiers fighting against them on Indian soil.

The **Battle of Imphal-Kohima (1944)** was, however, a catastrophic defeat for the Japanese — and the INA. Monsoon, supply failures, and Allied counterattacks shattered the Japanese advance. The INA retreated and effectively disintegrated as an organised force during the retreat through Burma.

**Bose's Death:**
On **18 August 1945**, Subhas Chandra Bose died in a plane crash near Taihoku (Taipei), Taiwan, after the plane he was on crashed on takeoff. He had been travelling toward Manchuria. The circumstances remain disputed in India — officially, death in the crash; various conspiracy theories have persisted ever since.

## The INA Trials and Their Unexpected Consequence

After Japan's surrender (August 1945), INA soldiers were brought back to India and the British decided to try the senior officers for treason and waging war against the King-Emperor. The **INA Trials** were held at the **Red Fort, Delhi** — three officers selected as the first defendants: **Shah Nawaz Khan** (Muslim), **P.K. Sehgal** (Hindu), **G.S. Dhillon** (Sikh) — the selection itself a statement about the INA's composite character.

The defence was led by **Bhulabhai Desai** (a Congress lawyer), with **Tej Bahadur Sapru** and **Jawaharlal Nehru** (who wore his barrister's gown for the first time in 25 years) also appearing.

The trials backfired catastrophically. Instead of discrediting INA soldiers as traitors, the trials made them heroes. The entire country rallied to their defence — Congress, Hindu Mahasabha, Muslim League, the press, the public. The British eventually had to release the accused to avoid making martyrs.

The **Royal Indian Navy Mutiny (February 1946)** — sailors of the RIN mutinied on 18 February 1946 in Bombay (spread to Karachi and other stations), flying the flags of Congress, Muslim League, and the INA together. It was suppressed, but it shook British confidence in the loyalty of the Indian military. The combination of the INA trials, the RIN mutiny, and the evidence of declining loyalty in the Indian services accelerated the British decision to transfer power. Lord Wavell's reports to London increasingly emphasised that the British could no longer rely on Indian troops to hold down a rebellious India.

## Key Comparison — Bhagat Singh vs. Subhas Bose

| | Bhagat Singh | Subhas Chandra Bose |
|---|---|---|
| Born | 28 September 1907 | 23 January 1897 |
| Organisation | HSRA (from 1928) | Forward Bloc (1939), INA (1943) |
| Method | Armed propaganda, targeted violence | Armed military campaign |
| Key act | Assembly bomb (8 April 1929) | INA Imphal-Kohima march (1944) |
| Death | Hanged 23 March 1931, age 23 | Plane crash 18 August 1945, age 48 |
| Slogan | Inquilab Zindabad | Jai Hind / Give me blood... |
| Congress relation | Outside Congress, critical of Gandhi | Inside then outside Congress |

## Exam Traps — 18 Things SSC Will Test

1. **HRA founded 1924** (Sachindra Sanyal + Ram Prasad Bismil); **renamed HSRA in 1928** at Ferozeshah Kotla. These are two different years.
2. **Kakori Train Robbery = 9 August 1925** (NOT 1924, the HRA founding year). Four hanged: Bismil, Ashfaqullah Khan, Roshan Singh, Rajendra Lahiri.
3. **Ashfaqullah Khan was Muslim** — his friendship with Hindu Bismil is the Hindu-Muslim unity angle SSC tests.
4. Bhagat Singh killed **John P. Saunders** (DSP), NOT James Scott. He intended to kill Scott (who had ordered the Lajpat Rai lathi charge). Saunders was a case of mistaken identity.
5. **Assembly bomb = 8 April 1929**, thrown with **Batukeshwar Dutt** (NOT Sukhdev, NOT Rajguru).
6. The bombs were **deliberately weak** — designed to make noise and create propaganda, NOT to kill. "It takes a loud noise to make the deaf hear."
7. Bhagat Singh was hanged on **23 March 1931** — one day EARLY (scheduled 24 March). With Sukhdev and Rajguru.
8. Bhagat Singh was **23 years old** when hanged — not 24, not 21.
9. **Chandrashekhar Azad died 27 February 1931** at Alfred Park, Allahabad — shot himself to avoid capture. The park is now named after him.
10. **Subhas Bose won the 1939 Congress presidency** against Pattabhi Sitaramayya (Gandhi's candidate). Tripuri Session. Gandhi said "Sitaramayya's defeat is my own defeat."
11. **Forward Bloc = May 1939** — formed by Subhas AFTER resigning from Congress presidency.
12. **First INA = Captain Mohan Singh** (1942) — Subhas reorganised the SECOND INA from 1943. Rash Behari Bose was the bridge.
13. **INA had three brigades named Gandhi, Nehru, Azad** — women's regiment was **Rani of Jhansi Regiment**, commanded by **Captain Lakshmi Sahgal**.
14. **Azad Hind Government established 21 October 1943** in Singapore.
15. INA marched to **Imphal and Kohima** (inside India, in Manipur/Nagaland) — failed and retreated in 1944.
16. **Bose died 18 August 1945** — plane crash near **Taihoku (Taipei), Taiwan**. Do NOT say he went missing — the official position is death in the crash.
17. **INA Trials at Red Fort, Delhi** — three defendants: Shah Nawaz Khan (Muslim) + P.K. Sehgal (Hindu) + G.S. Dhillon (Sikh). Defence lawyer: **Bhulabhai Desai** (primary); Nehru also appeared.
18. **Royal Indian Navy Mutiny = February 1946** (18 February, Bombay). Do NOT confuse with the INA Trials (1945).
