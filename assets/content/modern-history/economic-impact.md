## A Country That Was Rich, Then Wasn't

When the British East India Company arrived in the 17th century, India accounted for roughly 25% of world GDP. When the British left in 1947, that figure had collapsed to around 4%. What happened in between is not just a story of bad luck or slow development — it is the story of systematic economic extraction, deliberate policy choices, and the destruction of industries that had made India one of the world's greatest producers for thousands of years.

Three processes drove this collapse: the **Drain of Wealth** (money leaving India and never coming back), **Deindustrialisation** (the destruction of Indian handicraft industries), and **Famines** (mass starvation partly caused by colonial economic policy). Understanding these isn't just important for the exam — it explains why Indian nationalism had a strong economic dimension from the very beginning.

## 1. Drain of Wealth

### The Concept

The **Drain of Wealth** (or "Economic Drain") refers to a one-way flow of money from India to Britain — money that left India and for which India received no equivalent in return. Not trade (where goods go out and payment comes in) — extraction (goods go out and nothing comes back).

### Who Named It?

**Dadabhai Naoroji** (1825–1917) is the man most associated with this theory. He was a Parsi intellectual, mathematician, and politician who:
- Presented a paper titled "England's Debt to India" in 1867 — the first systematic articulation
- Published **"Poverty and Un-British Rule in India"** in 1901 — the definitive account
- Was elected to the British Parliament in 1892 (for Finsbury, London) — the first Indian MP in Westminster — and raised these issues on the floor of the House of Commons
- Was called the **"Grand Old Man of India"** by his contemporaries

**R.C. Dutt (Romesh Chandra Dutt)** wrote the **"Economic History of India"** (2 volumes, 1902–04), documenting with statistics how British policy had systematically impoverished India.

### What Was the Drain?

**Home Charges**: India was made to pay for the entire cost of its own administration from London — including:
- Salaries and pensions of British officials (who retired to Britain)
- Interest on debt incurred for railways, public works, and wars fought on Britain's behalf
- Military expenditure when British Indian troops were used in wars outside India (Afghanistan, China, Africa)
- Cost of maintaining the India Office in London — the department that governed India
- Costs of the telegraph to India, the India Civil Service examinations held in London

**Profit Remittances**: British companies operating in India — trading houses, railways, banks, planters — remitted their profits to Britain rather than reinvesting in India.

**Export Surplus Without Return**: India consistently exported more than it imported — but the surplus did not flow back as gold or silver. It was absorbed into the Home Charges and profit remittances.

Naoroji estimated the drain at approximately **£30 million per year** in the mid-19th century. In modern terms, economists have tried to quantify it — some estimates suggest the total drain over the colonial period was equivalent to trillions of dollars.

## 2. Deindustrialisation

### India Before the British

For centuries before British rule, India was famous for its textiles:
- **Dacca (Dhaka) muslin** — so fine it was called "woven air" or "running water"; it could pass through a finger ring; exported to Mughal courts and Roman Empire
- **Calico and chintz** — cotton prints from Calicut and Coromandel that gave English words their names (calico, chintz)
- **Kashmiri shawls, Banaras silk, Surat embroidery** — premium products with global markets

India's weavers, artisans, and craftspeople were organized in sophisticated guilds. Cities like Dacca, Surat, Murshidabad, and Masulipatnam were production centers for a world market.

### How the British Destroyed Indian Industry

**Differential Duties (the decisive blow)**:
- In Britain: Import duties on Indian textiles were **70–80%** — effectively barring Indian cloth from the British market
- In India: Import duties on British machine-made cloth were kept **minimal** (2.5–5%) — British goods flooded Indian markets
- This was not free trade — it was a rigged game where British manufacturing was protected at home while Indian manufacturing was exposed to competitive destruction in its own home market

**Charter Act 1813**: Ended the East India Company's monopoly over India trade. Opened India to all British traders. The result: a flood of cheap machine-made British textiles from Lancashire and Manchester into Indian markets.

**Technology Gap**: British factories (post-Industrial Revolution) produced cloth at a fraction of the cost of Indian handlooms. Indian weavers, who needed several hours per yard, could not compete with machines that produced yards per minute.

**Railway as a Tool of Import**: The railways that the British built (celebrated as a great gift) also served to carry cheap British cloth from ports (Bombay, Calcutta) into interior markets — reaching every village with British textiles.

### The Human Catastrophe

Governor-General William Bentinck wrote in 1834:
> *"The misery hardly finds a parallel in the history of commerce. The bones of the cotton-weavers are bleaching the plains of India."*

**Dacca** — once a city of over 150,000 people, with most employed in muslin weaving — shrank to about 30,000 by the 1840s as its weaving industry collapsed. The weavers had nowhere to go — no factories to absorb them, no alternative employment. They went back to subsistence farming, often on unfavorable terms.

India went from being a **textile exporter** to being a **raw cotton exporter** — exporting raw material for British factories and then importing the finished cloth back. This complete reversal is what economists call "deindustrialisation."

### Commercialisation of Agriculture

As handicraft income dried up, the rural economy became entirely dependent on agriculture. But agriculture was also being transformed:

- **Revenue demands** required cash payment — farmers had to sell crops rather than consume them
- **British demand for raw materials**: cotton (for Lancashire), indigo (for dye), jute (for bags and rope), opium (for trade with China)
- Farmers shifted from food crops to cash crops under pressure — less food grown → greater vulnerability to any monsoon failure

The **American Civil War (1861–65)** provides a dramatic illustration: When the US Civil War cut off American cotton supply to Lancashire, British demand for Indian cotton spiked. Bombay Deccan farmers borrowed heavily to expand cotton cultivation. When the war ended and American supply resumed (1865), cotton prices crashed — Bombay farmers were left with massive debts and land was seized by moneylenders. The "Deccan Riots" (1875) followed.

## 3. Famines

### The Scale of the Catastrophe

Under British rule, India experienced some of the worst famines in human history — not despite British administration, but partly because of it.

| Famine | Year | Deaths (est.) | Viceroy/Governor |
|--------|------|---------------|-----------------|
| Great Bengal Famine | 1770 | ~10 million | Company (Clive era) |
| Madras (Great) Famine | 1876–78 | 5–10 million | Lord Lytton |
| Indian Famine | 1896–97 | 1–5 million | Lord Elgin II |
| Indian Famine | 1899–1900 | 1–4 million | Lord Curzon |
| Bengal Famine | 1943 | 2–3 million | Lord Linlithgow |

### Why Famines Happened Under British Rule

**1. High revenue even in drought years**: Revenue demands continued regardless of harvest. Farmers who couldn't sell crops at good prices sold food stocks to pay revenue — converting a scarcity into a starvation.

**2. Commercialisation reduced food security**: Farmers growing cash crops instead of food were entirely dependent on the market. When the market failed, they had neither food nor money.

**3. Export of food during famine**: In the 1876–78 famine, India continued to export grain to Britain while millions starved. Lord Lytton actually opposed stopping grain exports — he believed market forces should determine grain distribution.

**4. Lytton's 1877 Delhi Durbar**: During the peak of the 1876–78 famine that killed millions in Madras and Bombay, Lord Lytton held an extravagant Delhi Durbar to celebrate Queen Victoria's proclamation as Empress of India. The contrast — imperial celebration while millions starved — was denounced by critics like Florence Nightingale and William Digby.

**5. 1943 Bengal Famine**: Winston Churchill's war policies contributed directly — war priorities meant food was exported rather than distributed; relief was delayed; Churchill reportedly dismissed Bengal's suffering with contempt. Amartya Sen's Nobel Prize-winning work showed the 1943 famine was a "man-made" famine, not a natural one.

### Famine Commissions

After each major famine, the British set up inquiries:
- **Strachey Famine Commission (1878–80)**: After 1876–78 famine; established principles for famine relief
- **Lyall Commission (1898–1901)**: After 1896–97 famine; created the **Famine Code** — a set of procedures for drought monitoring and relief
- **Famine Code**: Defined "famine" thresholds; specified relief works, food distribution mechanisms

The Famine Codes were too little, too late — and often not implemented fully.

## Key Economic Critics — The Nationalist Economists

| Thinker | Work | Contribution |
|---------|------|-------------|
| Dadabhai Naoroji | *Poverty and Un-British Rule in India* (1901) | Coined "Drain of Wealth"; quantified extraction |
| R.C. Dutt | *Economic History of India* (2 vols, 1902–04) | Statistical documentation of impoverishment |
| M.G. Ranade | Essays on Indian Economics | Advocated Indian industrialisation and economic nationalism |
| Bal Gangadhar Tilak | Speeches and *Kesari* newspaper | Connected economic exploitation to demand for Swaraj |
| G.K. Gokhale | Legislative Council speeches | Criticized poverty data; demanded budget reforms |

These thinkers essentially created **economic nationalism** — the argument that political independence was necessary because economic exploitation would continue as long as India was governed by Britain. This became the foundation of the Swadeshi movement (boycott British goods; use Indian goods) and eventually of the Congress's economic program.

## Infrastructure — The Double-Edged Sword

The British did build railways, canals, and telegraphs — often cited as positive contributions. The nationalist critique:

**Railways**:
- Built primarily to move raw materials from interior to ports (cotton, jute, grain) and British goods from ports to interior markets
- Iron, steel, rolling stock — all from Britain; Indian manufacturers excluded
- Construction financed by Indian debt; interest paid by Indian taxpayers
- Profits went to British railway companies; losses guaranteed by Indian government

**Canals**:
- Punjab canal colonies — genuinely improved agriculture in dry Punjab
- But canal irrigation also enabled export of cotton — further commercialising agriculture

**Telegraph and postal system**:
- Benefited British administration and trade primarily

**Plantation System**:
- Tea plantations (Assam, Darjeeling): British-owned; workers under **indentured labor** contracts
- **Workmen's Breach of Contract Act (1859)**: Indentured workers who left before contract ended could be **criminally prosecuted** (not just sued) — essentially criminalising their freedom of movement

## Exam Traps

1. **Dadabhai Naoroji** coined the term "Drain of Wealth" — NOT Tilak or Gokhale
2. **Naoroji's book** = "Poverty and Un-British Rule in India" (1901) — NOT "Economic History of India"
3. **R.C. Dutt's book** = "Economic History of India" (1902) — NOT Naoroji's
4. **Naoroji was the first Indian MP in British Parliament** (1892, Finsbury) — before Partition
5. **Differential duties were the core mechanism** of deindustrialisation — NOT just competition alone
6. **Charter Act 1813** = opened India to British free trade (ended Company monopoly) — key date for deindustrialisation
7. **"Bones of cotton-weavers bleaching the plains of India"** = Bentinck (Governor-General) — NOT Naoroji
8. **1876–78 Madras Famine** = Lord Lytton — the same Viceroy who was holding the Delhi Durbar
9. **1943 Bengal Famine** = Lord Linlithgow — Churchill's war policies cited as a cause
10. **Workmen's Breach of Contract Act (1859)** = criminalised plantation workers leaving contracts — NOT the same as ordinary contract law
11. **Home Charges** = specific term for what India paid Britain for governance; Naoroji calculated these
12. **Deccan Riots (1875)** = consequence of American Civil War cotton boom-bust cycle in Bombay Deccan
