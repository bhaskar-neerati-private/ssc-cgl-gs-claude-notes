## The Day the Empire Trembled

On 10 May 1857, sepoys at the Meerut cantonment broke open the jail, freed eighty-five of their comrades who had been sentenced to hard labour, killed their British officers, and marched through the night toward Delhi. By dawn on 11 May, they had reached the Red Fort and proclaimed the aged Mughal Emperor **Bahadur Shah Zafar II** — a poet and figurehead barely in control of his own palace — as the Emperor of Hindustan.

What followed was the most serious challenge to British rule in India since the conquest of Bengal a century earlier. For nearly two years, across a large swathe of northern and central India, the British empire fought for its survival. And although it ultimately crushed the uprising, nothing was ever quite the same again.

## What To Call It?

This event has been named differently by different people, and understanding the debate is itself exam-relevant:

| Name | Who Used It | Implication |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Sepoy Mutiny** | British officials; early British historians | Just a military mutiny; no popular basis; not national |
| **First War of Independence** | V.D. Savarkar (*The Indian War of Independence*, 1909) | A planned national uprising; India's first freedom struggle |
| **Great Rebellion / National Uprising** | Indian nationalists | Wider than a mutiny; had popular dimension |
| **Feudal Revolt / Composite Rebellion** | Modern historians (Bipin Chandra, R.C. Majumdar) | Neither purely national nor purely military; a mix of different grievances |

For SSC: The term "First War of Independence" is widely used in NCERT; Savarkar coined it.

## Causes — Why Did It Happen?

### 1. The Immediate Trigger: The Greased Cartridge

The **Enfield Rifle (Pattern 1853)** required soldiers to bite off the top of a paper cartridge before loading. Rumours spread — and were impossible to completely disprove — that the cartridges were greased with:
- **Pig fat** (offensive to Muslims — pig is haram)
- **Cow fat** (offensive to Hindus — cow is sacred)

This rumour ignited pre-existing resentment like a match on dry wood. When 85 sepoys of the **3rd Cavalry at Meerut** refused to use the cartridges in April 1857, they were court-martialled, publicly stripped of their uniforms, and sentenced to 10 years of rigorous imprisonment. The humiliation of the public degradation — shackling old soldiers in front of their comrades — was the final straw.

Before this, on **29 March 1857**, **Mangal Pandey** — a sepoy of the 34th Bengal Native Infantry at Barrackpore — had attacked British officers and called on his fellow soldiers to revolt. He was arrested and **hanged on 8 April 1857**. He became the first martyr symbol of the revolt.

### 2. Military Grievances (Long-Standing)

- **General Service Enlistment Act (1856)**: Required new recruits to serve overseas if ordered. Crossing the sea ("kala pani") was ritually polluting for upper-caste Hindus — it meant losing caste
- Low pay compared to British soldiers; caste restrictions in uniform
- Fear that the British intended to convert them to Christianity (some missionaries actively proselytised in cantonments)
- The Bengal Army was recruited heavily from Awadh — and their families had just been dispossessed by Awadh's annexation (1856)

### 3. Political Causes

- **Doctrine of Lapse** (Dalhousie): Princes had seen their states annexed — Satara, Jhansi, Nagpur, Udaipur. Their retinues, armies, and nobles were suddenly unemployed and resentful
- **Annexation of Awadh (1856)**: The Nawab Wajid Ali Shah, beloved for his cultural patronage, was deposed and exiled to Calcutta. This personal humiliation hit the Bengal Army soldiers — most of whom were from Awadh — directly
- **Nana Sahib's pension denied**: Nana Sahib (adopted son of the last Peshwa Baji Rao II) was not recognized under the Doctrine of Lapse; his pension was stopped. He became one of the revolt's leaders at Kanpur
- **Bahadur Shah Zafar's family evicted**: The British had told the Mughal royal family they must vacate the Red Fort; the symbolic humiliation of the last emperor fed resentment

### 4. Socio-Religious Causes

- Interference in Hindu and Muslim practices — abolition of Sati (1829), Hindu Widows' Remarriage Act (1856) — seen as steps toward forcible Christianisation
- Activities of missionaries in cantonments
- Education Act reforms — English medium schools seen as tools of cultural conversion

### 5. Economic Causes

- Destruction of Indian handicrafts (weavers unemployed)
- Permanent Settlement creating new landlords and dispossessing old ones
- High land revenue and peasant indebtedness
- Zamindars losing estates under various British policies

## The Revolt Spreads: Centers and Leaders

### Timeline of Key Events

| 8 April 1857 | Mangal Pandey hanged |
| 10 May 1857 | Sepoys mutiny at Meerut; march to Delhi |
| 11 May 1857 | Bahadur Shah Zafar proclaimed Emperor at Delhi |
| June 1857 | Kanpur falls to Nana Sahib; massacre of British survivors |
| July 1857 | Havelock retakes Kanpur; Lucknow Residency besieged |
| September 1857 | Delhi recaptured by British; Bahadur Shah Zafar captured |
| November 1857 | Lucknow finally relieved by Colin Campbell |
| April 1858 | Jhansi falls to Hugh Rose; Lakshmibai escapes to Gwalior |
| June 1858 | Rani Lakshmibai dies in battle at Gwalior |
| April 1859 | Tatya Tope captured and executed; revolt effectively ends |

### The Leaders

**Bahadur Shah Zafar II (Delhi)**: The last Mughal Emperor — nearly 82 years old; more a poet than a warrior. He became the symbolic leader reluctantly. After Delhi fell in September 1857, he was captured at **Humayun's Tomb**. His two sons were shot dead by **William Hodson** at Humayun's Tomb. Zafar was tried and **exiled to Rangoon (Burma)**, where he died in **1862** — the last ember of the Mughal Empire.

**Nana Sahib (Kanpur)**: Adopted son of the last Peshwa; bitter about his denied pension. Led the revolt at Kanpur; initially captured the British garrison. A massacre of British prisoners (women and children) at Bibighar, Kanpur became a rallying point for British rage. Nana Sahib escaped after British retaliation; was never captured; disappeared into Nepal.

**Tatya Tope (Kanpur and beyond)**: Real name Ramchandra Pandurang; Nana Sahib's military commander. The most tactically capable rebel leader — kept up guerrilla warfare until 1859. He was **betrayed by his friend Man Singh** (Raja of Narwar), captured, and **executed in April 1859**.

**Begum Hazrat Mahal (Lucknow)**: Wife of the deposed Nawab Wajid Ali Shah. Organised the defence of Lucknow; proclaimed her son Birjis Qadr as Nawab. The Lucknow Residency siege (where a small British garrison held out) became a British legend of endurance. Begum Hazrat Mahal escaped to Nepal; died there in 1879.

**Rani Lakshmibai (Jhansi)**: The most celebrated figure of 1857. Her state of Jhansi was annexed under Doctrine of Lapse (she had an adopted son). When the British refused to recognise her adopted son, she famously said: *"Main apni Jhansi nahi doongi"* ("I will not give up my Jhansi"). She fought brilliantly but Jhansi fell to **General Hugh Rose** in April 1858. She escaped on horseback to Gwalior, where she died fighting — described by Hugh Rose as "the best and bravest military leader of the rebels." She died in **June 1858**.

**Kunwar Singh (Arrah, Bihar)**: An ageing zamindar from Jagdispur — said to be around 80 years old. Defeated several British columns; fought a remarkable campaign across Bihar and UP. Died in **April 1858** from wounds received in his last battle — reportedly after cutting off his own wounded arm and offering it to the Ganga.

**Khan Bahadur Khan (Bareilly)**: Led the revolt in Rohilkhand; declared himself the Nawab Nazim of Bareilly; tried and hanged after suppression.

**Maulvi Ahmadullah Shah (Faizabad)**: Called "Light of God" (Noor-e-Mujassam); a fiery preacher who had predicted the revolt would come; fought bravely at Lucknow; killed in 1858.

## Why Did the Revolt Fail?

### Geographic Gaps

The revolt was largely confined to the **Gangetic plains and central India**. These major regions stayed loyal to the British or uninvolved:
- **Punjab**: Recently conquered Sikhs, still resentful of Mughals, helped British suppress Delhi
- **Madras and Bombay Presidencies**: Largely uninvolved; Madras and Bombay armies stayed loyal
- **Bengal**: Mostly quiet; Bengal civilian population did not join
- **Hyderabad, Rajputana, Kashmir**: Princes remained loyal (some were rewarded with land for this)
- **Sindh**: Quiet
- **Nepal**: The Gurkha kingdom sent troops to help the British

### No Unified Leadership or Ideology

Each center fought separately. Nana Sahib in Kanpur did not coordinate with Lakshmibai in Jhansi. Bahadur Shah Zafar was a symbol, not a commander. The absence of a common vision — there was no idea of "India as a nation" — meant the revolt was ultimately a series of local uprisings, not a single national war.

### British Military Superiority

- Better weapons — particularly artillery
- Telegraph gave British commanders instant communication; rebels had none
- British were able to rush reinforcements from Punjab (after securing it) and from the Cape (South Africa)
- The Indian troops who remained loyal were quickly used to suppress the revolt

### The Doctrine of "Martial Races"

The British identified "loyal" communities — Sikhs, Gurkhas, Pathans, some Rajputs — as naturally warrior-like ("martial races") and preferentially recruited them. Communities heavily involved in the revolt (the old Bengal Army's castes) were sidelined. This transformed the composition of the British Indian Army permanently.

## Consequences — The World Changed

### End of Company Rule

The **Government of India Act 1858** abolished the East India Company and transferred all governance to the British Crown. The Board of Control and the Court of Directors were replaced by the **Secretary of State for India** (a Cabinet minister in London with a Council of India). The Governor-General became the **Viceroy**. Lord Canning was the first Viceroy.

### Queen's Proclamation (1 November 1858)

Queen Victoria's proclamation promised:
- No further annexation (Doctrine of Lapse abandoned)
- Religious freedom and non-interference
- Indians eligible for government service (though not senior positions in practice)
- Amnesty for rebels (except those who killed Europeans)
- Respect for rights of Indian princes

### Army Reorganisation

- Ratio of British to Indian soldiers increased (1:2 roughly; had been about 1:5 before)
- **Indians barred from artillery** — the most powerful weapon arm
- "Divide and rule" in the army — no single community could dominate; mix communities in each unit
- "Martial races" doctrine formalised — Sikhs, Gurkhas, Pathans favoured

### Policy Shifts

- Missionary activity discouraged in cantonments
- More cautious about interfering in social customs
- More careful engagement with Indian princes

## Exam Traps

1. **Mangal Pandey = Barrackpore (29 March 1857)** — NOT Meerut; the Meerut revolt was 10 May 1857
2. **Meerut mutiny = 10 May 1857** — the date that is universally tested
3. **Bahadur Shah Zafar exiled to Rangoon (Burma); died there 1862** — NOT killed at the Red Fort
4. **Bahadur Shah Zafar's sons = killed by William Hodson** at Humayun's Tomb (NOT in the Red Fort)
5. **Rani Lakshmibai died June 1858 at Gwalior** — NOT at Jhansi (Jhansi fell in April; she escaped)
6. **"I will not give up my Jhansi"** — Rani Lakshmibai, NOT Begum Hazrat Mahal
7. **Begum Hazrat Mahal = Lucknow; wife of Wajid Ali Shah** — NOT Rani Lakshmibai's title
8. **Tatya Tope = Executed April 1859; betrayed by Man Singh** — NOT escaped
9. **Nana Sahib = disappeared; never captured** — contrasts with Tatya Tope who was caught
10. **Kunwar Singh = Bihar (Arrah/Jagdispur)** — NOT UP; ~80 years old
11. **V.D. Savarkar coined "First War of Independence"** — 1909 book; NOT an Indian government position of 1857
12. **S.N. Sen's famous phrase** = "What began as a fight for religion ended as a war of independence"
13. **General Service Enlistment Act (1856)** = required overseas service; direct military grievance
14. **Greased cartridges = Enfield Rifle (Pattern 1853)** — not the Brown Bess
15. **Lord Canning = "Clemency Canning"** — opposed mass retribution; first Viceroy; presided over aftermath

1. What was the "greased cartridge" controversy and why did it have the power to spark a revolt across the Bengal Army?
2. Name the leaders of the revolt at: (a) Kanpur; (b) Lucknow; (c) Jhansi; (d) Arrah (Bihar)
3. Why did the revolt fail to spread to Madras, Bombay, and Punjab?
4. What did the Government of India Act 1858 change about how India was governed?
5. How does V.D. Savarkar's interpretation of 1857 differ from R.C. Majumdar's?

*(Answers: 1. The Enfield rifle cartridges were rumoured to be greased with pig fat (haram for Muslims) and cow fat (sacred cow for Hindus). Soldiers had to bite the cartridge — direct oral contact with the offensive substance. This touched the deepest religious anxieties of a force already angry about overseas service, pay, and the annexation of Awadh. The physical act of touching/biting the cartridge became a symbol of British indifference to Indian religion. 2. (a) Kanpur: Nana Sahib (leader) and Tatya Tope (military commander); (b) Lucknow: Begum Hazrat Mahal; (c) Jhansi: Rani Lakshmibai; (d) Arrah/Bihar: Kunwar Singh. 3. Madras and Bombay Presidencies had different armies (Madras Army, Bombay Army) not recruited from Awadh or upper-caste UP; their grievances were different. Punjab: Sikhs resented Mughals more than British; Punjab had just been conquered (1849) and the British had treated Sikhs with relative respect; Sikh soldiers actively helped British retake Delhi. No unified ideology meant the revolt couldn't transcend regional grievances. 4. Government of India Act 1858: abolished East India Company; Crown took over governance; Secretary of State for India (London Cabinet minister) replaced Board of Control; Viceroy replaced Governor-General; Council of India (15 members) advised Secretary of State. India officially became part of the British Empire. 5. Savarkar (1909): It was a planned national uprising — a war of independence — representing a unified Indian national consciousness against British rule. Majumdar: No national consciousness existed in 1857; different groups (sepoys, princes, zamindars) had different and often selfish grievances; calling it a "national war" is retrospective projection. S.N. Sen's middle position: "What began as a fight for religion ended as a war of independence.")*
