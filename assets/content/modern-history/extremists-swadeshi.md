## When Patience Ran Out

For twenty years the Moderates had written polite letters to an empire that wasn't listening. Then in October 1905, Lord Curzon partitioned Bengal — despite a massive, peaceful, well-organized protest. The empire had spoken: it didn't care what Indians thought.

Into the silence after that verdict came three voices that would reshape Indian nationalism. Bal Gangadhar Tilak in Maharashtra. Bipin Chandra Pal in Bengal. Lala Lajpat Rai in Punjab. History remembered them together: **Lal-Bal-Pal**.

They didn't want to ask the British for justice. They wanted to take it.

---

## The Three Pillars: Lal-Bal-Pal

### Bal Gangadhar Tilak (1856–1920) — "Lokmanya"

"Lokmanya" means "Accepted by the People" — a title given to Tilak by his followers, not by any institution. He earned it.

Tilak was a mathematics teacher in Pune who understood something the Moderates didn't: you cannot build a mass movement in English, in writing, among educated elites. You need festivals. You need heroes. You need music and crowds and emotion.

**His innovations in political mobilization:**

- **Ganpati Festival (1893)**: The worship of Ganesha had been a private, household affair. Tilak turned it into a ten-day public event — processions, speeches, cultural performances. The British couldn't ban it (religious freedom) but it functioned as an annual nationalist rally. Still celebrated as a massive public festival today.

- **Shivaji Birthday Festival (1896)**: Shivaji Maharaj — the 17th-century Maratha king who fought the Mughals — was reimagined as a symbol of Hindu resistance to foreign rule. His birthday became an annual occasion for nationalist speeches and songs.

These weren't just cultural events. They were the first experiments in mass political mobilization in modern India.

**His newspapers:**
- ***Kesari*** (Marathi) — "Lion"; spoke directly to Marathi-speaking people; ferocious in its nationalism
- ***Mahratta*** (English) — for the educated class

**His philosophy (contrasted with Gokhale):**
- Gokhale: Social reform first, then political freedom
- Tilak: **"Swaraj is my birthright and I shall have it"** — political freedom first; social reform can follow
- Gokhale: Constitutional methods; trust in British fair play
- Tilak: Mass agitation; confront British power; people must act, not petition

**Trials and imprisonment:**

| Year | Charge | Outcome |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1897 | Sedition — writings after Pune plague commissioner Rand's assassination by the Chapekar brothers | 18 months imprisonment |
| 1908 | Sedition — writings justifying Khudiram Bose's bomb throwing at Muzaffarpur | **6 years imprisonment in Mandalay, Burma (1908–1914)** |

In Mandalay prison, Tilak wrote ***Gita Rahasya*** — his philosophical commentary on the Bhagavad Gita, arguing that its message was active engagement with the world, not passive renunciation.

**Home Rule League (April 1916)**: Tilak founded his Home Rule League in April 1916, headquartered at Poona (Pune). It was active in Maharashtra, Karnataka, Central Provinces, and Berar. The demand: self-government (home rule) for India within the British Empire.

**Death (1 August 1920)**: Gandhi called Tilak "My strongest bulwark is gone." India observed national mourning.

---

### Bipin Chandra Pal (1858–1932) — "Father of Revolutionary Thought in India"

Pal was the most powerful orator of the Extremist trio — thunderous, visionary, spiritually charged. He connected nationalism to Vivekananda's message of spiritual awakening.

- Founded newspaper ***New India*** (1902)
- Organized the Swadeshi movement in Bengal with enormous passion
- Stressed that India's liberation must come from a spiritual awakening of the Indian people — not from negotiations with the British
- **Contempt of court imprisonment (1907)**: When Aurobindo Ghosh was tried for sedition, Pal refused to testify against him, citing his principles. He was imprisoned for contempt of court — a dramatic personal statement
- After the Surat Split and Aurobindo's withdrawal from politics (1910), Pal gradually drifted to more moderate positions and effectively retired from active politics by 1911

---

### Lala Lajpat Rai (1865–1928) — "Punjab Kesari" / "Sher-e-Punjab"

"Punjab Kesari" means "Lion of Punjab." Lajpat Rai was deeply connected to the Arya Samaj — he brought the Arya Samaj's social energy into the nationalist movement in Punjab.

- Organized famine relief in Punjab; was a powerful agitator
- **Deportation (1907)**: During the Punjab unrest of 1907 (opposition to Colonization Bill and Canal Charges) — Lajpat Rai and Ajit Singh were deported to Mandalay without trial; released after 6 months
- Spent time in USA during WWI; wrote books there
- ***Unhappy India*** (1928): Written as a response to Katherine Mayo's *Mother India* (1927), which attacked Indian society and culture while ignoring British responsibility; Lajpat Rai's book was a detailed rebuttal
- **Simon Commission lathi charge (October 1928)**: When the all-British Simon Commission arrived in Lahore, Lajpat Rai led a peaceful protest march. Police superintendent James Scott ordered a lathi (baton) charge; Lajpat Rai was beaten severely. He died of his injuries on **17 November 1928**. His last public words: *"Every blow that falls on my body will be a nail in the coffin of British imperialism."*
- His death directly inspired Bhagat Singh, Rajguru, and Sukhdev to avenge him — leading to the shooting of police officer J.P. Saunders (whom they mistook for Scott) in December 1928.

## The Swadeshi Movement (1905–1908)

### The Trigger

On **16 October 1905**, the British officially partitioned Bengal into East Bengal and Assam (Muslim majority, capital Dacca) and West Bengal (Hindu majority, capital Calcutta). Curzon's justification was administrative efficiency; nationalists saw "divide and rule."

But the response started before the partition: on **7 August 1905**, at a town hall meeting in Calcutta, Indian leaders formally resolved to boycott British goods. This day — 7 August — is celebrated as **National Handloom Day** in India today.

### What the Movement Did

**Boycott**: Bonfires of Manchester cloth in streets; picketing shops that sold foreign goods; students refusing to buy imported pencils and pens.

**Swadeshi enterprises**: Indian textile mills (to replace imported cloth); Swadeshi Mills; Indian-owned banks (e.g., Bank of Bengal), insurance companies. Bengal Chemical & Pharmaceutical Works was founded by Prafulla Chandra Roy as a Swadeshi enterprise.

**National Education**: Bengal National College established (1906) — outside British university control; **Aurobindo Ghosh** was its first principal. Rabindranath Tagore founded Visva-Bharati at Shantiniketan (later, 1921) partly in this spirit.

**Samitis (volunteer organizations)**: Youth organizations for social service and nationalist work. **Anushilan Samiti** — most famous Bengali samiti; later evolved into a revolutionary organization involved in bomb-making and political assassination.

**Rakhi Bandhan**: Rabindranath Tagore organized the tying of rakhis (sacred threads) between Hindus and Muslims on partition day as a gesture of unity — a direct counter to Curzon's communal division.

**Vande Mataram** (Vandemataram): Bankim Chandra Chatterjee had written this song in his 1882 novel *Anandamath* — a song to the motherland. The Swadeshi movement adopted it as its battle cry. Every procession, every boycott bonfire, every public meeting resonated with "Vande Mataram" (I bow to thee, Mother). It became so central to Indian nationalism that the British later banned its public singing.

### Who Led It

| Leader | Role |
|--------|------|
| Aurobindo Ghosh | Radical intellectual; Bengal National College principal; later turned to spirituality (Pondicherry Ashram) |
| Bipin Chandra Pal | Mass orator; organized boycott in Bengal |
| Rabindranath Tagore | Cultural leadership; songs; Rakhi Bandhan; later distanced from the movement's religious overtones |
| Surendranath Banerjee | Moderate who joined the movement's boycott phase |
| Bal Gangadhar Tilak | Led Swadeshi campaigns outside Bengal |
| Krishna Kumar Mitra | Launched the formal boycott resolution at the town hall meeting |

### Significance

- **First mass movement** after 1857 — involving ordinary people, not just educated professionals
- Translated **economic nationalism** (Drain of Wealth theory) into direct action — stopping the drain at source
- Developed new political techniques: **boycott, passive resistance, mass agitation** — later used by Gandhi
- Spread outside Bengal — Punjab (Lajpat Rai), Maharashtra (Tilak), Andhra, Tamil Nadu
- But: had communal limitations — the movement's Hindu cultural imagery (Durga, Kali, Shivaji) alienated some Muslim leaders

## The Surat Split (1907)

### Why the Congress Split

After 1905, the Congress was two parties in one body, held together by decorum. The Extremists believed the 1906 Calcutta session's four resolutions (Swaraj, Swadeshi, Boycott, National Education) represented their victory — and they wanted to institutionalise them.

The **1907 annual session was held in Surat** (Gujarat). The contest: who would be Congress president?

- **Extremists wanted**: Bal Gangadhar Tilak as President
- **Moderates wanted**: Rash Behari Ghosh (a Moderate from Bengal)

The Moderates had the votes and elected Rash Behari Ghosh. When Tilak tried to address the session, chaos broke out — shoes were thrown, the microphone was pulled away, physical altercations occurred on the floor. The session dissolved into pandemonium.

The Surat session broke up without passing any resolutions. The Congress formally **split into two factions**.

### Aftermath of the Split

- Moderates took control of the official INC organization
- Extremists were excluded from Congress (1907–1916)
- The British took advantage of the split — arrested and prosecuted Extremist leaders
- Bipin Chandra Pal and Lajpat Rai gradually withdrew; **Aurobindo Ghosh** was arrested (Alipore Bomb Case, 1908), acquitted, and then renounced politics entirely — moving to Pondicherry in 1910 to found his ashram
- Tilak was imprisoned (1908–1914)

The split weakened the nationalist movement at a critical moment.

### Re-merger: Lucknow (1916)

The Congress reunited at the **Lucknow session (1916)** — partly because Tilak was free (1914), Besant's Home Rule League was creating momentum, and WWI had opened new political opportunities. The Extremists re-entered Congress.

---

## Lucknow Pact (1916)

### The Congress-League Unity Pact

At the same 1916 Lucknow session, the INC and the **All-India Muslim League** reached an agreement — the **Lucknow Pact**.

**Terms:**
- Both organizations agreed to demand **responsible government (self-government)** for India
- INC accepted the principle of **separate electorates for Muslims** (a major concession — legitimising communal representation)
- Muslims were to get a fixed proportion of seats in each legislature (weighted in Muslim-minority provinces)

**Significance:**
- First time Congress and League united on a political demand
- **M.A. Jinnah** played a key role — Sarojini Naidu called him "**Ambassador of Hindu-Muslim Unity**"
- Tilak also played a key role on the Congress side
- But: Congress's acceptance of separate electorates was later criticised — it implicitly legitimised the League's claim to speak for all Muslims

## Home Rule Leagues (1916)

Two parallel organisations demanding self-government:

| Feature | Tilak's Home Rule League | Besant's Home Rule League |
|---------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Founded | April 1916 | September 1916 |
| Founder | Bal Gangadhar Tilak | Annie Besant |
| HQ | Poona (Pune) | Adyar, Madras |
| Active in | Maharashtra, Karnataka, CP, Berar | Rest of India |
| Style | Mass agitation | Propaganda, press, lectures |

**Combined effect**: Together they revived mass nationalist agitation after a period of quiet (1908–1915). When the British interned Annie Besant in June 1917 (for her activities), it caused a public outcry — and paradoxically boosted the Home Rule cause, leading to the Montagu Declaration of August 1917.

## Exam Traps

1. **"Lokmanya" = Tilak** — NOT Lajpat Rai or Pal
2. **"Swaraj is my birthright and I shall have it" = Tilak** — NOT Gandhi or Nehru
3. **Tilak's festivals**: Ganpati (1893) AND Shivaji Birthday (1896) — both his innovations
4. **Kesari = Marathi; Mahratta = English** — both Tilak's papers
5. **Tilak imprisoned in Mandalay 1908–1914** — wrote Gita Rahasya there
6. **Gita Rahasya = written by Tilak** in Mandalay — NOT Dayananda's (that was Satyarth Prakash)
7. **Swadeshi Movement boycott resolution = 7 August 1905** (before actual partition on 16 October)
8. **Vande Mataram = from Bankim Chandra's Anandamath (1882)** — NOT composed during Swadeshi (it was adopted then, not composed)
9. **Bengal National College (1906) = Aurobindo Ghosh as principal** — NOT Tagore
10. **Surat Split = 1907** — NOT 1905 (Partition of Bengal) or 1906 (Calcutta session)
11. **Surat Split: Moderates got Rash Behari Ghosh elected** — Extremists wanted Tilak
12. **"Father of Revolutionary Thought" = Bipin Chandra Pal** — NOT Tilak (Tilak is "Lokmanya")
13. **"Punjab Kesari" = Lala Lajpat Rai** — NOT Tilak (Tilak is "Kesari" newspaper, not himself)
14. **Lajpat Rai died** after Simon Commission lathi charge (1928) — NOT in Mandalay
15. **Tilak's Home Rule League = April 1916, Poona** — Besant's = September 1916, Adyar
16. **Lucknow Pact (1916)** = Congress + Muslim League; Jinnah = "Ambassador of Hindu-Muslim Unity"
17. **Anushilan Samiti = Bengal revolutionary organization** — NOT the same as INC or Swadeshi merchants

## Self-Test (5 Questions)

1. What did Tilak mean by "Swaraj is my birthright"? How did his approach to nationalism differ fundamentally from Gokhale's?
2. What were Tilak's two major political festivals, and why were they politically effective?
3. What was the Swadeshi Movement and what new political techniques did it introduce?
4. What was the Surat Split (1907) — what caused it and what were its consequences?
5. What was the Lucknow Pact (1916) and why is Jinnah's role in it historically ironic?

*(Answers: 1. "Swaraj is my birthright" = political freedom is not something Indians should beg for — it is their natural right. Tilak's approach: mass mobilization, using religion and culture to build a broad political base, confronting British power rather than petitioning it, Swaraj as immediate goal. Gokhale: constitutional methods; social reform alongside political demands; trust in British fair play; Swaraj as distant aspiration. Fundamental difference: Tilak sought to mobilize the masses and confront; Gokhale sought to persuade the educated and negotiate. 2. Ganpati Festival (1893) — turned private Ganesha worship into a public, community event; could not be banned as religious but functioned as nationalist rally; brought diverse people together annually. Shivaji Birthday Festival (1896) — used Maratha hero to inspire anti-British sentiment without explicitly naming the British; historical symbolism. Politically effective: they reached people who didn't read newspapers; used emotion and community; built a mass base beyond the educated elite. 3. Swadeshi Movement (1905-1908): boycott of British goods + promotion of Indian goods. New techniques: (a) Boycott as economic weapon — refusing to buy British cloth, burning foreign goods; (b) Swadeshi enterprise — building Indian alternatives (mills, banks, schools); (c) National education outside British institutions; (d) Samitis (volunteer organizations) building grassroots organization; (e) Mass public events (bonfires, processions); later Gandhi adopted all of these. 4. Surat Split (1907): at the Surat session, Moderates maneuvered to elect their candidate (Rash Behari Ghosh) as president, blocking Tilak. When Tilak tried to speak, chaos erupted — shoes thrown, physical altercations; session dissolved. Congress split into Moderate faction (took over official Congress) and Extremist faction (excluded). Consequences: weakened nationalism; British prosecuted Extremists; Tilak imprisoned (1908-1914); Aurobindo withdrew to Pondicherry; movement went quiet until 1916. 5. Lucknow Pact (1916): INC and Muslim League agreed jointly to demand self-government; Congress accepted separate electorates for Muslims. Jinnah's role: he negotiated the pact between the two organizations; Sarojini Naidu called him "Ambassador of Hindu-Muslim Unity." Historically ironic: the same Jinnah who in 1916 was the great symbol of Hindu-Muslim unity became, by 1940, the leader demanding a separate Pakistan. The Lucknow Pact's concession on separate electorates that he helped negotiate in 1916 also legitimised the idea of Muslims as a separate political unit — which he later used as the foundation for his two-nation theory.)*
