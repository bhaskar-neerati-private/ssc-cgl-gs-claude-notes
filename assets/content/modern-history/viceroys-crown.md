## The Men Who Held a Shrinking Empire

In 1858, the British Crown took over from the East India Company. The Governor-General's title became **Viceroy** — signifying that he now represented the Queen/King directly, not a trading company. But this was also the beginning of the end: each decade brought stronger Indian resistance, each Viceroy inherited an empire that demanded more repression or more concession to survive.

This is the story of twelve Viceroys — from Canning who inherited the aftermath of 1857, to Mountbatten who handed power to two new nations ninety years later.

## 1. Lord Canning (1856–1862) — The Hinge Point

Canning was **Governor-General** when the 1857 Revolt broke out and became the **first Viceroy** when the Crown took over in 1858. He is the only person to hold both titles.

**Queen's Proclamation (1 November 1858)**:
- Announced that the Crown had taken over governance of India from the Company
- Promised to respect Indian rulers' rights (treaties, succession)
- Promised no discrimination by religion (but this was largely not followed)
- Announced amnesty for rebels (except those who killed Europeans)
- Called "Magna Carta of Indian rulers" for its guarantees to princely states

**Legislation under Canning**:
- **Indian Penal Code (1860)**: Drafted by Macaulay; comprehensive criminal code still largely in use (now replaced by Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023)
- **High Courts Act (1861)**: Established High Courts at Calcutta, Bombay, Madras (replaced earlier Supreme Courts and Sadar Courts)
- **Indian Councils Act (1861)**: Gave portfolio system — each member of Executive Council responsible for a department
- Universities of Calcutta, Bombay, Madras established (1857)

Canning was called **"Clemency Canning"** for his moderate response to the 1857 Revolt — he opposed indiscriminate retribution against Indians, which angered many British officials.

## 2. Lord Mayo (1869–1872)

**First Viceroy to be assassinated** — stabbed by Sher Ali, a Pathan convict, during a visit to the Andaman Islands penal colony in February 1872.

**Key contributions**:
- Financial decentralization — transferred revenues and responsibility for roads, jails, education to provincial governments; seeds of provincial autonomy
- Introduction of regular census operations
- Established the Mayo College at Ajmer (for Indian princes)

---

## 3. Lord Lytton (1876–1880) — The Reactionary

Lytton is remembered as one of the most anti-Indian Viceroys — his policies are studied as examples of British imperial arrogance.

**Royal Titles Act (1876)**:
- Queen Victoria was proclaimed **Empress of India (Kaisar-i-Hind)** at the Delhi Durbar of 1877
- This gave the British Crown a ceremonial claim to successor the Mughal Empire

**Vernacular Press Act (1878)**:
- Known as the "**Gagging Act**"
- Allowed the government to seize printing presses of Indian-language newspapers that published "seditious" content
- Applied to vernacular (Indian-language) papers only — English papers were exempt
- Widely condemned as racist and repressive
- Repealed by his successor Ripon in 1882

**Arms Act (1878)**:
- Indians required a license to possess arms; **Europeans were exempt**
- Hugely resented — symbolized racial inequality under British law

**Famine and the Grand Durbar**: While famine devastated parts of India (1876–78), Lytton organized a grand Delhi Durbar to celebrate Queen Victoria's proclamation as Empress. The optics were terrible — celebrated by nationalists as symbol of colonial indifference.

**Second Anglo-Afghan War (1878–80)**: Ended in British reverses; resolved under Ripon.

---

## 4. Lord Ripon (1880–1884) — The Most Popular Viceroy

Ripon is the most celebrated Viceroy among Indian nationalists — almost alone among British Viceroys for being genuinely sympathetic to Indian aspirations.

**Repeal of Vernacular Press Act (1882)**: First major act — abolished Lytton's "Gagging Act."

**Local Self-Government Resolution (1882)**:
- Created elected local bodies (municipalities, district boards) across India
- Ripon is called the **"Father of Local Self-Government in India"**
- This is the constitutional precursor to the 73rd/74th Amendments of 1992

**First regular Census (1881)**: Ripon made census a systematic, decennial exercise.

**Hunter Commission on Education (1882)**: Reviewed primary and secondary education; recommended expansion of vernacular education.

**Ilbert Bill Controversy (1883)**:
- The Law Member, C.P. Ilbert, proposed that Indian judges and magistrates should have the power to try European British subjects — removing the racial exemption
- **Massive backlash from European community** in India — plantations owners, traders, officials — who feared being tried by Indian judges
- Bill was diluted — the racial privilege was partially restored
- **Significance**: The European community's organized agitation showed Indians that organized pressure could change government policy. This lesson was not lost — it directly inspired the founding of the Indian National Congress two years later (1885)

## 5. Lord Curzon (1899–1905) — The Grand Imperial

Curzon was the most imperious of all Viceroys — brilliant, hardworking, arrogant, and convinced of Britain's civilizing mission. His tenure saw both significant administrative reform and the most galvanizing blunder of the era.

**Administrative contributions**:
- Ancient Monuments Preservation Act (1904): Protected historical monuments; ASI (Archaeological Survey of India) revived
- Indian Universities Act (1904): Brought universities under tighter government control — reduced autonomy; resented
- Police Commission (1902–03): Improved police efficiency
- Agricultural Research: set up agricultural institutes

**Partition of Bengal (16 October 1905)**:
- Curzon divided Bengal (then the largest province) into two: **East Bengal and Assam** (Muslim majority, capital Dacca) and **West Bengal** (Hindu majority, capital Calcutta)
- Official reason: Administrative efficiency — Bengal was too large to govern
- Real reason (Indian nationalists believed): Divide Bengal along religious lines to weaken the Bengali nationalist movement; "divide and rule"
- **Effect**: The Partition triggered the **Swadeshi Movement** and the **Anti-Partition Movement** — the most intense nationalist agitation since 1857
- **Reversed by Lord Hardinge II in 1911** at the Delhi Durbar
- Curzon resigned in 1905 over a conflict with Lord Kitchener (Commander-in-Chief) over control of the military

---

## 6. Lord Minto II (1905–1910)

**Morley-Minto Reforms (Indian Councils Act 1909)**:
- John Morley (Secretary of State) + Lord Minto
- Increased Indian membership in Legislative Councils
- Introduced **separate electorates for Muslims** — Muslims would vote only for Muslim candidates
- This is the most significant constitutional change: separate electorates planted the seed of the two-nation theory
- First Indian (Satyendra Prasanna Sinha) appointed to Viceroy's Executive Council

**Muslim League (1906)**: Founded at Dacca (Dhaka) during Minto's tenure — the political organization that would eventually demand Pakistan. A delegation led by the Aga Khan had met Minto in 1906 to request separate Muslim representation.

---

## 7. Lord Hardinge II (1910–1916)

**Partition of Bengal reversed (1911)**:
- At the Delhi Durbar (December 1911), King George V (who visited India in person) announced:
  - Partition of Bengal was **reversed** — Bengal reunified
  - Capital shifted from **Calcutta to Delhi** (New Delhi construction began)
- The Indian nationalist movement celebrated Bengal's reunification; Curzon's policy was repudiated

**Bomb attack (1912)**: During the ceremonial entry into Delhi, a bomb was thrown at Hardinge's elephant during the procession. He was wounded but survived. The attackers (Rash Behari Bose and associates) escaped.

**Ghadar Movement (1913–15)**: Revolutionary movement by Indian immigrants in North America (mainly Punjabis); they returned to India to foment revolution during WWI. Suppressed; leaders hanged.

---

## 8. Lord Chelmsford (1916–1921)

**Montagu Declaration (August 1917)**:
- Secretary of State Edwin Montagu made a landmark declaration: British policy was "increasing association of Indians in every branch of administration" and eventually "responsible government"
- This was the first explicit British acknowledgment that self-government was the goal

**Montagu-Chelmsford Report (1918)**:
- Basis of Government of India Act 1919
- Introduced "Dyarchy" in provinces — some subjects transferred to Indian ministers responsible to elected legislatures; "reserved" subjects remained with the Governor

**Rowlatt Act (1919)**:
- Extended wartime emergency powers into peacetime — allowed detention without trial, trials without jury
- Indians called it the "Black Act"
- Gandhi launched the first India-wide Satyagraha against it

**Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (13 April 1919)**:
- General Dyer ordered troops to fire on a crowd gathered at Jallianwala Bagh, Amritsar — a walled garden with narrow exits
- **379 killed** (official); thousands wounded (unofficial estimate much higher)
- The massacre transformed Indian opinion permanently — Tagore returned his knighthood; Gandhi abandoned cooperation with the British
- Hunter Committee investigation; Dyer censured but not criminally charged; widespread anger in India

**Non-Cooperation Movement (1920)**: Gandhi launched the first mass movement against British rule — under Chelmsford's tenure.

---

## 9. Lord Irwin (1926–1931)

**Simon Commission (1928)**:
- All-British commission to review India's constitutional progress
- No Indian members — boycotted by all Indian parties
- "Simon Go Back" protests; Lala Lajpat Rai was beaten at Lahore protest; he died weeks later (1928)

**Salt March / Dandi March (12 March – 6 April 1930)**:
- Gandhi led a 241-mile march from Sabarmati Ashram to Dandi on the Gujarat coast
- On 6 April 1930, he picked up a handful of salt — symbolically breaking the salt law
- Launched the **Civil Disobedience Movement**

**Gandhi-Irwin Pact (5 March 1931)**:
- Gandhi suspended Civil Disobedience; agreed to attend Second Round Table Conference in London
- Irwin agreed to: release political prisoners (not those convicted of violence); restore confiscated property; allow coastal villages to make salt; permit Congress to organize
- **Significance**: British negotiated with Gandhi as an equal — acknowledged Congress as a representative body

Bhagat Singh, Rajguru, and Sukhdev were **executed on 23 March 1931** — just weeks after the Gandhi-Irwin Pact. Gandhi's inability to secure their clemency was a source of criticism.

## 10. Lord Linlithgow (1936–1944) — The Longest Serving

Linlithgow was Viceroy for eight years — the longest tenure in the Crown era. His period covered the most critical years of WWII and the most intense phase of the independence movement.

**Provincial elections (1937)** under Government of India Act 1935:
- Congress won majority in 7 of 11 provinces; formed ministries
- Muslim League did poorly — won only 4.8% of Muslim seats

**Congress ministries resigned (October 1939)**:
- When WWII began (September 1939), Linlithgow declared India at war without consulting Indian leaders
- Congress demanded a declaration of future independence as the price of cooperation
- Linlithgow refused; Congress ministries resigned; Jinnah declared a "Day of Deliverance"

**Cripps Mission (March-April 1942)**:
- Sir Stafford Cripps came with an offer: Dominion Status after the war; right to secede; provinces could opt out of the Indian Union
- Congress rejected it — called it a "post-dated cheque on a crashing bank" (Gandhi)
- Muslim League rejected it too; Cripps returned empty-handed

**Quit India Movement (8 August 1942)**:
- Gandhi's "Do or Die" speech at Gowalia Tank, Bombay
- Congress passed the Quit India Resolution; British arrested all Congress leaders within hours
- Spontaneous uprising across India — British suppressed it brutally; ~100,000 arrested

**Bengal Famine (1943)**:
- Estimated 2–3 million deaths in Bengal from starvation
- Partly caused by wartime export of rice, denial of relief, and Churchill's deliberate policy of not diverting resources
- Linlithgow's response was inadequate; the famine remains one of the darkest chapters of British India

---

## 11. Lord Mountbatten (March–August 1947) — The Last Viceroy

Mountbatten arrived with a clear mandate: transfer power quickly. What no one anticipated was how quickly.

**Mountbatten Plan (3 June 1947)**:
- Partition of British India into two dominions: **India** and **Pakistan**
- Punjab and Bengal to be divided (not given wholly to either dominion)
- Princely states free to choose — but geographically had to accede to one
- Date of transfer advanced from June 1948 to **15 August 1947** — barely 10 weeks away

**Indian Independence Act 1947**: Passed by British Parliament; established two dominions.

**14–15 August 1947**: Pakistan came into existence at midnight 14 August; India at midnight 15 August. Mountbatten became the first **Governor-General of independent India** (until June 1948, when C. Rajagopalachari succeeded him).

**Radcliffe Line**: Sir Cyril Radcliffe drew the boundary between India and Pakistan — never having visited India before; completed in six weeks. The resulting boundary has been called one of history's most consequential rushed decisions.

**Integration of Princely States**: Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel (with V.P. Menon) integrated 562 princely states into the Indian Union — all but Hyderabad, Kashmir, and Junagadh acceded before Independence.

## Exam Traps

1. **First Viceroy = Lord Canning** — also the last Governor-General; the only person to be both.
2. **Vernacular Press Act = Lytton (1878)** — gagging act; English press exempt; repealed by Ripon.
3. **Ripon = Father of Local Self-Government** — Local Self-Government Resolution 1882; not Curzon.
4. **Ilbert Bill = Ripon's tenure (1883)** — Indian judges to try Europeans; European backlash diluted it.
5. **Partition of Bengal = Curzon (1905)** — reversed = Hardinge II (1911, Delhi Durbar).
6. **Separate electorates for Muslims = Morley-Minto Reforms (1909)** — Minto II.
7. **Capital shifted Calcutta → Delhi = 1911 Delhi Durbar** — Hardinge II; George V visited.
8. **Jallianwala Bagh = 13 April 1919** — Chelmsford; General Dyer; 379 killed officially.
9. **Gandhi-Irwin Pact = March 1931** — Irwin (not Willingdon or Linlithgow).
10. **Cripps Mission = 1942** — failed; Linlithgow; "post-dated cheque on a crashing bank."
11. **Quit India = 8 August 1942** — Linlithgow; Gandhi arrested; "Do or Die."
12. **Mountbatten Plan = 3 June 1947** — Independence: 15 August 1947.
13. **Mayo = first Viceroy to be assassinated** — at Andaman Islands, 1872.
14. **Lord Irwin = Lord Halifax** — same person; Irwin was his Viceroy title, Halifax later as Foreign Secretary.
15. **Montagu Declaration (1917)** — "increasing association of Indians"; Chelmsford was Viceroy.
