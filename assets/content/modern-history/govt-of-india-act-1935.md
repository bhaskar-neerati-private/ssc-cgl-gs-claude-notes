# Government of India Act 1935 & Congress Ministries (1935–1939)

## The Biggest Law That Half-Worked

The **Government of India Act 1935** is one of the most paradoxical documents in Indian constitutional history. It was the longest piece of legislation ever passed by the British Parliament up to that point — **321 sections and 10 schedules**. It took years to draft, drawing from the Simon Commission Report (1930), three Round Table Conferences, a White Paper (1933), and a Joint Select Committee Report (1934). It contained the most elaborate federal scheme for India anyone had yet put on paper.

And its most important feature — the All-India Federation — never came into operation. Not for a year, not for a day.

Nehru called it **"a machine with strong brakes but no engine"** — designed with elaborate safeguards to prevent Indians from actually governing, rather than to enable them.

Yet the Act is constitutionally crucial. Large parts of India's 1950 Constitution were directly borrowed from it. The Federal Court became the Supreme Court. The Federal Public Service Commission became the UPSC. The bicameral provincial legislatures survived. Understanding this Act explains both why Congress and Jinnah collided so badly in 1937-1939, and why that collision set India on the path to partition.

---

## How the Act Was Built — Its Foundations

The Act emerged from a four-stage process:
1. **Simon Commission Report (1930)** — recommended provincial autonomy but a weak federation
2. **Three Round Table Conferences (1930-32)** — brought in Indian voices (minus Congress for most of it)
3. **White Paper (1933)** — the British government's proposal based on RTC deliberations
4. **Joint Select Committee (1934)** — Parliament examined the White Paper; the committee's report formed the basis of the Act

The Act was passed in August 1935 and was supposed to come into effect in stages.

---

## The Three Core Features

### 1. All-India Federation — The Feature That Never Worked

The Act envisioned an **All-India Federation** that would unite:
- The 11 British Indian provinces (Governors' provinces)
- The Princely States (600+ states, controlled by Indian rulers who had treaty relationships with the British Crown)

For the Federation to come into force, it needed: provinces with at least half the seats in the federal legislature, AND Princes ruling states that accounted for at least 52 of the 104 seats allocated to Princely States.

The Princes never agreed. They debated, delayed, and eventually the outbreak of World War II in September 1939 made the question moot. **The federal provisions of the GoI Act 1935 never came into operation** — not a single day.

This matters enormously: the most discussed, most elaborate feature of the Act was stillborn.

### 2. Provincial Autonomy — The Feature That Did Work

Provincial autonomy was the part of the Act that came into force in 1937, following provincial elections.

**What it replaced:** Under the Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms (GoI Act 1919), provinces had Dyarchy — part responsible to the legislature (Transferred subjects), part not (Reserved subjects). Provincial Autonomy abolished this split.

**What it introduced:**
- Governors' provinces got fully elected legislatures
- The entire cabinet of ministers was responsible to the elected legislature
- If a government lost the legislature's confidence, it had to resign
- This was real parliamentary government at the provincial level — for the first time

**The catch:** Governors retained extensive **special powers and safeguards** — they could act against the advice of ministers in matters of law and order, minority rights, and British interests. Congress ministers found that Governors sometimes used these reserve powers to override elected governments, a constant source of friction during 1937-1939.

Eleven provinces: Madras, Bombay, Bengal, United Provinces, Punjab, Bihar, Central Provinces, Assam, Orissa (new), Sindh (new), North-West Frontier Province.

Note: **Sindh was separated from Bombay** and **Orissa was separated from Bihar** under the 1935 Act — both became separate provinces.

**Burma:** Separated from India entirely — the companion **Government of Burma Act 1935** made Burma a separate British colony with its own constitution, no longer part of British India.

### 3. Dyarchy at the Centre — The Feature That Also Never Worked

In an ironic reversal: the Act **abolished Dyarchy in provinces** (replacing it with full autonomy) but **introduced Dyarchy at the Centre**. The federal government would have:
- **Reserved subjects** (defence, external affairs, ecclesiastical affairs): under the Viceroy, NOT accountable to the federal legislature
- **Transferred subjects** (everything else): under ministers responsible to the federal legislature

Since the federal provisions never came into force, this central Dyarchy also never operated.

## Other Provisions of the 1935 Act

**Federal Court:** A **Federal Court** was established in January 1937 in New Delhi — a new apex judicial body separate from the Privy Council, with jurisdiction over constitutional matters between the federation and provinces. Its first Chief Justice was Sir Maurice Gwyer. The Federal Court became the **Supreme Court of India** in 1950 — the 1935 Act is thus the direct ancestor of India's supreme judicial institution.

**Reserve Bank of India:** The GoI Act 1935 provided for the establishment of the **Reserve Bank of India** — which was duly established in April 1935. This is a frequently tested factual link.

**Federal Public Service Commission:** The Act provided for a Federal Public Service Commission (expanding on the 1919 Act's Public Service Commission provision) — the direct predecessor of the UPSC.

**Bicameral Legislatures in Provinces:** Six of the eleven provinces were given **bicameral** provincial legislatures (Legislative Council + Legislative Assembly). The other five had unicameral legislatures.

**Separate electorates extended further:** Building on 1909 (Muslims) and 1919 (Sikhs, Christians, etc.), the 1935 Act further extended separate electorates to include **Scheduled Castes, women, and labour representatives** — reflecting the Poona Pact compromise.

**Council of India abolished:** The 15-member India Council (established in 1858 to advise the Secretary of State) was abolished — replaced by a smaller advisory body.

## The 1937 Elections and the Congress Ministries

With provincial autonomy now in force, elections were held across all eleven provinces in February 1937. The results were striking:

- **Congress** won absolute majorities in **6 provinces**: Madras, United Provinces, Central Provinces, Bihar, Bombay, Orissa
- Congress formed governments in **7 provinces** (including NWFP through a coalition)
- **Muslim League** won only **22%** of the Muslim seats in Muslim constituencies — a poor showing in its supposed constituency
- The Punjab went to the Unionist Party (Fazl-i-Husain's party, not the League); Bengal went to a coalition

The Congress's strong performance, and the Muslim League's poor showing, had profound consequences for Jinnah's strategy and India's communal trajectory.

**The Congress-League tussle over coalition governments:**
In United Provinces, Congress won a majority but initially included a League member in the cabinet. Congress then insisted that League members in the cabinet must join the Congress parliamentary party and abide by Congress policy decisions. Jinnah refused — he wanted the League to participate as an equal partner, not subordinate to Congress discipline. The League was excluded. This episode is central to Jinnah's later argument that Congress majority rule meant Muslim subordination.

## The Congress Ministries (July 1937 – October 1939)

Congress governments were sworn in across 7 provinces in July 1937. The period of the **Congress Ministries** (also called the Congress rule in provinces, 1937-1939) was significant on multiple counts:

**What Congress did in office:**
- Released political prisoners
- Promoted Hindi in some provinces (this alarmed Muslim communities in UP where Urdu was culturally important)
- Land reform measures (limited)
- Constructive work: Wardha educational scheme (basic education based on craft-centred learning, designed by Gandhi)
- Civil liberties expanded

**The friction with Governors:**
Congress ministers repeatedly clashed with Governors over the use of special powers. The Governors would sometimes refuse to act on ministerial advice regarding political prisoners, civil liberties, and press freedom. This friction confirmed nationalist fears that the 1935 Act's "provincial autonomy" was constrained autonomy.

**Resignation — 3 September 1939:**
On 3 September 1939, Britain declared war on Germany and Viceroy Lord Linlithgow simultaneously declared that India was also at war — without consulting Indian political leaders, the Central legislature, or the provincial governments. Congress was outraged: India's 350 million people had been committed to a global war without a moment's consultation with their elected representatives.

Congress demanded a declaration that India would become independent after the war, and that a provisional national government be formed at the Centre immediately. Linlithgow refused. All **Congress ministries resigned in October-November 1939**.

---

## The Day of Deliverance — 22 December 1939

When the Congress ministries resigned, Jinnah and the Muslim League declared **22 December 1939 a "Day of Deliverance"** — a day of thanksgiving that Congress's "Hindu Raj" had ended.

This was a watershed moment in communal politics. The League's argument: Congress rule, despite its secular claims, had effectively functioned as Hindu majority rule. The promotion of Hindi over Urdu, the singing of Vande Mataram at government functions (which League Muslims objected to as a Hindu religious song), the behaviour of Congress ministers in some provinces — all were cited as evidence.

Whether the Congress ministries had genuinely discriminated against Muslims, or whether the League's complaints were politically exaggerated, is contested by historians. But the political damage was real: the 1937-1939 experience hardened Jinnah's position that Muslims needed a separate homeland, leading to the Lahore Resolution (March 1940) in which the League first officially demanded separate Muslim states.

## The 1935 Act's Legacy — Borrowed by the Constitution

Despite its failures, the GoI Act 1935 is arguably the most important single document in the making of India's Constitution:

- The **structure of federal-provincial (centre-state) relations** was largely retained
- The **emergency provisions** (Governor's Rule in states) derive from the 1935 Act's Governor's special powers
- The **three-list system** (Union List, State List, Concurrent List) comes from the 1935 Act's division of legislative subjects
- The **Federal Court** → Supreme Court
- The **Federal Public Service Commission** → UPSC
- The **bicameral pattern** in certain states

Dr. Ambedkar (chairman of the Constitution Drafting Committee) acknowledged the debt: large sections of the 1950 Constitution were drafted with the 1935 Act as the template, adapted and democratised.

## Key Comparison: Dyarchy Under Two Acts

| Feature | GoI Act 1919 | GoI Act 1935 |
|---|---|---|
| Dyarchy location | Provinces | Centre (never operated) |
| Provincial government | Split (Reserved + Transferred) | Full autonomy — ministers responsible to legislature |
| Central legislature | Bicameral (Council of State + Legislative Assembly) | Would be bicameral federal legislature (never came to be) |
| Separate electorates | Muslims, Sikhs, Christians, Anglo-Indians, Europeans | Extended to include Scheduled Castes, women, labour |
| New institutions | Public Service Commission (first mention) | Federal Court, RBI, Federal PSC |

## Exam Traps — 17 Things SSC Will Test

1. **GoI Act 1935 had 321 sections and 10 schedules** — the longest British parliamentary act at the time.
2. **The federal provisions NEVER came into operation** — Princes refused to join; no All-India Federation was ever formed under this Act.
3. **Dyarchy was ABOLISHED in provinces** under the 1935 Act — replaced by full provincial autonomy.
4. **Dyarchy was INTRODUCED at the Centre** under the 1935 Act — but also never operated.
5. The irony: 1919 = Dyarchy at provinces; 1935 = Dyarchy abolished at provinces, introduced at Centre (which also never worked).
6. **Sindh separated from Bombay** and **Orissa from Bihar** under the 1935 Act.
7. **Burma separated from India** under the Government of Burma Act 1935 — NOT the Government of India Act 1935 itself.
8. **Federal Court established in January 1937** (NOT 1935 — the act passed 1935, court set up 1937). First Chief Justice: Sir Maurice Gwyer.
9. **Reserve Bank of India was established under GoI Act 1935** — RBI came into being April 1935.
10. **Congress won majority in 6 provinces** in 1937; formed governments in **7 provinces**.
11. **Muslim League won only 22% of Muslim seats** in 1937 — a poor performance that intensified Jinnah's need for a stronger communal platform.
12. Congress ministries lasted from **July 1937 to October-November 1939** — NOT a full 3-year term.
13. Congress resigned because **Viceroy Linlithgow declared India at war without consultation** (3 September 1939).
14. **Day of Deliverance = 22 December 1939** — Muslim League's celebration of Congress resignations. Declared by Jinnah.
15. **Nehru's famous quote about GoI Act 1935**: "a machine with strong brakes but no engine."
16. The **Lahore Resolution (March 1940)** — the League's demand for separate Muslim states — came AFTER and was directly triggered by the Congress Ministries episode (1937-39) and Day of Deliverance.
17. The **GoI Act 1935 was the template for India's 1950 Constitution** — three-list system, emergency provisions, Federal Court → Supreme Court, Federal PSC → UPSC.
