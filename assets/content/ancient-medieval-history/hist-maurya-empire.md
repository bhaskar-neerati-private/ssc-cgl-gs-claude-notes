# Mauryan Empire — Chandragupta, Ashoka, Edicts & Administration

## Chanakya's Masterplan

In 326 BCE, Alexander the Great reached the banks of the Beas river in Punjab and his exhausted soldiers refused to march further east. He turned back, dying in Babylon in 323 BCE at 32. His generals divided his empire. But Alexander's brief presence in northwestern India triggered a political earthquake: a young man named Chandragupta Maurya, reportedly meeting Alexander or observing his campaigns, watched and learned.

With the brilliant, ruthless strategist **Chanakya** (also called Kautilya or Vishnugupta) as his teacher and minister, Chandragupta raised an army in 323 BCE, overthrew the Nanda king Dhana Nanda at Pataliputra, and by 320 BCE controlled Magadha and was building the first pan-Indian empire. Within thirty years, that empire would stretch from modern Afghanistan to the Bay of Bengal — the largest political entity the Indian subcontinent had ever seen.

---

## Part 1 — Chandragupta Maurya (322–298 BCE)

- **Founded** the Mauryan Empire after defeating Dhana Nanda (last Nanda king) with Chanakya's support
- **Pushed back Seleucus Nicator** (Alexander's general, ruler of eastern part of his empire): **Treaty of 305 BCE** — Seleucus gave up territories (modern Afghanistan, Balochistan) in exchange for 500 war elephants; Seleucus' daughter or granddaughter married into the Mauryan family
- **Megasthenes** was Seleucus' ambassador at Chandragupta's court — his *Indica* is a key source
- **Capital**: Pataliputra (modern Patna, Bihar) — described by Megasthenes as one of the world's greatest cities
- **Last years**: Chandragupta converted to **Jainism**, abdicated in favour of his son **Bindusara**, migrated south with Jain monk Bhadrabahu, and allegedly fasted to death (Sallekhana) at **Shravanabelagola** (Karnataka)

---

## Part 2 — Chanakya / Kautilya and the Arthashastra

**Chanakya** (also Kautilya, Vishnugupta): Brahmin scholar from Taxila; Chandragupta's mentor and prime minister.

**Arthashastra** ("Science of Material Gain/Politics"): a comprehensive manual on statecraft, economics, military strategy, and espionage. Rediscovered in 1905 by R. Shamasastry. Key contents:
- Theory of the state (Saptanga — seven elements: king, minister, territory, fort, treasury, army, ally)
- **Mandala theory**: concentric circles of states — immediate neighbour is a natural enemy; neighbour's neighbour is a natural friend
- Detailed espionage system: network of spies and secret agents
- Revenue collection, trade regulation, judicial system
- Recommendations sometimes ruthlessly pragmatic — often compared to Machiavelli's *The Prince*

---

## Part 3 — Bindusara (298–272 BCE)

- Son of Chandragupta; known as **Amitrochates** or **Amitraghata** ("Slayer of Enemies")
- Maintained/expanded the empire southward
- Greek ambassador **Deimachus** was at his court
- Father of Ashoka
- Legend: he requested the Seleucid king (Antiochus I) to send wine, figs, and a philosopher; Antiochus replied he could send wine and figs but a philosopher is not for sale

## Part 4 — Ashoka the Great (273–232 BCE)

Ashoka is arguably the most remarkable ruler in Indian history — a conqueror who renounced conquest, a king who governed by moral example, and a proselytiser who sent Buddhism across Asia.

### Key Events

| Event | Year | Significance |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Accession | 273 BCE | (coronation 269 BCE; 4-year interregnum) |
| **Kalinga War** | **261 BCE** | Turning point — 100,000 killed, 150,000 deported; Ashoka was horrified |
| Conversion to Buddhism | After Kalinga | Became an ardent Buddhist layman |
| 3rd Buddhist Council | ~250 BCE | Pataliputra; sent missionaries including **Mahendra** (son/brother) to Sri Lanka |
| Dhamma Policy | After 261 BCE | Governing principle of tolerance, non-violence, respect for all sects |
| Death | 232 BCE | Empire began to fragment under successors |

### Ashoka's Dhamma (not simply Buddhism)

Ashoka's Dhamma was a syncretic moral code — not sectarian Buddhism:
- Respect for parents, elders, teachers
- Non-violence (Ahimsa) — banned animal slaughter at court; reduced meat consumption
- Tolerance of all religious sects
- Truth-speaking, compassion
- Respect for Brahmins, Shramanas (Buddhist monks), and all sects

### Ashoka's Edicts

**Rock Edicts** (Major — 14; Minor): inscribed on large boulders at the empire's edges; announce Dhamma principles; first issued after Kalinga war

**Pillar Edicts** (Major — 7; Minor): inscribed on sandstone pillars; concentrated in heartland; later edicts; refine Dhamma

**Cave Inscriptions**: Barabar caves (Bihar) — dedicated to Ajivika monks

| Edict | Location | Significance |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Rock Edict XIII | Various | Describes Kalinga war horror; Dhamma conquest |
| Rock Edict II | Various | Welfare measures (hospitals, roads, wells, shady trees) |
| Kandahar Bilingual Edict | Afghanistan | In Greek and Aramaic — shows empire's extent |
| **Rummindei (Lumbini) Pillar** | Nepal | Records Ashoka's visit to Buddha's birthplace; tax remission |
| **Sarnath Pillar** | UP | Famous **Lion Capital** — adopted as India's national emblem |
| **Sanchi Stupa** | MP | Major Buddhist monument; built/expanded by Ashoka |

### Ashokan Script and Language
- Most edicts in **Brahmi script** (left to right; ancestor of all modern Indian scripts)
- Northwestern edicts in **Kharosthi** (right to left; derived from Aramaic)
- Languages: Prakrit (most), Sanskrit (minor), Greek, Aramaic
- **Brahmi deciphered** by James Prinsep in **1837** — this opened up the entire epigraphic record of ancient India

### National Symbols from Ashoka

| Symbol | Source | Modern Use |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| **Lion Capital (Sarnath)** | Ashoka's pillar at Sarnath | **National Emblem of India** |
| **Ashoka Chakra** | 24-spoked wheel on Sarnath capital | **Centre of Indian National Flag** |
| **Dharma Chakra Pravartana** | First Sermon symbol | On national flag |

## Part 5 — Mauryan Administration

### Central Government
- **King** — absolute sovereign; assisted by council of ministers (Mantriparishad)
- **Mantriparishad**: key ministers — Mantrin (chief minister), Purohita (head priest), Senapati (army chief), Yuvaraja (crown prince)
- **Amatyas**: high officials; bureaucratic class

### Provincial Administration
- Empire divided into **4 provinces** each under a **Kumara** (prince/viceroy):
  - Uttarapatha (NW: capital Taxila)
  - Avantiratha (W: capital Ujjain)
  - Kalinga (E: capital Tosali) — added after Kalinga war
  - Dakshinapatha (S: capital Suvarnagiri)
- **Janapadas** (districts) → **Sthaniya** (800 villages) → **Dronamukha** (400 villages) → **Kharvatika** (200 villages) → **Grama** (village)

### Pataliputra's City Administration (per Megasthenes)
- City Council of 30 members divided into 6 committees (5 each):
  1. Industry and handicrafts
  2. Foreigners
  3. Registration of births and deaths
  4. Trade and commerce
  5. Supervision of manufactured goods
  6. Tax collection

### Economy and Revenue
- **Land revenue** (1/4 to 1/6 of produce): primary source
- **Sita**: royal agricultural land
- **Shulka**: customs/tolls on goods
- **Viskambha**: pasture tax
- **Bali, Bhaga, Hiranya**: different types of taxes

### Army
- **Six-fold**: infantry, cavalry, chariots, elephants, navy, commissariat
- 600,000 infantry, 30,000 cavalry, 9,000 elephants (per Megasthenes — probably exaggerated)
- Elephants were India's "tanks" — decisive in battle

### Decline of the Mauryan Empire
- After Ashoka's death (232 BCE), empire rapidly fragmented
- **Brihadratha**: last Mauryan king — killed by his own general **Pushyamitra Shunga** in **185 BCE** during a military parade
- Pushyamitra founded the Shunga dynasty
- Causes of decline: Ashoka's non-violence weakened military; provincial governors became independent; economic strain from welfare expenditure; Brahminical reaction to Buddhism; no strong successor to Ashoka

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Kalinga War was in 261 BCE** (not 272, not 268). It was the 8th year of Ashoka's reign. The war is described in Rock Edict XIII. After Kalinga, Ashoka replaced Dig-vijaya (conquest by force) with Dhamma-vijaya (conquest by righteousness).

2. **Chandragupta's treaty with Seleucus (305 BCE)**: India got territory (Afghanistan + Balochistan); Seleucus got 500 war elephants. This is the reverse of what students often assume (Chandragupta DID NOT lose territory).

3. **Megasthenes** was Seleucus' ambassador at Chandragupta's court (~302 BCE). **Deimachus** was at Bindusara's court. Do NOT say Megasthenes was at Ashoka's or Bindusara's court.

4. **Chanakya's other names**: Kautilya (his gotra), Vishnugupta (his personal name). His treatise is the Arthashastra. James Shamasastry rediscovered and published it in 1909.

5. **Ashoka's Lion Capital (Sarnath)**: four lions back-to-back; below them a frieze with four animals (bull, lion, elephant, horse) and four dharma chakras; at base, an inverted lotus. Only THREE lions are visible from the front → national emblem shows three. The original is at the **Sarnath Museum**.

6. **Brahmi script deciphered by James Prinsep in 1837**. This was a monumental achievement — it unlocked all Ashokan edicts and centuries of Indian epigraphic history. Kharosthi (used in northwestern edicts) was deciphered around the same time.

7. **Ashoka sent Dhamma missions** to: Sri Lanka (Mahendra his son, Sanghamitra his daughter with a Bodhi tree sapling), Syria, Egypt, Macedonia, Cyrene, Epirus — recorded in Rock Edict XIII. Mahendra is credited with establishing Buddhism in Sri Lanka.

8. **The Sarnath Pillar**: the Lion Capital on this pillar was adopted as India's National Emblem after independence. The 24-spoked wheel (Ashoka Chakra) from the abacus of this capital is the central feature of the Indian national flag.

9. **Pushyamitra Shunga killed Brihadratha** (last Maurya) in 185 BCE — this ended the Mauryan empire. Pushyamitra was supposedly a Brahmin general who conducted several Ashvamedha sacrifices — seen as a Brahminical reaction to Buddhist patronage.

10. **Arthashastra's Mandala Theory**: your immediate neighbour is your natural enemy; your neighbour's neighbour is your natural ally. The theory recommends this as the basis for all foreign policy — a form of "the enemy of my enemy is my friend."

11. **Pataliputra (modern Patna)** was the Mauryan capital. Megasthenes described it as a city of 80 stadia (14 km) in length, 15 stadia wide, surrounded by a wooden palisade with 570 towers and 64 gates, and a moat. Archaeological evidence at Kumrahar (Patna) shows wooden pillared hall remnants.

12. **Chandragupta converted to Jainism** (not Buddhism). He abdicated, migrated to Shravanabelagola (Karnataka) with the Jain monk Bhadrabahu, and practised Sallekhana (ritual fasting to death). A statue of Chandragupta stands at Shravanabelagola.

13. **Ashoka's Dhamma ≠ Buddhism**. Dhamma was a broad moral code — universal ethics. He did not impose Buddhism. He respected all sects (Brahmins, Jains, Ajivikas, etc.) and built rest houses and hospitals for all. This syncretic approach distinguished him from a purely sectarian patron.

14. **Mauryan provinces**: four main provinces each governed by a prince (Kumara). The provincial capitals were Taxila (NW), Ujjain (W), Tosali/Samapa (Kalinga), and Suvarnagiri (S). District administration below was very detailed.

15. **Rummindei (Lumbini) Pillar Edict**: records that Ashoka visited Lumbini (Buddha's birthplace) in the 20th year of his reign and reduced the land tax to 1/8th (from 1/4) for the people of Lumbini because it was the birthplace of the "Shakyamuni Buddha." This is crucial evidence for establishing Buddha's historical existence and connection to Lumbini.

16. **The last Mauryan emperor was Brihadratha** — killed by Pushyamitra Shunga in 185 BCE. Pushyamitra founded the **Shunga dynasty** (185–73 BCE), which was Brahminical in orientation and supported by the Manusmriti tradition. The Sungas patronised the rebuilding of Sanchi Stupa's gateways (toranas).
