# Delhi Sultanate — Slave, Khalji, Tughlaq, Sayyid & Lodi Dynasties

## The First Woman on the Throne of Delhi

In 1236 CE, something happened that had never occurred in the entire history of Islamic kingdoms: a woman ascended the throne. Razia Sultana — daughter of Iltutmish, trained by him in statecraft, publicly acknowledged by him as his most capable child — became Sultan of Delhi. She rode an elephant in public without a veil, held court unveiled, refused to be confined to the zenana. She appointed a devoted officer as lord of the stables — a position of great trust — despite opposition from the nobility. She was betrayed, captured, and executed in 1240, after just three and a half years.

But the fact that she happened at all — that the Delhi Sultanate, in only its second generation, installed a woman as supreme ruler — says something extraordinary about the pragmatic, flexible nature of the Sultanate's politics. Power mattered. Competence mattered. Gender was a later imposition. Delhi under the Sultans was nothing if not surprising.

## Part 1 — Slave/Mamluk Dynasty (1206–1290 CE)

Also called **Ghulam (Slave) Dynasty** or **Mamluk Dynasty** (*Mamluk* = Arabic for owned; slave-trained military commander who became ruler).

### Key Rulers

| Ruler | Reign | Key Acts |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **Qutbuddin Aibak** | 1206–1210 | Founded sultanate; began Qutub Minar, Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque (Delhi's first mosque); titled "Lakh Baksh" (giver of lakhs — charity); died falling from horse playing chaugan (polo) |
| **Iltutmish** | 1211–1236 | **Greatest Slave Sultan**; consolidated sultanate; defeated Qubacha (Sindh) and Yalduz (Punjab); completed Qutub Minar; introduced **iqta system** (land revenue assignment); first Sultan to get investiture from Caliph of Baghdad; silver **tanka** and copper **jital** coins |
| **Razia Sultana** | 1236–1240 | First woman ruler; appointed Malik Yakut (Abyssinian) as lord of stables; deposed by "Forty" nobles (Chahalgani) |
| **Balban** | 1266–1287 | Real name Ulugh Khan; most powerful Slave Sultan; **introduced Persian court etiquette** (Sijda — prostration before king; Paibos — kissing feet); suppressed the "Forty" nobility; efficient spy system; repelled Mongol invasions; wrote Theory of Kingship |

**Iqta system**: revenue-collecting rights over land parcels assigned to officers (iqtadars) in lieu of salary. Not hereditary — officers rotated. An important administrative innovation.

**Chahalgani (The Forty)**: group of 40 powerful Turkish nobles who dominated Sultanate politics; broken up by Balban.

## Part 2 — Khalji Dynasty (1290–1320 CE)

The Khaljis were of mixed Afghan-Turkish origin, distinct from the Turkish Slave dynasty.

### Key Rulers

| Ruler | Reign | Key Acts |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **Jalaluddin Khalji** | 1290–1296 | Old, relatively mild ruler; murdered by nephew Alauddin |
| **Alauddin Khalji** | 1296–1316 | **Most powerful Delhi Sultan**; greatest military and administrative achievements |

### Alauddin Khalji — Key Reforms and Campaigns

**Military Campaigns:**
- Conquered Gujarat (1299); Rajput states: Ranthambore (1301), **Chittorgarh (1303)** (Padmavati legend), Malwa (1305), Jalor (1311)
- **South India**: sent general **Malik Kafur** on campaigns to Deccan; defeated Yadavas (Devagiri), Kakatiyas (Warangal), Hoysalas (Dwarasamudra), Pandyas (Madurai) — unprecedented northern reach into South India; brought vast treasure

**Administrative Reforms:**
1. **Market Control (Regulation of Prices)** — four markets in Delhi: grain, cloth, cattle and horses, general goods; fixed prices strictly enforced; efficient spy network; severe punishment for cheating
2. **Revenue Reform**: fixed land revenue at 50% of produce; abolished grants and intermediaries
3. **Standing army**: first Delhi Sultan to maintain a large paid standing army; **dahsala** (decimal) system of army classification
4. **Intelligence system**: barids (spies) and munhiyan (secret intelligence agents)
5. **Banned alcohol** in Delhi
6. **Forbidden nobles to socialise** without permission — to prevent conspiracies

## Part 3 — Tughlaq Dynasty (1320–1414 CE)

### Key Rulers

| Ruler | Reign | Key Acts |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq** | 1320–1325 | Founded dynasty; efficient but died (killed by his son?) in a building collapse |
| **Muhammad bin Tughlaq** | 1325–1351 | **Most controversial Sultan**; brilliant but erratic; five famous experiments |
| **Firuz Shah Tughlaq** | 1351–1388 | Benevolent but weak; extensive public works; forbade torture |

### Muhammad bin Tughlaq's Five "Mad" Experiments

| Scheme | Plan | Why It Failed |
|--------|------|--------------|
| **1. Transfer of Capital** | Moved capital from Delhi to Devagiri (Daulatabad, Maharashtra, ~1327) | 1,500 km march in summer; many died; Devagiri too distant to control north; capital moved back |
| **2. Token Currency** | Issued copper and brass coins as tokens for silver — currency debasement (~1329) | No effective safeguards; forgers flooded the market; withdrawn at enormous loss |
| **3. Khorasan Expedition** | Planned conquest of Central Asia via Khorasan (~1328) | Abandoned; army assembled for months but expedition never launched; enormous wastage |
| **4. Qarachil Expedition** | Sent army to conquer Chinese territory via Himalayas (~1337) | Army destroyed by difficult terrain and enemy; near-total disaster |
| **5. Taxation in Doab** | Increased taxes in the Ganga-Yamuna Doab | Famine hit the region simultaneously; revolts; Sultan was inflexible; region ruined |

**Muhammad bin Tughlaq's paradox**: Hieun Tsang called him "a king to whom the pen would refuse to do justice." He was highly educated, polyglot, interested in philosophy and science, and personally kind — yet his policies were catastrophic. Ibn Battuta (Moroccan traveller, 1333–1342 CE) visited his court and left a detailed account.

### Firuz Shah Tughlaq (1351–1388)
- **Benevolent**: freed slaves (~180,000); built hospitals; forbade torture; remitted many taxes
- **Public works**: 300 towns, 40 mosques, 30 caravanserais; Feroz Shah Kotla (Delhi); dug 5 canals including the Western Yamuna Canal (predecessor of modern canals)
- **Brought Ashokan pillars** from Topra (Haryana) and Meerut to Delhi — transported by rivers on boats — a remarkable engineering feat
- **Weak administration**: army discipline collapsed; provincial governors became semi-independent
- **Timur's invasion (1398)**: Mongol/Turkic conqueror Timur Lang (Tamerlane) invaded India, sacked Delhi, massacred thousands, left with enormous plunder — Delhi did not recover for decades

## Part 4 — Sayyid Dynasty (1414–1451 CE)

- **Founded by**: Khizr Khan — claimed descent from Prophet Muhammad (Sayyid = descendant of Prophet)
- Appointed by Timur as governor of Punjab; later claimed throne of Delhi
- Nominal power; controlled only Delhi and surrounding area
- Four rulers; replaced by Lodis in 1451 CE

---

## Part 5 — Lodi Dynasty (1451–1526 CE)

Last dynasty of the Delhi Sultanate — first Afghan (not Turkish) rulers of Delhi.

| Ruler | Reign | Key Acts |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **Bahlul Lodi** | 1451–1489 | Founded dynasty; unified north India somewhat; treated nobles as brothers (less autocratic) |
| **Sikandar Lodi** | 1489–1517 | Greatest Lodi Sultan; founded **Agra** (1504); efficient administration; blind in one eye; patronised Persian literature; moved capital to Agra |
| **Ibrahim Lodi** | 1517–1526 | Autocratic; alienated nobles; Daulat Khan Lodi (governor of Punjab) invited Babur — leading to his DEFEAT at First Battle of Panipat (1526) → end of Delhi Sultanate |

## Part 6 — Delhi Sultanate: Administration and Culture

### Key Administrative Features
- **Sultan** = absolute ruler; in theory governed by Islamic law (Sharia); in practice, pragmatic
- **Wazir** (prime minister): most important officer; ran day-to-day administration
- **Diwan-i-Wizarat**: revenue department
- **Diwan-i-Arz**: military department (Ariz-i-Mumalik = military officer)
- **Diwan-i-Insha**: correspondence department
- **Diwan-i-Risalat**: foreign affairs
- **Sadr-us-Sadur**: religious affairs/charity

### Architecture
| Monument | Sultan | Style |
|----------|--------|-------|
| **Qutub Minar** | Started Aibak; completed Iltutmish | 72.5 m; 5 storeys; UNESCO; first minaret in India |
| **Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque** | Aibak | First mosque in India; built using demolished Hindu/Jain temple material |
| **Alai Darwaza** | Alauddin Khalji | Gateway to Qutub complex; first true arch in India |
| **Tughlaqabad Fort** | Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq | Delhi's third fort |
| **Feroz Shah Kotla** | Firuz Shah Tughlaq | Contains Ashokan pillar |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **"Lakh Baksh"** = Qutbuddin Aibak (for his generosity in giving lakhs in charity). He died playing polo (chaugan). He is buried in Lahore (Pakistan) — not Delhi.

2. **Iltutmish** completed the Qutub Minar (Aibak only began the first storey). Iltutmish introduced the **silver tanka** and **copper jital** — the first systematic coinage of the Delhi Sultanate. He also introduced the **iqta system**.

3. **Razia Sultana (1236–1240)**: first and only woman Sultan of Delhi. Iltutmish publicly named her successor. She was deposed by the Chahalgani nobles and killed by them (some accounts: killed by Altuniya to whom she was married, then both killed by revolting nobles).

4. **Balban's "Theory of Kingship"**: king is God's shadow on earth (Zill-i-Ilahi); must inspire awe and fear; no mixing with nobles. He introduced **sijda** (prostration) and **paibos** (kissing feet) — Persian court ceremonials. He also ran an extensive spy network.

5. **Alauddin Khalji's four markets** were a unique experiment in state price control. The grain market was most important (fixed prices for wheat, barley, rice). He had **barids** (spies) who reported any price violations; punishments were severe (cutting off body parts).

6. **Malik Kafur** = Alauddin's general for South Indian campaigns. He was a slave (bought for 1,000 dinars — hence "Hazarddinari"). He conquered Deccan kingdoms and brought enormous wealth north. After Alauddin's death, Malik Kafur briefly controlled the state.

7. **Muhammad bin Tughlaq's capital transfer** (Delhi to Devagiri/Daulatabad, ~1327) was not random — Devagiri was more central geographically and could control both north and south. But the logistics and scale were disastrous. People who refused to move were forcibly marched; many died.

8. **Muhammad bin Tughlaq's token currency** (1329): copper/brass coins with the value of silver. The principle is sound (used in modern currency) but failed because the state couldn't prevent forgery and didn't control the supply properly.

9. **Ibn Battuta** (1333–1342): Moroccan traveller; visited Muhammad bin Tughlaq's court; wrote *Rihla* ("The Travels") — the most extensive account of 14th-century Delhi; described the Sultan's contradictory nature vividly.

10. **Timur's invasion of 1398**: Mongol-Turkic conqueror from Samarkand; crossed the Indus in September 1398; sacked Delhi in December 1398; left a trail of massacre; departed with enormous plunder. Delhi was so devastated it "remained without a king" for some time (Ziauddin Barani's account). The Tughlaq dynasty never recovered.

11. **Firuz Shah Tughlaq brought Ashokan pillars** to Delhi — from Topra (Haryana) and Meerut. The pillar from Topra stands in Feroz Shah Kotla. This was transported by river on specially built boats — a remarkable logistics feat. He was interested in archaeology before that was a concept.

12. **Sikandar Lodi founded Agra** in 1504 CE (before the Mughals, who later made it famous). He moved his capital from Delhi to Agra. He also introduced the **gaz-i-Sikandari** (a new measurement standard) and crushed non-Muslims' religious practices.

13. **The Delhi Sultanate lasted 320 years** (1206–1526) — ended by the First Battle of Panipat (1526) when Babur defeated Ibrahim Lodi.

14. **Qutub Minar** (72.5 m, 5 storeys): first two storeys by Aibak, third by Iltutmish, last two by Firuz Shah Tughlaq. UNESCO World Heritage Site. The **Quwwat-ul-Islam Mosque** adjacent to it used 27 demolished Hindu/Jain temples' material — columns are visible with temple inscriptions.

15. **The "Alai Darwaza" (1311)** by Alauddin Khalji: the gateway to the Qutub complex; first structure in India to use the true arch and true dome in a fully developed form. This distinguishes it from earlier "corbelled" structures.

16. **Diwan-i-Arz** = military department (NOT revenue). **Diwan-i-Wizarat** = revenue and finance. **Diwan-i-Insha** = correspondence/state letters. **Diwan-i-Risalat** = religious affairs/foreign. These four are the key departments tested in SSC — and Diwan-i-Arz (military) vs Diwan-i-Wizarat (revenue) is the most commonly confused pair.
