# Later Mughals — Jahangir, Shah Jahan & Aurangzeb

## The Marble Dream

On a June morning in 1631, Arjumand Banu Begum — the wife of Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan, known to the world as **Mumtaz Mahal** (Jewel of the Palace) — died in Burhanpur while giving birth to her 14th child. She was 38 years old. Shah Jahan was devastated. He went into mourning for two years, refused to wear jewellery or fine clothes, and reportedly aged visibly. Out of his grief, he conceived a tomb that would make the whole world weep.

The **Taj Mahal** — completed in 1653, after 22 years of construction, with 20,000 workers, marble brought from Makrana (Rajasthan) and inlaid with 28 types of precious and semi-precious stones — is the world's most famous building. It is called **"a teardrop on the cheek of time"** (Rabindranath Tagore).

But the man who built it would spend his last years imprisoned in the Agra Fort by his own son, looking at the Taj Mahal across the Yamuna River until he died.

## Part 1 — Jahangir (1605–1627 CE)

- **Full name**: Nuruddin Muhammad Jahangir; Salim before accession; son of Akbar and Harkha Bai (Jodha Bai)
- **Meaning of name**: "Conqueror of the World" (*jahan* = world, *gir* = conqueror)

### Key Events

**Rebellions at the Start:**
- Prince Khurram (later Shah Jahan) and Prince Khusrau (elder son) both rebelled at different times; Khusrau rebelled immediately after Akbar's death (1606) — captured, blinded on Jahangir's orders

**Guru Arjan Dev's Execution (1606):**
- The 5th Sikh Guru was executed by Jahangir — first Sikh martyr; reportedly associated with his blessing of rebel Khusrau; had a profound impact on Sikhism, leading to its increasing militarisation under Guru Hargobind (6th Guru)

**Nur Jahan (Mehr-un-Nissa):**
- Married Jahangir in 1611; became the most powerful woman in Mughal history
- **Real name**: Mehr-un-Nissa; widow of Sher Afghan (a Persian noble)
- Effectively **co-ruled** the empire — her name appeared on imperial farmans (orders), she sat behind a screen at court proceedings, coins were struck in her name alongside Jahangir's
- Her family (*Nur Jahan Junta*): her father **Ghiyas Beg** was given the title **Itimad-ud-Daula** (Pillar of the State); her brother **Asaf Khan** was another powerful figure
- Nur Jahan's niece **Arjumand Banu Begum** (Mumtaz Mahal) married Prince Khurram/Shah Jahan — through her matchmaking

**Itimad-ud-Daula's Tomb (Agra, ~1622–1628):**
- Built by Nur Jahan for her father; the first Mughal building entirely made of marble (instead of sandstone); first use of *pietra dura* (inlaid stone work/parchin kari) — the technique later perfected in the Taj Mahal; called the "Baby Taj"

### European Missions Under Jahangir
- **Captain William Hawkins (1608)**: first English envoy; met Jahangir; was received well initially but Portuguese opposition meant no trade agreement
- **Sir Thomas Roe (1615–1618)**: English ambassador from King James I; obtained a *firman* (royal order) allowing the East India Company to trade at Surat — the foundation of British commercial presence in India

### Jahangir's Memoirs
- **Tuzuk-i-Jahangiri** (Jahangirnama): Jahangir's autobiography — written in Persian; describes nature, art, his love of painting, drinking, and opium; the famous "Chain of Justice" story (a golden chain with 60 bells hung from the fort to Yamuna — anyone could pull it for imperial justice)

### Jahangir and Painting
- The greatest patron of **Mughal miniature painting** among all emperors
- Employed masters like **Ustad Mansur** (nature paintings — "Wonders of Nature"), **Abul Hasan** (Nadir-ul-Zaman)
- Jahangir could allegedly identify a painting's artist from a single face in it

## Part 2 — Shah Jahan (1628–1658 CE)

- **Full name**: Shahab-ud-din Muhammad Khurram Shah Jahan; son of Jahangir and Jagat Gosain (Man Bai)
- **Meaning of name**: "King of the World"
- **Capital**: Agra, later **Shahjahanabad** (Old Delhi)

### Construction — Shah Jahan's Legacy

| Monument | Location | Year | Notes |
|----------|---------|------|-------|
| **Taj Mahal** | Agra (UP) | 1632–1653 | Tomb of Mumtaz Mahal; white Makrana marble; 28 types of precious stones; UNESCO; architect: Ustad Ahmad Lahori (chief architect); designed by a team including Ismail Afandi (dome) |
| **Red Fort (Lal Qila)** | Delhi | 1639–1648 | Capital shifted here; sandstone + marble; Diwan-i-Aam, Diwan-i-Khas, Rang Mahal, Moti Masjid inside; UNESCO |
| **Jama Masjid** | Delhi | 1644–1656 | Largest mosque in India; can hold 25,000 worshippers; red sandstone + marble |
| **Shalimar Bagh** | Lahore (now Pakistan) | 1641 | Magnificent Mughal garden |
| **Moti Masjid** | Agra Fort | 1654 | Pure white marble; "Pearl Mosque" |
| **Peacock Throne (Takht-i-Taus)** | Delhi | ~1635 | Jewel-encrusted; removed by Nadir Shah (Persia) in 1739; lost to India forever |

### Reign Events
- **Deccan Wars**: Shah Jahan's primary military preoccupation; fought Ahmadnagar (finally conquered 1637, absorbing it into the empire); wars with Bijapur and Golconda were less conclusive
- **Mughal-Safavid War**: Shah Jahan tried to reconquer Kandahar (Safavid-controlled) multiple times — failed all three times; Kandahar was a recurring obsession of the Mughals
- **War of Succession (1657–1658)**: Shah Jahan fell seriously ill (1657); his four sons — Dara Shikoh, Shah Shuja, Murad Bakhsh, and Aurangzeb — fought each other; **Aurangzeb** won, imprisoned Shah Jahan in **Agra Fort** (1658)

### Shah Jahan's Last Years
- Imprisoned by Aurangzeb from 1658 until his death in 1666
- Spent 8 years in the Agra Fort, reportedly looking at the Taj Mahal across the Yamuna
- His daughter **Jahanara Begum** (Dara Shikoh's sister) voluntarily stayed with him in captivity

### Dara Shikoh (1615–1659)
- Shah Jahan's favourite son and chosen successor; brilliant scholar
- Translated 50 Upanishads into Persian (*Sirr-i-Akbar* — "The Great Secret")
- Wrote *Majma-ul-Bahrayn* ("The Mingling of Two Oceans" — comparing Sufism and Vedanta)
- Captured by Aurangzeb and executed (1659) — charged with apostasy
- His translations of Upanishads were later translated into Latin and reached European philosophers

## Part 3 — Aurangzeb (1658–1707 CE)

- **Full name**: Muhi-ud-din Muhammad Aurangzeb Alamgir
- **Meaning**: Alamgir = "Conqueror of the Universe"
- **Reign**: 1658–1707 CE (49 years — tied with Akbar for longest Mughal reign)
- Most controversial Mughal emperor — a complex figure who has been both vilified and defended

### Aurangzeb's Rise to Power
- Won the War of Succession through military skill and political cunning
- Defeated his brothers: **Battle of Samugarh (1658)**: defeated Dara Shikoh; **Battle of Khajwa (1659)**: defeated Shah Shuja; had Murad Bakhsh imprisoned and executed
- Imprisoned Shah Jahan; crowned himself Emperor

### Religious Policy — More Orthodox than Predecessors
- **Reimposed Jizyah (1679)**: poll tax on non-Muslims — abolished by Akbar in 1562; reimposition caused widespread resentment
- **Destroyed temples**: ordered destruction of Hindu temples in various campaigns; most notably in Mathura and Varanasi; built Gyanvapi Mosque at the site of Kashi Vishwanath Temple (Varanasi) and Shahi Idgah at the Keshav Dev Temple site (Mathura)
- **Banned music** from the Mughal court; dismissed court musicians (though music continued elsewhere)
- **Banned alcohol** (enforced more strictly than predecessors)
- Wrote the **Fatawa-e-Alamgiri** — a compilation of Islamic law (Hanafi school) for governance; the largest Islamic legal compilation in history at the time

### Sikh Persecution
- Executed **Guru Tegh Bahadur** (9th Sikh Guru) in 1675 CE in Delhi — refusing to convert to Islam; he died to protect Kashmiri Brahmins who had approached him (according to Sikh tradition)
- This martyrdom led his son **Guru Gobind Singh** (10th Guru) to transform the Sikh community into the **Khalsa** (1699 CE) — the saint-soldier community
- Executed **Guru Gobind Singh's two younger sons** (Sahibzade Zorawar Singh and Fateh Singh, aged 8 and 5 — bricked alive at Fatehgarh Sahib on the orders of Wazir Khan of Sirhind)

### Aurangzeb's Deccan Campaigns (1681–1707)
- **Moved court to Deccan** (Aurangabad) for the last 25 years of his reign
- Fought Bijapur (conquered 1686) and Golconda (conquered 1687) — finally absorbed these two major Deccan Sultanates into the Mughal Empire
- **Maratha Wars**: initially captured Shivaji's son **Sambhaji** (executed 1689); captured many Maratha forts; but the Maratha guerrilla resistance (especially under Rajaram and later Tarabai) could not be crushed
- The Deccan campaigns were a catastrophic drain: 500,000 soldiers in the field; enormous cost; 27 years of continuous warfare; still the Marathas survived and expanded

### Decline of the Mughal Empire
Aurangzeb's policies directly caused the decline:
1. **Religious policy**: alienated Hindus (comprising 80% of the population), Rajputs, Marathas, and Sikhs — all of whom had been co-opted under Akbar-Jahangir-Shah Jahan
2. **Financial exhaustion**: Deccan campaigns bankrupted the treasury
3. **Overextension**: territory too large to administer; provincial governors became independent
4. **No strong successor**: after Aurangzeb's death (1707), his sons fought a succession war; in 18th century, Mughal emperors became puppets of nobles, then Marathas, then British

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Nur Jahan's real name** was **Mehr-un-Nissa**. She was given the title Nur Jahan (Light of the World) after marrying Jahangir in 1611. Her father Ghiyas Beg was given the title **Itimad-ud-Daula**. **Coins were struck in her name** — unprecedented for a Mughal woman.

2. **Itimad-ud-Daula's Tomb (Agra)** = first fully marble Mughal building; first use of *pietra dura* (inlaid stonework); built by Nur Jahan for her father (~1622–1628). Called the "Baby Taj." Do not confuse with the Taj Mahal (built by Shah Jahan for Mumtaz, 1632–1653).

3. **Sir Thomas Roe (1615–1618)** = English ambassador from James I who obtained the first formal trade *firman* from Jahangir for the East India Company — basis of British commercial presence in India. **William Hawkins (1608)** came earlier but got no formal treaty.

4. **Tuzuk-i-Jahangiri** = Jahangir's autobiography (Jahangirnama). He was the Mughal emperor most interested in art and nature — described plants, animals, and paintings in detail. He said Ustad Mansur could depict animals so realistically they seemed alive.

5. **Taj Mahal facts**: built 1632–1653; tomb of Mumtaz Mahal; chief architect **Ustad Ahmad Lahori**; white Makrana marble (Rajasthan); inlaid with 28 types of precious/semi-precious stones (*pietra dura* = *parchin kari*); 20,000 workers; 4 minarets each 41.6 m tall; the main dome is 58 m high. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

6. **Red Fort (Shahjahanabad/Delhi)**: built by Shah Jahan (1638–1648); red sandstone; his capital after he shifted from Agra; inside: Diwan-i-Aam (public audience), Diwan-i-Khas (private audience; "If there is paradise on earth, it is this, it is this" — *agar firdaus bar ru-ye zamin ast, hamin ast-o hamin ast-o hamin ast*), Moti Masjid, Rang Mahal. Independence Day speech by PM delivered from here annually.

7. **Peacock Throne (Takht-i-Taus)**: made by Shah Jahan (~1635); encrusted with rubies, emeralds, diamonds; looted by **Nadir Shah of Persia** during his invasion of India in 1739. India never got it back. It is now lost (pieces incorporated into other thrones).

8. **War of Succession (1657–58)**: Shah Jahan fell ill; 4 sons fought: Dara Shikoh (eldest, favourite), Shah Shuja (Bengal governor), Murad Bakhsh (Gujarat), Aurangzeb (Deccan). Aurangzeb won; imprisoned Shah Jahan in Agra Fort; had Dara Shikoh executed (1659) on charges of apostasy.

9. **Dara Shikoh** translated 50 Upanishads into Persian (*Sirr-i-Akbar*). His work was later translated into Latin by Anquetil-Duperron (1802) and reached Schopenhauer and other European philosophers — so Dara Shikoh's translations are partly responsible for Indian philosophy's influence on European Romanticism.

10. **Guru Tegh Bahadur (9th Sikh Guru)** was executed by Aurangzeb in 1675 in Delhi (Chandni Chowk) — **Gurudwara Sis Ganj Sahib** marks the spot. He refused to convert to Islam; Aurangzeb had him beheaded. This led his son Guru Gobind Singh to found the **Khalsa** in 1699 at Anandpur Sahib.

11. **Khalsa (1699)**: founded by Guru Gobind Singh; the initiated Sikh community; the Five Ks (*Panj Kakars*): Kesh (uncut hair), Kara (steel bracelet), Kachera (cotton undergarment), Kanga (wooden comb), Kirpan (steel sword). Transformed Sikhism from a devotional to a saint-soldier tradition.

12. **Aurangzeb reimposed Jizyah in 1679** — 117 years after Akbar abolished it. This is one of the most tested facts about Aurangzeb in SSC. It sparked the Jat revolt (Gokula's revolt near Mathura, 1669) and Rajput revolt (Mewar and Marwar).

13. **Fatawa-e-Alamgiri**: compiled under Aurangzeb's direction; a comprehensive compilation of Hanafi Islamic law (*fiqh*); largest Islamic legal compilation of its time; used for governance of the Mughal Empire. It confirmed Aurangzeb's orthodox Islamic approach.

14. **Bijapur and Golconda** were the last two of the Five Deccan Sultanates to fall. Aurangzeb conquered **Bijapur (1686)** and **Golconda (1687)** — the two most powerful; the others had already been absorbed. This briefly made the Mughal Empire the largest in Indian history.

15. **Jama Masjid (Delhi)**: built by Shah Jahan (1644–1656); largest mosque in India (holds 25,000 worshippers); red sandstone and white marble; three domes; 40-metre minarets; located on a hillock in Old Delhi (Shahjahanabad); India's most visited mosque.

16. **Mughal decline after Aurangzeb (1707 onwards)**: after his death, 12 emperors in 50 years (1707–1757); Nadir Shah sacked Delhi (1739) and took the Peacock Throne and Kohinoor; Ahmad Shah Abdali's raids (Panipat III, 1761 — defeated the Marathas); British East India Company exploited the chaos; last Mughal Bahadur Shah Zafar exiled to Rangoon after 1857. The empire had become a ghost.
