# Sources of Ancient History, Prehistoric Cultures & Indus Valley Civilisation

## The Day Harappa Shocked the World

In 1921, a young Indian archaeologist named Daya Ram Sahni was excavating a mound near a small town in Punjab (now Pakistan) called Harappa. What he expected was a Buddhist stupa. What he found was something far older — baked bricks of extraordinary precision, a sophisticated drainage system, and a planned city that predated anything previously known from the Indian subcontinent by two thousand years. The following year, R.D. Banerji found the same at Mohenjo-Daro, 640 km away. Sir John Marshall, Director General of the Archaeological Survey of India, announced the discovery to the world in 1924: India had a civilisation as old as Egypt and Mesopotamia — and nobody had known it existed.

That discovery shattered the colonial assumption that India's history began with the Aryan migrations and the Vedas. It pushed the story of organised urban life in India back to 3300 BCE — before the Pyramids, before the earliest Chinese cities. History had to be rewritten.

## Part 1 — Sources of Ancient Indian History

Historians reconstruct the past from three broad categories of sources:

### 1. Archaeological Sources
- **Material remains**: pottery, tools, weapons, ornaments, coins, buildings
- **Inscriptions (Epigraphy)**: stone pillars, copper plates, cave walls — Ashoka's edicts are the most famous
- **Numismatics (Coins)**: coins reveal rulers, dates, religions, trade networks. Punch-marked coins are the oldest (6th century BCE)
- **Monuments and architecture**: temples, forts, stupas

### 2. Literary Sources

**Indigenous Literature:**

| Text | Period | Significance |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **Vedas** (Rigveda, Samaveda, Yajurveda, Atharvaveda) | 1500–1000 BCE | Oldest literature; social/religious life |
| **Upanishads** | 800–500 BCE | Philosophy; Brahman-Atman concept |
| **Epics**: Ramayana (Valmiki), Mahabharata (Vyasa) | 400 BCE–400 CE | Society, geography, values |
| **Puranas** (18 major) | Variable | Genealogies, legends, cosmology |
| **Arthashastra** (Kautilya/Chanakya) | ~300 BCE | Administration, economy, statecraft |
| **Indica** (Megasthenes) | ~300 BCE | Description of Mauryan India |
| **Mudrarakshasa** (Vishakhadatta) | ~400 CE | Chandragupta Maurya's rise to power |
| **Harshacharita** (Banabhatta) | ~630 CE | Harsha's biography |

**Foreign Accounts:**

| Source | Period | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| **Herodotus** (Greek) | 5th century BCE | "Father of History"; mentions India |
| **Megasthenes** (Greek ambassador) | ~302–298 BCE | *Indica* — account of Chandragupta's court |
| **Fa-Hien** (Chinese Buddhist pilgrim) | 399–414 CE | Visited during Gupta period (Chandragupta II) |
| **Hieun Tsang** (Chinese) | 630–645 CE | Detailed account of Harsha's India |
| **Al-Beruni** (Arab scholar) | ~1017–1030 CE | *Kitab-ul-Hind* — came with Mahmud of Ghazni |
| **Ibn Battuta** (Moroccan) | 1333–1342 CE | Visited during Muhammad bin Tughlaq's reign |
| **Abdur Razzaq** (Persian) | 1443 CE | Description of Vijayanagara (Hampi) |

### 3. Inscriptions (Important Examples)

| Inscription | Who/Where | Significance |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Hathigumpha Inscription** | Kharavela of Kalinga (Udayagiri, Odisha) | Non-Ashokan; describes military campaigns |
| **Allahabad Pillar Inscription** | Samudragupta | "Indian Napoleon"; composed by Harishena |
| **Aihole Inscription** | Pulakesi II (Chalukya) | Victory over Harsha; composed by Ravikirti |
| **Junagarh Inscription** | Rudradaman I (Saka) | First inscription in polished Sanskrit |

## Part 2 — Prehistoric Cultures of India

Human habitation in India spans over a million years. Prehistory is divided by the type of tools used:

| Age | Period | Key Features | Indian Sites |
|-----|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Lower Palaeolithic** | 5,00,000–1,00,000 BCE | Hand axes, cleavers; no pottery | Soan Valley, Hunsgi (Karnataka) |
| **Middle Palaeolithic** | 1,00,000–40,000 BCE | Flakes, scrapers | Narmada Valley |
| **Upper Palaeolithic** | 40,000–10,000 BCE | Blades, points; cave paintings begin | Bhimbetka (MP) |
| **Mesolithic** | 10,000–4000 BCE | Microliths; hunter-gatherers to pastoralists | Bagor (Rajasthan), Adamgarh (MP) |
| **Neolithic** | 4000–1000 BCE | Polished stone tools; farming begins; pottery | Mehrgarh (Pakistan), Burzahom (Kashmir) |
| **Chalcolithic** | 2000–700 BCE | Copper + stone tools; pre-Harappan cultures | Inamgaon (Maharashtra), Navdatoli |
| **Iron Age** | 1000–600 BCE | Iron tools; Painted Grey Ware (PGW) | Painted Grey Ware sites in Ganga plains |

**Bhimbetka Cave Paintings** (near Bhopal, Madhya Pradesh): UNESCO World Heritage Site. Upper Palaeolithic cave art; paintings of animals, hunting scenes — among the oldest in the world (~30,000 years).

**Mehrgarh** (Balochistan, Pakistan): One of the earliest farming settlements in South Asia (~7000 BCE). Evidence of wheat/barley cultivation and cattle domestication.

## Part 3 — Indus Valley Civilisation (Harappan Civilisation)

### Timeline and Extent

- **Period**: ~3300–1300 BCE; **Mature Phase**: ~2600–1900 BCE
- **Area**: ~1.3 million km² — larger than Egypt + Mesopotamia combined; from Afghanistan to Gujarat, Punjab to UP
- **Named after**: Indus (Sindhu) river system; also called Harappan Civilisation (after first site excavated)

### Major Sites

| Site | Modern Location | Notable Feature |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| **Harappa** | Punjab, Pakistan | First excavated (Sahni, 1921); two rows of 6 granaries |
| **Mohenjo-Daro** | Sindh, Pakistan | "Mound of the Dead"; Great Bath; Priest-King statue |
| **Kalibangan** | Rajasthan, India | Evidence of earliest ploughed field; fire altars |
| **Lothal** | Gujarat, India | Only dockyard; bead factory; rice cultivation |
| **Dholavira** | Gujarat (Rann of Kutch) | Largest Indian site; unique water conservation system; signboard |
| **Banawali** | Haryana, India | Barley cultivation; lapis lazuli beads |
| **Rakhigarhi** | Haryana, India | Largest Harappan site overall (larger than Mohenjo-Daro) |
| **Surkotada** | Gujarat, India | Remains of a horse; fortified citadel |
| **Chanhudaro** | Sindh, Pakistan | Only site without a citadel; ink pot found; bead-making |

### Town Planning — The Greatest Achievement

The Harappans built the world's first planned cities. Key features:

1. **Grid pattern**: streets at right angles; main streets up to 10 m wide
2. **Two-part city**: **Citadel** (raised; elite/official; western side) + **Lower Town** (residential; eastern side)
3. **Drainage system**: covered brick drains along every street; manholes for cleaning — more advanced than contemporary Egyptian or Mesopotamian cities
4. **Standardised bricks**: ratio 1:2:4 (thickness:width:length) used across all sites — suggests a central authority
5. **Multi-storey buildings**: evidence at Mohenjo-Daro; staircases to upper floors
6. **Great Bath (Mohenjo-Daro)**: 12 × 7 × 2.4 metres; waterproofed with bitumen; likely ritual bathing

### Economy and Trade

- **Agriculture**: wheat, barley (staples); rice (Lothal); cotton (world's first; they called it "sindon" in Greek from "Sindhu"); dates, peas, sesame
- **Animals**: humped bull (zebu), buffalo, sheep, goat, elephant, dog, cat — horses NOT reliably established
- **Weights & Measures**: standardised binary system (1, 2, 4, 8, 16...); cuboid weights; no cheating in trade
- **Trade**: external trade with Mesopotamia (called Meluhha in Sumerian texts), Oman (Magan), Bahrain (Dilmun); imported copper from Rajasthan/Oman, lapis lazuli from Afghanistan, gold from Karnataka
- **Craft**: bronze (Lost-wax technique), beads (carnelian, lapis lazuli), pottery (wheel-made, black-on-red), terracotta figurines, seals

### Seals and Script

- Over **2,000 seals** found; mostly soapstone (steatite); used for trade/administration
- **Unicorn** is the most common motif on seals
- **Indus Script**: ~400–700 signs; pictographic; **NOT YET DECIPHERED** (written right-to-left, boustrophedon in some cases)
- **Pashupati Seal**: man seated in yogic position surrounded by animals (proto-Shiva? still debated)

### Society and Religion

- **No clear evidence of a king** — possibly priest-rulers or a council; no large palaces or royal burials
- **Evidence of trade guilds** and merchants
- **Female figurines** (Mother Goddess worship?); **pipal tree** worshipped
- **Fire altars** found at Kalibangan and Lothal
- **Burials**: extended burials in brick-lined graves; grave goods placed inside; later cremation

### Decline (c. 1900–1300 BCE)

Multiple theories — no single agreed cause:
1. **Climate change / aridification**: Ghaggar-Hakra river (possibly Rigvedic Sarasvati) dried up
2. **Flood**: geological evidence of flooding at Mohenjo-Daro
3. **Aryan invasion theory** (now largely rejected): suggested by Mortimer Wheeler; V. Gordon Childe
4. **Tectonic activity**: earthquakes, shifting rivers
5. **Epidemics**: skeletal evidence at Mohenjo-Daro of unburied bodies
6. **Internal decline**: trade network breakdown, economic collapse

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Harappa excavated by Daya Ram Sahni (1921); Mohenjo-Daro by R.D. Banerji (1922)**. Sir John Marshall announced the discovery in 1924. Do not confuse the two excavators or dates.

2. **Lothal (Gujarat) is the only Harappan site with a dockyard** — evidence of maritime trade. It also has evidence of rice cultivation and a bead factory. Lothal = the most frequently tested Harappan site in SSC after Mohenjo-Daro.

3. **Mohenjo-Daro means "Mound of the Dead"** (Sindhi). It had the famous Great Bath, the Priest-King bronze statue, and the Dancing Girl bronze figurine. Located in Sindh (now Pakistan).

4. **The Indus Valley Script has NOT been deciphered** — it remains one of the world's undeciphered scripts. Do not confuse it with other ancient scripts.

5. **Unicorn is the most common motif on Harappan seals** — appears on more seals than any other animal or figure.

6. **Rakhigarhi (Haryana) is the LARGEST Harappan site** — larger than Mohenjo-Daro. Dholavira (Gujarat) is the largest within India and is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (inscribed 2021).

7. **Standardised brick ratio 1:2:4** throughout all Harappan sites — suggests central administrative control or a widely followed standard, even across sites 1,000 km apart.

8. **Cotton was first cultivated by Harappans** — Greeks called it "sindon" from Sindhu. The Harappans are credited with introducing the world to cotton textiles.

9. **Horses are NOT clearly established** in the Harappan Civilisation. Surkotada site has equid bones but the identification as domestic horse is debated. The horse is a key feature of the later Vedic culture, not the IVC.

10. **Kalibangan** (Rajasthan) has the world's earliest evidence of a ploughed field and also fire altars. One of the few sites in India (rather than Pakistan). Also unique: it was built on both banks of the Ghaggar river.

11. **Chanhudaro is the only major Harappan site WITHOUT a citadel**. It has evidence of ink pot, bead-making, and toy-cart manufacture.

12. **Fa-Hien** visited India during the Gupta period (Chandragupta II Vikramaditya, ~399–414 CE). **Hieun Tsang** visited during Harsha's reign (630–645 CE). **Al-Beruni** came with Mahmud of Ghazni (~1017 CE). Getting these Chinese travelers mixed up is a very common SSC error.

13. **Megasthenes** was the Greek ambassador to Chandragupta Maurya's court (~302 BCE). His work *Indica* is a primary source for Mauryan India. Do not confuse with Hieun Tsang or Fa-Hien.

14. **Bhimbetka caves** (MP) are Palaeolithic cave painting sites, not Harappan. They are UNESCO World Heritage Sites and contain among the world's oldest rock art (~30,000 years ago).

15. **Mehrgarh** (Balochistan, Pakistan) — one of the world's earliest settled communities (~7000 BCE); predates the Indus Valley Civilisation; shows early domestication of wheat/barley and cattle. Not strictly a Harappan site.

16. **Indus Valley trade partner Mesopotamia** called India "Meluhha" in Sumerian texts. They imported cotton, timber, ivory, lapis lazuli, and carnelian from the Harappans. This is the earliest documented international trade involving India.
