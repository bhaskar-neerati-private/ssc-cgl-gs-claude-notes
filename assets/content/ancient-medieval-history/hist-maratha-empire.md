# Maratha Empire — Shivaji, Peshwas & Panipat III

## The Trap at Pratapgarh

In November 1659, Bijapur's greatest general **Afzal Khan** — a veteran of countless campaigns, an enormous man physically — arranged to meet Shivaji for a peace conference in an open pavilion below the fort of Pratapgarh. The meeting was formally unarmed. But both men had hidden weapons.

When they embraced in greeting, Afzal Khan drove a dagger into Shivaji's back. The dagger caught Shivaji's armour — he had worn it under his clothes. In the same moment, Shivaji drove *bagh nakh* (tiger claws — a concealed iron weapon worn across the knuckles) into Afzal Khan's belly. The general staggered out. Shivaji had already given the signal: his men attacked the stunned Bijapur army camped below. Within hours, one of Bijapur's greatest armies was destroyed.

This episode captures everything about **Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj** — his intelligence, his audacity, his refusal to fight by rules that larger enemies set, and his understanding that a smaller kingdom's survival required a different kind of warfare. Over the next 20 years, he built the first major Maratha state from nothing.

## Part 1 — Shivaji (1627–1680 CE)

- **Full name**: Shivaji Bhonsle; born February 19, 1630 at Shivneri Fort (some sources say 1627) in Maharashtra
- **Father**: Shahaji Bhonsle (a Bijapur general); **Mother**: **Jijabai** (his most important influence — instilled Hindu pride and determination)
- **Guru**: **Samarth Ramdas Swami** — the saint-philosopher who gave spiritual guidance
- Spent early years at Pune with Jijabai and the trusted official **Dadoji Konddeo**

### Military Career and Rise

**Early Conquests (1645–1659):**
- Captured **Torna Fort** (1645) — age 15; first military action
- Captured **Kondana** and **Rajgad** — beginning to build a fortified Maratha territory
- **Surat (1664)**: sacked the wealthiest port city of the Mughals — enormous plunder; shook the Mughal Empire's confidence
- **Surat (1670)**: second sack

### Key Battles and Episodes

| Event | Year | Significance |
|-------|------|-------------|
| **Battle of Pratapgarh** | 1659 | Defeated and killed Afzal Khan (Bijapur general); reversed Bijapur threat |
| **Battle of Kolhapur** | 1659 | Defeated Bijapur army |
| **Sack of Surat** | 1664 | First sack; enormous wealth; Mughals under shock; English factory nearby paid him tribute |
| **Battle of Purandar** | 1665 | Fought with Mughal general **Mirza Raja Jai Singh** (Amber Rajput); Shivaji defeated; signed **Treaty of Purandar** — gave up 23 forts; agreed to go to Agra |
| **Agra Incident** | 1666 | Taken to Aurangzeb's court; humiliated (given low rank in *darbar*); placed under house arrest; **escaped hidden in baskets of sweets**; returned to Deccan |
| **Coronation** | June 6, 1674 | Crowned **Chhatrapati** at **Raigad Fort** (Maharashtra); legitimised as Hindu king; Sage Gaga Bhatta performed the ceremony; Samarth Ramdas present |
| **Death** | April 3, 1680 | Died at Raigad; cause of illness (some say fever); successor was his son Sambhaji |

### Shivaji's Military Innovations

1. **Guerrilla warfare (*Ganimi Kava*)**: hit-and-run tactics using knowledge of Deccan terrain; avoided pitched battles against Mughal strength
2. **Fortification**: built/renovated 300+ forts (hill forts in the Western Ghats); each fort was a self-sufficient military unit
3. **Navy**: built the first significant **Maratha navy**; used to protect the Konkan coast; forts like Sindhudurg (built 1664) and Vijaydurg protected sea routes — called "Father of Indian Navy" by some historians
4. **Cavalry**: fast, mobile cavalry used for rapid raids and retreats
5. **Intelligence network**: maintained spies (*huzurnat*) across enemy territory

### Shivaji's Administration

| Department/Office | Function |
|-------------------|---------|
| **Ashtapradhan** | Council of 8 ministers (unlike Mughal centralised model) |
| **Peshwa** | Prime minister (chief minister) |
| **Mazumdar** | Accountant General |
| **Dabir** | Foreign secretary |
| **Sar-i-Naubat (Senapati)** | Commander-in-Chief |
| **Nyayadhish** | Chief Justice |
| **Panditrao** | Head of religious affairs |

**Revenue**: land revenue collected directly by the state (not through intermediaries); fair assessment; abolished (*saranjam*) system of land grants to nobles

**Character and legacy**: Shivaji treated women — including women of enemies — with great respect; did not allow destruction of mosques; his soldiers disciplined; he is considered the founder of Maharashtra as a political concept

## Part 2 — After Shivaji: Peshwa Dominance (1689–1761)

### Sambhaji and Aurangzeb
- **Sambhaji** (son, 1680–1689): captured by Aurangzeb; publicly tortured and executed (1689) to break Maratha spirit — it had the opposite effect; made Sambhaji a martyr
- **Rajaram** (Shivaji's other son, 1689–1700): continued resistance from Jinji Fort (Tamil Nadu); Aurangzeb besieged Jinji for 8 years (1689–1698) but Rajaram escaped
- **Tarabai** (Rajaram's widow, regent 1700–1707): effectively led Maratha resistance; raided into Mughal territory as far as Malwa and Gujarat while Aurangzeb was alive

### Rise of the Peshwas

After Aurangzeb's death (1707), the Maratha Chhatrapati was based at Satara, but real power shifted to the **Peshwas** (prime ministers), based at **Pune**.

| Peshwa | Period | Key Achievement |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| **Balaji Vishwanath** | 1713–1720 | First effective Peshwa; consolidated power; helped Sayyid Brothers in Delhi politics — got the right to collect chauth and sardeshmukhi from Deccan |
| **Baji Rao I** | 1720–1740 | **Greatest Peshwa**; expanded Maratha empire north; never lost a battle; raided Delhi (1737); conquered Malwa, Gujarat, Bundelkhand |
| **Balaji Baji Rao (Nana Saheb)** | 1740–1761 | Further expansion; Marathas reached Bengal; lost Panipat III |
| **Madhav Rao I** | 1761–1772 | After Panipat disaster; brilliantly reconstructed Maratha power |

### Baji Rao I (1700–1740) — Greatest Peshwa
- Considered one of the greatest cavalry generals in Indian history; **never lost a battle**
- Expanded Maratha control over: Malwa (1723), Gujarat, Bundelkhand, parts of Rajputana
- **March on Delhi (1737)**: Baji Rao led a lightning cavalry march of 900+ km to the outskirts of Delhi — the Mughal Emperor Muhammad Shah was terrified; this demonstrated how weak the Mughal Empire had become
- Quote attributed to him: "Let us strike at the trunk of the withering tree, the branches will fall of themselves" — his argument for pushing north into Mughal territory
- Died young (1740) at age 39; some say of exhaustion and grief (his beloved Mastani, a Muslim princess from Bundelkhand, had been forcibly separated from him)

### Chauth and Sardeshmukhi (Maratha Revenue)
- **Chauth**: 1/4th of the land revenue of a territory collected by Marathas as "protection" — paid by territories not directly controlled
- **Sardeshmukhi**: additional 10% — as hereditary right of the Maratha Chhatrapati over the Deccan
- This system extended Maratha economic control over vast territories without governing them directly

## Part 3 — Third Battle of Panipat (January 14, 1761)

The most catastrophic single-day military defeat in Maratha — and arguably Indian — history.

### Background
- After Baji Rao I, the Marathas had effectively become the dominant power in India — the Mughal Emperor was their puppet
- **Vishalwas Rao** and **Sadashivrao Bhau** (the Peshwa's relative) commanded the Maratha army
- **Ahmad Shah Abdali (Durrani)**: Afghan king; had already raided India 7 times; the Rohillas (Afghan chiefs in UP) and **Shuja-ud-Daula** (Nawab of Awadh) allied with Abdali against the Marathas
- The Marathas had the **biggest army** in the field — estimates suggest 45,000–70,000 Maratha cavalry + large camp followers (including non-combatant families and pilgrims — a critical mistake)

### The Battle (January 14, 1761)
- After months of positional warfare near Panipat, both sides had been cut off from supply lines
- Maratha soldiers were starving; morale was low; camp followers were a liability
- On the day of battle, the Marathas fought brilliantly at first — but Abdali's reserves (a fresh body of cavalry held back) decided the outcome
- **Viswas Rao** (the young Peshwa heir) was shot early in battle; news spread panic
- **Sadashivrao Bhau** died on the battlefield
- Maratha losses: estimates of 28,000 combatants killed; total including civilians may have reached 100,000

### Significance
- "The rout was complete... every house in Pune went into mourning; there was not a family which had not lost a son, a husband, a father" — contemporary accounts
- Ended Maratha northward expansion
- **Did NOT end the Maratha Empire**: within a decade, under Peshwa **Madhav Rao I**, the Marathas had largely recovered; they remained India's dominant power until the **Anglo-Maratha Wars**

### Anglo-Maratha Wars
| War | Period | Outcome |
|-----|--------|---------|
| **First Anglo-Maratha War** | 1775–1782 | **Treaty of Salbai** (1782) — status quo; British failed to take Pune |
| **Second Anglo-Maratha War** | 1803–1805 | British gained Delhi and Agra; defeated Scindia and Bhonsle; Peshwa became a pensioner |
| **Third Anglo-Maratha War** | 1817–1818 | Peshwa Bajirao II defeated; Peshwaship abolished; British supremacy established over all of India |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Shivaji's coronation** as **Chhatrapati** took place on **June 6, 1674** at **Raigad Fort** (Maharashtra). The ceremony was performed by **Gaga Bhatt** (Gagabhatta), a Brahmin from Varanasi, who certified Shivaji's Kshatriya lineage. Samarth Ramdas, his guru, was present.

2. **Jijabai** = Shivaji's mother — the most important influence on his life; instilled pride in Hindu traditions and the idea of *Hindavi Swarajya* (self-rule of Hindus). She died just 12 days after his coronation in 1674.

3. **Samarth Ramdas Swami** = Shivaji's spiritual guru; author of *Dasbodha* (a philosophical text in Marathi). He founded a network of temples/monasteries across Maharashtra that supported Maratha identity and resistance.

4. **Ashtapradhan**: Shivaji's council of 8 ministers. The **Peshwa** (prime minister) was the most important. After Shivaji, the Peshwa office grew so powerful that they became the de facto rulers of the Maratha Empire while the Chhatrapati remained nominal.

5. **Agra escape (1666)**: Shivaji and his son Sambhaji were taken to Aurangzeb's court; humiliated with a low rank; placed under house arrest; **escaped hidden in large fruit baskets** (or sweet-baskets — versions vary) with his son; made their way back to Maharashtra. This episode is one of the most popular stories of Maratha legend.

6. **Battle of Pratapgarh (1659)**: Shivaji killed **Afzal Khan** using tiger claws (*bagh nakh*) during a peace meeting that both sides had prepared as a trap. The name Afzal Khan refers to a Bijapur general; do not confuse with figures from other conflicts.

7. **Treaty of Purandar (1665)**: between Shivaji and Mughal general **Mirza Raja Jai Singh** (of Amber). Shivaji surrendered 23 forts and agreed to serve the Mughals. In return, he retained 12 forts. He later repudiated this treaty after the Agra episode.

8. **Maratha Navy**: Shivaji built the first significant Indian navy for coastal defense in the Konkan; he is called "Father of the Indian Navy" by Maharashtra tradition. **Sindhudurg Fort** (1664, Malvan coast) is the most famous Maratha sea fort.

9. **Chauth and Sardeshmukhi**: the two taxes the Marathas levied on territories under their protection. Chauth = 1/4 of land revenue; Sardeshmukhi = additional 10%. These made the Maratha Empire economically powerful without requiring direct administration.

10. **Baji Rao I** (1720–1740) = greatest Peshwa; **never lost a battle**. He argued for pushing into Mughal territory from the Deccan; his famous lightning march to Delhi (1737) showed Mughal weakness. He expanded the Maratha Empire into Malwa, Gujarat, and Rajputana. Died at 39.

11. **Third Battle of Panipat (January 14, 1761)**: Marathas vs Ahmad Shah Abdali (Durrani) + Rohillas + Shuja-ud-Daula (Awadh). Maratha commanders Sadashivrao Bhau and Viswas Rao (heir to Peshwaship) both died. This is the third and last famous Battle of Panipat (First = 1526 Babur vs Ibrahim Lodi; Second = 1556 Akbar/Bairam Khan vs Hemu).

12. **Sambhaji** (1680–1689): Shivaji's son and successor; captured by Aurangzeb after the fort of Sangameshwar fell; executed in March 1689 — his tongue was cut out, his body mutilated, and he was finally beheaded (for refusing to convert). His martyrdom galvanised Maratha resistance.

13. **Balaji Vishwanath** (1713–1720) = first of the Bhat family Peshwas; he won the right from the Mughal Emperor through political maneuvering for the Marathas to collect **chauth and sardeshmukhi** from the entire Deccan — the formal beginning of Maratha expansion as a pan-Indian power.

14. **Three Anglo-Maratha Wars**: First (1775–1782, Treaty of Salbai); Second (1803–1805, British took Delhi/Agra); Third (1817–1818, Peshwa abolished, British supremacy). The Third Anglo-Maratha War ended the last major Indian power before British consolidation. The ex-Peshwa Bajirao II was pensioned off at Bithur (near Kanpur) — his adopted son was **Nana Saheb** (Dhondu Pant), who led the 1857 revolt.

15. **Mastani** = Baji Rao I's beloved companion; daughter of the Raja of Bundelkhand and a Muslim woman; their relationship was opposed by the Brahmin Peshwa family (who objected to the Muslim connection); she was forcibly separated from Baji Rao I by his family; Baji Rao I died shortly after (1740). The story was the subject of the 2015 Bollywood film *Bajirao Mastani*.

16. **Madhav Rao I** (1761–1772) = the Peshwa who rebuilt Maratha power after the Panipat catastrophe within a decade; considered the greatest Peshwa after Baji Rao I; died at age 27 of tuberculosis. His death plunged the Peshwa house into succession wars and began its terminal decline. He is called "Thorle Madhavrao" (senior/great Madhavrao) by Maharashtra tradition.
