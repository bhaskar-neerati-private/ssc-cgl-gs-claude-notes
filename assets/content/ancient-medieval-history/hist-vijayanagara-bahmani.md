# Vijayanagara & Bahmani Kingdoms — The Deccan's Golden Centuries

## The City That Took Your Breath Away

In 1443 CE, a Persian ambassador named Abdur Razzaq arrived at Vijayanagara — the capital of the greatest Hindu empire of medieval India. He was prepared to be impressed by a wealthy city. He was not prepared for what he found. "The city of Vijayanagara," he wrote, "is such that the pupil of the eye has never seen a place like it and the ear of intelligence has never been informed that there existed anything to equal it in the world." The market he described stretched for miles. Every imaginable commodity from across the world was available. The streets were so crowded with people and elephants that movement was difficult. The temples were so numerous and so large that he could not count them.

At the center of all this was King Devaraya II — commanding an army of 100,000 men and a cavalry of 100,000 horses, ruling a territory stretching from the Deccan to the tip of the subcontinent.

Eighty years later, the empire would produce its greatest king: Krishnadeva Raya. And less than a century after that, four neighboring sultanates would combine at the **Battle of Talikota** (1565) to destroy a city so magnificent that people would write about its ruins for centuries.

## Part 1 — Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646 CE)

### Founding

- **Founded**: 1336 CE by brothers **Harihara I (Hakka)** and **Bukka I** — originally Kampili chiefs, captured by Muhammad bin Tughlaq, converted to Islam, sent back as governors, then reverted to Hinduism with the help of the sage **Vidyaranya** (Madhava) of the Sringeri Matha
- **Capital**: Vijayanagara (modern **Hampi**, Karnataka) — on the south bank of the Tungabhadra River
- **Name meaning**: "City of Victory" (Sanskrit: *vijaya* = victory + *nagara* = city)

### Four Dynasties of Vijayanagara

| Dynasty | Period | Key Ruler |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| **Sangama** | 1336–1485 | Harihara I, Bukka I, Devaraya I, **Devaraya II** |
| **Saluva** | 1485–1503 | Narasimha Saluva (usurped; replaced incompetent Sangamas) |
| **Tuluva** | 1503–1565 | **Vira Narasimha, Krishnadeva Raya** — peak of empire |
| **Aravidu** | 1565–1646 | After Talikota; nominal/declining power |

### Key Rulers

**Devaraya II (1424–1446 CE)**
- Greatest ruler of the Sangama dynasty
- Recruited Muslim archers and cavalry into his army (pragmatic)
- Built a dam across the Tungabhadra; extended the empire
- Patronised Kannada literature; wrote *Sobagina Sone* and *Mahanatakasutradhara* in Sanskrit
- Received Abdur Razzaq (Persian ambassador, 1443) — most famous foreign account of Vijayanagara

**Krishnadeva Raya (1509–1529 CE) — Greatest Vijayanagara Ruler**
- **Tuluva dynasty**; considered the greatest king of the Vijayanagara Empire and among the greatest rulers of Indian history
- **Military**: defeated the Bidar Sultanate (1512), Bijapur (1512, 1516), Golconda; conquered Raichur Doab (1520) from Bijapur; won the battle of Raichur against Adil Shah I — brought the last major Muslim invasion to a halt
- **Literary genius**: wrote **Amuktamalyada** (Telugu poem) — one of the greatest Telugu literary works; also wrote Sanskrit texts (*Madalasa Charita, Satyavadu Parinaya, Rasamanjari, Jambavati Kalyana*)
- **Patronage**: called "Telugu king of Telugu kings" (*Telugu rājula rāju*); supported 8 Telugu poets called **Ashtadiggajas** — the eight pillars of Telugu literature
- **Ashta Diggajas**: Allasani Peddana (greatest; *Manuchritra*), Nandi Thimmana, Madayyagari Mallana, Dhurjati, Ayyalaraju Ramabhadrudu, Pingali Suranna, Ramaraja Bhushana (Bhattu Murti), Tenali Ramakrishna (Tenali Raman — the clever jester)
- Built the **Vittala Temple** (famous stone chariot) and **Krishna Temple** at Hampi; expanded Hazara Rama Temple
- **Portuguese** traveller Domingo Paes visited ~1520; described Krishnadeva Raya as "the most feared and perfect king... a great ruler and a man of much justice"
- Maintained friendly relations with the Portuguese (who sold him horses for his cavalry)

### Vijayanagara Administration
- King at apex; **Mahamandalesvara** (provincial governors) below him
- **Revenue**: land tax (assessed on crop type); merchants paid customs duties
- **Army**: infantry, cavalry, war elephants; Brahmin military commanders unusual for this period
- Women bodyguards (female guards for the king's person) noted by travellers
- Efficient **irrigation**: tanks, dams, canals extensively built/maintained

### Vijayanagara — Religion, Culture, Art
- **Hindu revival**: patronised Shaivism, Vaishnavism, Jainism; Brahmin scholars supported
- **Architecture**: **Vijayanagara style** — tall *Gopurams* (gateway towers) with elaborate sculpture; pillared *Kalyana Mandapas* (marriage halls); **stone chariot** at Vittala Temple (now on 50-rupee note); monolithic Narasimha image; Ugra Narasimha (Lakshminarasimha) monolith
- **Women**: relatively high status in the empire; women wrestlers, female guards described by travellers; widows remarried under some conditions
- **Trade**: horse trade with Portuguese and Arabs crucial for cavalry; pepper, textiles exported; the empire was immensely wealthy from trade

### Battle of Talikota (1565 CE)

- **Also called**: Battle of Raksasi-Tangadi
- **Sides**: Vijayanagara (Aliya Rama Raya, ruler) vs **coalition of four Deccan Sultanates** (Bijapur, Bidar, Ahmadnagar, Golconda — Berar was not involved)
- **Why combined**: all four sultans had been raided/humiliated by Rama Raya; he had also played them against each other
- **Outcome**: Vijayanagara decisively defeated; Aliya Rama Raya was **captured and executed on the battlefield**; Vijayanagara city was sacked and burned for months; "the spoils were so immense that every private man in the allied army became rich in gold, jewels, effects and slaves" (Firishtah)
- **Aftermath**: The royal family fled to Penukonda; the Aravidu dynasty continued as a rump state until 1646 but never recovered; Hampi (Vijayanagara city) was abandoned and remains uninhabited to this day

## Part 2 — Bahmani Sultanate (1347–1518 CE)

### Founding and Decline

- **Founded**: 1347 CE by **Alauddin Bahman Shah** (Hasan Gangu) — a rebel governor who broke away from Muhammad bin Tughlaq's Delhi Sultanate after the humiliation of the forced capital transfer
- **Capital**: Gulbarga (Ahsanabad) → later moved to **Bidar** (by Ahmad Shah Wali, 1424)
- Lasted 171 years before fragmenting into five successor states

### Key Rulers

| Ruler | Reign | Notable |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **Alauddin Bahman Shah** | 1347–1358 | Founded sultanate |
| **Muhammad Shah I** | 1358–1375 | First major wars with Vijayanagara |
| **Ahmad Shah I (Wali)** | 1422–1435 | Moved capital to Bidar; great patron of learning; mystic |
| **Mahmud Gawan** (minister) | served 1461–1481 | Greatest Bahmani minister; brilliant military commander and scholar |

### Mahmud Gawan (1411–1481 CE)
- Persian immigrant who rose to be **wazir (prime minister)** under three Sultans
- Greatest statesman of the Bahmani Sultanate
- **Military**: conquered Konkan (Goa), parts of Orissa; won back the Raichur Doab from Vijayanagara
- **Administrative reform**: divided the kingdom into 8 provinces with checks on governors; reduced corruption
- **Scholarship**: built the **Madrasa of Mahmud Gawan** in Bidar — a library-college; collected 3,000 manuscripts from across the Islamic world; wrote Persian poetry and prose
- **Executed (1481)**: enemies forged a letter to Vijayanagara with his seal and showed it to the Sultan — falsely accused of treason; Sultan Shamsuddin Gawan ordered his execution in a fit of anger; the Sultan repented immediately but could not undo the act
- His death hastened the Bahmani Sultanate's collapse

### Five Successor (Deccan) Sultanates

When the Bahmani Sultanate broke apart (1490s–1518), five independent sultanates emerged in the Deccan:

| Sultanate | Capital | Dynasty | Notable Fact |
|-----------|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Ahmadnagar** | Ahmadnagar | Nizam Shahi | Founded by Malik Ahmad (~1490) |
| **Bijapur** | Bijapur | Adil Shahi | **Ibrahim Adil Shah II** — "father of the poor"; built **Gol Gumbaz** (second-largest dome in world) |
| **Bidar** | Bidar | Barid Shahi | Smallest; eventually absorbed by Bijapur |
| **Golconda** | Golconda | Qutb Shahi | Famous for diamonds; Hyderabad founded by **Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah** (1591) |
| **Berar** | Ellichpur | Imad Shahi | Absorbed by Ahmadnagar |

**Gol Gumbaz (Bijapur)**: tomb of Muhammad Adil Shah; completed 1656; 44 m diameter dome — one of the largest in the world; famous "Whispering Gallery" where a sound on one side of the dome is heard on the opposite side.

## Part 3 — Sangam of Cultures: The Deccan's Unique Heritage

The Vijayanagara and Bahmani kingdoms created remarkable cultural syntheses:

- **Architecture**: Bahmani architecture combined Persian (pointed arches, minarets) and indigenous Deccan styles; Bijapur's Ibrahim Rauza and Gol Gumbaz are masterpieces
- **Languages**: Telugu, Kannada, Marathi, and Persian all flourished simultaneously in the Deccan
- **Music**: Carnatic classical music traces its development to the Vijayanagara court; the composer trinity (Tyagaraja, Muthuswami Dikshitar, Syama Sastri) drew on traditions established there
- **Bidri ware**: metalwork inlaid with silver; named after Bidar; a surviving craft from the Bahmani period

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Vijayanagara was founded in 1336 CE** by **Harihara I and Bukka I** of the Sangama dynasty. The sage **Vidyaranya** (Madhava) of Sringeri Matha guided their reconversion to Hinduism and the founding of the empire. The capital was Hampi (Karnataka) on the Tungabhadra River.

2. **Krishnadeva Raya** (Tuluva dynasty, 1509–1529) = greatest Vijayanagara ruler. He wrote **Amuktamalyada** in Telugu — considered one of the finest Telugu literary works. He is called "Telugu rājula rāju" (king of Telugu kings). His court had the Ashta Diggajas (8 Telugu poets).

3. **Tenali Raman (Tenali Ramakrishna)** = one of the Ashta Diggajas; the famous clever jester/court wit; wrote *Panduranga Mahatmyamu* in Telugu. The stories of Tenali Raman's wit are traditional tales taught to children.

4. **Battle of Talikota (1565)** = four Deccan Sultanates (Bijapur, Bidar, Ahmadnagar, Golconda) combined to destroy Vijayanagara. Aliya Rama Raya was executed. The great city of Hampi was abandoned and remains so — it is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Berar did NOT participate.

5. **Abdur Razzaq** (1443): Persian ambassador who visited Vijayanagara during Devaraya II's reign; left the most famous and detailed foreign account of the empire. "The city of Vijayanagara is such that the pupil of the eye has never seen a place like it."

6. **Vittala Temple (Hampi)** — built during Krishnadeva Raya's era; has the famous **stone chariot** (ratha) which is now the image on the **50-rupee note** (and previously used as a symbol by Tourism of India). It also has 56 musical pillars that produce different sounds when struck.

7. **Bahmani Sultanate founded in 1347** by **Hasan Gangu (Alauddin Bahman Shah)** — a rebel against Muhammad bin Tughlaq. The capital was originally Gulbarga, then moved to **Bidar**. It broke up into 5 Deccan Sultanates between 1490–1518 CE.

8. **Mahmud Gawan** = greatest Bahmani minister; Persian; built the famous Madrasa in Bidar (a library-college with 3,000 manuscripts); executed on false treason charges in 1481 — his death was a turning point in Bahmani decline.

9. **Five Deccan Sultanates**: Ahmadnagar (Nizam Shahi), Bijapur (Adil Shahi), Bidar (Barid Shahi), Golconda (Qutb Shahi), Berar (Imad Shahi). These five were formed from the break-up of the Bahmani Sultanate. The SSC often asks "which sultanate was known for diamonds" — **Golconda** (whose mines produced the Kohinoor and Hope Diamond).

10. **Gol Gumbaz (Bijapur)**: tomb of Muhammad Adil Shah; one of the largest domes in the world (44m diameter); famous Whispering Gallery; built by Bijapur Adil Shahi dynasty; completed 1656 CE. Located in Vijayapura (Bijapur), Karnataka. Architect: Yaqut of Dabhol.

11. **Hyderabad city was founded in 1591 CE** by **Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah** (Golconda Sultanate) — it was built as a new city across the river from the old Golconda fort; originally called **Bhagyanagar** (city of good fortune). The Charminar was built the same year (1591).

12. **Bidar** was the capital of the Bahmani Sultanate after 1424. It is also the origin of **Bidri ware** — a craft of dark metal (zinc alloy) inlaid with silver/brass designs; a UNESCO-recognised craft tradition still practiced in Bidar.

13. **Vijayanagara's horse trade with the Portuguese**: the empire desperately needed horses for its cavalry (horses couldn't breed easily in the Deccan); the Portuguese controlled the Arabian sea trade and sold horses at high prices. Krishnadeva Raya cultivated good relations with the Portuguese for this reason.

14. **Sangama dynasty**: the first and founding dynasty of Vijayanagara (1336–1485). Within it, **Devaraya I** built a great dam across the Tungabhadra. **Devaraya II** was the greatest Sangama ruler and fought Vijayanagara's peak wars.

15. **Aliya Rama Raya** (ruling at the time of Talikota, 1565): not from the Sangama or Tuluva dynasty — he was of the Aravidu dynasty (son-in-law of Krishnadeva Raya's brother). He had been playing the Deccan Sultanates against each other for decades before they united against him.

16. **Domingo Paes (~1520)** = Portuguese traveller who visited Vijayanagara during Krishnadeva Raya's reign; described him as "a great ruler and a man of much justice." **Nicolo Conti** (1420s) and **Fernão Nunes** (1530s) are other important Portuguese accounts of Vijayanagara.
