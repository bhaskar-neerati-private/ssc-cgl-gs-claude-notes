# Ancient South India — Sangam Age, Satavahanas, Pallavas & Early Cholas

## The Poems That Survived Two Millennia

Around 300 BCE to 300 CE, in the southernmost tip of the Indian peninsula, Tamil poets composed a body of literature so sophisticated, so emotionally direct, and so formally complex that modern scholars call it classical. Over 2,000 poems survive — by 473 named poets, including 30 women. They describe love in five "landscapes" (hill, forest, pastoral, coastal, desert), each associated with a season, a time of day, a flower, a bird, and a stage of love. They also describe kings, wars, merchants, pearls, and pepper — the goods that made the Coromandel coast the eastern anchor of the Indian Ocean trade network reaching Egypt and Rome.

This Sangam literature — compiled in three "academies" (Sangams) in Madurai — is the oldest surviving body of literature in any Dravidian language. The kingdoms that produced it — Chera, Chola, Pandya — are among the longest-lived political entities in world history, persisting in some form for over two thousand years.

## Part 1 — The Sangam Age (300 BCE–300 CE)

**Sangam** = literary academy/assembly. Three Sangams held in Madurai (the last of which produced surviving literature).

### The Three Tamil Kingdoms

| Kingdom | Capital | Region | Emblem | Key Port | Main Work |
|---------|---------|--------|--------|---------|-----------|
| **Chera** | Vanji/Karur | Kerala + Western TN | Bow | Muzaris (Kodungallur) | Pattinappalai, Purananuru |
| **Chola** | Uraiyur → Kaveri delta | Eastern TN | Tiger | Puhar (Kaveripattinam) | Purananuru, Akananuru |
| **Pandya** | Madurai | Southern TN | Fish | Korkai | Tolkappiyam (grammar); Maturai Kanchi |

### Sangam Literature

| Text | Type | Significance |
|------|------|-------------|
| **Tolkappiyam** | Grammar (Tamil); 3 parts | Oldest surviving Tamil text; by Tolkappiyar |
| **Ettuthokai** (8 anthologies) | Poetry | Includes Purananuru (war/ethics), Akananuru (love) |
| **Pattuppattu** (10 idylls) | Poetry | Includes Pattinappalai (description of Puhar port) |
| **Pathinenkilkanakku** (18 minor didactic works) | Ethics | Includes **Tirukkural** (Kural) |
| **Tirukkural** | Ethics; 1,330 couplets on Aram, Porul, Inbam | By **Tiruvalluvar**; "Tamil Veda"; translated into 60+ languages |
| **Silappatikaram** | Epic | By Ilango Adigal (Chera prince); story of Kannagi and Kovalan |
| **Manimekalai** | Epic | By Sattanar; sequel to Silappatikaram; Buddhist philosophy |
| **Chilapathikaram** | Epic | Often grouped with Silappatikaram as Tamil epic pair |

### Economy and Trade (Sangam Age)
- **Pepper** — Rome called it "black gold"; exported from Muzaris (Kodungallur, Kerala)
- **Pearls** — from Korkai and the Gulf of Mannar (Pandya territory)
- **Textiles, spices, ivory, iron, gold** exported; wine, gold coins, slaves imported
- **Roman trade**: Augustus Caesar sent an embassy to Pandya; Roman gold coins found in large numbers at Tamil sites; Roman merchants had colonies (Tamil term: *Yavana* for Greeks/Romans)
- **Puhar (Kaveripattinam)**: Chola port — described in detail in Pattinappalai; a cosmopolitan, prosperous city

### Society and Religion
- **Kural Society**: four varnas existed but were less rigid than north India; significant Brahmin presence but also non-Brahmin priests
- **Hero stones** (Viragal): memorial stones for fallen heroes; unique South Indian tradition
- **Religion**: worship of Murugan (Kartik), Mayon (Vishnu), Seyon (Murugan), Kotravai (Durga), Varunan (Varuna), Indiran; Vedic Brahminism and indigenous traditions coexisted
- **Position of women**: more freedom than later periods; women poets in Sangam literature (Avvaiyar, Nappinnai)

## Part 2 — Satavahanas (1st century BCE–3rd century CE)

Already covered briefly in the Gupta context — but essential for South Indian history:

- **Greatest ruler**: Gautamiputra Satakarni (106–130 CE) — defeated Western Kshatrapas (Sakas), Yavanas (Greeks), Pahlavas; his mother's inscription (*Nasik Inscription*) is the primary source
- Patronised **Amaravati Stupa** (Andhra Pradesh, on Krishna river) — finest Buddhist sculptural art in southern India; white marble railings and gateways
- Used Prakrit (not Sanskrit) in inscriptions; promoted Buddhism and Brahminism simultaneously
- **Capital**: Pratishthana (Paithan, Maharashtra); later Dhanyakataka (Amaravati)

## Part 3 — Pallavas (3rd–9th century CE)

The Pallavas were the dominant power in Tamil Nadu/Andhra from the 3rd to 9th centuries CE and are responsible for some of the greatest achievements in South Indian temple architecture.

### Key Rulers

| Ruler | Period | Key Achievement |
|-------|--------|----------------|
| **Simhavishnu** | ~575–600 CE | Consolidated Pallava power; defeated Kalabhras |
| **Mahendravarman I** | 600–630 CE | Rock-cut cave temples; battle of Pullalur (defeated Chalukya Pulakesin II initially) |
| **Narasimhavarman I (Mamalla)** | 630–668 CE | Greatest Pallava king; defeated Chalukya Pulakesin II (who died in battle); built **Mamallapuram** (Mahabalipuram) |
| **Narasimhavarman II (Rajasimha)** | 700–728 CE | Built **Kailasanatha Temple** (Kanchipuram) — first structural stone temple in South India |

### Pallava Art and Architecture

| Monument | Location | Type | Notes |
|----------|---------|------|-------|
| **Shore Temple** | Mahabalipuram | Structural | 8th century; UNESCO World Heritage (along with other monuments) |
| **Pancha Rathas** (Five Rathas) | Mahabalipuram | Monolithic rock-cut | Five chariots carved from single rock; named after Pandavas; proto-Dravidian style |
| **Arjuna's Penance** (Descent of Ganga) | Mahabalipuram | Open-air rock relief | Largest bas-relief in world (~27 × 9 m) |
| **Kailasanatha Temple** | Kanchipuram | Structural | Oldest stone temple in Kanchipuram; prototype of later Dravidian temples |
| **Vaikunta Perumal Temple** | Kanchipuram | Structural | Later Pallava; tells Pallava dynasty story in sculptural panels |

**Dravidian Style**: distinguishing features — **Vimana** (tower over garbhagriha, pyramid-shaped), **Mandapa** (pillared hall), **Gopuram** (towering gateway — developed later). This is the template for all Tamil Nadu temples.

### Pallavas and Learning
- **Kanchipuram** was the capital and a great centre of learning
- Patronised Sanskrit learning; Mahendravarman I wrote the satirical play *Mattavilasaprahasana*
- Influenced architecture of Southeast Asia (Cambodia, Java) — Pallava traders and priests spread Indian culture

## Part 4 — Early Cholas (Medieval Period, 9th–13th century CE)

(Note: the Sangam Age Cholas were ancient; the "Medieval Cholas" are a separate and later dynasty that rose to tremendous power — briefly noted here for completeness.)

**Vijayalaya Chola** (c. 850 CE): founder of the medieval Chola dynasty; captured Thanjavur from Muttaraiyars.

The Medieval Cholas (Rajaraja I, Rajendra I) are so important they deserve treatment in the medieval section — they built the Brihadeeswarar Temple (Thanjavur) and sent navies to Southeast Asia.

## Part 5 — Chalukyas of Badami (543–755 CE)

Essential for understanding the Deccan in this period:

| Ruler | Period | Achievement |
|-------|--------|------------|
| **Pulakesin I** | 543–566 CE | Founded dynasty; Vatapi (Badami) capital |
| **Kirtivarman I** | 566–598 CE | Expanded territory |
| **Pulakesin II** | 610–642 CE | Greatest ruler; defeated Harsha at **Narmada River battle**; defeated Pallava Mahendravarman I (Battle of Pullalur); received Persian embassy; **Hieun Tsang** visited |
| Vikramaditya II | 733–745 CE | Defeated Pallavas; reached Kanchipuram |

**Pulakesin II's achievement**: defeated Harsha — stopping Harsha's southward expansion. The **Aihole Inscription** (composed by poet Ravikirti) records this victory. Pulakesin II died fighting the Pallavas (Narasimhavarman I sacked Vatapi and earned title "Vatapikonda").

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Tolkappiyam** is the oldest surviving Tamil text — a grammar (not a poem collection). Written by **Tolkappiyar**. The literary anthologies (Ettuthokai, Pattuppattu) came later in the Sangam period.

2. **Tirukkural** (1,330 couplets on ethics, polity, love) by **Tiruvalluvar** — the most important Sangam-era literary text for SSC. Called the "Tamil Veda" and "Kural." Translated into 60+ languages. Do not say it was written by Ilango Adigal (that's Silappatikaram).

3. **Silappatikaram** (epic) by **Ilango Adigal** (Chera prince) — story of Kannagi who burned Madurai after her husband Kovalan was unjustly executed. **Manimekalai** (sequel) by Sattanar — Buddhist in orientation.

4. **Three Tamil kingdoms' emblems**: Chera = Bow; Chola = Tiger; Pandya = Fish. These are frequently tested in SSC.

5. **Amaravati Stupa** (Andhra Pradesh, on Krishna river) — built/expanded by Satavahanas, especially Gautamiputra Satakarni's era. Famous for its white marble sculptural panels. Do not confuse with Sanchi Stupa (Madhya Pradesh, Ashoka/Shunga era).

6. **Narasimhavarman I (Mamalla)** built **Mamallapuram** (Mahabalipuram) — named after him. He defeated Chalukya Pulakesin II (who died in battle). The Shore Temple, Pancha Rathas, and Arjuna's Penance are all here. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

7. **Arjuna's Penance (Descent of Ganga)** at Mahabalipuram is the largest bas-relief in the world. It shows the descent of the Ganga from heaven with hundreds of figures including nagas, animals, and celestial beings.

8. **Kailasanatha Temple** (Kanchipuram, by Narasimhavarman II/Rajasimha) = first structural stone temple in South India (as opposed to rock-cut). This is the prototype for the Dravidian temple style.

9. **Dravidian style** has a pyramidal **Vimana** (tower/shikhara) over the garbhagriha — contrasting with the Nagara (North Indian) style's curvilinear shikhara. This distinction is frequently tested in art history.

10. **Pulakesin II defeated Harsha** at the Narmada River — stopping Harsha's southern expansion. Source: **Aihole Inscription** by Ravikirti. Hieun Tsang also mentioned Pulakesin II as "Paramesvaravarma" — the most powerful king in the Deccan.

11. **Muzaris** (modern Kodungallur, Kerala) = most important port for Roman trade with India. Pepper was the primary export; Roman coins found in great quantity in Kerala. Pliny the Elder complained that Rome was losing gold to India for pepper.

12. **Puhar (Kaveripattinam)** = Chola's most important port; described in Pattinappalai as a cosmopolitan city with streets, mansions, and a bustling harbour. The city is now submerged under the Bay of Bengal (confirmed by underwater archaeology).

13. **Gautamiputra Satakarni's** mother's **Nasik Inscription** gives the fullest account of his conquests. He described himself as "destroyer of Sakas, Yavanas and Pahlavas" — showing the multiple foreign ruling groups he expelled.

14. **Pancha Rathas** (Five Chariots) at Mahabalipuram = five monolithic rock-cut temples, named after the five Pandava brothers (Draupadi Ratha, Arjuna Ratha, Bhima Ratha, Dharmaraja Ratha, Nakula-Sahadeva Ratha). Each has different architectural style — a showcase of Pallava experimentation.

15. **Pallava influence on Southeast Asia**: Pallava traders, priests, and culture spread to Cambodia, Java, Sumatra, and mainland Southeast Asia. The **Pallava script** is the ancestor of scripts used in Thailand, Cambodia, and Indonesia. This makes Pallavas particularly significant in the broader Indian Ocean cultural history.

16. **The Sangam age had Roman trade**: Roman gold coins (Augustus, Tiberius, Nero era) found in large numbers at Tamil sites. Sangam poems mention "Yavana" merchants. A Roman trading post existed at Arikamedu (Pondicherry) — confirmed by amphorae, lamps, and glass from the Mediterranean.
