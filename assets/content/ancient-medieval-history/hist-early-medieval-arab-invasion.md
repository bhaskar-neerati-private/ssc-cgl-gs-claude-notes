# Early Medieval India — Mahmud of Ghazni, Muhammad Ghori & Rajput Kingdoms

## The Man Who Came Seventeen Times

In 1025 CE, Mahmud of Ghazni stood before the temple of Somnath on the Gujarat coast — one of the wealthiest Hindu temples in existence, rumoured to hold treasures accumulated over centuries of royal donations. Thousands of Brahmin priests were inside, praying and ringing bells in the belief that their god would destroy the invader. Mahmud ordered the gates broken open, the idol smashed, and the gold stripped. Contemporary accounts say 50,000 defenders died. The treasure filled 20 camels. This was Mahmud's 17th — and last — raid into the Indian subcontinent.

He never intended to conquer India. He came to plunder — systematically, methodically, returning home each time to his court in Ghazni (Afghanistan) which he was turning into the most glittering cultural center of the Islamic world, funded by Indian gold. He brought a scholar with him: **Al-Beruni**, whose *Kitab-ul-Hind* ("Book of India"), written after observing Indian society, remains one of the most sophisticated analyses of Indian civilisation ever written by an outsider.

## Part 1 — Rajput Kingdoms (7th–12th Century CE)

The term **Rajput** ("son of a king") refers to a warrior aristocracy that dominated northern India between the fall of Harsha (647 CE) and the establishment of the Delhi Sultanate (1206 CE).

### Origin of Rajputs (Debated)
1. **Foreign origin theory** (V.A. Smith): descendants of Central Asian tribes (Hunas, Gurjaras, Sakas) who were absorbed into Indian society as warriors
2. **Agnikula legend**: born from the sacrificial fire at Mount Abu — divine/ritual origin; claimed by Pratiharas, Paramaras, Chandellas, Chahamanas
3. **Indigenous Kshatriya theory**: descendants of ancient Kshatriya lineages

Most modern historians accept a mixed origin — some indigenous, many absorbed foreigners given Kshatriya status through Brahminical ceremonies.

### Key Rajput Clans and Kingdoms

| Clan | Kingdom | Capital | Notable Ruler |
|------|---------|---------|--------------|
| **Chahamana (Chauhan)** | Rajasthan/Delhi | Ajmer/Delhi | **Prithviraj III (Prithviraj Chauhan)** |
| **Paramar** | Malwa (MP) | Dhara/Dhar | **Bhoja I** (scholar-king) |
| **Chandella** | Bundelkhand | Khajuraho/Mahoba | Famous for Khajuraho temples |
| **Gahadavala** | Varanasi/UP | Varanasi | Govindachandra |
| **Kachchhapaghata** | Gwalior | Gwalior | |
| **Tomara** | Delhi | Dhillika (Delhi) | Founded Delhi |
| **Sena** | Bengal | Navadwip | Defeated Palas; Hemantasena, Vijayasena, Ballalasena |

### Rajput Society and Culture
- **Feudal system**: land grants to subordinate chiefs (samantas) in exchange for military service
- **Chivalric code (Rajdharma)**: valour, honour, protection of women; elaborate farewell ceremonies before battle
- **Jauhar**: mass self-immolation by Rajput women when fort was about to fall (to avoid captivity)
- **Saka**: self-sacrifice in battle by Rajput warriors — fight to the death
- Great patrons of **Sanskrit literature, poetry, temples**

### Prithviraj III (Chauhan) (1178–1192 CE)
- **Last major Rajput king** of north India
- Unified Chahamana territories; defeated many Rajput rivals
- **Prithviraj Raso** — heroic epic written by **Chand Bardai** (court poet); describes Prithviraj's exploits (partially legendary)
- Relationship with Jaichand (Gahadavala king of Varanasi): bitter rivalry; Jaichand allegedly collaborated with Muhammad Ghori against Prithviraj

## Part 2 — Mahmud of Ghazni (971–1030 CE)

- **Dynasty**: Ghaznavid; son of Subuktigin; capital Ghazni (Afghanistan)
- Became Sultan in 997 CE; title: "**The Idol-Smasher**" (Buth-shikan)
- Conducted **17 raids on India** between **1000 and 1027 CE**

### Key Raids

| Year | Target | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| 1001 | Peshawar | Defeated Jayapala (Hindu Shahi king) |
| 1008 | Waihind | Defeated Anandapala (son of Jayapala) |
| **1025** | **Somnath (Gujarat)** | Most famous; sacked the Somnath temple; 50,000 died; enormous treasure looted |
| 1027 | Jat raid (Sindh) | Last raid; punished Jats for harassing his retreating army |

### Key Points About Mahmud
- **Primary motive was loot** — not permanent conquest (except Punjab)
- Only **Punjab** was annexed as a permanent territory
- Funded his court and the cultural splendor of Ghazni with Indian wealth
- **Court scholars**: **Al-Beruni** (*Kitab-ul-Hind*), **Firdausi** (*Shahnamah* — Persian epic) — though Firdausi later fell out with Mahmud over payment
- **Al-Beruni**: brilliant Islamic scholar who learnt Sanskrit; his *Kitab-ul-Hind* (1030 CE) is a masterpiece of comparative scholarship — discusses Hindu philosophy, mathematics, astronomy, geography

## Part 3 — Muhammad Ghori (Muiz ud-Din Muhammad, 1149–1206 CE)

Unlike Mahmud, Muhammad Ghori (ruler of the Ghurid dynasty, based at Ghur in Afghanistan) came to **conquer** — not merely plunder.

### The Two Battles of Tarain (Taraori, Haryana)

#### First Battle of Tarain (1191 CE) — **Prithviraj III WINS**
- Ghori's first major attempt to enter the Gangetic plain
- Prithviraj III defeated Muhammad Ghori decisively; Ghori was seriously wounded; his generals carried him away
- Prithviraj showed chivalric mercy — did not pursue Ghori

#### Second Battle of Tarain (1192 CE) — **Muhammad Ghori WINS**
- Ghori returned with a better-prepared army (reportedly 120,000 cavalry vs Prithviraj's 300,000 according to sources — numbers likely exaggerated)
- Ghori's key tactical advantage: **horse archery** — used mounted archers to tire and disorder the Rajput elephant corps before a decisive cavalry charge
- Prithviraj was captured and executed
- This battle ended Rajput dominance in northern India and opened the door to the Delhi Sultanate

#### Battle of Chandawar (1194 CE)
- Ghori defeated **Jaichand** (Gahadavala king of Varanasi) at Chandawar
- Bengal and Varanasi fell

### Other Key Events
- **Bakhtiyar Khilji** (Ghori's general): conquered Bihar (~1197 CE) and **destroyed Nalanda University** — burning its library (which burned for weeks, according to legend); conquered Bengal (~1203 CE)
- **Qutbuddin Aibak** (Ghori's slave and viceroy in Delhi): after Ghori's death (1206 CE, assassinated), established the **Slave Dynasty** → Delhi Sultanate begins
- Muhammad Ghori never made Delhi his capital — he ruled from Ghazni; appointed slaves/viceroys in India

## Part 4 — Factors Behind Rajput Failure

1. **Lack of unity**: Rajput clans constantly fought each other; no unified front against Ghori
2. **Military conservatism**: reliance on slow war elephants vs Ghori's fast cavalry archers
3. **No standing army**: feudal levy system; irregular forces
4. **Chivalric code was a military liability**: did not pursue enemy after Tarain I; no night attacks; formal battle announced in advance
5. **No naval/cavalry tradition**: cavalry was central to Ghori's strategy; Rajputs had superior elephants but elephants panicked under arrow fire

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Mahmud of Ghazni conducted exactly 17 raids on India** (1000–1027 CE). The most famous was the **1025 raid on Somnath** (Gujarat). Mahmud permanently annexed only **Punjab** — not all of India.

2. **Al-Beruni** accompanied Mahmud of Ghazni to India and wrote **Kitab-ul-Hind** — a comprehensive account of Indian science, philosophy, religion, and society. He learned Sanskrit. This is the most important external account of early 11th century India.

3. **The two Battles of Tarain**: 1191 = Prithviraj wins (1st); 1192 = Ghori wins (2nd). These dates and outcomes are directly asked in SSC — do not confuse.

4. **Muhammad Ghori (Ghurid dynasty) ≠ Mahmud of Ghazni (Ghaznavid dynasty)**. Mahmud came from Ghazni; Ghori came from Ghur (different dynasties, different goals). Mahmud plundered; Ghori conquered.

5. **Battle of Chandawar (1194)**: Ghori defeated Jaichand (Gahadavala of Varanasi). Jaichand's role in "betraying" Prithviraj is legendary but not entirely documented — the popular account comes from the later (and partially legendary) *Prithviraj Raso*.

6. **Bakhtiyar Khilji destroyed Nalanda University (~1197–1200 CE)** — the greatest centre of Buddhist learning. He reportedly mistook it for a fort. This ended the Buddhist monastic tradition in India (surviving monks fled to Nepal, Tibet). Nalanda had stood for 700–800 years.

7. **Prithviraj Raso** by Chand Bardai = court epic about Prithviraj III. While it has legendary elements, it preserves cultural memory of Rajput values and warfare. Do not take its numbers and narratives as strictly historical.

8. **Ghori's military advantage**: horse archers (mounted bowmen) who could shoot rapidly while riding — this could tire and disorganise elephant corps from a distance. The Rajputs had more troops but their battlefield tactics were poorly suited to counter this.

9. **Firdausi** wrote the **Shahnamah** (Book of Kings) — the great Persian epic; written at Mahmud of Ghazni's court. Mahmud reportedly promised a gold coin for each couplet but paid in silver — Firdausi was furious and wrote a bitter satire of Mahmud before leaving. Shahnamah is the national epic of Iran.

10. **Qutbuddin Aibak** was Muhammad Ghori's slave who became viceroy and, after Ghori's assassination (1206 CE), founded the **Slave/Mamluk Dynasty** — the first dynasty of the Delhi Sultanate. Aibak began construction of **Qutub Minar** in Delhi.

11. **The Hindu Shahi dynasty** (NW India: Kabul, Gandhara) was the first to resist Mahmud — king Jayapala, then his son Anandapala. They were defeated multiple times. Jayapala immolated himself in shame after his defeat.

12. **Somnath temple** (Prabhas Patan, Gujarat): sacred Shiva temple on the coast; one of the 12 Jyotirlingas; destroyed by Mahmud 1025 CE; rebuilt multiple times by Hindu kings; the current structure is post-independence (consecrated 1951 by Sardar Patel's initiative).

13. **Jauhar** (mass self-immolation by Rajput women) is attested multiple times in Rajput history — most famously during the Alauddin Khalji siege of Chittorgarh (1303 CE, involving Rani Padmavati — though Padmavati's historicity is disputed). Jauhar distinguished Rajput martial culture from other Indian traditions.

14. **Mahmud's raids were concentrated in Punjab, Sindh, Rajasthan, and Gujarat** — he did not reach eastern or southern India. He sacked Mathura and Kanauj but did not rule them permanently.

15. **Paramara king Bhoja I** (Bhojadeva, ~1000–1055 CE): great scholar-king of Malwa; wrote works on Sanskrit grammar, yoga, architecture (Samarangana Sutradhara), poetics; built Bhojeshvara Temple (Bhopal area); founded the city of Bhojapur (Bhopal); patronised Dhar as his capital. One of the few medieval kings celebrated both as warrior and scholar.

16. **Chahamana = Chauhan** — same clan, different names. Prithviraj Chauhan III = Prithviraj III of the Chahamana dynasty. His capital was Ajmer (main) and Delhi (secondary — captured from Tomaras). The Chahamanas had ruled for generations before Prithviraj; he was not the dynasty's founder.
