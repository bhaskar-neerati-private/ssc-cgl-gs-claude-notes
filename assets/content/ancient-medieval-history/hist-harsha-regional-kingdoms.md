# Harsha's Empire, Regional Kingdoms & Arab Invasion of Sindh

## The Stranger at Kanauj

In 629 CE, a Chinese Buddhist monk named Hieun Tsang (Xuanzang) arrived at the court of Emperor Harshavardhana at Kanauj. He had already walked 10,000 miles across Central Asia through snow and desert. What he found in Harsha's court surprised him: a king who fed thousands of poor people from his personal treasury every day, who had banned the eating of meat throughout his kingdom, who fasted regularly, and who knelt before Buddhist monks. Every five years, Harsha held a great assembly at Prayag (Allahabad) where he gave away everything he owned — his treasury, his clothes, even the jewellery off his body — until his sister had to give him her own garments. Then he would start accumulating again for the next five years.

Hieun Tsang's account of this monarch — the last great emperor before the subcontinent fractured into competing sultanates — is one of history's most vivid royal portraits. Harsha was simultaneously warrior, Buddhist devotee, playwright, and social reformer. But he died without an heir, and his empire disintegrated the moment he was gone.

## Part 1 — Harsha's Empire (606–647 CE)

### Rise to Power
- **Full name**: Harshavardhana; dynasty: **Pushyabhuti/Vardhana**
- **Capital**: Kanauj (Kannauj, UP); previously Thanesar (Haryana)
- **Father**: Prabhakaravardhana; **Sister**: Rajyashri (married Grahavarman of Maukhari); **Brother**: Rajyavardhana (killed by Shashanka of Gauda/Bengal)
- Ascended at age 16 (606 CE) after his brother was treacherously killed by **Shashanka** (king of Gauda/Bengal — also the one who allegedly destroyed the Bodhi Tree at Bodh Gaya)

### Key Events and Reign

| Event | Detail |
|-------|-------|
| **Defeat by Pulakesin II** | Harsha tried to expand south of Narmada; repulsed by Chalukya king Pulakesin II (~634 CE); Narmada = boundary |
| **Prayag Assembly** | Every five years at Allahabad; Harsha distributed his wealth to the poor, monks, and Brahmins; Hieun Tsang attended the 6th assembly |
| **Nalanda patronage** | Made Nalanda University a great international institution; gave massive endowments |
| **Wang Xuance Mission** | After Harsha's death (647 CE), a Chinese ambassador Wang Xuance was attacked by Harsha's successor Arunasva; Wang Xuance raised an army from Nepal and Tibet, defeated Arunasva — remarkable episode |

### Administration
- **Decentralised**: Harsha's empire was a confederation of regional rulers who paid tribute; he did not build a Mauryan-style centralised state
- **Revenue**: 1/4 from land; used for government, religious endowments, and charity
- **No systematic taxation** like the Mauryas; depended on tribute from subordinate kings

### Sources on Harsha
- **Harshacharita** (Life of Harsha) by **Banabhatta** — Harsha's court poet; first political biography in Sanskrit literature
- **Hieun Tsang's** account (*Si-yu-ki* — "Records of the Western Regions") — most detailed description of 7th century India
- **Nagananda, Ratnavali, Priyadarshika** — three plays attributed to Harsha himself (Sanskrit) — rare example of a monarch who was also a playwright

## Part 2 — Regional Kingdoms (7th–10th century CE)

After Harsha's death, India entered the **Rajput Age** — a period of fragmentation into multiple competing kingdoms, primarily led by Rajput (and other) dynasties.

### The Tripartite Struggle (8th–10th century CE)
Three major powers fought for control of Kanauj (symbolic center of north Indian political legitimacy) for about 200 years:

| Dynasty | Base | Key Ruler |
|---------|------|-----------|
| **Gurjara-Pratiharas** | Rajasthan/UP | Mihira Bhoja (~836–885 CE) — greatest; defeated Arab expansion |
| **Palas** | Bengal/Bihar | Dharmapala (~770–810 CE) — patron of Nalanda, Vikramashila |
| **Rashtrakutas** | Deccan (Maharashtra) | Dhruva, Govinda III, Amoghavarsha — most powerful; controlled from Deccan |

The Rashtrakutas generally prevailed but all three weakened each other — creating the vacuum exploited by later invaders.

### Gurjara-Pratiharas (c. 725–1036 CE)
- **Nagabhata I** (c. 730 CE): stopped Arab expansion from Sindh into Rajasthan
- **Mihira Bhoja** (~836–885 CE): greatest Pratihara king; rebuilt Kanauj as a great city; Arab traveller Sulaiman described him as the "greatest enemy of Islam" (because he stopped Arab advance)
- **Patronised** the Khajuraho temples (though built by their feudatories, the Chandellas)
- **Decline**: Chandella, Paramar, Chahamana (Chauhan), Chalukya of Gujarat feudatories became independent; Rashtrakutas sacked Kanauj (916 CE); Mahmud of Ghazni delivered final blow

### Palas of Bengal (750–1174 CE)
- **Founded by**: Gopala (~750 CE) — elected by Bengali nobles (a unique form of election)
- **Dharmapala** (770–810 CE): greatest Pala king; controlled much of northern India
- **Devapala** (810–850 CE): further expansion; sent grant to Nalanda
- **Patronage**: Nalanda University, **Vikramashila University** (founded by Dharmapala), **Odantapuri Monastery**
- **Pala art**: characteristic bronze images of Buddha and Bodhisattvas; influenced Tibetan Buddhist art
- **Decline**: Senas replaced Palas in Bengal; the last great Pala ruler was Mahipala I

### Rashtrakutas (753–982 CE)
- **Founded by**: Dantidurga (~753 CE) — overthrew Chalukyas of Badami
- **Capital**: Manyakheta (Malkhed, near Gulbarga, Karnataka)
- **Greatest rulers**: Dhruva, Govinda III (controlled from Cape Comorin to Himalayas briefly), **Amoghavarsha I** (814–878 CE) — 64 years reign; great patron of Kannada literature; wrote *Kavirajamarga* (first Kannada literary work)
- **Kailasa Temple, Ellora** (Cave 16) — built by Krishna I (~757–773 CE); largest monolithic rock-cut temple in the world; carved from top to bottom out of a single rock; dedicated to Shiva
- **Patrons of all religions**: Brahminism, Buddhism, Jainism; Arab traders in their ports were treated well
- **Decline**: Chalukyas of Kalyani (Western Chalukyas) defeated them in 982 CE

### Other Important Regional Kingdoms

| Dynasty | Region | Notable Feature |
|---------|--------|----------------|
| **Chandellas** | Bundelkhand (MP) | Built **Khajuraho temples** (950–1050 CE); famous erotic sculptures |
| **Paramaras** | Malwa | **Bhoja I** (1000–1055 CE) — scholar-king; built Bhoja Stambha; works on Sanskrit grammar, yoga, architecture |
| **Chahamanas (Chauhans)** | Rajasthan | **Prithviraj III (Chauhan)** — hero of First Battle of Tarain |
| **Chalukyas of Gujarat** | Gujarat | **Solanki dynasty**; Anhilwara Patan capital |
| **Kalachuris** | Central India | |

## Part 3 — Arab Invasion of Sindh (711 CE)

This is the first major Islamic incursion into the Indian subcontinent — though its long-term impact was limited to Sindh.

### Background
- **Umayyad Caliphate** (Damascus) was expanding east; already controlled Persia (637 CE) and moved toward Central Asia and NW India
- **Dahir**: last Hindu king of Sindh; ruled from Aror (or Brahmanabad)
- **Immediate cause**: Arab ships carrying gifts to the Caliph from the king of Sri Lanka were allegedly looted by pirates near Debal (Karachi port) — possibly Dahir's responsibility (disputed); the Hajjaj ibn Yusuf (governor of Iraq) used this as pretext

### Muhammad bin Qasim's Campaign (711–712 CE)
- **Commander**: Muhammad bin Qasim, nephew of Hajjaj ibn Yusuf
- Defeated and killed **Dahir** at the **Battle of Raor** (712 CE)
- Conquered Sindh and parts of Punjab
- **Relatively tolerant**: Hindus and Buddhists treated as Zimmis (protected people paying jizya); local administrators kept; temples not destroyed
- Removed by Caliph Sulaiman (712 CE) when Hajjaj died; Muhammad bin Qasim recalled and executed — Sindh administration reverted to local customs

### Significance and Limitations
- Arab rule was confined to **Sindh and southern Punjab** — never expanded into mainland India
- **Gurjara-Pratiharas** (Nagabhata I) stopped subsequent Arab advances eastward from Rajasthan
- Arab scholars came to India: **Al-Fazari** translated Indian mathematical texts (*Brahmasphutasiddhanta*) into Arabic at Baghdad's House of Wisdom — transmitting Indian numerals (including zero) to the Islamic world and eventually to Europe
- The conquest opened Sindh to **Islam** — which has remained there to this day

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Hieun Tsang** visited India during **Harsha's** reign (630–645 CE), NOT during the Gupta period. **Fa-Hien** visited during Chandragupta II (Gupta period, ~399 CE). Getting these two Chinese pilgrims mixed up is the most common error in SSC Ancient History.

2. **Harshacharita** by **Banabhatta** = Harsha's biography in Sanskrit. **Hieun Tsang's Si-yu-ki** = "Records of the Western Regions" in Chinese. Both are primary sources for Harsha's reign.

3. **Harsha was defeated by Pulakesin II** at the Narmada River — he could not expand south of the Narmada. The Narmada remained the boundary between Harsha's northern empire and the Deccan under the Chalukyas.

4. **Harsha's 3 plays**: Nagananda, Ratnavali, Priyadarshika — written in Sanskrit. He is one of very few Indian kings who were also literary figures. The plays are genuine works, not ghostwritten.

5. **Prayag Assemblies** (Kumbha-type gathering every 5 years): Harsha gave away all his wealth at Allahabad. Hieun Tsang attended the **6th Assembly** (~643 CE). After giving everything away including his own clothes, Harsha's sister gave him her garment.

6. **The Tripartite Struggle** (8th–10th century CE): Pratiharas (Rajasthan), Palas (Bengal), Rashtrakutas (Deccan) fought for control of **Kanauj**. Kanauj was the prize because it symbolised north Indian political supremacy. All three dynasties sacked Kanauj at different times.

7. **Kailasa Temple, Ellora (Cave 16)**: largest monolithic rock-cut temple in the world; built by **Krishna I** (Rashtrakuta); dedicated to Shiva; took 150 years to build. Do not confuse with Kailasanatha Temple in Kanchipuram (Pallava; structural temple).

8. **Dharmapala (Pala)** founded **Vikramashila University** — the second most important Buddhist university (after Nalanda) in medieval India. Located in Bihar. Destroyed by Bakhtiyar Khilji (1203 CE).

9. **Mihira Bhoja (Pratihara)**: greatest Pratihara king; Arab traveller Sulaiman (~851 CE) described him as the greatest enemy of Arab expansion. This is directly tested in SSC.

10. **Amoghavarsha I (Rashtrakuta)**: ruled for 64 years (814–878 CE); wrote **Kavirajamarga** — the first Kannada literary work (a grammar/poetics manual). Also wrote Ratnamalika in Sanskrit. Was a Jain devotee. Built the capital at Manyakheta (Malkhed).

11. **Arab invasion of Sindh (711 CE)**: commander = **Muhammad bin Qasim** (NOT Mahmud of Ghazni — that's the 11th century). The Arab conquest was limited to Sindh. Muhammad bin Qasim defeated King Dahir and was executed by the next Caliph soon after.

12. **Khajuraho temples** (950–1050 CE): built by **Chandellas** (feudatories of Pratiharas). UNESCO World Heritage Site. Famous for their erotic sculptures (which represent about 10% of the total sculptural programme — the rest is devotional). Located in Madhya Pradesh.

13. **Shashanka** of Gauda (Bengal): killed Harsha's brother Rajyavardhana; reportedly destroyed the Bodhi Tree at Bodh Gaya; was Harsha's main enemy early in his reign. He represents the powerful regional forces Harsha had to overcome to build his empire.

14. **Gopala (Pala dynasty)** was elected by Bengali nobles — making the Pala dynasty's founding a rare example of something like electoral consent in medieval India. This is occasionally tested as a "unique" historical fact.

15. **Al-Beruni's Kitab-ul-Hind** was written about Indian knowledge in the context of Mahmud of Ghazni's invasions (early 11th century) — much later than the Arab conquest of Sindh (711 CE). Do not conflate the two.

16. **Wang Xuance episode** (647 CE): after Harsha's death, the Chinese ambassador Wang Xuance was attacked by Harsha's usurper successor Arunasva. Wang Xuance went to Nepal and Tibet, raised an army of ~7,000 Nepali and Tibetan soldiers, returned to India, defeated Arunasva, and sent him as a prisoner to China. This remarkable episode shows the importance of Sino-Indian diplomatic relations in the 7th century.
