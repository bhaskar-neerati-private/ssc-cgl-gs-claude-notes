# Post-Maurya Period & Gupta Empire — Samudragupta, Chandragupta II & Golden Age

## The Pilgrim Who Found Paradise

In 399 CE, a Chinese Buddhist monk named Fa-Hien set out from Chang'an to find the original Buddhist scriptures in India. After a perilous journey across the Gobi Desert and the Hindu Kush, he arrived in the realm of Chandragupta II Vikramaditya — and was astounded. He found a land of well-ordered governance, prosperous cities, hospitals that treated patients free of charge, and a people who lived without fear. "The people are numerous and happy," he wrote. "They do not kill any living creature, nor drink intoxicating liquor... Throughout the whole country the people do not kill any living creature."

Fa-Hien had arrived at the high-water mark of what later generations would call the **Golden Age of India** — the Gupta period. It was a time when Kalidasa wrote poetry that would be read for two thousand years, when Aryabhata calculated the value of pi and the earth's rotation, when zero and the decimal system were systematised, and when Indian art reached heights it would never again surpass.

## Part 1 — Post-Maurya Period (185–320 CE): The Interregnum

After the Mauryas collapsed, northern India fragmented into multiple competing kingdoms. This period saw important dynasties and remarkable cultural developments.

### Shunga Dynasty (185–73 BCE)
- **Founder**: Pushyamitra Shunga (killed last Maurya Brihadratha, 185 BCE)
- **Capital**: Pataliputra
- **Notable ruler**: Agnimitra (son of Pushyamitra; hero of Kalidasa's play *Malavikagnimitra*)
- Conducted Ashvamedha sacrifices — Brahminical revival
- Patronised Bharhut Stupa (UP) — famous Buddhist gateway sculptures

### Kanva Dynasty (73–28 BCE)
- Replaced Shungas; also Brahminical; short-lived

### Indo-Greek Kings (200–100 BCE)
- Greek successors of Alexander's Bactrian kingdom; ruled NW India
- **Menander (Milinda)**: ~165–130 BCE; the most famous Indo-Greek king; converted to Buddhism; dialogues with Buddhist monk Nagasena recorded in **Milindapanha** ("Questions of Milinda")
- Introduced portrait coins — with realistic ruler faces; greatest contribution to Indian numismatics
- Combined Greek and Indian artistic traditions → basis of Gandhara art

### Saka (Scythians) (1st century BCE–4th century CE)
- Nomadic Central Asian people; established kingdoms in NW India and western Deccan
- **Rudradaman I** (~130–150 CE): most important Saka ruler; first inscription in polished Sanskrit (*Junagarh Inscription*); repaired Sudarshana Lake (built by Chandragupta Maurya's governor)
- **Western Kshatrapas** (branch of Sakas) ruled Gujarat/Rajasthan until conquered by Chandragupta II

### Kushana Empire (1st–3rd century CE)
- Central Asian people (Yuezhi/Tocharians); controlled Bactria → NW India → Gangetic plain
- **Kanishka** (~127–150 CE): most important Kushana king
  - Capital: Purushapura (modern Peshawar, Pakistan)
  - Patron of Buddhism; convened the **4th Buddhist Council** in Kashmir → Mahayana Buddhism developed
  - Built the Kanishka stupa at Peshawar (one of the tallest structures of antiquity)
  - Patronised **Gandhara art** (Greek-Buddhist sculpture style) and **Mathura art** (indigenous style)
  - Vasudeva (his minister) corresponded with Rome — Silk Road flourished
  - **Charaka** (medicine) and **Nagarjuna** (Buddhist philosopher) were in his court

### Satavahana Dynasty (1st century BCE–3rd century CE)
- Deccan; claimed descent from Brahmin origin
- **Simuka**: founder
- **Gautamiputra Satakarni**: greatest ruler; defeated Western Kshatrapas (Sakas); title "Destroyer of Sakas, Yavanas and Pahlavas"
- Capital: Pratishthana (Paithan, Maharashtra)
- Patronised Amaravati Stupa (Andhra Pradesh) — finest Buddhist art in southern India
- First rulers to issue coins with portrait of a ruler — significant numismatic contribution

## Part 2 — Gupta Empire (320–550 CE)

### Founding and Early Rulers

| Ruler | Reign | Key Event |
|-------|-------|----------|
| **Sri Gupta** | ~240–280 CE | Founder; obscure; possibly built Buddhist temple at Gaya |
| **Ghatotkacha** | ~280–319 CE | Son of Sri Gupta |
| **Chandragupta I** | 320–335 CE | First important ruler; title **Maharajadhiraja**; married Lichchhavi princess Kumaradevi → legitimacy; issued gold coins (dinara); **Gupta Era began 319/320 CE** |
| **Samudragupta** | 335–375 CE | "Napoleon of India"; greatest conqueror |
| **Chandragupta II** | 375–415 CE | **Vikramaditya**; Golden Age peak |
| Kumaragupta I | 415–455 CE | Founded **Nalanda University** |
| **Skandagupta** | 455–467 CE | Repelled first Huna invasion; after him empire declined |

### Samudragupta (335–375 CE) — "Napoleon of India"

The **Allahabad (Prayagraj) Pillar Inscription** composed by his court poet **Harishena** describes Samudragupta's conquests in four phases:
1. **Aryavarta** (N. India): uprooted 9 kings completely
2. **Dakshinapatha** (South India): defeated 12 kings but released them after submission (policy of capture-release)
3. **Forest kingdoms**: reduced to tributary status
4. **Frontier states and foreign rulers**: Sakas, Kushanas, Murundas, Ceylon sent embassies and tribute

Called "Napoleon of India" by historian V.A. Smith — for his extensive military campaigns across the subcontinent.

Also a **poet, musician, veena player** — coins show him playing the veena. Epithet: **Kaviraj** (king of poets).

### Chandragupta II Vikramaditya (375–415 CE) — The Golden Age

**Key achievements:**
- **Conquered Western Kshatrapas** (Sakas of Gujarat/Rajasthan) — extended empire to western sea coast; controlled Gujarat's ports → significant boost to overseas trade
- **Capital**: Pataliputra (primary); **Ujjain** (secondary — western capital and cultural hub)
- **Fa-Hien** visited his empire (399–414 CE) and described its prosperity
- Issued **silver coins** for Gujarat after conquering it — adapting to local traditions
- His court had the **Navratnas** (Nine Gems):

| Navratna | Field |
|---------|-------|
| **Kalidasa** | Poetry, drama (Shakuntala, Meghaduta, Raghuvamsha, Kumarsambhava, Vikramorvashiya) |
| **Aryabhata** | Mathematics, astronomy (Aryabhatiya) |
| **Varahamihira** | Astronomy, astrology (Panchasiddhantika, Brihat Samhita) |
| **Dhanvantari** | Medicine (Ayurveda) |
| **Amarasimha** | Lexicography (Amarakosha — Sanskrit dictionary) |
| **Vararuchi** | Grammar |
| **Vetala Bhatta** | Magic |
| **Ghatakarpara** | Poetry/Sculpture |
| **Shanku** | Architecture |

### Gupta Golden Age — Achievements

**Mathematics and Astronomy:**
- **Aryabhata** (476–550 CE): *Aryabhatiya* (499 CE)
  - π (pi) ≈ 3.1416 (more accurate than Archimedes)
  - Earth rotates on its axis (heliocentrism — 1000 years before Copernicus)
  - Solar/lunar eclipses explained scientifically (shadow of Moon/Earth)
  - Quadratic equations; sine tables
- **Brahmagupta** (7th century CE, slightly later): rules for working with zero and negative numbers; Brahmasphutasiddhanta
- **Decimal system** and the concept of **zero** (shunyata) systematised in this period

**Literature:**
- **Kalidasa**: greatest Sanskrit poet; lived in Chandragupta II's court; *Abhijnanasakuntala* (Shakuntala) translated by William Jones (1789) — inspired Goethe
- **Vishakhadatta**: *Mudrarakshasa* (play about Chandragupta Maurya)
- **Sudraka**: *Mrichchhakatika* (Little Clay Cart)

**Art and Architecture:**
- **Dashavatara Temple** (Deogarh, UP) — finest Gupta stone temple; Vishnu panels
- **Ajanta Caves** (Maharashtra) — Gupta-period paintings (Caves 16, 17, 19) — tempera technique; finest surviving Buddhist frescoes
- **Gupta sculpture**: naturalistic, serene; Buddha images from Mathura and Sarnath schools

**Medicine:**
- **Charaka Samhita** (internal medicine) and **Sushruta Samhita** (surgery — Sushruta described 300 surgical procedures and 120 surgical instruments; father of plastic surgery — described rhinoplasty/nose reconstruction)

## Part 3 — Decline of the Guptas

- **Huna invasions** (~460s–470s CE onwards): Toramana and Mihirakula invaded; Skandagupta repelled initial invasions but later Hunas overran NW India
- **Provincial governors' independence** (Pushyamitras, Maitrakas of Gujarat)
- **Feudalism growing**: land grants to officials led to decentralisation
- Last recognised Gupta emperor: Vishnugupta (c. 540 CE)

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Fa-Hien visited during Chandragupta II's reign** (not Ashoka, not Harsha). He was Chinese. He visited ~399–414 CE. He described peaceful, prosperous Gupta India. Hieun Tsang (also Chinese) visited later, during Harsha's reign (630–645 CE).

2. **"Napoleon of India" = Samudragupta** — coined by V.A. Smith. The Allahabad Pillar Inscription by Harishena is the primary source. Do not confuse with Alexander, Ashoka, or Akbar.

3. **Chandragupta I started the Gupta Era** in 319/320 CE — not Chandragupta II. The Gupta Era is the calendrical system used in Gupta inscriptions.

4. **Kalidasa's works**: Plays — Abhijnanasakuntala (Shakuntala), Vikramorvashiya, Malavikagnimitra. Poetry — Meghaduta (Cloud Messenger), Raghuvamsha, Kumarsambhava, Ritusamhara. Kalidasa was in Chandragupta II's court (Vikramaditya), NOT Chandragupta I's.

5. **Aryabhata wrote Aryabhatiya** (499 CE) — calculated π ≈ 3.1416; explained eclipses scientifically; stated earth rotates on its axis; gave value of year as 365.3586 days. He was from **Kusumapura** (Pataliputra/Patna area) and later Nalanda.

6. **Nalanda University** was founded by **Kumaragupta I** (~415 CE) and reached its peak under later Gupta rulers and Harsha. It attracted students from China, Korea, Japan, and Southeast Asia. Destroyed by Bakhtiyar Khilji in 1197 CE.

7. **Sushruta** = father of surgery; described 300 surgical procedures and 120 surgical instruments; pioneered rhinoplasty. **Charaka** = author of Charaka Samhita (internal medicine, 8 branches of Ayurveda). Both are associated with ancient India — Sushruta with Gupta/pre-Gupta era, Charaka with Kushana era (Kanishka's court).

8. **Menander (Milinda)** = the most famous Indo-Greek king; his philosophical dialogues with the Buddhist monk Nagasena form the *Milindapanha*. He converted to Buddhism. Do not confuse with Demetrius (another important Indo-Greek king).

9. **Kanishka's 4th Buddhist Council** in Kashmir → led to formal Mahayana-Hinayana split. He also patronised **Gandhara art** (Greco-Buddhist style: Buddha in Greek drapery and features) and **Charaka** (medicine) and **Asvaghosha** (author of Buddhacharita — first Sanskrit biography of Buddha).

10. **Rudradaman I (Saka)** wrote the first inscription entirely in polished Sanskrit (*Junagarh Inscription*, ~150 CE). All earlier royal inscriptions were in Prakrit. He also mentions repairing the Sudarshana Lake — showing continuity of infrastructure across centuries and dynasties.

11. **Gupta coinage**: primarily gold coins called **Dinara** (from Roman denarius — shows Roman trade contact). Chandragupta II issued silver coins for Gujarat. Samudragupta's coins show him playing the veena — unique portrait coins.

12. **Ajanta Caves**: Cave 1 (Bodhisattva Padmapani — most famous painting); Caves 16, 17, 19 (Gupta period). The paintings use natural mineral pigments and a plaster base. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Located in Aurangabad district, Maharashtra.

13. **Vikramaditya = Chandragupta II's epithet**. The Vikramaditya legend (king with nine gems, defeated Sakas, ruled from Ujjain) is historically associated with Chandragupta II. There was also a semi-legendary king Vikramaditya of Ujjain (from whom the Vikrama Samvat, 57 BCE, is reckoned) — different figure.

14. **Satavahanas**: ruled Deccan; their greatest king Gautamiputra Satakarni defeated Western Kshatrapas; they are described in Tamil and Jain sources as well as their own inscriptions. Important for Andhra Pradesh history. Amaravati Stupa (Andhra) represents the finest Satavahana Buddhist art.

15. **Gupta Golden Age covered only north India** — the Deccan and South India had their own kingdoms (Vakatakas, Pallavas, early Chalukyas) in the same period. The "Golden Age" label applies primarily to the Gangetic heartland.

16. **Skandagupta** (455–467 CE) repelled the Hunas but the effort exhausted the empire. The Huna king **Mihirakula** (early 6th century CE) was a particularly cruel ruler in northwest India — described as bloodthirsty by both Chinese (Hieun Tsang) and Indian sources. Yashodharman (a local king in Malwa) eventually defeated him.
