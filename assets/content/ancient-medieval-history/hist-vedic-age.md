# Vedic Age — Rigvedic & Later Vedic Period, Vedic Literature & Society

## The Hymns That Survived Three Thousand Years

Imagine a body of poetry composed sometime around 1500 BCE — composed orally, passed from teacher to student without a single word changed, memorised with such precision that modern scholars can reconstruct the exact pronunciation of words not spoken in two thousand years. The Rigveda is one of the oldest surviving literary works in any Indo-European language. It was not written down for centuries — the ancient Indians believed the act of writing it might corrupt it. So they developed elaborate systems of memorisation: entire schools dedicated to preserving every syllable, every pause, every tone, as exactly as when the rishis first sang them to the eternal fire.

These hymns — 1,028 of them, to gods like Indra, Agni, and Varuna — are the foundation of Indian civilisation. Understanding them means understanding the society that composed them: pastoral, tribal, cattle-rich, and slowly spreading eastward across the plains of northern India.

## Part 1 — The Vedic Corpus (Literature)

The term **Veda** means "knowledge" (Sanskrit). There are four Vedas:

| Veda | Content | Notable Feature |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **Rigveda** | 1,028 hymns (suktas) to deities; arranged in 10 mandalas (books) | Oldest; earliest Sanskrit text; mainly in Sapta Sindhu region |
| **Samaveda** | Melodic arrangements of Rigvedic hymns set to musical notes | "Veda of melodies/chants"; source of Indian classical music |
| **Yajurveda** | Prose formulas/rituals for sacrificial ceremonies | Two versions: Krishna (black) and Shukla (white) Yajurveda |
| **Atharvaveda** | Spells, charms, magical formulas; popular/domestic rituals | Reflects folk traditions; medicine, demonology |

### Vedic Literature: Layers

Each Veda has four layers of texts:

1. **Samhitas** — the core hymns/mantras (the Vedas themselves)
2. **Brahmanas** — prose commentaries on rituals and their meanings
3. **Aranyakas** — "forest texts" — transitional, contemplative
4. **Upanishads** — philosophical texts on Brahman, Atman, liberation

**Upanishads** (108 total; 10 principal): philosophical dialogues on the nature of reality. Key concepts: **Brahman** (universal soul/ultimate reality), **Atman** (individual soul), **Moksha** (liberation), **Maya** (illusion). Karl Schopenhauer called the Upanishads "the most rewarding and uplifting reading in the world."

**Other important Vedic texts:**
- **Vedangas** (6 auxiliary sciences): Shiksha (phonetics), Kalpa (rituals), Vyakarana (grammar), Nirukta (etymology), Chhanda (metre), Jyotisha (astronomy)
- **Panini's Ashtadhyayi**: 4th century BCE; the world's first scientific grammar (of Sanskrit)
- **Later Vedic texts**: Ramayana (Valmiki), Mahabharata (Vyasa/attributed); 18 Puranas

## Part 2 — The Rigvedic Period (c. 1500–1000 BCE)

### Geography
The Rigvedic people (early Aryans) lived in the **Sapta Sindhu** region — "land of seven rivers":
- Sindhu (Indus), Vitasta (Jhelum), Asikni (Chenab), Parushni (Ravi), Vipasa (Beas), Sutudri (Sutlej), and the mythical Saraswati

This is roughly modern Punjab (India + Pakistan). The Ganga is rarely mentioned; the Rigvedic people had not yet moved far east.

### Political Organisation
- **Jana** (tribe/people) → **Vis** (clan/settlement group) → **Grama** (village)
- King called **Rajan** — primarily a war-leader; not yet an absolute monarch
- **Sabhas** and **Samitis**: tribal assemblies that checked royal power; Sabha was smaller (elders); Samiti was larger (people's assembly)
- **Gramani**: village headman
- **Purohita**: chief priest; extremely important — advised the king, performed rituals for victory
- **Senani**: chief of the army

### Social Structure (Rigvedic)
- **Relatively flexible** — varna based on occupation, not entirely birth-based
- Four varnas mentioned in the **Purusha Sukta** (Rigveda, 10th Mandala): Brahmin (mouth), Kshatriya (arms), Vaishya (thighs), Shudra (feet)
- **Women had more freedom** than later: women could participate in sabhas; had choice in marriage (Svayamvara mentioned); female rishis (Gargi, Maitreyi, Lopamudra) composed Rigvedic hymns
- **Dasas/Dasyus**: indigenous people; described as "dark-skinned, snub-nosed" — conquered by Aryans; gradually absorbed

### Economy
- **Cattle-based** economy — wealth measured in cows; cattle raiding (Gavishthi = "search for cows") was a major activity
- **Agriculture** secondary — but present; ploughing (sira), threshing mentioned
- **No currency** — barter; cattle as medium of exchange; gold (*nishka*) as ornament/gift
- **Craftsmen**: carpenters (*taksha*), blacksmiths, weavers — mentioned but not prominently
- No large trade network; mostly self-sufficient pastoral communities

### Religion (Rigvedic)
- **Nature worship**: gods personify natural forces
- **Indra**: most important — god of thunder, storm, war; "Destroyer of cities" (Purandra)
- **Agni**: fire god; second most hymns; intermediary between humans and gods
- **Varuna**: upholder of cosmic order (Rita); god of sky and water; omniscient
- **Soma**: god of the sacred plant/drink; ecstatic ritual drink
- **Others**: Surya (Sun), Usha (Dawn), Mitra, Rudra, Vishnu (minor role here)
- **No idol worship** — prayers offered to personified natural forces
- **Yajna (sacrifice)** was the central ritual — fire sacrifice with offerings of ghee, milk, soma
- **Not a belief in temple worship or caste rigidity** — these came later

## Part 3 — The Later Vedic Period (c. 1000–600 BCE)

The Aryans gradually expanded eastward into the Ganga-Yamuna Doab and beyond. This expansion transformed their society, economy, and religion.

### Geography
- Moved from Punjab to **Aryavarta** (northern India between Himalayas and Vindhyas)
- **Painted Grey Ware (PGW)** pottery: archaeological marker of Later Vedic culture; found across the Ganga-Yamuna plains (c. 1200–600 BCE)
- Ganga and Yamuna now central; Sarasvati mentioned less

### Political Changes
- **Kingdoms (Janapadas) replaced tribal confederacies** — larger, more settled political units
- King became more powerful — claimed divine right; elaborate **Ashvamedha** (horse sacrifice) and **Rajasuya** (royal consecration) rituals to legitimise power
- **Sabha and Samiti declined** in power as monarchies strengthened
- **Brahmin-Kshatriya conflict** for dominance

### Social Changes (Hardening of Caste)
- **Varna system became hereditary and rigid** — birth-based, not occupation-based
- **Shudras banned from religious rites**; only the upper three varnas could undergo Upanayana (sacred thread ceremony)
- **Women's status declined** — no longer attended Sabhas; restricted to household; child marriage began; Sati (widow immolation) mentioned in Atharvaveda
- **Gotra system**: lineage groups; marriage within the same gotra prohibited
- **Ashrama system** formalised: Brahmacharya (student), Grihastha (householder), Vanaprastha (forest-dweller), Sannyasa (renunciant)

### Economic Changes
- **Iron technology adopted** (~1000 BCE) — iron ploughs increased agricultural productivity; forest clearing for farming; settled agriculture became primary
- **Black and Red Ware (BRW)** pottery replaced earlier types
- **New crafts**: iron smelting, weaving; beginning of surplus production
- **Trade expanded** along rivers; some long-distance commerce

### Religious Changes
- **Vedic gods changed in importance**: Prajapati (creator) became supreme; Vishnu and Rudra (= later Shiva) rose in importance; Indra declined
- **Rituals became elaborate and expensive** — priests monopolised religion; only Brahmins could perform complex yajnas correctly
- **Philosophical inquiry** grew — led to Upanishads; questions of Brahman, Atman, rebirth

### Later Vedic Texts
- **Shatapatha Brahmana**: most important Brahmana text; describes elaborate ritual
- **Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Chandogya Upanishad**: oldest/most important Upanishads
- **Manusmriti** (compiled later but tradition placed in Vedic era): law code; codified caste duties

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Rigveda has 1,028 hymns (suktas) in 10 mandalas**. The 2nd–7th mandalas are the oldest (family books); the 1st, 8th, 9th, 10th are later additions. The 10th mandala contains the Purusha Sukta (origin of the four varnas).

2. **Samaveda is the Veda of melodies** — it set Rigvedic hymns to musical notes; is considered the origin of Indian classical music. Yajurveda is for ritual formulas; Atharvaveda is for charms/spells.

3. **Indra is the most important god in the Rigveda** — more hymns are dedicated to Indra than to any other deity. Agni is second. In the later Vedic period, Prajapati became supreme, while Vishnu and Rudra (Shiva) rose.

4. **Gargi and Maitreyi were famous female philosophers** of the Vedic age — both appear in the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad debating philosophical questions. This shows women had intellectual standing in early Vedic society (which later declined).

5. **Sabha** (smaller, elders/nobles) and **Samiti** (larger, popular assembly) were the two Vedic democratic assemblies. Both could limit the king's power in the Rigvedic period. They declined in the Later Vedic period as monarchies strengthened.

6. **Ashvamedha** (horse sacrifice) = royal ritual for territorial conquest and legitimacy. **Rajasuya** = royal consecration ceremony. **Vajapeya** = chariot race ritual. These three are the most important Vedic royal rituals tested in SSC.

7. **Painted Grey Ware (PGW)** is the archaeological marker of Later Vedic culture (~1200–600 BCE). Found in the Ganga-Yamuna Doab. Do not confuse with Black and Red Ware (BRW), which also appears in this period but is associated with different contexts.

8. **Sapta Sindhu** = "seven rivers" — the geographic heartland of Rigvedic civilisation. These seven rivers are in the Punjab region. The Ganga is conspicuously absent from early Rigvedic geography — this changed in the Later Vedic period.

9. **The four Vedas were not written down for centuries** — preserved entirely orally through the guru-shishya tradition. The oral tradition was so rigorous that scholars can reconstruct exact Vedic pronunciation today using preserved texts.

10. **Upanishads** (108; 10 principal ones) are the philosophical backbone of Vedanta philosophy. The equation Brahman = Atman is the central insight. Key phrase: "Tat tvam asi" (That art thou — Chandogya Upanishad).

11. **Purohita** (chief priest) was arguably the most powerful person in early Vedic society — more important than the senani (military chief) in many respects. The king needed the purohita to perform essential rituals. The king without a purohita was considered impotent.

12. **Cattle = wealth in the Rigvedic period**. Wars were fought over cattle (*Gavishthi* = search for cattle/cows). The cow became sacred — not as a purely religious matter initially, but because cattle were the measure of all wealth and their slaughter would impoverish the community.

13. **Iron was introduced in India around 1000 BCE** (Later Vedic period). Iron ploughs enabled forest clearance and intensive agriculture in the Gangetic plains — the material foundation of the later Mahajanapadas and Mauryan Empire.

14. **Panini's Ashtadhyayi** (4th century BCE) — the world's first scientific grammar, systematising Sanskrit. It has 3,959 sutras (rules) that can generate virtually all Sanskrit sentences. Described by modern linguists as one of the greatest intellectual achievements of antiquity.

15. **Women's status declined in the Later Vedic period** — from participation in Sabhas and philosophical debate (Gargi) to restrictions on education, re-marriage, and independent religious practice. Child marriage began; Sati references appear in Atharvaveda.

16. **Vedic religion had no idol worship and no temples** — the Rigvedic Aryans worshipped at fire altars; gods were personified natural forces. Temple worship, idol worship, and the great bhakti traditions emerged much later. This is a key distinction from later Hinduism.
