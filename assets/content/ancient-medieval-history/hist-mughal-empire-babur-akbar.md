# Mughal Empire — Babur, Humayun, Sher Shah & Akbar

## The Diary of a Conqueror

Babur was 12 years old when he became king of Fergana (Uzbekistan). He spent the next 20 years losing it, winning it back, losing it again — always running from a bigger enemy (the Uzbek chief Shaibani Khan). By 1525, he was king of Kabul — a cold, mountainous kingdom — and was already 42 years old, an age when medieval people expected to die soon. He had made five previous raids on India. Now he decided to try for the real prize.

On April 21, 1526, at **Panipat** in Haryana, Babur's army of 12,000 faced Ibrahim Lodi's force of 100,000 (with 1,000 war elephants). What Babur had that Ibrahim did not: **cannons** (the first significant use of artillery in Indian warfare) and the **tulughma tactic** (flanking manoeuvre). By afternoon, Ibrahim was dead, and Babur was master of Hindustan. He described the battlefield in his diary: "we encamped on the field... I myself the next morning, with some begs and chiefs, set out to look at the dead... I surveyed all the battle-field on horseback, and Ibrahim's body was discovered among the slain. He had been killed near the centre."

He wrote all of this down — this king wrote like a novelist. The **Baburnama** is one of the great autobiographies in world literature.

## Part 1 — Babur (1526–1530 CE)

- **Full name**: Zahiruddin Muhammad Babur
- **Born**: 1483 CE, Fergana (modern Uzbekistan); Timurid prince (descendant of Timur on father's side, Genghis Khan on mother's side)
- **Capital**: Agra (after 1526)
- **Language**: Chagatai Turkic; also wrote in Persian

### Three Decisive Battles of Babur

| Battle | Year | Against | Outcome |
|--------|------|---------|---------|
| **First Battle of Panipat** | April 1526 | **Ibrahim Lodi** (last Delhi Sultan) | Babur wins; uses **artillery + tulughma**; Delhi Sultanate ends; Mughal Empire begins |
| **Battle of Khanwa** | March 1527 | **Rana Sanga** (Sangram Singh of Mewar) — the most powerful Rajput chief | Babur wins; announced **Jihad**; titled himself **Ghazi** (warrior of faith) after this battle |
| **Battle of Ghaghra** | May 1529 | **Afghan chiefs** (Mahmud Lodi, who claimed Delhi) + Bengal Sultanate | Babur wins; consolidates control of Bihar and Bengal border |

### Baburnama
- **Written in**: Chagatai Turkic; later translated into Persian (*Baburnama* or *Tuzuk-i-Baburi*); then translated into English
- One of the first true autobiographies in Eastern literature
- Describes battles, gardens (he loved gardens — introduced the **charbagh garden** style to India), wine, friends, nature, and India's climate (which he hated — too hot)
- **Charbagh style**: four-quadrant garden divided by water channels; Persian concept; later used in Humayun's Tomb, Taj Mahal

### Assessment of Babur
- He founded the Mughal Empire but did not live long to consolidate it
- Died 1530 CE in Agra; buried in Kabul (as per his wish)
- Loved gardens, poetry, and melons — wrote that India had no good fruits except mangoes

## Part 2 — Humayun (1530–1540 CE and 1555–1556 CE)

- **Full name**: Nasiruddin Humayun; Babur's eldest son
- **Reign**: 1530–1540 (first reign); 1555–1556 (restored after 15 years exile)

### Humayun's First Reign and Defeat

**Key weakness**: divided the empire among brothers (Kabul to Kamran, Sindh to Askari, Sambhal to Hindal) — this fragmented power and caused civil war

**Sher Shah Suri** (Sur Afghan chief):
- **Battle of Chausa (1539)**: Sher Shah defeated Humayun; Humayun barely escaped by swimming across the Ganga (a water carrier named Nizam held the inflated water skin that saved him — Humayun later made him king of Delhi for one day as thanks)
- **Battle of Kanauj (1540)**: Sher Shah decisively defeated Humayun; Humayun fled India; spent 15 years in exile (Sindh → Iran → Kabul)

### Humayun's Exile (1540–1555)
- Found refuge at the court of the **Safavid Shah Tahmasp of Iran** (gave him 12,000 soldiers in exchange for Kandahar)
- Married **Hamida Banu Begum** in Iran — their son **Akbar** was born in 1542 in Amarkot (Sindh)
- Reconquered Kabul from his brothers; eventually reconquered Delhi (1555)

### Humayun's Death (1556)
- Fell from the stairs of his library (Sher Mandal, Delhi Purana Qila) while carrying books
- Died from the fall — only 6 months after recovering the empire
- His tomb (**Humayun's Tomb**, Delhi, built by Hamida Banu Begum in 1565–1572) is considered the **precursor/prototype of the Taj Mahal**; UNESCO World Heritage Site

## Part 3 — Sher Shah Suri (1540–1545 CE)

One of the greatest administrators in Indian history — though his reign was only 5 years.

- **Full name**: Farid Khan (later Sher Khan → Sher Shah)
- **Dynasty**: Sur (Afghan)
- Defeated a lion single-handedly in youth → earned title "Sher" (lion)

### Sher Shah's Reforms

**Administration:**
- Divided empire into 47 **sarkars** (districts), each divided into **parganas** (subdistricts)
- Established efficient revenue system: measured land, assessed tax at fair rates (generally 1/3 of produce)
- **Land measurement**: introduced the *bigha* as standard unit; detailed *cabulator* (crop register)

**Communication and Trade:**
- Built **Grand Trunk Road (GTR)** — from Sonargaon (Bengal) to Peshawar: ~2,400 km; lined with trees for shade; sarais (rest houses) every 2 kos; postal system (horse relay)
- "The road is long and the journey is difficult, but Sher Shah Suri built inns along the way" — Mughal and British empires both used and extended the GTR; it is still used today as NH-44

**Currency:**
- Introduced the **rupiya (rupee)** — silver coin of fixed weight and purity; became the standard currency of India (and the basis of the modern rupee)
- Also issued **dam** (copper) and **muhar** (gold) coins

**Military:**
- Maintained a **branding system** (*dagh*) for horses to prevent substitution
- Maintained a **descriptive roll** (*chehra*) for soldiers

**Death (1545)**: killed by an accidental gunpowder explosion at the siege of Kalinjar fort (Bundelkhand, MP)

## Part 4 — Akbar (1556–1605 CE): The Greatest Mughal

- **Full name**: Jalaluddin Muhammad Akbar
- **Born**: October 1542, Amarkot (Sindh) — while Humayun was in exile
- **Reign**: 1556–1605 CE (49 years — longest Mughal reign)
- **Regent initially**: **Bairam Khan** (until 1560; tutor-guardian; fought Panipat II for Akbar)

### Key Battles of Akbar

| Battle | Year | Against | Outcome |
|--------|------|---------|---------|
| **Second Battle of Panipat** | Nov 5, 1556 | **Hemu** (Hindu minister of Adil Shah Sur; had captured Delhi) | Akbar/Bairam Khan wins; Delhi secured; Hemu captured and executed |
| **Battle of Haldighati** | June 1576 | **Rana Pratap Singh** of Mewar | Mughal forces (led by Raja Man Singh) win tactically; but Rana Pratap escapes and continues resistance from forests |

### Akbar's Administrative Reforms

**Mansabdari System:**
- All military and civil officers held a *mansab* (rank) — expressed in two numbers: *zat* (personal rank/salary) and *sawar* (number of cavalry to be maintained)
- Ranks ranged from 10 to 10,000 (later 12,000 for princes)
- Not hereditary — appointments by Emperor; abolished feudal independence
- Created a **unified civil-military bureaucracy**

**Revenue Reform (Todar Mal's Bandobast / Ain-i-Dahsala):**
- **Raja Todar Mal** (finance minister): 10-year (*dahsala*) average of produce and prices used to fix revenue
- Land classified into 4 types based on fertility and cultivation regularity
- Cash payments preferred over kind; standardised weights/measures
- Considered the most scientific pre-modern revenue system in India

**Rajput Policy:**
- **Married Rajput princesses**: Akbar married Harkha Bai (daughter of Raja Bharmal of Amer/Amber, Rajasthan) — this Rajput princess is often called Jodha Bai (though the name is disputed by historians)
- Their son was **Jahangir** (Salim)
- Employed Rajput chiefs as Mansabdars; gave them high ranks without requiring religious conversion
- Key Rajput allies: Raja Man Singh (Amber), Raja Birbal, Raja Todar Mal, Raja Bhagwan Das
- **Mewar (Rana Pratap)** was the main Rajput kingdom that refused to submit; Akbar fought them but never fully conquered Mewar

**Religious Policy:**
- **1562**: Abolished *jizyah* (poll tax on non-Muslims) — restored later by Aurangzeb
- **1563**: Abolished pilgrimage tax on Hindus
- **1575**: Built **Ibadat Khana** (House of Worship) at Fatehpur Sikri — for religious discussions among scholars of all faiths
- **1582**: Announced **Din-i-Ilahi** (Divine Faith) — an eclectic spiritual order with Akbar at center; combined ideas from Hinduism, Zoroastrianism, Islam, Christianity; followed by very few; **NOT a religion**, just a spiritual court order
- **Sulh-i-Kul** = "peace with all" — his principle of equal respect for all religions

### Akbar's Court — Navratnas (Nine Gems)

| Navratna | Role |
|----------|------|
| **Abul Fazl** | Chief minister; wrote *Akbarnama* (biography) and *Ain-i-Akbari* (administrative manual) |
| **Faizi** | Poet Laureate; Abul Fazl's brother; Persian poetry |
| **Raja Birbal** | Wit and counsel; the only Hindu to accept Din-i-Ilahi; died in Yusufzai expedition 1586 |
| **Raja Todar Mal** | Finance minister; architect of revenue system |
| **Raja Man Singh** | Military commander (Amer/Amber Rajput); led Battle of Haldighati |
| **Khan-i-Khana (Abdur Rahim)** | Military commander; poet (*dohas* in Hindi still famous); son of Bairam Khan |
| **Miyan Tansen** | Greatest classical musician of the Mughal era; founder of Dhrupad; sang Ragas; one of the originators of Hindustani classical music tradition |
| **Mulla Do Piyaza** | Advisor; famous for wit in folklore |
| **Hakim Humam** | Physician |

### Fatehpur Sikri
- Built by Akbar (~1571–1585) near Agra in honour of Sufi saint **Salim Chishti** (whose blessings preceded the birth of Akbar's heir Salim/Jahangir)
- Grand complex: **Buland Darwaza** (52.4 m — highest gateway in Asia; built to commemorate Gujarat conquest 1572); **Panch Mahal**; **Diwan-i-Aam**; **Diwan-i-Khas** (single pillar with a circular platform for the emperor — where Akbar held religious debates); **Jodha Bai's Palace**
- Abandoned around 1585 (water supply issues; also Afghan frontier campaigns needed presence in Lahore)
- UNESCO World Heritage Site

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **First Battle of Panipat (1526)**: Babur vs Ibrahim Lodi. Babur's victory marked the end of the Delhi Sultanate and the founding of the Mughal Empire. Key novelty: Babur used **artillery (cannons)** and the **tulughma (flanking) tactic** — first significant use of gunpowder artillery in Indian warfare.

2. **Battle of Khanwa (1527)**: Babur vs **Rana Sanga** (Sangram Singh, king of Mewar). This was actually a harder battle than Panipat — Rana Sanga commanded more troops. Babur declared jihad and titled himself Ghazi after winning. **Rana Sanga ≠ Rana Pratap** (Rana Pratap fought Akbar in 1576 at Haldighati — 50 years later).

3. **Baburnama** written in Chagatai Turkic — one of the earliest autobiographies in Asiatic literature. Translated into Persian (*Tuzuk-i-Baburi*) during Akbar's reign by Abdur Rahim Khan-i-Khana. It is also notable for its nature descriptions, wine appreciation, and grief for lost kingdoms.

4. **Sher Shah Suri introduced the rupee** — the silver coin that became the currency of India (and is still the currency today as the Indian Rupee). He also built the **Grand Trunk Road** (~1540s) from Bengal to Peshawar; it is still NH-44 in India.

5. **Sher Shah defeated Humayun** at: (1) Battle of Chausa (1539) and (2) Battle of Kanauj (1540). After Kanauj, Humayun spent **15 years in exile** — in Sindh, then Iran (Safavid court), then Kabul. Akbar was born in this exile period (1542, Amarkot, Sindh).

6. **Second Battle of Panipat (1556)**: Akbar (with regent Bairam Khan) vs **Hemu** (Vikramaditya — Hindu Hindu minister/general of Adil Shah Sur who had captured Delhi). Hemu was wounded by an arrow, captured, and executed by Bairam Khan/Akbar. This battle secured Mughal control over Delhi.

7. **Mansabdari System**: Akbar's unique administrative-military hierarchy; *zat* = personal rank; *sawar* = cavalry contingent; ranks 10 to 10,000; not hereditary; all appointments by Emperor. This made Akbar's empire more meritocratic than the feudal Delhi Sultanate.

8. **Ain-i-Dahsala** = Todar Mal's 10-year revenue system (dahsala = ten-year average). This is also called *Zabt* or *Bandobast*. It measured land, classified soil fertility, and set fair tax rates in cash. The most scientific pre-modern revenue system in India.

9. **Din-i-Ilahi (1582)** was NOT a religion — it was an eclectic spiritual court order/fraternity. Very few accepted it (Birbal was one). Akbar never made it compulsory. The **Ibadat Khana** (1575) was earlier — a house of religious debate where scholars argued; initially open to Muslims only, later to all faiths.

10. **Buland Darwaza** (Fatehpur Sikri, 1575): built to commemorate Akbar's conquest of Gujarat. 52.4 meters high — tallest gateway in Asia. Fatehpur Sikri itself was built in honour of Sufi saint **Sheikh Salim Chishti** (of the Chishti order), near whose cell the city was constructed.

11. **Tansen** (Miyan Tansen) = greatest Mughal-era classical musician; originally at the court of Raja Ramchandra of Rewa, brought to Akbar's court. Associated with **Dhrupad** style. Legends say his Raga Deepak could light lamps and Raga Megh Malhar could bring rain. He is buried in Gwalior near his guru Hazrat Ghaus Muhammad.

12. **Haldighati (1576)**: Akbar's forces (led by Raja Man Singh) vs **Rana Pratap Singh** of Mewar. Rana Pratap escaped on his horse **Chetak** (the famous Rajput warhorse story). He continued guerrilla warfare from the Aravalli forests for the rest of his life; never surrendered. Mewar never fully submitted to Akbar.

13. **Abul Fazl** (Akbar's court historian) wrote: (1) **Akbarnama** — 3-volume biography of Akbar; (2) **Ain-i-Akbari** — administrative/statistical manual (volume 3 of Akbarnama); the most detailed account of Mughal administration. Abul Fazl was killed in 1602 by **Bir Singh Bundela** — hired by Prince Salim (Jahangir) who resented Abul Fazl's influence over Akbar.

14. **Humayun's Tomb (Delhi)**: built by Hamida Banu Begum (Humayun's wife); completed 1572; designed by Persian architect Mirak Mirza Ghiyas; first example of the garden-tomb (*charbagh*) in India; **precursor of the Taj Mahal**. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

15. **Bairam Khan** = Akbar's regent (1556–1560); won the Second Battle of Panipat for Akbar; Turkic Shia Muslim of great loyalty to the Mughal family. When Akbar came of age (1560), Bairam Khan was dismissed; later assassinated by an Afghan in Gujarat while on pilgrimage. His son **Abdur Rahim Khan-i-Khana** became one of Akbar's Navratnas.

16. **Akbar abolished jizyah in 1562** — the poll tax on non-Muslims (dhimmis) required by Islamic law. This was one of his most significant religious policy decisions. Aurangzeb **reimposed jizyah in 1679** — this is a directly tested contrast in SSC questions on Mughal religious policy.
