# Bhakti & Sufi Movements — Saints, Songs & Spiritual Revolution

## The Weaver Who Changed India

Kabir was a Muslim weaver from Varanasi. He called himself the child of both Ram and Allah. He mocked priests, pandits, and mullahs with equal relish. "I have been thinking," he said, "if God lives in the mosque, then who owns the open fields? If Ram lives in the idol at the temple, who lives in the heart?" Kabir's *dohas* — couplets in Hindi, memorised by millions — cut through every religious boundary he encountered. He was claimed after his death by both Hindus (who wanted to cremate him) and Muslims (who wanted to bury him). According to legend, when they lifted the cloth from his body, they found only flowers — half cremated, half buried.

This story — almost certainly legendary — captures something real about the Bhakti movement: it was a spiritual force so powerful that neither side could entirely contain or claim it.

## Part 1 — Origins of the Bhakti Movement

The word **Bhakti** means "devotion" — specifically, intense personal love for a personal God, bypassing priestly intermediaries, caste, and ritual.

### Origins in South India (6th–9th century CE)

The Bhakti movement began in South India with the **Alvars** (Vaishnava saints) and **Nayanmars** (Shaiva saints).

| Saint Group | Tradition | Period | Number | Language |
|-------------|-----------|--------|--------|----------|
| **Alvars** | Vaishnavism (Vishnu) | 6th–9th century CE | 12 | Tamil |
| **Nayanmars** | Shaivism (Shiva) | 6th–8th century CE | 63 | Tamil |

- **Alvars**: composed the *Nalayira Divya Prabandham* (4,000 divine verses) — equivalent of the Vedas in Tamil devotional tradition
- Most famous Alvar: **Nammalvar** (considered the greatest); also Andal (the only woman Alvar) and Thirumangai Alvar
- Most famous Nayanmar: **Appar, Sambandhar, Sundarar, Manikkavasagar** — their hymns compiled in *Tirumurai* and *Tevaram*

### Philosophical Foundation

| Philosopher | Period | School | Key Idea |
|-------------|--------|--------|---------|
| **Adi Shankaracharya** | 788–820 CE | **Advaita Vedanta** | Non-dualism: Brahman = only reality; world is Maya (illusion); Atman = Brahman; established 4 mathas (monasteries) |
| **Ramanujacharya** | 1017–1137 CE | **Vishishtadvaita** | Qualified non-dualism: God (Vishnu) is real; souls and world are his body; Bhakti = path to God |
| **Madhvacharya** | 1238–1317 CE | **Dvaita** | Dualism: God and soul are eternally distinct; Vaishnavism; strict bhakti |

Ramanuja's philosophy gave the strongest theological foundation for Bhakti by making devotion (bhakti) the primary path to moksha, and by insisting that God (Vishnu) cares about devotees regardless of caste.

## Part 2 — Bhakti Movement in North India (12th–17th century CE)

### Key North Indian Bhakti Saints

| Saint | Period | Region | God | Language | Key Works / Facts |
|-------|--------|--------|-----|----------|------------------|
| **Ramananda** | c. 1360–1440 CE | Varanasi | Rama | Hindi | Bridge between South and North Bhakti; accepted disciples of all castes including Kabir; "Caste, creed, or sect — none matters" |
| **Kabir** | c. 1440–1518 CE | Varanasi | Nirgun (formless) | Hindi (Avadhi/Braj) | Weaver; disciple of Ramananda; dohas (couplets); *Bijak*; rejected caste, idol worship, pilgrimage; influenced both Hindus and Muslims |
| **Guru Nanak** | 1469–1539 CE | Punjab (Talwandi) | Ik Onkar | Punjabi | Founder of Sikhism; rejected caste and idolatry; first of 10 Sikh Gurus; composed *Japji Sahib*; *Guru Granth Sahib* includes his compositions |
| **Mirabai** | c. 1498–1547 CE | Rajasthan | Krishna | Rajasthani/Hindi | Rajput princess; passionate devotee of Krishna; bhajans (devotional songs); "Mira ke prabhu Giridhar nagar" |
| **Surdas** | c. 1478–1583 CE | Mathura/Agra | Krishna | Braj Bhasha | Blind poet; *Sur Sagar* — 100,000 verses; devotion to child Krishna; disciple of Vallabhacharya |
| **Tulsidas** | 1511–1623 CE | Varanasi | Rama | Awadhi Hindi | *Ramcharitmanas* (Hindi Ramayana); one of the most influential texts in Indian history |
| **Chaitanya Mahaprabhu** | 1486–1534 CE | Bengal (Navadwip) | Krishna | Bengali/Sanskrit | Krishna bhakti; ecstatic devotion (kirtan/dancing); founded **Gaudiya Vaishnavism**; International Society for Krishna Consciousness (ISKCON) traces lineage to him |
| **Tukaram** | 1609–1649 CE | Maharashtra | Vitthal (Vishnu) | Marathi | *Abhangas* (devotional poems); Warkari movement; against caste hierarchy |
| **Vallabhacharya** | 1479–1531 CE | Gujarat/Braj | Krishna | Sanskrit/Braj | **Shuddhadvaita** (pure non-dualism); *Pushti Marga* (path of grace); guru of Surdas |
| **Namdev** | c. 1270–1350 CE | Maharashtra | Vitthal | Marathi | Tailor; Warkari tradition; compositions in *Guru Granth Sahib* |
| **Eknath** | 1533–1599 CE | Maharashtra | Vitthal | Marathi | Social reformer; translated Ramayana into Marathi; accepted Untouchables |

### Key Features of North Indian Bhakti

1. **Saguna Bhakti**: worship of God with attributes (form) — like Kabir's Rama, Mirabai's Krishna, Tulsidas's Rama
2. **Nirguna Bhakti**: worship of formless, attribute-less God — like Kabir, Guru Nanak
3. **Rejection of caste**: all major saints attacked caste discrimination; accepted disciples from lower castes
4. **Use of vernacular languages**: Hindi, Braj, Awadhi, Marathi, Bengali — making devotion accessible to all
5. **Opposition to ritual**: criticised elaborate temple rituals, animal sacrifice, pilgrimage as unnecessary
6. **Social equality**: women saints (Mirabai, Andal, Akka Mahadevi) broke gender barriers

## Part 3 — Sufi Movement in India

### What is Sufism?

**Sufism** (*tasawwuf*) = the mystical dimension of Islam. Sufis sought direct personal experience of God (*Allah*) through love, devotion, and spiritual practice — rather than through mere adherence to religious law. The name derives from *suf* (wool) — early Muslim mystics wore coarse woollen garments as a sign of asceticism.

Key Sufi concept: **Fana** (annihilation of the self in God); **Baqa** (permanent existence in God); love (*ishq*) as the path to God.

### Sufi Orders (Silsilas) in India

| Order (Silsila) | Key Figure in India | Period | Base | Notable Feature |
|-----------------|---------------------|--------|------|----------------|
| **Chishti** | **Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti** | ~1141–1230 CE | **Ajmer** (Rajasthan) | Most popular in India; emphasis on poverty, music (*sama* = qawwali), service to poor; Dargah at Ajmer draws millions |
| **Chishti** | **Nizamuddin Auliya** | 1238–1325 CE | Delhi | Most famous Chishti saint; "Mehboob-e-Ilahi" (beloved of God); disciple of Fariduddin Ganjshakar; Amir Khusrau's guru; buried at Nizamuddin (Delhi) |
| **Chishti** | **Fariduddin Ganjshakar (Baba Farid)** | 1173–1265 CE | Pakpattan (Punjab) | Disciple of Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki; compositions in *Guru Granth Sahib* — Guru Nanak met his spiritual heir |
| **Suhrawardi** | Sheikh Bahauddin Zakariya | 1170–1267 CE | Multan | Accepted gifts; less austere than Chishti |
| **Qadiri** | Shah Abdur Razaq | 15th century | Sind | Less influential in India; later important in Bijapur |
| **Naqshbandi** | Sheikh Ahmad Sirhindi | 1564–1624 CE | Punjab | **Mujaddid Alf Sani** ("Reformer of the Second Millennium"); opposed Akbar's Din-i-Ilahi and Dara Shikoh's syncretic ideas; influential on Aurangzeb |

### Amir Khusrau (1253–1325 CE)
- **Disciple of Nizamuddin Auliya**; born in Etah (UP); father was a Turkic soldier, mother was an Indian
- Called "Tuti-e-Hind" (Parrot of India) — greatest poet-musician of Delhi Sultanate era
- **Invented/popularised**: *Qawwali* (Sufi devotional music); *Sitar* (arguably); *Khayal* style of classical music; *Tabla* (disputed but traditional attribution); *Ghazal* in Braj and Khari Boli
- Wrote in Persian, Hindavi (early Hindi), and mixed them innovatively
- His Hindi/Braj compositions are among the earliest examples of literary Hindi

### Impact of Sufi Movement

- **Integration**: Sufi dargahs became spaces where Hindus and Muslims met; promoted social harmony
- **Vernacular literature**: Sufi poets used local languages → development of Hindi, Punjabi, Sindhi, Bengali literature
- **Conversion**: Sufi saints' personality and message attracted many low-caste Hindus to Islam — a more important conversion mechanism than conquest
- **Music**: Qawwali, sama, and later ghazal — all rooted in Sufi practice

## Part 4 — Social Impact of Bhakti-Sufi Movements

1. **Challenged caste hierarchy**: both movements explicitly rejected birth-based discrimination
2. **Elevated women's status**: women saints like Mirabai, Andal, Akka Mahadevi (Kannada), Lal Ded (Kashmir — Sufi-Bhakti) were revered
3. **Development of vernacular languages**: Hindi (Kabir, Tulsidas, Mirabai, Surdas), Marathi (Tukaram, Eknath), Bengali (Chaitanya), Punjabi (Guru Nanak) — all developed as literary languages through Bhakti/Sufi poetry
4. **Hindu-Muslim synthesis**: the shared space of devotion allowed cultural exchange; many Hindu-Muslim syncretic traditions emerged (especially in Bengal and Punjab)
5. **Limited political impact**: the movements were spiritual, not political; they did not directly challenge caste or property structures at a systemic level

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Ramananda** is the crucial link between South and North Bhakti — he was trained in the Vaishnava Bhakti of the south and preached in the north in Hindi, accepting disciples regardless of caste. **Kabir was his disciple** — this chain (Ramanuja → Ramananda → Kabir) is a direct SSC question.

2. **Shankaracharya's Advaita**: Brahman alone is real; world is Maya (illusion); individual soul (Atman) = Brahman. He established 4 mathas: Sringeri (South), Dwaraka (West), Puri (East), Joshi Math (North). He wrote commentaries on Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita, Brahma Sutras.

3. **Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita** is the philosophical basis for Vaishnava Bhakti — it said God (Vishnu/Narayana) is real, souls are real, but they exist as God's "body." Bhakti (devotion) is the path to moksha. This theologised popular Bhakti.

4. **Kabir's *Bijak*** = his collected sayings and dohas. He rejected the Quran, Vedas, pilgrimage, fasting, caste — all forms of external religion. He is one of the clearest examples of **Nirguna Bhakti** (formless God).

5. **Tulsidas wrote *Ramcharitmanas*** in Awadhi (a dialect of Hindi) — NOT Sanskrit. The original Ramayana by Valmiki is in Sanskrit. Tulsidas also wrote Vinaya Patrika, Kavitavali, Hanuman Chalisa.

6. **Chaitanya Mahaprabhu** introduced **kirtan** (communal singing and dancing before God) as a devotional practice — which became central to Bengali Vaishnava culture and later the Hare Krishna movement (ISKCON). He is regarded as an avatar of Vishnu by followers.

7. **Guru Nanak (1469–1539)** = first Sikh Guru; founded Sikhism; rejected idol worship, caste, and meaningless ritual; composed the *Japji Sahib* (foundational Sikh prayer). The 10 Sikh Gurus span from Guru Nanak (1469) to Guru Gobind Singh (1666–1708). Guru Granth Sahib = eternal Guru.

8. **Khwaja Moinuddin Chishti** (Ajmer) = most revered Sufi saint in India. His dargah in Ajmer is visited by millions of Hindu and Muslim pilgrims every year. The Urs (death anniversary) is a major annual event. He came to India with Muhammad Ghori's army.

9. **Nizamuddin Auliya** (Delhi) = greatest Chishti saint; refused to meet any Sultan (maintained independence); performed *sama* (listening to music as devotion); buried at Nizamuddin Dargah (Delhi). **Amir Khusrau** was his devoted disciple and died the same year (1325).

10. **Amir Khusrau** = "Tuti-e-Hind" (Parrot of India). He is credited with creating or popularising **qawwali** music. He wrote in Persian, Hindawi, and mixed both. His poetic riddles and games are still sung in North India. He served seven Sultans of Delhi.

11. **Mirabai** was a Rajput princess from Merta (Rajasthan); married Bhoj Raj (son of Rana Sanga of Mewar); dedicated her life to Krishna; rejected royal life; her bhajans are still sung; she reportedly died merging with the idol of Krishna at Dwarka.

12. **Tukaram** and the **Warkari movement** (Maharashtra): a tradition of pilgrimage to Pandharpur (Vitthal temple) twice a year — still practiced by millions. The Warkaris include saints from all castes; Tukaram's *abhangas* criticised Brahmin privilege directly.

13. **Sheikh Ahmad Sirhindi (Naqshbandi)** was opposed to Akbar's syncretic policies and Din-i-Ilahi. He argued for a return to strict Islamic law and influenced Aurangzeb's more orthodox approach. He is called Mujaddid Alf Sani (renewer of the second Islamic millennium).

14. **Sufism came to India via**: (1) the Arab conquest of Sindh (711 CE); (2) Ghaznavid invasions (11th century); (3) the Delhi Sultanate period. Sufi orders (*silsilas*) had chains of master-disciple transmission; the *silsila* = the order/lineage.

15. **Alvars and Nayanmars** = South Indian Bhakti saints. Alvars (12) worshipped Vishnu; Nayanmars (63) worshipped Shiva. Their compositions were compiled under **Nathamuni** (Alvars' *Nalayira Divya Prabandham* = 4,000 verses) and patronised by Chola kings.

16. **Akka Mahadevi** (12th century, Karnataka) = a Veerashaiva (Lingayat) Bhakti saint; addressed her poems to Lord Shiva as her husband; rejected social conventions; her *vachanas* (prose-poems) are in Kannada. The Lingayat/Veerashaiva movement founded by **Basavanna** (~1131–1167 CE) was a major Bhakti/reform movement in Karnataka.
