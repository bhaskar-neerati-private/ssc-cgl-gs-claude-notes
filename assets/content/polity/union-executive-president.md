## The Flywheel, Not the Rubber Stamp

The President of India is often dismissed in casual conversation as a "rubber stamp" — a ceremonial figurehead who nods to whatever the elected government decides. That description is partly true and dangerously misleading at the same time.

A rubber stamp is passive, mechanical, interchangeable. The President is better understood as a **constitutional flywheel** — normally spinning quietly in the background, providing continuity and stability to the system while the PM and Cabinet run day-to-day governance. But during a constitutional crisis — a hung parliament, a government that has lost its majority and refuses to resign, a state descending into breakdown — the flywheel becomes the most critical component in the machine. The President then has genuine discretionary power, and who occupies that office can make a decisive difference.

Understanding the President means understanding both their everyday invisibility and their rare but real power. That's this lesson.

---

## Article 52: The Office Exists

The journey begins simply enough: **Article 52** says "There shall be a President of India." Part V of the Constitution (Articles 52–151) deals with the Union — and the President heads it.

The Union Executive consists of: the President, the Vice-President, the Prime Minister, the Council of Ministers, and the Attorney General of India.

---

## The Election: Why Such a Complex Method?

The President is **not directly elected by the people** — you don't vote for a presidential candidate on election day. Instead, they are elected by an **Electoral College** (Article 54):

- Elected members of **both Houses of Parliament** (Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha)
- Elected members of **State Legislative Assemblies** (Vidhan Sabhas of all states)
- Elected members of **Legislative Assemblies of UTs with a legislature** — currently Delhi, Puducherry, and Jammu & Kashmir

**Nominated members are excluded** — only *elected* members count. So the 12 nominated Rajya Sabha members, 2 Anglo-Indian nominees (before their abolition), etc., do not vote.

### Why this method?

Because the President represents the whole country — not just the Lok Sabha majority. A direct presidential election could produce a President from a different political camp than the PM, creating American-style gridlock. Involving state legislatures ensures that India's **federal character** is reflected in who sits in Rashtrapati Bhavan.

### The Vote-Value Formula

Each vote in the Electoral College is not equal in raw terms — the value is calculated to ensure two things:

1. **Uniformity among states**: A state with more population gets more weight per MLA vote, so larger states aren't equal to tiny ones just because both have 100 MLAs.
2. **Parity between Parliament and states combined**: The total value of all MP votes approximately equals the total value of all MLA votes — so neither the Centre nor the states dominate.

Formula (don't memorise the arithmetic — understand the logic):
- **MLA vote value** = State population ÷ (Number of elected MLAs × 1000), rounded to nearest whole number
- **MP vote value** = Total value of all MLA votes across all states ÷ Total elected MPs (LS + RS)

Method: **Proportional Representation by Single Transferable Vote** — voters rank candidates by preference. This ensures the winner reflects a genuine majority of the Electoral College, not just a plurality.

## Qualifications, Term, and Removal

**To be President (Article 58), you must:**
- Be a citizen of India
- Be at least **35 years of age**
- Be qualified to be elected as a member of the **Lok Sabha**
- Not hold any office of profit under the government

**Term**: 5 years from the date of taking oath (Article 56). Can be re-elected — and Rajendra Prasad was, serving two full terms (1950–1962).

**Oath**: Administered by the **Chief Justice of India** (or the senior-most SC judge if CJI is absent).

**Vacancy**: If the President dies, resigns, or is removed, the **Vice-President acts as President** until a new one is elected. The election must happen within **6 months** of the vacancy.

**Removal — Impeachment (Article 61)**:

The President can be removed only for **violation of the Constitution** — a deliberately vague ground. The process:
- Resolution can be initiated in **either House**
- The House that initiates must pass the resolution by **2/3rd of total membership** of that House (not just members present and voting — total membership is a higher bar)
- The other House then **investigates** the charges
- If it too passes the resolution by 2/3rd of total membership, the President stands removed

*Note:* "Violation of the Constitution" has never been precisely defined, and no President has ever been impeached. The threat of impeachment is constitutional more than practical.

**Salary**: Charged on the **Consolidated Fund of India** — cannot be reduced during term of office (same protection as SC judges, CAG, etc.).

## The President's Powers: Seven Domains

### 1. Executive Powers

All executive action of the Government of India is formally taken in the **name of the President** (Article 77). The President appoints:
- The Prime Minister (and on PM's advice, other ministers)
- Governors of states
- Chief Justice and judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts
- Attorney General, Comptroller and Auditor General
- Ambassadors and High Commissioners
- Members of UPSC, Election Commission, Finance Commission

In practice, all these appointments follow the advice of the elected government — but the formal power matters in moments of political uncertainty.

### 2. Legislative Powers

- **Summons and prorogues** Parliament sessions; **dissolves** Lok Sabha
- **Addresses** Parliament at the beginning of each year's first session and after every general election (Article 87 — not the President's "speech," it's the *government's* policy agenda read out by the President)
- **Nominates 12 members** to Rajya Sabha — persons with expertise in literature, science, art, or social service (Article 80)
- **Nominates 2 Anglo-Indian members** to Lok Sabha — this power was abolished by the **104th Amendment (2019)**
- Can call a **joint sitting** of both Houses when a bill (other than a Money Bill or Constitutional Amendment) is deadlocked
- **Gives assent** to bills — without which no bill becomes law

### 3. Financial Powers

- No **Money Bill** can be introduced in Parliament without the President's prior recommendation
- The Annual **Budget** is laid before Parliament in the President's name
- The President constitutes the **Finance Commission** every five years

### 4. Judicial Powers — The Pardoning Power (Article 72)

The President can grant mercy in five forms:

| Power | What it means |
|-------|--------------|
| **Pardon** | Complete acquittal — wipes the conviction entirely |
| **Commutation** | Substitutes a lighter form of punishment (e.g., death → life imprisonment) |
| **Remission** | Reduces the *quantum* of punishment without changing its nature |
| **Respite** | Grants a lesser sentence due to a special fact (e.g., physical disability, pregnancy) |
| **Reprieve** | Temporary suspension of execution — usually pending appeal |

**Article 72 vs Governor's Article 161:**

| Feature | President (Art 72) | Governor (Art 161) |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Death sentence | **Can pardon** | Cannot pardon |
| Court Martial cases | **Can pardon** | Cannot |
| Offences under Union laws | **Can pardon** | Cannot |
| Offences under State laws | No (Governor's domain) | Can pardon |

This distinction — that **only the President** can pardon death sentences and court martial convictions — is a standard exam question.

### 5. Emergency Powers

The President declares three types of emergencies (studied in depth in the Salient Features lesson — review the 352/356/360 mnemonic):
- **National Emergency** (Article 352)
- **President's Rule** in a state (Article 356)
- **Financial Emergency** (Article 360)

All three require cabinet recommendation and subsequent parliamentary approval.

### 6. Diplomatic and Military Powers

- All treaties and international agreements are negotiated and concluded in the President's name (though they need parliamentary approval to take effect)
- The President is the **Supreme Commander of the Armed Forces** — declares war and concludes peace (subject to Parliament)

### 7. Ordinance-Making Power (Article 123)

When Parliament is **not in session** and urgent action is required, the President can promulgate an **Ordinance** — which has the same force as a law passed by Parliament. But:
- The Ordinance must be laid before Parliament when it reconvenes
- It automatically lapses **6 weeks** after Parliament reassembles (unless converted into a law)
- Parliament can also pass a resolution rejecting it

*The Ordinance route cannot be used during a joint sitting, and cannot be used to amend the Constitution.*

## The Three Vetoes: When the President Says No to a Bill

When a bill is sent to the President for assent, three options exist:

**1. Give Assent** → Bill becomes law.

**2. Absolute Veto** → Withhold assent entirely. The bill dies. Typically used for private member bills or bills the President's government clearly opposes. For a **Money Bill**, the President can only give assent or return it — not hold it (in practice, Presidents always assent to Money Bills recommended by Cabinet).

**3. Suspensive Veto (Return for Reconsideration)** → The President can return the bill (except a Money Bill or Constitutional Amendment Bill) to Parliament with a message requesting reconsideration. If Parliament passes it *again* — even without changes — the President **must give assent**. One bite at the apple, not two.

**4. Pocket Veto** → The President neither gives assent nor returns the bill — simply keeps it. This works because the Indian Constitution **sets no time limit** for the President to act on a bill. The American President has 10 days; India's President has no clock. President Zail Singh famously used this in 1986 regarding the Indian Post Office (Amendment) Bill — he never returned or assented to it, and it lapsed.

*Note:* Money Bills cannot be returned by the President — only assented to or (in theory) pocket-vetoed.

## The Constitutional Position: Nominal vs Real Executive

Here is the most important conceptual point about the President:

**De jure executive** (formal head) = President  
**De facto executive** (real power) = Prime Minister + Council of Ministers

**Article 74(1)** (as amended by 42nd and 44th Amendments) states: *"There shall be a Council of Ministers with the Prime Minister at the head to aid and advise the President, who shall, in the exercise of his functions, act in accordance with such advice."*

The President *can* return the advice once for reconsideration — but if the Cabinet sends the same advice again, the President **is bound** to accept it. No second return.

This makes India's President fundamentally different from the USA's President:
- US President: directly elected, real executive power, can veto Congress independently
- India's President: indirectly elected, nominal executive, acts on Cabinet advice

### When Does the President Have Real Discretion?

In practice, discretionary power surfaces in very specific situations:
- **Appointment of PM when no party has a clear majority** — the President can determine who is most likely to command a majority
- **Dismissal of a Council of Ministers** that has lost the confidence of the House but refuses to resign
- **Dissolution of the Lok Sabha** in contested political circumstances
- **Giving or withholding assent** to bills in edge cases

These moments are rare but historic. How individual Presidents handled them has shaped Indian political history — Governor-General C. Rajagopalachari's successor Rajendra Prasad often sparred with Nehru over presidential discretion in the early republic.

## The Vice-President of India (Articles 63–71)

If the President is the flywheel, the Vice-President is the **understudy** — present at every rehearsal, ready to take the stage if needed, but not in the spotlight.

### Qualifications (Article 66)

- Citizen of India
- At least **35 years of age**
- Qualified to be a member of **Rajya Sabha** (not Lok Sabha — this differs from the President)
- Must not hold any office of profit

**The qualification difference**: President → qualified for Lok Sabha. Vice-President → qualified for Rajya Sabha. This is a favourite exam trap.

### Election: The Critical Difference from President

The Vice-President is elected by an Electoral College of **all members (elected AND nominated) of both Houses of Parliament** — there is no involvement of State Legislatures. This is the single most important distinction to remember:

| Feature | President | Vice-President |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| Electoral College | Elected MPs + Elected MLAs + Elected UT members | All members (elected + nominated) of both Houses of Parliament only |
| State Legislatures involved? | **Yes** | **No** |
| Method | PR by single transferable vote | PR by single transferable vote |

**Term**: 5 years. Can be re-elected any number of times. (No limit specified.)

**Oath**: Administered by the **President of India** (the President of India swears in the VP — and the CJI swears in the President).

### The Vice-President's Two Roles

**Role 1 — Ex-officio Chairman of Rajya Sabha (Article 64)**  
This is the day-to-day job. The VP presides over Rajya Sabha when sitting as its Chairman. Unlike the Lok Sabha Speaker, the VP/Chairman votes only to break a tie. The Deputy Chairman of Rajya Sabha handles sessions when the VP is absent.

**Role 2 — Acting President (Article 65)**  
When the President dies, resigns, is removed, or is unable to perform duties, the VP immediately becomes Acting President — with full powers. The VP also takes over temporarily when the President is on foreign travel or otherwise incapacitated.

### Removal of Vice-President

The VP can be removed by a **resolution of Rajya Sabha passed by a majority of all members**, and agreed to by Lok Sabha by a simple majority. Note:
- **No formal impeachment** (unlike the President)
- **14 days' advance notice** must be given before the resolution
- Only Rajya Sabha *initiates* — Lok Sabha only agrees (reversed from usual bill procedure)

## Exam Traps

1. **VP's Electoral College has no state legislatures** — only Parliament members (both elected AND nominated). President's college includes state assemblies, VP's does not.
2. **VP is qualified for Rajya Sabha; President for Lok Sabha** — reversed from what you'd expect.
3. **Pocket veto is India's unique constitutional oddity** — no time limit = unlimited pocket power.
4. **Only the President can pardon death sentences** — Governors cannot.
5. **Impeachment requires 2/3rd of total membership** (not just present and voting) — a much harder bar.
6. **Nominated members do NOT vote in Presidential election** — only elected members of Parliament and state assemblies.
7. **President cannot return a Money Bill** — can only assent to it or pocket-veto it.
8. **104th Amendment (2019) abolished Anglo-Indian nominations** to Lok Sabha and State legislatures.
9. **VP is removed by Rajya Sabha resolution** — not impeachment. Lok Sabha merely agrees.
10. **CJI swears in the President; President swears in the VP** — these two are reversed in every mock test.
