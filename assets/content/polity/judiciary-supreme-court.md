## Who Guards the Guardians?

In most democracies, the legislature makes laws and the executive implements them. But who checks whether those laws respect the Constitution? Who stops Parliament from stripping citizens of their rights through a majority vote? Who holds the government accountable when it acts arbitrarily?

In India, that job belongs to the **Supreme Court** — sitting atop a three-tiered judicial hierarchy (SC → High Courts → District Courts) as the final arbiter of the Constitution. The doctrine it uses to do this work — **Judicial Review** — is one of the most consequential ideas in Indian governance. A court that can strike down Parliament's laws is not a luxury but a necessity in a constitutional democracy: without it, Fundamental Rights are just suggestions.

The Supreme Court has called itself the **"sentinel on the qui vive"** — a watchman on constant alert, standing between the State and the citizen. That is precisely what this lesson is about.

---

## Composition: A Flexible Court

**Article 124** establishes the Supreme Court. Unlike most constitutional features, the **strength of the SC is not fixed in the Constitution** — Parliament can, by law, increase the number of judges.

- Original strength (1950): CJI + 6 judges = **7 total**
- Current sanctioned strength: **CJI + 33 judges = 34 total** (Parliament has increased this over the decades)

The SC sits in New Delhi. It can sit in other places too, but only on the CJI's discretion with the President's approval — which has never actually happened.

## Who Can Be a Supreme Court Judge?

**Qualifications (Article 124(3))**:
1. **Citizen of India**, AND one of these three:
   - Judge of a **High Court for at least 5 years**, OR
   - Advocate of a **High Court for at least 10 years**, OR
   - A **distinguished jurist** in the opinion of the President

The "distinguished jurist" route has never been used in India — no academician or legal scholar has been appointed directly to the SC under this category, though the provision exists.

**Retirement age**: **65 years** (High Court judges retire at 62 — a younger bar.)

**Salary**: Charged to the **Consolidated Fund of India** — Parliament cannot reduce it during the judge's tenure. This protects judicial independence from financial pressure.

## Appointment: The Collegium Story

The Constitution says judges are appointed by the **President**, in "consultation" with the CJI (and other judges for SC appointments). For seven decades, the meaning of "consultation" has been the most contested word in Indian constitutional law. Its evolution is told through three landmark cases:

### Three Judges Cases — A Story in Three Acts

**Act 1 — SP Gupta v. Union of India (1981)**
The SC held that "consultation" does not mean "concurrence." The President (i.e., the executive) was not bound by the CJI's views. This gave the government of the day enormous influence over judicial appointments.

**Act 2 — Supreme Court Advocates on Record Association v. Union of India (1993)**
The SC reversed itself. It held that "consultation" with the CJI means **concurrence** — the CJI's opinion is binding. The court also evolved the **Collegium System**: the CJI plus the two senior-most SC judges would together recommend appointments. The executive lost its dominance.

**Act 3 — Special Reference Case (1998)**
The collegium was expanded: now **CJI + 4 senior-most SC judges** make recommendations. This remains the system today.

### The NJAC Episode (2014–2015)

Parliament was unhappy with the collegium — a system where judges appoint judges, with minimal government say. In 2014, the **99th Constitutional Amendment** established the **National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC)**: a six-member body with the CJI, 2 senior SC judges, the Law Minister, and 2 eminent persons — giving the executive a clear voice.

The SC struck down the 99th Amendment in **2015** as violating the **Basic Structure** of the Constitution — specifically, the principle of judicial independence. One of the most dramatic confrontations between Parliament and Judiciary in post-Emergency India. The collegium system continues.

## Removal of SC Judges (Article 124(4))

A SC judge can be removed only by the President, on the **address of both Houses of Parliament** in the same session:
- The address must be passed by **2/3rd of members present and voting** in each House
- *Plus* a **majority of the total membership** of each House
- Grounds: **proved misbehaviour or incapacity**

This combined threshold (2/3rd present + majority of total) is deliberately designed to be extremely difficult — protecting judicial independence from parliamentary pressure.

**No SC judge has ever been removed in India.** One motion against Justice V. Ramaswami (1993) failed in the Lok Sabha — many ruling party MPs abstained, preventing the required majority.

## Jurisdiction: What Cases the SC Can Hear

The Supreme Court's power flows from five types of jurisdiction:

### 1. Original Jurisdiction (Article 131)

The SC is the **first and only court** to hear disputes between:
- Union of India vs one or more States
- Two or more States among themselves
- Union + one or more States vs one or more other States

**Important exclusion**: This covers only *governmental* parties. A dispute between the Centre and a private citizen, or between a State and a private citizen, goes to a High Court first — not the SC.

*Exam trap*: Article 131 is an exclusive original jurisdiction — other courts cannot hear these inter-governmental disputes. But Article 131 does NOT cover disputes arising from a treaty, agreement, or covenant made before Independence.

### 2. Appellate Jurisdiction (Articles 132–136)

The SC hears appeals from High Courts in three tracks:

- **Article 132**: Constitutional matters — appeals against HC decisions involving a substantial question of constitutional interpretation (civil, criminal, or other)
- **Article 133**: Civil cases — HC must certify a substantial question of law of general importance
- **Article 134**: Criminal cases — death sentence cases from HC automatically qualify; HC can certify other criminal matters

**Article 136 — Special Leave Petition (SLP): The Master Key**

Article 136 is how most cases actually reach the SC. It gives the SC discretionary power to grant special leave to appeal from **any judgment, decree, determination, sentence or order** passed by **any court or tribunal in India** — except military tribunals.

The SC can refuse SLP without giving reasons. It can grant it even when no other appeal route exists. It is the extraordinary, catch-all jurisdiction that makes the SC the true apex court. When you read in the news "the Supreme Court dismissed the petition," it is almost always an SLP being refused.

### 3. Advisory Jurisdiction (Article 143)

The President can refer any question of law or fact of **public importance** to the SC for its **advisory opinion**. The SC *may* give the opinion — it is not obligated to. And critically, the opinion is **not binding** on the President.

When used: Constitutional questions where clarity is needed before a law is enacted, or matters of great national importance where the government wants the court's view without a litigated case. The SC has given opinions on issues ranging from Berubari (territorial cession — used in the Preamble lesson!) to the Presidential reference on Cauvery water disputes.

### 4. Writ Jurisdiction (Article 32)

The SC issues the five writs (Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Certiorari, Prohibition, Quo Warranto) for enforcement of Fundamental Rights — covered in full in the Fundamental Rights lesson. Remember: Article 32 itself is a Fundamental Right, and the SC cannot refuse to hear a genuine FR violation.

### 5. Review Jurisdiction (Article 137)

The SC can **review its own judgments** — subject to rules made by Parliament. This is the rare mechanism by which the SC can correct its own errors. A review petition is filed before the same bench that delivered the original judgment.

## Judicial Review: The Doctrine That Guards the Constitution

**Judicial Review** is the power of the Supreme Court (and High Courts) to examine the constitutionality of legislative and executive actions, and to declare them void if they violate the Constitution.

It is **not explicitly mentioned** anywhere in the Constitution by name. It is implied from:
- **Article 13**: Laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights are void
- **Article 32 and 226**: Courts can issue writs to enforce FRs
- **Articles 131–136**: SC hears constitutional questions
- **Article 372**: Pre-constitutional laws remain valid only subject to Constitution

The concept was pioneered in the USA by **Marbury v. Madison (1803)** — Chief Justice John Marshall established that the Supreme Court could strike down acts of Congress. India adopted the principle but built it into multiple articles rather than relying on one foundational judgment.

### What Can Be Reviewed?

1. **Ordinary legislation** — Parliament or state legislatures can pass laws that violate FRs or exceed their legislative competence (e.g., a state law on a Union List subject). SC can strike these down.

2. **Constitutional Amendments** — The most dramatic power. After Kesavananda Bharati (1973), the SC can review whether a constitutional amendment destroys the **Basic Structure**. Parliament can amend the Constitution — but cannot amend away its fundamental identity.

3. **Executive actions** — Administrative orders, government policies, executive decisions can be reviewed for constitutional validity.

### Judicial Review vs Judicial Activism vs Judicial Overreach

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Judicial Review** | Court checks constitutionality of laws/actions — formal, accepted power |
| **Judicial Activism** | Court proactively expands rights beyond strict text — Article 21's expansion to privacy, environment, livelihood |
| **Judicial Overreach** | Critics' term when courts seem to make policy rather than interpret law — line is contested |

## Public Interest Litigation (PIL): Opening the Court's Doors

Until the 1970s, only the directly affected party could file a case in court. If a bonded labourer was being exploited in a remote village, they could not realistically hire a lawyer and travel to Delhi to approach the Supreme Court.

**PIL changed this.** In the early 1980s, **Justice P.N. Bhagwati** and **Justice V.R. Krishna Iyer** relaxed the doctrine of **locus standi** — the requirement that only the aggrieved person can file a case. Now, any public-spirited individual or organisation can approach the SC (under Article 32) or HC (under Article 226) on behalf of those who cannot fight for themselves.

A PIL has been filed via a postcard, a letter to the CJI, or even a news article brought to the court's attention. The SC converts such communications into PILs if the cause is genuine.

**PIL has been used for**: prisoner rights (Hussainara Khatoon case), bonded labour, child labour, environmental protection (MC Mehta cases on Delhi pollution), right to food, women's safety in workplaces (Vishakha Guidelines), and many more.

**Criticism**: PILs have also been misused — filed for political motives, publicity, or to harass opponents. Courts have started imposing costs on frivolous PILs.

## Court of Record and Contempt Power (Article 129)

The SC is a **court of record** — its decisions are recorded and serve as evidence of law. No court can question the authenticity or authority of SC records.

As a court of record, the SC has the **inherent power to punish for contempt** — civil contempt (disobeying court orders) and criminal contempt (scandalising the court or interfering with the administration of justice). This power cannot be taken away even by Parliament.

---

## Article 141: The SC as Law-Maker for All Courts

**Article 141**: *"The law declared by the Supreme Court shall be binding on all courts within the territory of India."*

This is what makes the SC's role unique. When the SC decides a case, it isn't just resolving that one dispute — it is making **binding law** for every High Court, Sessions Court, district court, and tribunal across India. Every lower court must follow SC precedents.

But the SC is **not bound by its own previous decisions** — it can overrule itself. Larger benches can overrule smaller benches. The law evolves through this process of self-correction.

---

## The 1973 Supersession Controversy

By convention, the senior-most judge of the SC becomes the CJI when the incumbent retires. This convention was broken — twice, both under political pressure:

**1973**: When CJI S.M. Sikri retired, the Indira Gandhi government superseded three senior judges who had ruled against it in the Kesavananda Bharati case. Justice A.N. Ray was appointed CJI, jumping over judges Shelat, Grover, and Hegde — all of whom resigned in protest.

**1977**: The same happened again briefly under another government.

These incidents were watershed moments: they showed how vulnerable judicial independence could be to executive pressure when the appointment process gave the executive too much control. They also indirectly shaped the push for the Collegium System that emerged through the Three Judges Cases.

## Exam Traps

1. **SC strength is not fixed in Constitution** — Parliament sets it by law. Currently 34 (CJI + 33); original was 8.
2. **Retirement: SC judges retire at 65, HC judges at 62** — not the same.
3. **"Distinguished jurist" qualification exists but has never been used** — it's a valid qualification, not theoretical.
4. **Original jurisdiction (Art 131) = only government-vs-government disputes** — Centre vs citizen or State vs citizen → HC first.
5. **Article 136 (SLP) = most cases reach SC this way** — not Article 132/133/134.
6. **Advisory opinion (Art 143) is NOT binding** — it is an opinion, not a judgment.
7. **Judicial Review is not explicitly mentioned in the Constitution** — implied from Articles 13, 32, 226, etc.
8. **PIL is NOT in the Constitution** — it is a judicial innovation that relaxed locus standi.
9. **SC can review its own judgments (Art 137)** — a review petition goes to the same bench.
10. **NJAC was struck down in 2015** — 99th Amendment was declared unconstitutional. Collegium continues.
11. **No SC judge has ever been removed** — the threshold (2/3rd + majority of total in both Houses) is too high.
12. **Article 141: SC decisions bind ALL courts** — but the SC itself is not bound by its own prior decisions.
