## The Middle Floor

The Supreme Court is the penthouse — the court everyone hears about, the final word, the court that reshapes the Constitution. District courts are the ground floor — where actual disputes are filed, where millions of Indians seek justice every day. But the High Court is the critical **middle floor** that connects the two and holds the whole building together.

There are 25 High Courts in India. Most citizens will never reach the Supreme Court. But almost every Indian who engages with the legal system will at some point encounter a High Court — whether to challenge a government order, seek a writ, or appeal a subordinate court decision. The High Court is the real workhorse of Indian justice.

Understanding the HC's structure, jurisdiction, and its relationship with both the SC above it and the subordinate courts below it — this is the subject of this lesson.

---

## Part I: High Courts — Structure (Articles 214–231)

### How Many? Where? (Article 214)

**Article 214**: Every state shall have a High Court. But "every state" does not mean every state has its *own* HC — some HCs have jurisdiction over multiple states and Union Territories.

**Article 231**: Parliament can by law establish a **common High Court** for two or more states or UTs. Examples:
- **Punjab & Haryana HC** at Chandigarh — serves Punjab, Haryana, and Chandigarh UT
- **Guwahati HC** — has circuit benches serving Assam, Nagaland, Mizoram, Meghalaya, Manipur, and other northeastern states

**Currently: 25 High Courts** in India (as of 2024). Not 28 or 29 — there are more states than HCs because of shared jurisdiction.

The three oldest HCs were established in **1862**: Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras. They were established under the High Courts Act, 1861 — even before the Constitution.

**Article 230**: Parliament can extend HC jurisdiction to Union Territories. Delhi HC was established in 1966.

---

### Judges: How Many? How Appointed?

**Strength**: The Constitution does not fix the number of HC judges — the President determines it. The Allahabad HC has the highest sanctioned strength (over 160 judges), while small-state HCs may have fewer than 10.

#### Qualifications (Article 217)

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Citizenship | Citizen of India |
| HC Judge | Has been a judge of any HC for at least **10 years** |
| OR Advocate | Has been an advocate of any HC for at least **10 years** |
| Distinguished jurist | **Does NOT apply** to HC — only SC has this category |

This is a key trap: the **"distinguished jurist"** category exists only for the Supreme Court (Article 124), not for High Courts. Also note: **no minimum age** is specified for HC judges in the Constitution.

#### Retirement Age

**HC judges retire at 62**. Supreme Court judges retire at **65**. Three extra years at the top — remember this: *62 for the middle floor, 65 for the penthouse*.

#### Appointment (Article 217)

HC judges are appointed by the **President** — but in consultation with:
- The **Chief Justice of India** (mandatory)
- The **Governor** of the state
- The **Chief Justice of that High Court** (for judges other than the CJI of HC)

The Collegium system applies here too — the same evolution from *SP Gupta* (1981) through *SCAORA* (1993) to the Three Judges Cases: HC appointments are now in practice recommended by the Collegium (CJI + 4 senior SC judges).

**Oath**: Administered by the **Governor** of the state (not the CJI, not the HC Chief Justice — the Governor).

---

### Term, Transfer, Removal

**No fixed term** — until retirement at 62.

**Transfer (Article 222)**: The President may transfer a HC judge from one HC to another — after consultation with the CJI. Judges have been transferred as a form of "punishment" posting when they clashed with the government; the SC has held this power must be used in public interest, not punitively.

**Removal**: Identical procedure to SC judges — by an address by **both Houses of Parliament** requiring a **majority of total membership** and **not less than two-thirds of members present and voting**, followed by Presidential removal. This has never been achieved — no HC judge has ever been removed either.

**Salary**: Charged to the **Consolidated Fund of the State** (unlike SC judges whose salaries are charged to the Consolidated Fund of India).

## Part II: Jurisdiction — What a High Court Can Do

This is the examination heart of this topic.

### 1. Original Jurisdiction

The HC has original jurisdiction in:
- **Writ jurisdiction (Article 226)** — the most important
- Certain civil matters (varying by HC — Bombay, Calcutta, Madras have broader original civil jurisdiction historically)
- Election disputes (for state legislature elections)
- Revenue matters (in some HCs)

---

### 2. Article 226 vs Article 32 — The Critical Comparison

This is the single most important HC comparison for SSC exams.

| Feature | Article 32 (Supreme Court) | Article 226 (High Court) |
|---------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Writs available | All 5 | All 5 |
| Purpose | Only for **Fundamental Rights** enforcement | For FRs **AND for any other purpose** |
| Breadth | Narrower | **Broader** |
| Nature | Is itself a FR (cannot be suspended ordinarily) | Constitutional provision (not a FR) |
| Emergency | Can be suspended under Article 359 | **Cannot be suspended during Emergency** |
| Territorial | Whole of India | Only within HC's territorial jurisdiction |
| Discretion | Similar | HC has discretion to refuse writs |

The killer fact: **Article 226 is broader than Article 32** because HC can issue writs for "any other purpose" — meaning for statutory rights, contractual rights, or any legal right — not just Fundamental Rights. A citizen can approach the HC under 226 even when no FR is violated.

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar called Article 32 the "**heart and soul of the Constitution**" — the right to approach the SC for FR enforcement is itself a Fundamental Right. Article 226 is a more powerful tool in practice for most citizens, but it doesn't have that constitutional status.

---

### 3. Appellate Jurisdiction

High Courts hear appeals from subordinate courts:
- **Civil appeals** — from District Courts and other civil tribunals
- **Criminal appeals** — from Sessions Courts; also some direct appeals in cases involving death sentences (must be confirmed by HC)
- Appeals on questions of law, mixed fact-and-law, or substantial questions of law

---

### 4. Supervisory Jurisdiction (Article 227)

**Article 227** is the HC's administrative superpower: the power of **superintendence over all courts and tribunals** within its territorial jurisdiction (except military tribunals and courts-martial).

This means the HC can:
- Call for records from any subordinate court
- Transfer a case from one subordinate court to another
- Give general rules for subordinate courts to follow
- Revise any order made by a subordinate court (revision jurisdiction)

Article 227 is wider than ordinary appellate jurisdiction — the HC can intervene even when there's no formal appeal, simply to correct errors that shock the judicial conscience.

---

### 5. Article 228 — Withdrawal of Cases

If a case pending before a subordinate court involves a substantial question of Constitutional interpretation, the HC **may withdraw** the case, decide the Constitutional question itself, and either:
- Decide the whole case, or
- Return the case to the subordinate court with its decision on the Constitutional question

## Part III: The Subordinate Judiciary (Articles 233–237)

Below the HC sits the subordinate judiciary — the ground floor of the legal system. This is where the overwhelming majority of Indian litigation takes place.

### The District Judge — Article 233

The **District Judge** is the principal judicial officer at the district level. The same person typically functions as:
- **District Judge** — for civil matters
- **Sessions Judge** — for criminal matters (the court of session under CrPC/BNSS)

**Appointment (Article 233)**: By the **Governor** of the state, in consultation with the **High Court** of the state. This consultation is mandatory — the HC's role in appointing the district judiciary is constitutionally secured.

**Eligibility**: Must not already be in government service; must be recommended by the HC.

---

### Other Subordinate Judicial Officers — Article 234

For judicial officers other than district judges, appointments are made by the **Governor** in accordance with rules made after consultation with:
- The **State Public Service Commission**
- The **High Court** of the state

---

### HC's Control Over Subordinate Courts — Article 235

The High Court has **control** over subordinate courts in matters of:
- Posting
- Promotion
- Leave
- General supervision of judicial work

This is the constitutional guarantee of HC supervision — it ensures the district judiciary isn't left entirely to executive control.

---

### The Fast Tracks and Lok Adalats

These aren't in the Constitution directly but are tested frequently:

**Lok Adalats**: Established under the Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987. Awards of Lok Adalats are **deemed decrees of civil courts** and are **final and binding** — no appeal lies to any court. Based on the principle of conciliation and compromise.

**Fast Track Courts**: Established for specific purposes — POCSO cases, rape cases, senior citizen cases, etc. Set up by states, funded partly by Centre.

**National Lok Adalats**: Held periodically for bulk disposal of pending cases — one day per month across India.

---

## The HC's Special Position: Not Just a Middle Court

The High Court is not merely an intermediate appellate body. It has three qualities that make it constitutionally special:

1. **Broader writ power than SC** (Article 226 vs Article 32 — "any other purpose")
2. **Supervisory control** over all courts and tribunals below it (Article 227)
3. **Cannot be suspended** during Emergency — Article 226 remains functional even when Article 32 can be suspended under Article 359

This is the constitutional logic: even when the Centre suspends access to the SC for FR enforcement during Emergency (Article 359), citizens can still approach HCs. It's a safety net built into the design.

---

## Exam Traps

1. **HC has 25 HCs, not 28/29** — fewer than states because some are shared.
2. **Distinguished jurist category = SC only** — HCs do not have this qualification.
3. **HC retirement = 62; SC retirement = 65** — common reversal trap.
4. **Oath of HC judge = Governor** (not CJI, not HC Chief Justice).
5. **Salary = Consolidated Fund of the State** (SC salaries = Consolidated Fund of India).
6. **Article 226 is broader than Article 32** — "any other purpose" beyond FR enforcement.
7. **Article 226 cannot be suspended during Emergency** — but Article 32 can be (Article 359).
8. **Article 32 is a Fundamental Right** — Article 226 is not.
9. **Article 227 = supervisory** (administrative); Article 226 = writ (judicial). Don't confuse.
10. **District Judge appointment = Governor + HC consultation** (Article 233).
11. **Lok Adalat awards = final, no appeal** to any court.
12. **HC Chief Justice's advice** is consulted for appointment of other judges, not for appointment of HC Chief Justice.

## Self-Test (5 Questions)

1. Why is Article 226 considered broader than Article 32, even though both allow all 5 writs?
2. What happens to Article 226 during a National Emergency? What about Article 32?
3. Who administers the oath of office to a High Court judge? (Trap: this is not the CJI.)
4. What is the retirement age of HC judges? How does it differ from SC judges?
5. Under Article 227, what does "superintendence" mean — and how is it different from appellate jurisdiction?

*(Answers: 1. Art 226 allows writs "for any other purpose" beyond FR enforcement — statutory rights, legal rights, etc.; Art 32 is only for FRs. 2. Art 226 cannot be suspended even during Emergency; Art 32 can be suspended under Art 359 on Presidential proclamation. 3. The Governor of the state. 4. 62 for HC judges; 65 for SC judges. 5. Superintendence = administrative oversight including records, rules, transfers, and revision — can intervene even without an appeal; appellate jurisdiction requires a formal appeal.)*
