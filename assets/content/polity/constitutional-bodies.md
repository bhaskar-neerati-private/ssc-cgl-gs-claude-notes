## The Unseen Government

Most Indians know Parliament, the President, the Prime Minister, the Supreme Court. These are the visible faces of the Constitution. But behind them operates a second layer — bodies that the framers wrote directly into the Constitution and deliberately insulated from political pressure.

A **constitutional body** derives its existence, powers, and protections from the Constitution itself. It cannot be abolished by ordinary legislation; its members have security of tenure written into the Constitution; their independence is a constitutional guarantee, not a political courtesy.

This is the critical distinction: a **constitutional body** exists because the Constitution says it shall exist. A **statutory body** exists because Parliament passed a law creating it — Parliament can dissolve it just as easily. Constitutional bodies are harder to touch.

This lesson covers the major constitutional bodies, who appoints them, how they can be removed, and what they do.

## Body 1: Election Commission of India (Articles 324–329)

### What It Does

The Election Commission of India (ECI) is the custodian of Indian democracy. Under **Article 324**, it has superintendence, direction, and control of:
- Preparation of electoral rolls
- Conduct of elections to **Parliament**, **State Legislatures**, and the offices of **President** and **Vice-President**

It does NOT conduct Panchayat or Municipal elections — those are handled by State Election Commissions (Article 243K).

### Composition

| Member | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Chief Election Commissioner (CEC) | Head of the Commission |
| Other Election Commissioners | As determined by the President |

Originally a single-member Commission. Two additional ECs were added in 1989 and have been permanent since 1993. The ECI is now a three-member body.

### Appointment and Removal

**Appointed by**: The **President** (on advice of Cabinet, in practice)

**Removal**:
- **CEC**: Same procedure as a **Supreme Court judge** — address by both Houses of Parliament (majority of total membership + 2/3rd present and voting), then Presidential removal. Very difficult to remove.
- **Other ECs**: Removed by the President **on the recommendation of the CEC** — much easier. This asymmetry is intentional.

**Security of service**: CEC's conditions of service cannot be varied to their disadvantage after appointment. CEC cannot be removed except like an SC judge.

**Tenure** (by law, not Constitution): 6 years from assumption of office or 65 years of age, whichever is earlier. After retirement, the CEC cannot hold any further appointment under the Government of India.

### Key Powers

- Announces election schedule; decides polling dates
- Recognises political parties as national or state parties; allocates election symbols
- Enforces the **Model Code of Conduct** during elections
- Advises the **President** on disqualifications of sitting MPs (Article 103)
- Advises the **Governor** on disqualifications of sitting MLAs (Article 192)
- Can postpone or cancel elections in specific constituencies in case of serious violations

### The T.N. Seshan Transformation

Before the 1990s, the ECI was largely passive. **T.N. Seshan** (CEC 1990–96) transformed it into an assertive institution — strictly enforcing the Model Code of Conduct, ordering re-polling, disqualifying candidates for violations. He made Indian elections measurably cleaner and established the ECI's reputation as a credible independent institution.

## Body 2: Union Public Service Commission (Articles 315–323)

### What It Does

The UPSC conducts recruitment examinations for Union services — the Civil Services Examination (which selects IAS, IPS, IFS officers), engineering services, defence services, and others. It also advises the Government on promotions, transfers, and disciplinary matters for civil servants.

### Composition and Appointment

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Appointed by | **President** |
| Eligibility | At least 1/2 of members must have held office under Union or State Government for at least 10 years |

### Removal — Critically Different from Appointment

**Article 317**: The Chairman or any member of the UPSC can be removed by the **President only** — but NOT on parliamentary address. Removal requires:
1. An inquiry by the **Supreme Court**
2. SC recommends removal on grounds of misbehaviour
3. The President then acts on that recommendation

This is unusual: the SC (not Parliament) is the investigative body for UPSC member removal. The President can also **suspend** a UPSC member pending SC inquiry.

**Automatic removal**: A member is also automatically removed if they:
- Are adjudicated insolvent
- Engage in paid employment outside their duties
- Are declared of unsound mind

**After retirement**: Cannot hold any office under Union or State Government.

### Term

**6 years** or age **65**, whichever is earlier (for UPSC members).

### State PSC (SPSC) — The Key Difference

| Feature | UPSC | SPSC |
|---------|------|------|
| Appointed by | President | **Governor** |
| Removed by | **President** (after SC inquiry) | **President** (NOT Governor — trap!) |
| Retirement age | 65 | **62** |

The SPSC chairman/member is appointed by the Governor but removed by the President — the Centre retains this control to protect SPSC members from state government pressure. And SPSC retirement age is 62, not 65 — a frequently tested difference.

**Joint PSC**: Parliament can create a Joint PSC for two or more states. Appointed by the President. Its report goes to each state's Governor (who lays it before the state legislature).

## Body 3: Comptroller and Auditor General (Articles 148–151)

### The "Guardian of the Public Purse"

The CAG is India's supreme audit authority — the constitutional officer who ensures that government money is actually spent as Parliament authorised it to be spent. No other agency can audit government accounts; the CAG has exclusive constitutional authority.

**Article 148**: The CAG is appointed by the **President** by warrant under his hand and seal.

### Independence Protections

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Removal | Like a **Supreme Court judge** (parliamentary address, special majority) |
| Salary | Charged to **Consolidated Fund of India** (cannot be voted down) |
| Service conditions | Cannot be varied to disadvantage after appointment |
| After retirement | **Cannot hold any further office** under Union or State Government |

The post-retirement bar is absolute — stronger than even SC judges (who technically could hold constitutional appointments after retirement). This ensures the CAG has no incentive to be lenient with the government expecting future employment.

### Term

**6 years** from assumption of office or age **65**, whichever is earlier.

### Functions (Articles 149–151)

- Audits and certifies accounts of the **Union** and **all State governments**
- Audits receipts and expenditure from the Consolidated Fund of India and each state's Consolidated Fund
- Audits accounts of government companies, public corporations, and bodies that receive substantial government grants
- Reports to:
  - **President** → for Union accounts (President lays report before Parliament)
  - **Governor** → for State accounts (Governor lays report before state legislature)
- Parliament's **Public Accounts Committee (PAC)** examines CAG reports and questions the executive on audit findings

The CAG is often called the **"Friend, Philosopher, and Guide"** of the PAC — the PAC depends entirely on CAG reports for its oversight work.

## Body 4: Attorney General of India (Article 76)

### India's First Law Officer

**Article 76** requires the President to appoint a person qualified to be a **Supreme Court judge** as Attorney General.

Qualifications for SC judge:
- HC judge for 5 years, OR
- HC advocate for 10 years, OR
- Distinguished jurist (as per President's opinion)

### Role and Powers

- Gives legal advice to the Government of India on referred matters
- Appears before the Supreme Court and High Courts on behalf of the Government of India in important cases
- Performs duties assigned by the President
- Has **right of audience in all courts** throughout India

### What Makes the AG Different from Other Officers

| Feature | AG of India |
|---------|-------------|
| Appointed by | President |
| Tenure | Pleasure of the President (no fixed term; can be removed anytime) |
| Salary | Fees determined by President; NOT a fixed government salary |
| Private practice | **Allowed** — AG is not a full-time government servant |
| Parliament | Can speak in both Houses and their committees, but has **no right to vote** |

The Solicitor General and Additional Solicitors General are not constitutional posts — they are created by statute.

**Advocate General of the State (Article 165)**: The state-level equivalent. Appointed by the **Governor**, holds office at pleasure of the Governor, must be qualified to be a HC judge, has same rights in state courts.

## Body 5: Finance Commission (Article 280)

*(Covered in Centre-State Relations — quick summary here)*

- Constituted by the **President** every **5 years** (or earlier if needed)
- Recommends: distribution of Central taxes to states; grants-in-aid to states; other financial matters referred by President
- 16th Finance Commission is currently functional (constituted 2024)
- Recommendations are advisory but almost always followed

## Body 6: National Commissions for SCs, STs, and BCs

### National Commission for Scheduled Castes (Article 338)

- Added by the **65th Amendment (1990)** and restructured by the **89th Amendment (2003)**
- Composition: Chairperson + Vice-Chairperson + 3 other members
- Appointed by **President**; conditions of service determined by President
- Functions:
  - Investigates complaints of SCs against violations of constitutional safeguards
  - Monitors working of the safeguards for SCs under the Constitution and other laws
  - Advises Centre and States on socio-economic development of SCs
  - Reports annually to the President (who lays it before Parliament)
- Has the powers of a civil court in investigating complaints

### National Commission for Scheduled Tribes (Article 338A)

- Created by the **89th Amendment (2003)** — split the old combined SC/ST Commission
- Same structure and appointment as NCSC (Chairperson + Vice-Chair + 3 members; appointed by President)
- Same functions, but for Scheduled Tribes

### National Commission for Backward Classes (Article 338B)

- Given constitutional status by the **102nd Amendment Act (2018)**
- Before 2018: existed only as a statutory body under the National Commission for Backward Classes Act, 1993
- Same structure (Chairperson + Vice-Chair + 3 members; appointed by President)
- Advises the Central Government on socio-economic development of Other Backward Classes
- Investigates complaints about denial of OBC rights

---

## Body 7: Special Officer for Linguistic Minorities (Article 350B)

- Article 350B inserted by the **7th Amendment (1956)**
- Appointed by the **President**
- Investigates all matters relating to the safeguards provided for linguistic minorities under the Constitution
- Reports to the President, who lays the report before each House of Parliament
- Also sends report to concerned state governments

## Exam Traps

1. **ECI conducts elections for Parliament, state legislatures, President, VP** — NOT panchayats/municipalities (those = State Election Commission).
2. **CEC removed like SC judge** — Other ECs removed on CEC's recommendation (asymmetry!).
3. **UPSC members removed by President after SC inquiry** — NOT by parliamentary address.
4. **SPSC members appointed by Governor but removed by President** — Centre controls removal.
5. **SPSC retirement age = 62, UPSC = 65** — this difference is tested.
6. **CAG cannot hold any office after retirement** (absolute bar) — stronger than SC judges.
7. **AG is NOT a full-time servant** — fees-based; private practice allowed.
8. **AG can speak in Parliament but cannot vote** — right of audience, not membership.
9. **NCBC got constitutional status by 102nd Amendment (2018)** — earlier it was only statutory.
10. **89th Amendment (2003) split SC+ST Commission** into NCSC (Art 338) and NCST (Art 338A).
11. **Finance Commission = every 5 years** — same frequency as State Finance Commission.
12. **Article 350B = Linguistic Minorities** (7th Amendment, 1956) — appointed by President; NOT a commission with multiple members.

## Self-Test (5 Questions)

1. What is the key difference in the removal procedure for the CEC versus other Election Commissioners?
2. Who removes the Chairman of a State Public Service Commission — the Governor or the President? Why?
3. What post-retirement restriction applies to the CAG that makes their independence stronger than even Supreme Court judges?
4. The Attorney General is India's first law officer. Can they practise privately, and can they vote in Parliament?
5. Which constitutional amendment gave the National Commission for Backward Classes its constitutional status, and in which article?

*(Answers: 1. CEC requires parliamentary address (like SC judge removal — very difficult); Other ECs can be removed by the President on the CEC's recommendation (much easier). 2. The President removes SPSC Chairman — not the Governor. This protects the SPSC from pressure by the state government that appointed them. 3. After retirement, the CAG cannot hold ANY further office under Union or State Government — an absolute bar. SC judges technically could hold constitutional appointments post-retirement. 4.
