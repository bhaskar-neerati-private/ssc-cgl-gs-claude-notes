## Meet the Constitution: a personality profile

If you had to describe a person you've known for two lessons now (the Constitution, born 26 Nov 1949, raised on borrowed ideas from ten different countries — remember the "shopping trip" from Lesson 1?), how would you sum up its personality? That's exactly what "Salient Features" means: not new information, but the **character traits** that emerge once you stand back and look at the whole document instead of one Article at a time.

Most students treat this topic as a fresh list to memorize. It isn't. It's a *summary character sketch* of everything that's about to unfold in the rest of Polity — so every trait below is really a preview of a future lesson.

## Trait 1: It never stops growing (the lengthiest constitution on Earth)

You already know this from Lesson 1 — original 395 Articles, 22 Parts, 8 Schedules, now grown to roughly 470+ Articles, 25 Parts, 12 Schedules. No other country's constitution is this long. The U.S. Constitution, by comparison, fits on a few pages.

**Why so long?** Because the framers tried to cover *everything* in one document — not just the broad principles (like the U.S. does), but also administrative details that other countries leave to ordinary laws. It's less like a short company mission statement and more like the mission statement *plus* the entire employee handbook, bound into one book.

## Trait 2: It's a shape-shifter (blend of rigidity and flexibility)

Some constitutions (like the U.S.) are *rigid* — extremely hard to amend. Others (like the UK's, which isn't even one single document) are *flexible* — Parliament can change it like any ordinary law. India's Constitution refused to pick a side. Under **Article 368**, amendments come in three difficulty levels:

- **Level 1 (Easy):** Some provisions can be changed by Parliament through an ordinary law — not even a formal "amendment" (e.g., creating new states, citizenship rules).
- **Level 2 (Medium):** Most provisions need a **special majority** — 2/3 of members present and voting, in both Houses.
- **Level 3 (Hard):** Federal provisions (things affecting how power is split between Centre and States) need that same special majority **plus** ratification by at least half the State legislatures.

*Mnemonic: "Easy, Medium, Hard" — like difficulty settings in a game, except the "boss level" (Level 3) is reserved for anything that would mess with the federal balance.*

## Trait 3: It can't decide if it's Federal or Unitary — so it's both

Look at Article 1: India is called a "**Union of States**," deliberately *not* a "Federation of States." That single word choice tells you everything. In a true federation (like the USA), States voluntarily came together and could, in theory, leave. In India's "Union," the States didn't choose to assemble — the Centre created them, can redraw their borders, and (during an Emergency) can take over their functions entirely.

This is why constitutional expert K.C. Wheare called India's setup **"quasi-federal,"** and Dr. Ambedkar himself described it as **"federal in form, but unitary in spirit"** — normal federal-style division of powers in peacetime, but the Centre holds the trump card whenever there's a crisis.

*(This single trait is the seed of an entire future lesson: Centre-State Relations.)*

## Trait 4: It invited two rivals to the same party (Parliamentary Sovereignty + Judicial Supremacy)

Here's a genuinely clever bit of constitutional engineering. In the UK, Parliament is supreme — no court can strike down an Act of Parliament. In the USA, the Supreme Court can strike down *any* law, even a constitutional amendment, if it violates the Constitution — judicial supremacy. These are two competing philosophies that don't normally get along.

India invited **both** to the table and set ground rules: Parliament *can* amend the Constitution (a nod to UK-style sovereignty), but the **Supreme Court can still strike down any amendment that damages the "Basic Structure"** of the Constitution (a nod to US-style judicial supremacy) — remember the Kesavananda Bharati case from Lesson 2? That's exactly this trait in action. Neither rival gets to dominate the party completely.

## Trait 5: One judiciary to rule them all (integrated judicial system)

Unlike the USA, which runs two *parallel* court systems (separate federal courts and separate state courts), India has **one single, integrated hierarchy**: Supreme Court at the top, High Courts below it, then subordinate courts — and the *same* courts enforce both central and state laws. One ladder, not two separate ladders standing side by side.

## Trait 6: Rights with teeth, and rights that are more like promises

Part III of the Constitution gives you **Fundamental Rights** — these are *justiciable*, meaning you can walk into court and demand they be enforced. Part IV gives you **Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP)** — these are *non-justiciable*, meaning they're goals the State should work toward (reducing inequality, providing free legal aid, etc.), but you can't sue the government for failing to achieve them. Think of Fundamental Rights as a signed contract and DPSP as a sincere promise — both matter, but only one can be enforced in court.

## Trait 7: Duties arrived late to the party

**Fundamental Duties** (Article 51A) weren't part of the original 1949 Constitution at all — they were added by the **42nd Amendment, 1976** (the same amendment that gave the Preamble its "Socialist" and "Secular" twins, from Lesson 2). Originally there were **10 duties**; an **11th was added later by the 86th Amendment, 2002** — the duty of every parent/guardian to provide educational opportunities to their child between ages 6–14.

*Mnemonic: "1976 added the duties; 2002 added one more about a child's education."*

## Trait 8: One passport, not two

In the USA, you effectively hold dual citizenship — of your state and of the country. India rejected that model: there's only **single citizenship** — you're a citizen of India, full stop, regardless of which state you live in, with the same rights everywhere in the country.

## Trait 9: Everyone gets a vote — eventually, everyone over 18

**Universal Adult Franchise** (Article 326) means every adult citizen gets one vote, regardless of caste, religion, gender, or wealth — a radical commitment in 1950, when many democracies still restricted voting rights. The voting age was originally **21**, lowered to **18** by the **61st Amendment, 1988** — a fact that surprises people who assume 18 was always the rule.

## Trait 10: Three emergency red buttons

The Constitution keeps three "break glass in case of emergency" buttons, and the trick to remembering them is the article numbers themselves — they climb by 4 each time:

- **Article 352 — National Emergency** (war, external aggression, or armed rebellion)
- **Article 356 — President's Rule** (State Emergency — failure of constitutional machinery in a state)
- **Article 360 — Financial Emergency** (threat to India's financial stability)

*Mnemonic: "352, 356, 360 — climbing by 4: National, State, Financial."*

## Trait 11: A third tier joined the family later

Originally, government in India meant two tiers — Centre and State. The **73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendments (1992)** added a formal **third tier**: Panchayats (rural local government) and Municipalities (urban local government) — turning a two-tier system into three.

1. Why is India called a "Union of States" rather than a "Federation of States" — and what does that word choice actually imply during a crisis?
2. Name the three difficulty levels of constitutional amendment under Article 368.
3. How does India's blend of "Parliamentary Sovereignty" and "Judicial Supremacy" actually work — which body wins when they disagree?
4. When were Fundamental Duties added, how many were there originally, and when/why was an 11th added?
