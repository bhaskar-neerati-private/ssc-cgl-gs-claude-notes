## Why this topic matters

This is the "origin story" of every other Polity topic you'll study. Once you know *who* wrote the Constitution, *how long* it took, and *where each idea was borrowed from*, every later topic (Fundamental Rights, Parliament, Judiciary) slots into a timeline you already understand instead of feeling like a fresh pile of facts. SSC loves asking direct-recall questions from this topic — dates, numbers of members, committee chairs — so precision matters more than usual here.

## The Demand for a Constituent Assembly

- The idea of Indians writing their own constitution (rather than the British granting one) was first floated by **M.N. Roy** in 1934.
- The **Indian National Congress** officially demanded a Constituent Assembly at its **Faizpur Session (1936)**.
- The British accepted this demand in principle through the **August Offer (1940)**.
- It was finally constituted under the **Cabinet Mission Plan, 1946** — this is the direct legal origin of the Assembly.

**Why this sequence matters:** SSC often tests "who demanded it" vs "who accepted it" vs "who constituted it" as three separate facts disguised as one question.

## Composition of the Constituent Assembly

- Members were **indirectly elected** — by the elected members of the provincial legislative assemblies (not directly by the public).
- Original strength: **389 members** (296 from British Indian provinces + 93 from princely states).
- After Partition (and the princely states' representatives' withdrawal), the strength reduced to **299 members**.
- It had **15 women members** — a fact SSC has asked directly (names like Sarojini Naidu, Hansa Mehta, Durgabai Deshmukh are worth recognizing).

## Key Dates and People — the backbone of this topic

| Event | Date | Person/Detail |
|---|---|---|
| First meeting of the Constituent Assembly | 9 December 1946 | **Dr. Sachchidananda Sinha** was the temporary/provisional President (oldest member) |
| Permanent President elected | 11 December 1946 | **Dr. Rajendra Prasad** |
| Objectives Resolution moved | 13 December 1946 | **Jawaharlal Nehru** — laid down the philosophy later reflected in the Preamble |
| Constitutional Advisor appointed | — | **Sir B.N. Rau** — prepared the initial draft before the Drafting Committee took over |
| Drafting Committee formed | 29 August 1947 | Chaired by **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar**, 7 members |
| Constitution adopted | **26 November 1949** | Celebrated as **Constitution Day / Samvidhan Diwas** |
| Constitution came into force | **26 January 1950** | Celebrated as **Republic Day** — chosen deliberately to honour "Purna Swaraj" declared by INC on 26 Jan 1930 |

**Trap to remember:** the Constitution was *adopted* on 26 Nov 1949 but came *into force* on 26 Jan 1950 — a gap of about 2 months. A handful of provisions (citizenship, elections, provisional Parliament, and a few transitional clauses) came into force immediately on 26 Nov 1949 itself, ahead of the rest.

## The Drafting Committee (7 members) — frequently asked

Chaired by **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar**. The other six were: N. Gopalaswami Ayyangar, Alladi Krishnaswami Ayyar, K.M. Munshi, Syed Mohammad Saadulla, B.L. Mitter (later replaced due to health reasons by **N. Madhava Rau**), and D.P. Khaitan (died in 1948, replaced by **T.T. Krishnamachari**).

## Time taken & scale

- Total time taken to draft: **2 years, 11 months, 18 days**.
- The Assembly held **11 sessions** over **165 days**.
- The final session was on **24 January 1950**, where **284 members** present signed the Constitution.
- The original handwritten and calligraphed copies were prepared by **Prem Behari Narain Raizada** (calligraphy) and illustrated by artists from Shantiniketan.
- Original Constitution: **395 Articles, 22 Parts, 8 Schedules**. (Today, due to amendments, it has grown — currently around 470+ Articles, 25 Parts, and 12 Schedules; SSC sometimes asks about the *original* numbers specifically, so don't mix them up with the current numbers.)

## Borrowed Features — the most exam-friendly part of this topic

The Constitution is often called a "bag of borrowings" — but each borrowing has a clear logic, which makes it easy to remember instead of rote-learning a table.

| Borrowed From | Features |
|---|---|
| **United Kingdom** | Parliamentary form of government, Rule of Law, single citizenship, bicameral Parliament, Speaker's role, law-making procedure |
| **USA** | Fundamental Rights, Judicial Review, independence of Judiciary, impeachment of President, removal of judges |
| **Ireland** | Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP), method of election of President, nomination of members to Rajya Sabha |
| **Canada** | Federal system with a strong Centre, residuary powers vested in the Centre |
| **Australia** | Concurrent List, freedom of trade and commerce, joint sitting of two Houses of Parliament |
| **Germany (Weimar)** | Suspension of Fundamental Rights during Emergency |
| **South Africa** | Procedure for amendment of the Constitution |
| **Japan** | "Procedure established by law" |
| **France** | Republic, ideals of Liberty, Equality, Fraternity in the Preamble |
| **USSR (erstwhile)** | Fundamental Duties (added later by 42nd Amendment), the ideal of justice (social, economic, political) |

**Memory trick:** Group them by *what kind of feature* they are — "Rights & Judiciary" countries (USA), "Structure of government" countries (UK, Canada, Australia), "Philosophy/Preamble" countries (France, Ireland, USSR), "Emergency/Amendment" countries (Germany, South Africa). This turns one big table into four small, logical clusters.

## Common SSC trap areas (summary)

1. Temporary President (Sinha) vs Permanent President (Rajendra Prasad) — frequently swapped in options.
2. Adoption date (26 Nov 1949) vs Enforcement date (26 Jan 1950) — both are asked, often together.
3. Original number of Articles/Schedules (395/8) vs current numbers — make sure you know which one a question is asking about.
4. Drafting Committee size (7) and exact replacements (Khaitan → T.T. Krishnamachari) — a favourite "exact name" trap.
5. Total members (389 original vs 299 after Partition) — both numbers get tested.
