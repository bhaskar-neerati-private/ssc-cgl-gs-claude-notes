## The Mirror That Distorts

The Union Executive has a mirror image at the state level — a Governor instead of a President, a Chief Minister instead of a Prime Minister, a Vidhan Sabha instead of a Lok Sabha. Everything looks familiar. But look closely and the mirror distorts in revealing ways.

At the Union level, the President is **independently elected** by an Electoral College. At the state level, the Governor is **appointed by the President** — meaning, in practice, by the Central Government. The President acts on the binding advice of the Cabinet. The Governor acts on the advice of the state Cabinet — but has more real discretionary latitude, and can in certain situations act as the Centre's eyes and ears inside a state.

This is not accidental. India is a federation — but one that leans toward the Centre in moments of crisis. The state machinery reflects that lean. Understanding how the state executive and legislature work, and where they diverge from the Union model, is the subject of this lesson.

---

## Part I: The Governor (Articles 153–167)

### The Appointment: Why Not Elected?

The framers deliberately chose appointment over election for Governors. An elected Governor might build an independent political base and rival the Chief Minister, creating American-style gridlock within a state. An appointed Governor, by contrast, serves as a constitutional head without a competing political mandate.

**Article 155**: The Governor of each state shall be appointed by the **President** by warrant under his hand and seal. In practice, this means appointment by the Central Government.

**One person, multiple states**: Article 153 was amended to allow one person to be Governor of two or more states simultaneously — a practical arrangement for smaller states.

---

### Qualifications (Article 157)

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| Citizenship | Citizen of India |
| Age | At least **35 years** |
| Parliament/Legislature | Must **not** be a member of any Parliament or State Legislature |
| Office of profit | Must not hold any office of profit |

Notice what is absent: there is **no qualification requiring the person to be eligible for any House of Parliament** (unlike the President who must be LS-eligible). The Governor's qualification is simpler.

**Sarkaria Commission (1988)** recommendations on Governor appointment (not yet fully followed):
- Should be a person of eminence in public life from outside the state
- Should not have taken active part in state politics for some time
- Should not be closely connected to the ruling party at the Centre
- Should be appointed after consultation with the Chief Minister of the state

---

### Term and Removal (Article 156)

The Governor holds office for a term of **5 years** from the date of entering upon office — but with a critical caveat: they hold office **during the pleasure of the President**. This means the President (i.e., the Central Government) can recall a Governor before the 5 years are up, and no reason needs to be given.

**No formal removal procedure** exists for the Governor (unlike the President's impeachment process). A Governor can simply be transferred to another state or asked to resign — this has been used repeatedly to recall Governors appointed by previous Central governments when a new government takes over.

**Salary**: Charged to the Consolidated Fund of the State. Cannot be reduced during term.

**Oath**: Administered by the **Chief Justice of the High Court** of that state (or the senior-most HC judge if CJI is unavailable).

---

### Powers of the Governor

The Governor's powers mirror the President's — but with a crucial addition: **greater real discretion**.

#### Executive Powers

All executive action of the state government is taken formally in the Governor's name. The Governor appoints:
- The Chief Minister (real discretion when no single party has a majority)
- Other ministers (on CM's advice)
- The Advocate General of the State
- Members of the State Public Service Commission
- Members of the State Election Commission

#### Legislative Powers

- **Summons and prorogues** the state legislature; **dissolves** the Vidhan Sabha
- **Addresses** the legislature at the first session of each year and after each general election
- **Nominates members** to Vidhan Parishad (where it exists) — experts in literature, science, art, cooperative movement, social service
- **Assent to bills**: four options (explained below)
- **Ordinance power** (Article 213): same as President's Article 123 — can promulgate ordinances when legislature not in session; must be laid before legislature when it meets; lapses in 6 weeks

#### Pardoning Power (Article 161)

The Governor can pardon, commute, reprieve, respite, or remit sentences — but **only for offences against state laws**. Two critical limitations compared to the President (Article 72):
- **Cannot pardon death sentences** — only the President can
- **Cannot pardon court-martial convictions** — only the President can

#### Four Options on a Bill

When a bill is sent by the state legislature:
1. **Give assent** → Becomes law
2. **Withhold assent** → Bill dies (absolute veto)
3. **Return for reconsideration** → Legislature can pass again; Governor *must* then assent
4. **Reserve for President's consideration** → This is the Governor's unique power that the President does not have. The President then considers and may assent, withhold, or return.

**Bills that *must* be reserved**: Bills that would derogate the powers of the High Court; bills that clash with Union List legislation; bills amending certain constitutional provisions. These categories are constitutionally required to be reserved — not discretionary.

---

### The Governor's Real Discretion

The most contested aspect of the Governor's office is **when they can act without ministerial advice**. Article 163 says the Governor "shall in the exercise of his functions, act in accordance with such advice" — but with exceptions when the Constitution requires them to act in their *discretion*.

Discretionary situations include:
- **Appointment of CM when no party has a majority** — Governor decides who to invite to form government
- **Dismissal of CoM that has lost confidence** but refuses to resign
- **Dissolution of Vidhan Sabha** — may refuse dissolution to allow formation of an alternative government
- **Reserving a bill** for President's consideration even when not constitutionally required
- **Seeking information** from the CM on state administration matters

This discretionary zone has been the source of enormous political controversy in India. Governors have been accused of acting as "agents of the Centre" — recommending President's Rule when opposition governments are in power, delaying assent to bills, or refusing to invite the largest opposition bloc to form a government.

## Part II: Chief Minister and Council of Ministers

The Chief Minister is to the state what the Prime Minister is to the Union. The structure is a complete mirror:

| Feature | Prime Minister (Union) | Chief Minister (State) |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Appointment | By President | By Governor |
| Basis | Majority in Lok Sabha | Majority in Vidhan Sabha |
| Term | No fixed term; Lok Sabha confidence | No fixed term; Vidhan Sabha confidence |
| Collective responsibility | To Lok Sabha | To Vidhan Sabha |
| Can be from upper house? | Yes (Rajya Sabha) | Yes (Vidhan Parishad, where it exists) |

**Article 164(4)**: A minister who is not a member of the state legislature for a period of six months ceases to be minister — same 6-month rule as Parliament.

**Council of Ministers at state level**: Same three-tier structure as the Union — Cabinet Ministers, Ministers of State, Deputy Ministers. All collectively responsible to the Vidhan Sabha.

**15% cap applies here too**: The 91st Amendment (2003) applies to state legislatures as well — the total Council of Ministers including CM cannot exceed 15% of the Vidhan Sabha's total strength. Minimum: 12 ministers.

## Part III: The State Legislature

### Unicameral vs Bicameral: The Six-State Exception

Most states have only one House — the Vidhan Sabha. Only **6 states** have a second House (Vidhan Parishad):

**States with bicameral legislature (as of 2024)**:
1. **Andhra Pradesh**
2. **Karnataka**
3. **Maharashtra**
4. **Telangana**
5. **Bihar**
6. **Uttar Pradesh**

*Memory hook*: **AKMTBU** — "A King Made The Big Unicorns" — Andhra, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, Bihar, Uttar Pradesh.

All other states (Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, Tamil Nadu, etc.) are unicameral — only Vidhan Sabha.

---

### Vidhan Sabha — The State's Lok Sabha (Article 170)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Maximum strength | Not more than **500** members |
| Minimum strength | Not less than **60** members |
| Exceptions | Goa, Sikkim, Mizoram, Puducherry — less than 60 permitted by law |
| Minimum age | **25 years** |
| Directly elected | Yes — by adult franchise |
| Term | **5 years** (can be dissolved earlier by Governor) |
| Presiding officer | Speaker (elected by Vidhan Sabha members) |

The Vidhan Sabha alone can pass a **no-confidence motion** against the state government — the Vidhan Parishad cannot. The Vidhan Sabha alone handles Money Bills.

---

### Vidhan Parishad — The State's Rajya Sabha (Article 171)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Maximum strength | Not more than **1/3rd of Vidhan Sabha strength** |
| Minimum strength | Not less than **40** members |
| Minimum age | **30 years** |
| Term | **6 years**; 1/3rd retire every 2 years |
| Can be dissolved? | **No** — permanent body, like Rajya Sabha |
| Presiding officer | Chairman (elected by Vidhan Parishad members) |

**How VP members are chosen** — five groups:
- **1/3rd** elected by members of the Vidhan Sabha
- **1/3rd** elected by members of local bodies (municipalities, district boards, panchayats)
- **1/12th** elected by graduates of 3 or more years' standing in the state
- **1/12th** elected by teachers of secondary schools and above
- **1/6th** nominated by the Governor (for contribution to science, literature, art, cooperative movement, social service)

This multi-source composition is very different from Rajya Sabha (which is entirely elected by MLAs + 12 nominated). The Vidhan Parishad represents a broader cross-section — local governments, educated professionals, teachers — alongside politicians.

---

### Creating and Abolishing Vidhan Parishad (Article 169)

A state cannot unilaterally create or abolish its own upper house. The process:
1. The Vidhan Sabha passes a resolution by **special majority** (majority of total membership + 2/3rd of members present and voting) requesting Parliament to create or abolish the Vidhan Parishad
2. Parliament then passes legislation giving effect to the resolution

This is the constitutional irony: a state's upper house can be abolished, but only by the state's lower house asking the Centre to do it. Parliament acts here as the executing authority, not the decision-making one.

**Recent changes**: Andhra Pradesh abolished its Vidhan Parishad in 2020, then reconstituted it in 2024. Jammu & Kashmir lost its Vidhan Parishad when it became a Union Territory in 2019.

---

### Money Bills in States

Exactly mirrors the Union:
- Money Bills originate **only in Vidhan Sabha**
- Sent to Vidhan Parishad (where it exists)
- Vidhan Parishad can only recommend amendments; must return within **14 days**
- If not returned in 14 days, deemed passed
- Speaker of Vidhan Sabha certifies a bill as a Money Bill — **final, unchallengeable**

## Governor vs President: The Key Differences

| Feature | President | Governor |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| How appointed | Elected by Electoral College | Appointed by President |
| Removal | Impeachment (very difficult) | At President's pleasure (easy) |
| Discretionary powers | Very limited in practice | More — especially in political crises |
| Pardoning power | Death sentences, court-martial, Union laws | State laws only; no death sentence |
| Represents | Union (the whole of India) | State + Centre's agent in state |
| Reserves bills for | N/A | President's consideration |
| Reports on | N/A | State governance to Centre (Article 167) |

---

## The Article 167 Obligation: Governor as Information Channel

Article 167 requires the CM to communicate to the Governor:
- All decisions of the CoM relating to administration and legislative proposals
- Information about state administration if the Governor asks
- Any matter on which a minister has decided alone (on Governor's request, submit to CoM for decision)

This mirrors Article 78 (PM's duties to President). The Governor is the Centre's eyes and ears in the state — this information channel is one reason the Governor's role remains contested.

## Exam Traps

1. **Governor is appointed, not elected** — by the President, not by any electoral college.
2. **Governor's term is 5 years but not fixed** — holds office at President's pleasure; can be recalled anytime.
3. **Governor cannot pardon death sentences** — only the President can (Art 72 vs Art 161).
4. **Only 6 states are bicameral** — the rest have only Vidhan Sabha.
5. **Vidhan Parishad: max = 1/3rd of Vidhan Sabha, min = 40** — different formula from Rajya Sabha's fixed 250 max.
6. **Vidhan Parishad cannot be dissolved** — permanent body like Rajya Sabha.
7. **Article 169 = creating/abolishing Vidhan Parishad** — Vidhan Sabha requests; Parliament legislates.
8. **Governor qualifications: no LS/RS eligibility requirement** — only citizen + 35 years + not an MP/MLA.
9. **Ordinance by Governor (Art 213)** — mirrors Art 123 of President; lapses 6 weeks after legislature reassembles.
10. **104th Amendment (2019) abolished Governor's nomination of Anglo-Indian to state assemblies** — same as Lok Sabha change.
11. **Oath of Governor** administered by **Chief Justice of the High Court** (President's oath by CJI of SC — don't confuse).
12. **15% CoM size cap** (91st Amendment) applies to **state CoM too**, not just Union.
