## Created by Laws, Not by Constitution

Constitutional bodies are buildings only the Constitution can demolish. Statutory bodies are built on rented land — Parliament created them and Parliament can restructure or abolish them by passing another ordinary law. This is the essential difference, and it matters because statutory bodies' independence is more fragile.

India's Constitution envisioned a lean state: a few key institutions with clear mandates. But seventy-five years of governance complexity — economic regulation, anti-corruption oversight, environmental protection, human rights monitoring, disaster management — have produced dozens of statutory bodies that now do much of the real work of modern governance. Every time Parliament felt a need that no existing institution could meet, it passed a law and created a new body.

This lesson covers the major statutory (and some executive) bodies that SSC tests regularly.

---

## The Critical Distinction — Three Types

Before the bodies themselves, the framework:

| Type | How Created | Example | Can Parliament abolish? |
|------|------------|---------|------------------------|
| **Constitutional** | Written into the Constitution | Election Commission, UPSC, CAG | No — needs constitutional amendment |
| **Statutory** | Act of Parliament | NHRC, CIC, Lokpal, SEBI | Yes — by ordinary legislation |
| **Executive** | Cabinet resolution / executive order | NITI Aayog, Planning Commission | Yes — even without legislation |

**Executive bodies** are the most fragile: a government can create or dissolve them with a Cabinet decision, without passing any law at all.

## Body 1: NITI Aayog

### The Replacement Nobody Planned For

In January 2015, the Modi government dissolved the **Planning Commission** — a body that had existed since 1950 and shaped Indian economic policy through twelve Five-Year Plans — and replaced it with NITI Aayog (National Institution for Transforming India) by a **Cabinet Resolution**.

**Type**: Executive body — NOT statutory, NOT constitutional. Created by Cabinet resolution; can be dissolved the same way.

| Feature | NITI Aayog |
|---------|-----------|
| Established | **1 January 2015** |
| Created by | Cabinet Resolution (executive) |
| Chairperson | **Prime Minister** (ex-officio) |
| Vice-Chairperson | Appointed by PM |
| Governing Council | CMs of all states + Lt Governors of UTs |
| Headquarters | New Delhi |

### The Key Contrast: NITI Aayog vs Planning Commission

The most tested comparison in this area:

| Feature | Planning Commission (1950–2014) | NITI Aayog (2015–present) |
|---------|-------------------------------|--------------------------|
| Financial power | **Yes** — allocated funds to states (real power) | **No** — purely advisory think tank |
| Five-Year Plans | Prepared them | Replaced by Action Agendas, strategy documents |
| Status | Executive body (same) | Executive body |
| Centre-State dynamic | Top-down; states were supplicants | Cooperative federalism; states have voice through Governing Council |
| Mandate | Central planning, resource allocation | Policy vision, strategy, innovation |

**Punchline**: NITI Aayog cannot allocate a single rupee to any state. Money flows through the Finance Commission (constitutional) and direct Central budgetary allocations. NITI Aayog does ideas; it does not do money.

## Body 2: National Human Rights Commission (NHRC)

### The Human Rights Watchdog

**Act**: Protection of Human Rights Act, **1993**  
**Established**: 1993

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Chairperson | Retired **Chief Justice of India** |
| Members | 2 retired SC judges + 2 retired HC Chief Justices |
| "Deemed" members | Chairpersons of: NCSC, NCST, NCW, NCBC, NCPCR, Chief Commissioner for Disabled Persons — 7 total |
| Appointed by | **President** (on committee recommendation: PM + Speaker LS + Home Minister + Leader of Opposition in both Houses) |
| Term | **3 years** or age **70**, whichever earlier; no re-appointment |
| Headquarters | New Delhi |

### What NHRC Can and Cannot Do

**Can investigate**: Complaints of human rights violations by government agencies; initiate suo motu (on its own) investigations; visit government facilities.

**Cannot investigate**:
- Complaints more than **1 year old** from the date of the alleged violation
- Complaints against **armed forces** (can only seek a report from the Ministry of Defence — a significant limitation)
- Matters pending before a State Human Rights Commission

NHRC decisions are **not binding** — it can recommend compensation and remedial action, but cannot enforce. Enforcement is through approaching the Supreme Court.

**State Human Rights Commissions**: Chairperson must be a retired **HC Chief Justice** (not SC CJI — note the difference from NHRC).

## Body 3: Central Information Commission (CIC)

### The RTI Enforcer

**Act**: Right to Information Act, **2005**

The RTI Act created a two-tier information regime: Public Information Officers at the department level → First Appellate Authority within the department → Central Information Commission (final appellate authority).

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Head | Chief Information Commissioner |
| Members | Up to 10 Information Commissioners |
| Appointed by | **President** (on committee recommendation: PM + a Cabinet Minister nominated by PM + Leader of Opposition in LS) |
| Term | **5 years** or age **65**, whichever earlier; no re-appointment |

### Powers of the CIC

- Hear second appeals against denial of information under RTI
- Inquire into complaints about failure to disclose information
- **Impose penalty** on erring public information officers (up to ₹25,000)
- **Award compensation** to the applicant in certain cases

**State Information Commissions**: For state government information; appointments by Governor; same structure at state level.

## Body 4: Lokpal

### The Anti-Corruption Ombudsman (Finally)

India debated an anti-corruption ombudsman for decades. The Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, **2013** finally created it — after a landmark civil society movement led by Anna Hazare in 2011.

**Act**: Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, **2013**  
**First Lokpal**: Justice Pinaki Chandra Ghose (March 2019)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Chairperson | Retired CJI OR retired SC judge OR eminent person with impeccable integrity |
| Members | 8 (50% must be judicial members; 50% of total must be from SC/ST/OBC/minorities/women) |
| Appointed by | **President** (on selection committee: PM + Speaker LS + Leader of Opposition LS + CJI or a SC judge nominated by CJI + one eminent jurist) |
| Term | **5 years** or age **70**, whichever earlier |
| Headquarters | New Delhi |

### Jurisdiction

Lokpal covers:
- **Prime Minister** (with restrictions — national security, foreign relations, atomic energy excluded; full Bench must investigate)
- All Union Ministers
- Members of Parliament (for corruption, not for speeches/votes in Parliament)
- Group A, B, C, D government officers
- Employees of Central government-aided NGOs above a threshold

### Lokayuktas (State Level)

There is no single law — states create Lokayuktas through their own state legislation. Karnataka was the first state to establish a Lokayukta in **1973**. The 2013 Act requires states to establish Lokayuktas but implementation varies.

## Body 5: Central Vigilance Commission (CVC)

**Act**: Central Vigilance Commission Act, **2003**  
(Originally created by executive resolution in 1964; given statutory status in 2003)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Composition | Central Vigilance Commissioner + 2 Vigilance Commissioners |
| Appointed by | **President** (on committee: PM + Home Minister + Leader of Opposition in LS) |
| Term | **4 years** or age **65**, whichever earlier; no re-appointment |
| Removal | By President after SC inquiry (like UPSC members) |

### Functions

- Superintends vigilance (anti-corruption) administration in Central government
- Exercises superintendence over the CBI **in cases referred to it**
- Advises Central government on integrity matters
- Reviews work done by Departmental Vigilance Officers

The CVC is the nearest India has to a general anti-corruption oversight body within the executive — distinct from Lokpal (which is more of an ombudsman/complaint body) and the CBI (which is the investigating agency).

## Body 6: Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI)

### The Premier Investigating Agency

**Legal basis**: Delhi Special Police Establishment Act, **1946** (not created by the Constitution or by any specific "CBI Act")  
**Created**: 1941 as Special Police Establishment; renamed CBI in 1963

The CBI is unusual: it is not a statutory body in the clean sense — it operates under the DSPE Act (which was enacted pre-independence) and derives its existence from a government resolution. It is technically a police force under the Ministry of Personnel.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Supervising ministry | Ministry of Personnel, Public Grievances and Pensions |
| Director's tenure | Fixed **2-year term** (made statutory after Supreme Court directive in Vineet Narain case 1997) |
| Controlled by | CVC for anti-corruption cases; Ministry of Home Affairs otherwise |

### The Consent Requirement — A Key Limitation

The CBI is NOT a national investigative agency with automatic nationwide jurisdiction. To investigate a crime in any state, the CBI requires **consent** of that state government — unless the matter was referred by the Supreme Court or a High Court, or falls under specific Central legislation.

This "consent" requirement has led to political controversies: states ruled by opposition parties have sometimes refused CBI investigation into cases, claiming political interference.

### "Caged Parrot" — The Independence Problem

In 2013, during the investigation of the Coalgate scam, the Supreme Court called the CBI a "**caged parrot speaking in its master's voice**" — highlighting the agency's dependence on the government it investigates. This characterisation led to debates about giving CBI genuine statutory independence.

## Body 7: National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA)

**Act**: Disaster Management Act, **2005**

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Chairperson | **Prime Minister** (ex-officio) |
| Vice-Chairperson | Appointed by PM |
| Members | Up to 9, appointed by PM |
| State level | State Disaster Management Authorities — chaired by Chief Minister |

**Functions**: Lay down policies for disaster management; approve National/State Disaster Management Plans; coordinate with Central Ministries and state governments during disasters.

**National Disaster Response Force (NDRF)**: A specialist force under the NDMA for disaster response — 16 battalions, deployed during floods, earthquakes, cyclones, and other disasters.

---

## Body 8: National Green Tribunal (NGT)

**Act**: National Green Tribunal Act, **2010**

India is one of the few countries with a dedicated environmental court. The NGT provides specialized, fast-track resolution of environmental disputes.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Chairperson | Retired SC judge |
| Expert members | Environmental scientists and specialists |
| Headquarters | New Delhi; benches in Chennai, Pune, Bhopal, Kolkata |
| Jurisdiction | Civil cases involving enforcement of environmental laws; compensation for environmental damage |
| Powers | Civil court powers; can impose fines and order restoration |
| Limitation | **Cannot try criminal cases** — only civil environmental disputes |

---

## Body 9: SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India)

**Act**: SEBI Act, **1992**  
(Originally set up by executive resolution in 1988; given statutory status in 1992)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Chairperson + members | Appointed by Central Government |
| Headquarters | Mumbai |
| Jurisdiction | Stock markets, securities transactions, mutual funds, portfolio managers, brokers |

SEBI has three types of power concentrated in one body:
- **Quasi-legislative**: Makes regulations
- **Quasi-judicial**: Conducts hearings, adjudicates disputes
- **Quasi-executive**: Investigates and enforces compliance

## Exam Traps

1. **NITI Aayog is NOT a statutory body** — created by Cabinet Resolution; NOT by Parliament.
2. **Planning Commission had financial allocation power; NITI Aayog does not** — it is a pure think tank.
3. **NHRC Chairperson = retired CJI** (not serving CJI); **State HRC = retired HC Chief Justice** (not CJI).
4. **NHRC cannot probe armed forces** — can only seek a report from the Defence Ministry.
5. **NHRC: complaints must be within 1 year** of the alleged violation.
6. **CIC: 5 years or 65**, no re-appointment; CVC: **4 years or 65**, no re-appointment.
7. **CBI is NOT a statutory body in the clean sense** — operates under DSPE Act, 1946.
8. **CBI needs state consent** to investigate in states (unless court-directed).
9. **Lokpal: first appointed March 2019** (Justice Pinaki Chandra Ghose) — 6 years after the Act.
10. **NDMA Chairperson = PM** (ex-officio); **NDRF** is the specialist force under NDMA.
11. **NGT cannot try criminal cases** — only civil environmental disputes.
12. **CVC was originally 1964 executive resolution; statutory only since 2003** — the distinction matters.

## Self-Test (5 Questions)

1. What is the fundamental difference between a constitutional body, a statutory body, and an executive body? Give one example of each.
2. Why is NITI Aayog considered less powerful than the Planning Commission it replaced, even though both were executive bodies?
3. What type of cases can the NHRC NOT investigate, and why?
4. What is the "consent requirement" in the context of the CBI's jurisdiction, and why is it controversial?
5. Which body has a Prime Minister as its ex-officio Chairperson — NITI Aayog or NDMA or both?

*(Answers: 1. Constitutional = created by Constitution, needs constitutional amendment to abolish (e.g., ECI); Statutory = created by Parliament's law, ordinary legislation can abolish (e.g., NHRC); Executive = created by Cabinet resolution, no legislation needed (e.g., NITI Aayog). 2. Planning Commission allocated funds to states (real power); NITI Aayog only advises and recommends — has no power to transfer money. 3. NHRC cannot investigate (a) complaints more than 1 year old, (b) matters involving armed forces (only seeks Ministry of Defence report), (c) matters pending before a State HRC. 4. CBI needs the consent of a state government before investigating crimes in that state — it cannot enter a state's jurisdiction unilaterally (unless court-directed). Controversial because state governments (especially opposition-ruled ones) can deny consent to block CBI investigations. 5. Both — PM is ex-officio Chair of NITI Aayog AND Chairperson of NDMA.)*
