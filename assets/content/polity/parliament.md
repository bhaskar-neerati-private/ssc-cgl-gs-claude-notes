## Where India's Democracy Breathes

Every rupee the government spends, every law that governs your life, every treaty India signs with a foreign country — all of it must pass through one building on Sansad Marg in New Delhi. Parliament is where the abstract idea of democracy becomes concrete: elected representatives gather, argue, vote, and decide for a billion people.

But Parliament is not just one body. It is **three**, working in a careful constitutional balance:

**Parliament = President + Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha**

Students often forget the President. Article 79 says: "There shall be a Parliament of the Union which shall consist of the President and two Houses to be known respectively as the Council of States and the House of the People." The President is constitutionally part of Parliament — which is why bills need Presidential assent to become law.

Understanding Parliament means understanding why each of these three components exists, what each can do that the others can't, and where they must work together. That balance is the real story of this lesson.

## Lok Sabha — The House of the People (Articles 81, 83–85)

The Lok Sabha is the **directly elected lower house** — the one that directly represents the people's current will. Every five years (or earlier if dissolved), Indians vote for their local Member of Parliament, and the collective result determines who runs the country.

### Composition

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Maximum strength | 552 (530 states + 20 UTs + 2 Anglo-Indian nominated) |
| **Current strength** | **543** — the 104th Amendment (2019) abolished Anglo-Indian nominations |
| Directly elected | Yes — first-past-the-post system |
| Minimum age | **25 years** |
| Term | **5 years** from first sitting after general election |
| Can be dissolved? | **Yes** — by President on PM's advice |
| Quorum | 1/10th of total membership = ~55 members |

### Election Method

Members of Lok Sabha are elected by universal adult franchise — every citizen above 18 (lowered from 21 by the **61st Amendment, 1988**) can vote, using a first-past-the-post system (candidate with the most votes in a constituency wins, even without a majority).

### Presiding Officer — The Speaker

The **Speaker of Lok Sabha** is the most powerful neutral officer in the Indian constitutional system. Here's what makes this office extraordinary:

**How elected**: By Lok Sabha members from among themselves, by a simple majority of members present and voting.

**How removed**: By an **effective majority** — more than 50% of the **total membership** of Lok Sabha (not just present and voting). This is a deliberately high bar. 14 days' advance notice must be given before a removal resolution.

**Powers that make the Speaker unique**:
- **Certifies Money Bills** — if the Speaker says a bill is a Money Bill, that decision is **final and cannot be challenged in any court**. No judicial review.
- **Presides over joint sittings** of both Houses — not the VP/RS Chairman.
- **Controls anti-defection proceedings** under the 10th Schedule — decides whether an MP has defected and must be disqualified. (This power has been controversial, as the Speaker often belongs to the ruling party.)
- **Casting vote only** — the Speaker does not vote in ordinary proceedings. Only votes to break a tie.

**Pro-tem Speaker**: Appointed by the President when a new Lok Sabha meets for the first time. Usually the senior-most member. Administers oaths to new MPs, presides until the new Speaker is elected, then the pro-tem's role ends.

## Rajya Sabha — The Permanent Guardian (Articles 80, 83, 89)

If Lok Sabha reflects the current will of the people, Rajya Sabha reflects something different: **continuity, the states' voice, and the wisdom of revision**. It is the upper house, but more importantly, it is a **permanent body** — unlike Lok Sabha, it can never be dissolved.

Think of Rajya Sabha as the guardian who watches over government as PMs come and go. It cannot form or bring down governments (no no-confidence motions). It cannot block the budget (cannot reject Money Bills). But it can delay ordinary legislation, forcing reconsideration, and it holds two unique constitutional powers that Lok Sabha does not have.

### Composition

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Maximum strength | 250 (238 elected + 12 nominated) |
| Current strength | 245 |
| Elected by | Elected members of State Legislative Assemblies — proportional representation by single transferable vote |
| Nominated (12) | By President — persons with expertise in literature, science, art, or social service |
| Minimum age | **30 years** |
| Term | **6 years**; 1/3rd retire every **2 years** |
| Can be dissolved? | **No** — permanent body |

### Why Indirect Election?

Rajya Sabha represents the **states in the federal structure** — not individual voters. So states elect it through their own elected representatives (MLAs). A larger state sends more RS members. This mirrors the federal principle: states have a guaranteed voice in the national legislature that cannot be dissolved away.

### Presiding Officer — VP as ex-officio Chairman

The Vice-President is the ex-officio Chairman of Rajya Sabha (Lesson 7 flashback). The Deputy Chairman of Rajya Sabha is elected by RS members from among themselves — and handles sessions when the VP is absent or acting as President.

## Qualifications and Disqualification for MPs

### To Become an MP

| Qualification | Lok Sabha | Rajya Sabha |
|--------------|-----------|-------------|
| Citizen | Yes | Yes |
| Minimum age | **25 years** | **30 years** |
| Registered voter | Any parliamentary constituency | Any state in India |
| Must not hold office of profit | Yes | Yes |

### Disqualification (Article 102)

An MP is disqualified if they:
- Hold an **office of profit** under the Union or State government (with exceptions)
- Are declared of **unsound mind** by a competent court
- Are an **undischarged insolvent**
- Are not a citizen or have voluntarily acquired foreign citizenship
- Defect from their party — **10th Schedule** (Anti-Defection Law)

## Types of Bills: The Four-Way Split

How a bill is treated depends on what kind it is. Getting these categories wrong is one of the most common SSC exam errors.

### 1. Ordinary Bill

- Can originate in **either House**
- Both Houses must pass it (same version or conference version)
- If disagreement persists → **joint sitting** called by President
- Example: Criminal law amendments, social legislation

### 2. Money Bill (Article 110)

A bill is a Money Bill if it deals **only** with: taxation, government borrowing, custody of Consolidated Fund, appropriation of money from Consolidated Fund, declaration of expenditure charged on Consolidated Fund, or any purely incidental matter.

- Can originate **only in Lok Sabha**
- After LS passes it, sent to RS
- RS can only **recommend** amendments — Lok Sabha may accept or reject these
- RS must return it within **14 days** — whether or not it does, the bill is deemed passed
- **No joint sitting** for Money Bills — Lok Sabha's will is final
- **Speaker's certification is conclusive** — courts cannot question it

*The classic trap:* Finance Bill is NOT the same as Money Bill. A Finance Bill is the bill that gives effect to the budget — it contains some Money Bill provisions but also other provisions. Only a bill dealing exclusively with money matters gets the Money Bill tag.

### 3. Financial Bill (Category A)

Contains Money Bill provisions *plus* other matters. Treated as an ordinary bill for most purposes — can go to joint sitting, unlike a pure Money Bill.

### 4. Constitutional Amendment Bill (Article 368)

- Can originate in **either House**
- Must be passed by **both Houses separately** — **no joint sitting**
- Special majority required: 2/3rd of members present and voting + majority of total membership of each House
- Some provisions also need ratification by at least half the state legislatures

*Note:* For the exam, remember the two things that have **no joint sitting**: Money Bills and Constitutional Amendment Bills. One because Lok Sabha dominates, the other because they must both consent equally.

## Joint Sitting (Article 108)

When Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha cannot agree on an **ordinary bill** (not Money Bill, not Constitutional Amendment Bill), the deadlock is broken by a joint sitting of both Houses, called by the **President** and presided over by the **Speaker of Lok Sabha**.

In a joint sitting, the combined strength is roughly: 543 (LS) + 245 (RS) = 788. Lok Sabha has more than double the seats, so it almost always wins a joint sitting vote. The mechanism exists as a safety valve against Rajya Sabha obstruction — but it is also rare.

**Only three joint sittings in Indian history:**
1. **Dowry Prohibition Bill** — 1961
2. **Banking Service Commission (Repeal) Bill** — 1978
3. **Prevention of Terrorism Act (POTA)** — 2002

## Special Powers: What Only One House Can Do

### Powers Only Lok Sabha Has

1. **Money Bills**: Originate only here; Rajya Sabha cannot block
2. **No-confidence motion**: Only LS can pass; government falls if it does
3. **Joint sitting**: LS Speaker presides; LS has numerical dominance
4. Vote on **Financial Emergency (Article 360)** must be approved by LS

### Powers Only Rajya Sabha Has

1. **Article 249**: RS can pass a resolution (by 2/3rd of members present and voting) authorising Parliament to make laws on **State List subjects** — usually only state legislatures can legislate on these. The resolution is valid for 1 year (renewable).

2. **Article 312**: RS can pass a resolution (by 2/3rd of members present and voting) for creation of **new All-India Services** — services common to the Union and states (like IAS, IPS, IFS).

3. **Rajya Sabha can never be dissolved** — continuity of Parliament is guaranteed even when Lok Sabha is dissolved between elections.

## Budget and Financial Business

The **Union Budget** is India's annual financial statement (Article 112) — the government's income and expenditure plan. The Budget process follows a fixed path:

1. Finance Minister presents it in **Lok Sabha** (since 2017, on February 1st instead of the last day of February)
2. **General Discussion** in both Houses — broad debate, no voting at this stage
3. **Demands for Grants** voted on in Lok Sabha — department by department
4. **Appropriation Bill** authorises the spending — must pass Lok Sabha
5. **Finance Bill** makes tax changes — once passed, becomes Finance Act
6. **Vote on Account**: Parliament can grant a provisional sum for the first 2 months of the fiscal year, before the full budget process is complete — so the government doesn't run out of money

## Sessions, Question Hour, and Zero Hour

### Three Annual Sessions

| Session | Approximate timing | Notable |
|---------|---------------------|---------|
| **Budget Session** | February–May | Longest session; budget presented |
| **Monsoon Session** | July–August | Amendments, debates |
| **Winter Session** | November–December | Shortest; bills passed in year-end rush |

Between sessions, Parliament is in **recess**. The President summons sessions (on Cabinet advice). There is no fixed constitutional requirement for minimum sessions, but **at least two sessions per year** with not more than 6 months between them is the norm (Article 85).

### Question Hour (First Hour)

The first hour of every parliamentary sitting is Question Hour — MPs question ministers directly. Three types:

- **Starred Question**: Oral answer required; MPs can ask **supplementary questions** (follow-ups)
- **Unstarred Question**: Written answer; no supplementaries — 20+ are listed for the same day
- **Short Notice Question**: On urgent public matters; less than 10 days' notice; allowed at the Speaker's discretion

### Zero Hour

Not in the Constitution — an Indian parliamentary innovation. Begins right after Question Hour (hence "zero" — after the clock runs to end of Question Hour). MPs can raise any urgent matter of public importance without prior notice. No formal rules apply. It often generates the day's most dramatic parliamentary moments.

## Anti-Defection Law (10th Schedule) — A Quick Recap

When an MP votes against their party's whip or voluntarily gives up party membership, they can be disqualified under the 10th Schedule (studied in the Parts and Schedules lesson). The **Speaker of Lok Sabha** (or Chairman of Rajya Sabha) decides disqualification. An MP can merge their party faction without losing membership only if at least **2/3rd of the party's MPs** join the merger — this prevents small splits.

## Exam Traps

1. **Parliament = President + LS + RS** — not just the two Houses. Bills need Presidential assent.
2. **Rajya Sabha can never be dissolved** — even when Lok Sabha is dissolved between elections.
3. **Joint sitting only for ordinary bills** — not for Money Bills or Constitutional Amendment Bills.
4. **Money Bills can only originate in Lok Sabha** — never Rajya Sabha.
5. **Speaker's Money Bill certification = final** — cannot be challenged in any court.
6. **RS has 14 days for Money Bills** — whether it returns it or not, the bill is deemed passed after 14 days.
7. **No-confidence motion = Lok Sabha only** — Rajya Sabha cannot bring down a government.
8. **Article 249 and 312 are RS exclusive powers** — LS cannot pass these resolutions.
9. **Minimum age: 25 for Lok Sabha, 30 for Rajya Sabha** — not the same.
10. **104th Amendment (2019) abolished Anglo-Indian nominations** — Lok Sabha is now 543, not 552.
11. **Zero Hour is not in the Constitution** — it's a parliamentary convention that evolved in practice.
12. **Joint sitting presided over by Speaker (LS)**, not VP — many students get this reversed.
13. **RS members are elected by MLAs**, not directly by voters.
14. **Only 3 joint sittings ever** — POTA (2002) is the most recent.

## Self-Test (5 Questions)

1. What are the two types of bills for which joint sittings cannot be called, and why not?
2. What makes Rajya Sabha a "permanent body," and what two exclusive powers does it have under Articles 249 and 312?
3. Who presides over a joint sitting — the Speaker of Lok Sabha or the Vice-President?
4. Within how many days must Rajya Sabha return a Money Bill, and what happens if it fails to do so?
5. What is Zero Hour, and which article of the Constitution defines it?

*(Answers: 1. Money Bills (LS's will is final — no need for joint sitting) and Constitutional Amendment Bills (must pass both Houses separately with special majority). 2. RS cannot be dissolved even when LS is; Article 249 lets RS empower Parliament to legislate on State List; Article 312 lets RS create new All-India Services. 3. Speaker of Lok Sabha. 4. 14 days; if RS doesn't return it, the bill is deemed passed by both Houses. 5. Zero Hour is the informal period immediately after Question Hour when MPs raise urgent matters without prior notice — it is not defined in any article; it is a parliamentary convention.)*
