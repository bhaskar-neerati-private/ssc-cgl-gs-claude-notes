## The Most Powerful Office the Constitution Barely Describes

Here is a remarkable fact: the President of India has more than 20 articles dedicated to their office. The Prime Minister — who actually runs the country — gets essentially four: Articles 74, 75, 77, and 78. A few sentences, mostly about the Council of Ministers. And yet, in practice, the PM is the most powerful figure in the entire Indian constitutional system.

How does this work? By design. The framers followed the British model, where the PM's enormous power rests not on explicit constitutional grants but on **constitutional conventions** — unwritten but binding practices that have evolved over centuries. In Britain, the Prime Minister is not even mentioned in the main constitutional documents. India's framers, adopting a Westminster system, preserved this intentional gap.

The result: if you want to understand the Presidency, read the Constitution. If you want to understand the Prime Ministership, you need to read the Constitution *and* the conventions *and* the political history together.

---

## How the PM Gets the Job (and Keeps It)

**Appointment (Article 75(1))**: The President appoints the PM. But this is not a free choice — the President *must* appoint the leader who commands a majority in the Lok Sabha. Formally, the President has discretion; practically, it is exercised only in hung-parliament scenarios where no single party has a clear majority.

**No direct election**: Indians do not vote for a Prime Minister. They vote for their local MP. The PM is the leader of the party (or coalition) that wins a majority in the Lok Sabha.

**No minimum age for PM** — only the parliamentary qualification applies: the PM must be eligible for Parliament membership. That means 25 years (if from Lok Sabha) or 30 years (if from Rajya Sabha).

**Does the PM have to be from Lok Sabha?** No — the Constitution does not require it. A PM can be from Rajya Sabha. **Manmohan Singh** served as PM for a full decade (2004–2014) without ever winning a Lok Sabha seat — he was a Rajya Sabha member throughout. This was constitutionally valid and politically unprecedented.

**Does the PM have to be an MP when appointed?** Not immediately. A person can be appointed PM even without being an MP, but must become a member of either House within **6 months**. **H.D. Deve Gowda** (1996) was not an MP when sworn in — he entered Rajya Sabha shortly after.

**Term**: No fixed term. The PM holds office **during the pleasure of the President** (Article 75(2)) — but in practice this means "as long as they command a majority in Lok Sabha." If the PM loses a no-confidence vote, or if the Lok Sabha passes a resolution of no confidence in the government, the entire Council of Ministers must resign.

## The Council of Ministers: Three Tiers

The PM does not govern alone. **Article 74** mandates a Council of Ministers (CoM) with the PM at the head. The CoM has three distinct ranks — think of it as a military hierarchy:

**Tier 1 — Cabinet Ministers** (Generals)
The inner circle. Senior ministers who head major portfolios — Finance, Home, Defence, External Affairs, etc. They attend Cabinet meetings and take collective decisions. This is the real decision-making body.

**Tier 2 — Ministers of State (MoS)**
Can be of two types:
- *Independent charge*: Runs their own department without a Cabinet Minister above them
- *Attached*: Assists a Cabinet Minister in a large ministry (e.g., MoS Finance under the Finance Minister)
MoS with independent charge attend some Cabinet meetings when their subject is on the agenda; attached MoS generally do not.

**Tier 3 — Deputy Ministers**
Assist Cabinet Ministers. They do **not** attend Cabinet meetings. Rarely appointed in recent governments.

## Cabinet vs Council of Ministers: The Critical Distinction

This is one of the most tested distinctions in SSC Polity — and one of the most commonly confused.

| Feature | **Council of Ministers** | **Cabinet** |
|---------|------------------------|-------------|
| Who is in it | Cabinet Ministers + MoS + Deputy Ministers | Only Cabinet Ministers (senior) |
| Article | Article 74, Article 75 | Article 352 (Emergency) |
| Size | Larger | Smaller inner circle |
| Decision-making | Does not collectively decide | The real decision-making body |
| Constitutional status | Formal body | Mentioned in Art 352 specifically |

In everyday language, people say "cabinet" when they mean the whole government. Constitutionally, the *Cabinet* is a subset of the *Council of Ministers* — the senior ministers only. When Article 352 says the PM and majority of Cabinet must recommend a National Emergency, it means Cabinet Ministers — not every minister in the government.

**Kitchen Cabinet**: This is an informal, entirely non-constitutional concept — a small group of the PM's closest political advisers and confidants who meet informally. It has no legal status but often wields enormous real influence. Every PM has had one.

## The Size Cap: 91st Amendment (2003)

Before 2003, there was no constitutional limit on how many ministers a PM (or Chief Minister) could appoint. Some state governments had ballooned to 100+ ministers — portfolios were distributed as political patronage. The **91st Amendment** set a hard ceiling:

> The total number of ministers, including the PM, in the Council of Ministers shall not exceed **15% of the total strength of the lower House**.

For the Lok Sabha (543 seats), this means a maximum of approximately 81 ministers (15% of 543). But the amendment also provides: if 15% yields fewer than 12 ministers, the minimum is **12** — so even tiny legislatures must have at least a working Cabinet.

## Powers of the Prime Minister

The PM's power flows from four sources: formal constitutional provisions, control over the Cabinet, the political reality of commanding a parliamentary majority, and constitutional conventions.

### 1. Relationship with the President (Article 78)

Article 78 lists the PM's formal duties — and it is worth reading carefully because it quietly reveals how much the PM controls:

- The PM must *communicate* to the President all decisions of the CoM relating to administration
- The PM must *furnish information* as the President calls for
- If the President asks, the PM must *submit for Cabinet consideration* any decision a minister has taken unilaterally

In practice, this means the PM is the **sole channel** between the Council of Ministers and the President. No minister bypasses the PM to communicate directly with the President. The PM controls what the President knows and when.

### 2. Cabinet Formation and Dissolution

The PM **recommends all ministerial appointments** to the President — and the President must follow. The PM also determines who gets which portfolio, and can reshuffle portfolios at will. If a minister loses the PM's confidence, the PM can ask them to resign. If they refuse, the PM can advise the President to dismiss them. The PM's grip on the Cabinet is near-total.

### 3. Presiding over Cabinet Meetings

The PM chairs all Cabinet meetings and sets the agenda. Only what the PM allows onto the agenda gets discussed. Decisions are taken collectively — but the PM's voice carries decisive weight.

### 4. Emergency Recommendations

When a National Emergency (Article 352) is to be proclaimed, the Constitution specifically requires the written recommendation of the **Cabinet** (not the full CoM). The PM must head this recommendation.

### 5. Parliament Leadership

If from Lok Sabha, the PM is the **Leader of the House** — responsible for managing the parliamentary schedule, floor proceedings, and government business. Even when from Rajya Sabha, the PM leads the government's political position in Parliament.

### 6. All Other Key Appointments

The PM's recommendations shape virtually every major appointment in the country — Chief Election Commissioner, CJI (through the collegium process), CAG, Governors, Ambassadors, and the heads of constitutional and statutory bodies.

## Collective Responsibility: The Team Must Sink Together

**Article 75(3)**: *"The Council of Ministers shall be collectively responsible to the House of the People."*

Think of the Cabinet as a sports team. The captain (PM) picks the squad, allocates positions, and leads strategy. When a player underperforms, the captain can drop them. But when the team **loses the championship** (a no-confidence vote), everyone goes home — not just the captain. That is collective responsibility.

What this means in practice:
- Every Cabinet decision is a collective decision — no minister can publicly oppose a Cabinet decision while remaining in office
- If a minister disagrees, the choices are: argue inside the Cabinet meeting, resign before the decision is announced, or stay silent and implement it
- If the government loses a confidence vote in Lok Sabha, **all ministers resign together** — not just the PM
- No-confidence motions can only be brought in **Lok Sabha**, not Rajya Sabha

### Individual Responsibility

Alongside collective responsibility, each minister is individually responsible for their own ministry — to the PM and to Parliament. If a minister's conduct becomes a liability, they can be removed without taking down the whole government.

## Confidence and No-Confidence

**No-confidence motion**: Introduced by the opposition in Lok Sabha. If passed by simple majority of members present and voting, the government must resign. India's most famous no-confidence vote: the Vajpayee government fell in 1999 by **one vote** — 269 against 270.

**Confidence motion**: The government itself moves a motion seeking confidence (often as a political test or in response to political crisis). If it fails, the government must resign.

**Anti-defection**: A minister who votes against their own government doesn't trigger anti-defection disqualification (since there's no whip against your own party's government vote on a no-confidence motion — the party whip is on the other side of the floor).

## Exam Traps

1. **PM's tenure is NOT fixed** — "during pleasure of President" but in practice = Lok Sabha majority. No one ever says "5 years" for PM (that would be the President).
2. **PM can be from Rajya Sabha** — no constitutional requirement to be from Lok Sabha. Manmohan Singh proved this for 10 years.
3. **Cabinet ≠ Council of Ministers** — Cabinet is the senior inner circle; CoM is the full body including MoS and Deputy Ministers.
4. **91st Amendment cap = 15% of Lok Sabha** — roughly 81 ministers maximum for the Union government; minimum 12 even for smaller legislatures.
5. **No-confidence motion = Lok Sabha only** — cannot be initiated in Rajya Sabha. Government only needs Lok Sabha confidence.
6. **Article 78 = PM's duties**, not powers — the PM communicates decisions to the President; it also reveals PM's gatekeeper role.
7. **Kitchen Cabinet is not constitutional** — purely informal, no legal basis.
8. **Deputy Ministers do NOT attend Cabinet meetings** — only Cabinet Ministers do as a rule; MoS with independent charge may attend when relevant.
9. **Collective responsibility: all resign or none** — one minister's no-confidence cannot bring down just that minister via a Lok Sabha vote; it would bring down the whole government.
10. **PM need not be an MP at time of appointment** — must become one within 6 months.
