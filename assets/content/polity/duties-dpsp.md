## The Constitution's Three-Part Contract

After five lessons building the Constitution's skeleton, stop and look at what we have:

- **Fundamental Rights (Part III)**: A shield given to *citizens* — here is what the State cannot do to you.
- **DPSP (Part IV)**: A to-do list given to the *State* — here is what you are expected to do for the people.
- **Fundamental Duties (Part IVA)**: Homework given to *citizens* — here is what you owe the country in return.

Three parties, three sets of obligations. Today we meet the second and third — the ones that don't come with a court-enforceable guarantee but still shape the entire architecture of Indian governance.

---

## Part I: Directive Principles of State Policy

### Articles 36–51: The Government's To-Do List

Imagine you inherit a business from your parents. Along with the assets, they leave you a letter: *"We hope you'll pay your workers fairly, look after the neighbourhood, and keep the environment clean — but we know you'll sometimes face constraints, so we're not going to sue you for every missed item. These are our wishes, not a contract."*

That letter is the DPSP. It's not enforceable in court — Article 37 says so explicitly — but it is *fundamental to the governance of the country*, and every government since 1950 has had to reckon with it.

The DPSPs were borrowed from the **Irish Constitution of 1937**, which itself drew on the idea of a just social order. Ambedkar was actually sceptical — *why write provisions you can't enforce in court?* Dr. K.M. Munshi and others convinced him that they serve as a **moral compass** for future governments: a written record of what the framers hoped India would become, binding not in law but in political accountability.

### The Three Families of DPSPs

Scholars group the Directive Principles into three families based on what they're trying to achieve:

---

**Family 1 — The Socialist DPSPs** (building a welfare state)

These are the ones the framers borrowed from socialist thinking — the idea that economic justice requires active state intervention:

- **Article 38**: State shall work for social, economic, and political justice; reduce inequalities in income, status, and opportunities.
- **Article 39**: Six specific economic objectives — including adequate means of livelihood, equal pay for equal work (39d), and ensuring the ownership of resources serves the common good. (*Article 39A*, added later, requires free legal aid so justice isn't denied because of poverty.)
- **Article 41**: Right to work, to education, and to public assistance in cases of unemployment, old age, sickness, and disability.
- **Article 42**: Just and humane conditions of work and maternity relief.
- **Article 43**: Living wage and decent standard of life for workers.
- **Article 47**: Raise the level of nutrition and the standard of living; prohibit consumption of intoxicating drinks and drugs injurious to health. *(This is the constitutional basis for state-level prohibition laws.)*

---

**Family 2 — The Gandhian DPSPs** (rooted in Gandhi's vision of a village-centred India)

These reflect Gandhi's philosophy of *gram swaraj* — self-governing villages, cottage industries, and an agrarian economy built on dignity rather than exploitation:

- **Article 40**: Organise village panchayats and endow them with powers of self-government. *(This was the seed that grew into the 73rd Amendment and the 11th/12th Schedules we studied in the Parts & Schedules lesson.)*
- **Article 43**: Promote cottage industries in rural areas.
- **Article 43B**: Promotion of co-operative societies *(added by the 97th Amendment, 2011)*.
- **Article 46**: Promote educational and economic interests of Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and other weaker sections.
- **Article 47**: Prohibition of slaughter of cows, calves, and draught and milch cattle. *(The constitutional basis for state-level cow protection laws.)*
- **Article 48**: Organise agriculture and animal husbandry on modern, scientific lines.

---

**Family 3 — The Liberal/Intellectual DPSPs** (civil and international ideals)

These reflect the framers' commitment to liberal democratic values and India's aspirations as a newly independent nation in the global community:

- **Article 44**: Secure a **Uniform Civil Code** for all citizens. This is one of the most debated DPSPs in Indian history — it means one personal law (marriage, divorce, inheritance) for all religions instead of separate religious laws. It has been a DPSP since 1950, still not implemented.
- **Article 45**: *(Post-86th Amendment, 2002)* Early childhood care and education for children below the age of six. *(The 86th Amendment moved the 6–14 age group to Article 21A as a Fundamental Right; this article now covers 0–6.)*
- **Article 48A**: Protect and improve the environment and safeguard forests and wildlife. *(Added by the 42nd Amendment, 1976.)*
- **Article 49**: Protect monuments and places of national importance from spoliation, disfigurement, or destruction.
- **Article 50**: Separate the judiciary from the executive in public services. *(Still not fully achieved — the 1973 separation in subordinate courts is ongoing.)*
- **Article 51**: Promote international peace and security; maintain just and honourable relations between nations; encourage settlement of disputes by arbitration.

---

### The Great Constitutional Conflict: DPSPs vs Fundamental Rights

For the first two decades of the republic, Parliament tried to use DPSPs to justify laws — especially land reforms — that the Supreme Court kept striking down as violations of Fundamental Rights (particularly the Right to Property). The result was one of the longest constitutional battles in the world. Here's the story in five acts:

**Act 1 — Champakam Dorairajan (1951)**: A state government in Madras created reservation seats in medical colleges, citing the DPSP on protecting backward classes. The SC struck it down: *"When there is a conflict between a FR and a DPSP, the FR wins."* Parliament responded with the **1st Constitutional Amendment**, adding Article 15(4) — which explicitly allowed reservations for backward classes as an exception within Article 15 itself.

**Act 2 — Golak Nath (1967)**: The SC held that Parliament **cannot amend Fundamental Rights at all** — they are eternal. Parliament was furious.

**Act 3 — Kesavananda Bharati (1973)**: The Basic Structure doctrine. Parliament *can* amend FRs — but cannot destroy the Basic Structure. This opened the door for some DPSP-FR balancing.

**Act 4 — 42nd Amendment (1976)**: Indira Gandhi's government inserted **Article 31C** — laws implementing Articles 39(b) and (c) (the socialist DPSPs on resource distribution) *cannot* be challenged for violating Articles 14 and 19. The government essentially tried to declare DPSPs supreme over those FRs.

**Act 5 — Minerva Mills (1980)**: The SC struck down the broad version of 31C. The ruling established the final principle: **Harmony, not hierarchy.** The balance between FRs and DPSPs is itself *part of the Basic Structure* — one cannot be completely subordinated to the other. Courts must read them together.

*The Minerva Mills case is the exam's favourite ending to this story.* Remember: **Harmony between FRs and DPSPs = Basic Structure.**

---

### Why DPSPs Matter Even Without Court Enforcement

1. **Courts use DPSPs to interpret laws**: When the SC upholds a restriction on a Fundamental Right, it often cites a DPSP as evidence that the restriction serves a legitimate constitutional purpose.
2. **Political accountability**: Governments that ignore DPSPs face voters, not courts — but they do face them.
3. **Foundation for landmark legislation**: The Minimum Wages Act, Maternity Benefit Act, Forest Conservation Act, and many more statutes were framed to give effect to specific DPSPs.
4. **Article 37 wording**: The DPSPs are explicitly called "fundamental in the governance of the country" — even if non-justiciable.

## Part II: Fundamental Duties

### Article 51A: Citizens Get Homework Too

Here is the dark irony of how Fundamental Duties came to be.

It is 1976. The Emergency has suspended Fundamental Rights. Citizens cannot speak freely, assemble peacefully, or challenge arbitrary detention. At exactly this moment, the same government — through the **42nd Amendment** — adds a new Part IVA and Article 51A to the Constitution, listing **10 Fundamental Duties** of every Indian citizen.

Critics called it what it looked like: while curtailing rights, the State was lecturing citizens about their obligations. Defenders argued that a healthy democracy needs both rights and responsibilities. Both views have merit, and the tension is worth understanding.

The duties were based on recommendations of the **Swaran Singh Committee** (1976), which itself drew inspiration from the Soviet Constitution — one of the few constitutions in the world that explicitly lists citizens' duties.

A **11th duty** was added by the **86th Amendment (2002)**: the duty of every parent or guardian to provide opportunities for education to their child between 6 and 14 years of age. This pairs with Article 21A (Right to Education) — the child gets a right, the parent gets a duty.

---

### The 11 Fundamental Duties (Article 51A)

Group them in four clusters so they stick:

**Cluster 1 — Patriotism and the State (duties 1–4)**
1. Abide by the Constitution and respect its ideals, the National Flag, and the National Anthem
2. Cherish and follow the noble ideals of the freedom struggle
3. Uphold and protect the sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India
4. Defend the country and render national service when called upon

**Cluster 2 — Social Harmony (duties 5–6)**
5. Promote harmony and the spirit of common brotherhood among all people; renounce practices derogatory to the dignity of women
6. Value and preserve the rich heritage of our composite culture

**Cluster 3 — Environment and Public Conduct (duties 7–9)**
7. Protect and improve the natural environment — forests, lakes, rivers, wildlife
8. Develop the scientific temper, humanism, and the spirit of inquiry and reform
9. Safeguard public property and abjure violence

**Cluster 4 — Personal Excellence and Family (duties 10–11)**
10. Strive towards excellence in all spheres of individual and collective activity
11. **(86th Amendment, 2002)** Every parent or guardian shall provide opportunities for education to their child (6–14 years)

---

### Key Facts About Fundamental Duties

| Feature | Fact |
|---------|------|
| Added by | 42nd Amendment, 1976 (duties 1–10); 86th Amendment, 2002 (duty 11) |
| Article | Article 51A, Part IVA |
| Committee | Swaran Singh Committee recommended them |
| Inspired by | Soviet Constitution |
| Justiciable? | **No** — cannot be enforced in court |
| Applies to | Only **citizens** (DPSPs apply to the State; FDs apply to citizens) |
| Total duties | **11** |

**The Verma Committee (1999)**: A committee chaired by Justice J.S. Verma studied Fundamental Duties and recommended that Parliament enact laws to make certain duties legally enforceable. Some have been partially implemented — e.g., the Prevention of Insults to National Honour Act relates to Duty 1.

## The Most Tested Facts — Quick Reference

1. **DPSPs in Part IV, Articles 36–51** — borrowed from Ireland.
2. **DPSPs are non-justiciable** (Article 37 says so) but are "fundamental in governance."
3. **Three families**: Socialist (welfare state), Gandhian (villages/cottage industry), Liberal (civil/international ideals).
4. **Article 44** (Uniform Civil Code) — still a DPSP, still unimplemented.
5. **Article 48A** (environment protection) — added by 42nd Amendment, 1976.
6. **Minerva Mills (1980)** — FRs and DPSPs must be in harmony; neither can fully override the other. This harmony is part of Basic Structure.
7. **FDs in Article 51A, Part IVA** — added by 42nd Amendment, 1976.
8. **Originally 10 duties; 11th added by 86th Amendment, 2002** (parental duty to educate children 6–14).
9. **Swaran Singh Committee** recommended Fundamental Duties.
10. **FDs bind only citizens** — aliens and the State are not covered.

## Common Exam Traps

1. **DPSPs cannot be enforced in court — but they are not useless.** Courts use them to evaluate whether restrictions on FRs are reasonable. The trap: some students think "non-justiciable = irrelevant."
2. **Article 44 (Uniform Civil Code) is a DPSP, not a law.** If asked "which article mandates UCC," the answer is Article 44 — a DPSP. It has NOT been enacted as legislation.
3. **Article 47 covers two things**: (a) raise nutrition standards and (b) prohibit intoxicating drinks. Both are in the same article.
4. **Minerva Mills is about harmony, not supremacy.** DPSPs do not defeat FRs or vice versa — both must coexist.
5. **Fundamental Duties apply only to citizens.** The State (government) is not bound by Article 51A.
6. **The 11th FD (86th Amendment)** specifically says *parents/guardians* have a duty — not the child. The child has the right (Article 21A); the parent has the corresponding duty.
7. **Article 40 (Panchayats) in DPSPs** came before the 73rd Amendment — the 73rd Amendment gave it legal teeth, but Article 40 was always there as a directive.
