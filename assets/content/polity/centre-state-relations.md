## The Tug of War That Never Ends

In 1946, when the Constituent Assembly began its work, India had just survived a catastrophic Partition. The nation was fragile. The framers — Ambedkar foremost among them — made a deliberate choice: India would be a federation, but one that **leans toward the Centre** in moments of crisis. State autonomy was valued, but national unity was non-negotiable.

The result is a Constitution that creates two levels of government but gives the Centre enough tools to override the states when it judges necessary. This has generated decades of political controversy — states accusing the Centre of treating them as subordinates, not partners; the Centre arguing it must be able to maintain constitutional governance everywhere.

Understanding Centre-State relations means understanding this tension — where the Constitution draws the line, when the Centre can cross it, and how courts have pushed back to protect federalism.

---

## Three Dimensions: Legislative, Administrative, Financial

Centre-State relations operate across three domains, each with its own set of articles:
- **Legislative relations**: Articles 245–255 — who can make laws on what
- **Administrative relations**: Articles 256–263 — who controls executive action
- **Financial relations**: Articles 264–293 — who controls money

Let's take each in turn.

---

## Part I: Legislative Relations (Articles 245–255)

### The Three-List System (Article 246 + Seventh Schedule)

The Constitution divides all subjects of legislation into three lists in the **Seventh Schedule**:

| List | Who legislates | Number of subjects | Key subjects |
|------|---------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **List I — Union List** | Parliament only | 100 entries | Defence, foreign affairs, atomic energy, railways, banking, currency, insurance, communication |
| **List II — State List** | State legislatures only | 61 entries | Police, public order, agriculture, land, local government, public health, prisons |
| **List III — Concurrent List** | Both Parliament and State | 52 entries | Education, forests, trade unions, criminal law (now BNSS), marriage, family courts, labour |

**Residuary powers (Article 248)**: Anything NOT in any of the three lists falls to **Parliament**. This is the opposite of the US and Australian model, where residuary powers go to states. In India, residuary powers are with the Centre — another example of the Constitution's central tilt.

**Article 245**: Parliament can make laws for the whole of India or any part of it. A state legislature can make laws only for the territory of that state (or any part of it).

---

### When Can Parliament Legislate on the State List?

Normally, Parliament cannot legislate on List II subjects. But the Constitution provides **four exceptional windows**:

#### Window 1: Article 249 — Rajya Sabha Resolution (National Interest)

If the **Rajya Sabha** passes a resolution (supported by **2/3rd of members present and voting**) declaring that it is necessary in the national interest that Parliament should make laws on a State List subject, Parliament can legislate on that subject.

- Valid for **1 year** at a time (can be renewed)
- The law lapses 6 months after the resolution expires
- This is the only scenario where RS (not LS) triggers Parliament's expanded powers

#### Window 2: Article 250 — National Emergency

During a **National Emergency** (Article 352), Parliament can legislate on **any subject** in the State List. Such laws cease to have effect 6 months after the Emergency ends.

#### Window 3: Article 252 — Request by States

If **two or more state legislatures** pass resolutions requesting Parliament to legislate on a State List subject, Parliament may do so. The resulting law applies only to those states that passed the resolution (other states can later adopt it by passing their own resolution).

Example: This is how Parliament has legislated on matters like "Prize Competitions" and "Stray Dogs" — purely state subjects on which some states asked the Centre to create a uniform law.

#### Window 4: Article 253 — International Treaties

To implement **international treaties, agreements, or conventions**, Parliament can legislate on **any subject** — including a State List subject — without any state consent. This is how many environmental and wildlife protection laws were enacted on subjects that would otherwise be state territory.

---

### Article 254 — When Centre and State Laws Conflict (Concurrent List)

On Concurrent List subjects, both Parliament and state legislatures can make laws. If they conflict:

- **Parliament's law prevails** over the state law to the extent of the repugnancy
- **Exception**: If the state law was made after Parliament's law AND received **Presidential assent**, the state law prevails in that state — until Parliament amends or repeals it

This exception allows states to create tailored laws on Concurrent subjects, as long as the President consents.

---

## Part II: Administrative Relations (Articles 256–263)

### The Executive Power Alignment

Legislative power and executive power are designed to match: the executive power of the Union extends to matters on which Parliament can legislate; the executive power of the states extends to State List matters.

**Article 256**: The executive power of every state *must be exercised* so as to comply with laws made by Parliament. The Centre can give **directions** to state governments to ensure this compliance.

**Article 257**: The Centre can give directions to states regarding:
- Construction and maintenance of means of communication (national highways)
- Measures for protection of railways in the state
These directions are binding.

**Article 258**: The Centre can, with the state's consent, confer powers on a state or its officers for Union purposes — and vice versa (Article 258A, now largely defunct).

---

### Article 263 — Inter-State Council

Article 263 allows the **President to establish a council** for:
- Inquiring into and advising on inter-state disputes
- Investigating subjects of common interest
- Making recommendations on such subjects

The **Inter-State Council** was established in **1990** under Article 263 — it took 40 years after the Constitution's adoption to actually create this body. It is not automatic — Article 263 merely empowers the President to set it up.

*Note*: The ISC is NOT the same as the **Zonal Councils** — which are statutory (not constitutional) bodies set up under the States Reorganisation Act, 1956. Five zones: Northern, Central, Eastern, Western, Southern. Chaired by the Union Home Minister.

---

### Article 262 — Inter-State River Disputes

Parliament can legislate for adjudication of disputes relating to **inter-state rivers** or river valleys. Crucially, Parliament can **exclude the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court** over such disputes — and has done so through the Inter-State Water Disputes Act, 1956.

This is one of the rare cases where Parliament can oust SC jurisdiction. Famous river tribunals: Cauvery, Krishna, Godavari, Ravi-Beas.

---

## Part III: President's Rule — Article 356

This is the most controversial provision in Centre-State relations.

### The Trigger (Article 355 → 356)

**Article 355**: The Union has a duty to protect every state against external aggression, internal disturbance, and to ensure every state government is carried on in accordance with the Constitution. This duty is the stated basis for invoking Article 356.

**Article 356**: If the President is satisfied (on the Governor's report or otherwise) that the government of a state cannot be carried on in accordance with the Constitution, the President may proclaim an Emergency — commonly called **President's Rule** or **State Emergency**.

---

### What Happens When President's Rule is Proclaimed?

1. **President assumes** all functions of the state government
2. **Parliament assumes** the power of the state legislature — can make laws on State List subjects for that state
3. **High Court remains intact** — the HC's jurisdiction and powers are unaffected
4. The state assembly is either **suspended or dissolved**
5. The state's Council of Ministers is dismissed

---

### Duration

| Stage | Maximum period |
|-------|---------------|
| Initial proclamation (without Parliament approval) | **2 months** |
| With Parliament approval | **6 months** at a time |
| Maximum total | **3 years** |

After 1 year, every 6-month extension requires both:
- A National Emergency must be in operation in the whole state, **OR**
- The Election Commission certifies that general elections to the state assembly cannot be held

---

### S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) — The Game-Changer

Before 1994, President's Rule was frequently imposed to destabilise opposition-ruled states — a practice critics called "constitutional coup by the Centre." The Supreme Court's landmark ruling in the **S.R. Bommai case (1994)** transformed this:

- **Floor test is the right test**: The majority of a government must be tested on the floor of the Vidhan Sabha, not estimated by the Governor
- **Judicial review**: Courts can review whether the proclamation under Article 356 was justified — it is not immune from judicial scrutiny
- **Restore the government**: If President's Rule is held unconstitutional, the court can restore the dismissed government
- **Secularism is Basic Structure**: States where governments engage in anti-secular activities could, however, legitimately invite Central intervention

The Bommai case effectively ended the routine political misuse of Article 356 — imposition of President's Rule has been far less frequent since 1994.

---

### How Many Times Has It Been Used?

President's Rule has been imposed over **130 times** in India's history (counting multiple proclamations in the same state). The record: Uttar Pradesh (12 times), Manipur (10 times). Many of these pre-Bommai are considered politically motivated.

## Part IV: Financial Relations — The Headlines

Financial relations are covered in Articles 264–293, but for SSC CGL, the key landmarks are:

### Finance Commission (Article 280)

A **constitutional body** set up by the President every **5 years** (or earlier). It recommends:
- Distribution of net tax proceeds between Centre and states
- Grants-in-aid to states
- Any other matter referred by the President

The Finance Commission's recommendations are not binding — they are advisory — but are almost always accepted. The 16th Finance Commission is currently constituted (2024).

### GST Council (Article 279A)

Added by the **101st Constitutional Amendment (2016)**. The GST Council replaced the fragmented sales tax system with a unified Goods and Services Tax. It consists of:
- Union Finance Minister (Chair)
- Minister of State for Finance (Centre)
- Finance Ministers of all states

Voting weightage: Centre has 1/3rd weight; states collectively have 2/3rd weight. Most decisions require 3/4th majority. This is genuinely federal — states collectively can outvote the Centre.

### Article 275 — Grants-in-Aid

Parliament may make grants to states that are in need of assistance. These grants are charged to the Consolidated Fund of India.

---

## The Sarkaria and Punchhi Commissions

**Sarkaria Commission (1988)** — established to examine Centre-State relations after decades of complaints from opposition-ruled states. Key recommendations:
- Article 356 should be used as a **last resort**; other remedies should be exhausted first
- Governor should not be imposed from the ruling party at the Centre
- **Statutory Inter-State Council** should be created (led to ISC in 1990)
- Residuary powers should eventually be moved to the Concurrent List, not kept with Centre

**Punchhi Commission (2010)** — follow-up review. Key additions:
- Recommended "localization" — more subjects moved to Concurrent List
- On Governor: should be removable only by following a process involving Parliament (not at President's pleasure)
- Article 355 should be amended to allow Central intervention in parts of a state, not the whole state

Both commissions' recommendations are largely unimplemented — the political incentive to accumulate Central power remains stronger than the impulse to decentralise.

## Exam Traps

1. **Residuary powers go to Parliament (Centre)**, not states — opposite of US/Australia.
2. **Article 249 requires Rajya Sabha resolution**, not Lok Sabha — RS triggers this special Parliament power.
3. **Article 249 resolution valid for 1 year**, not indefinitely; can be renewed.
4. **Article 252 = two or more states request** Parliament to legislate; law binds only those states.
5. **Article 253 = treaties** — Parliament can legislate on any subject (including State List) to implement international agreements; no state consent needed.
6. **Article 254 repugnancy rule**: Parliament wins, UNLESS state law has Presidential assent.
7. **Article 356** = State Emergency / President's Rule; Article 352 = National Emergency; Article 360 = Financial Emergency.
8. **HC is NOT suspended under Article 356** — only the state executive and legislature are affected.
9. **Bommai case (1994)**: made Article 356 judicially reviewable; floor test is the correct test for majority.
10. **Zonal Councils ≠ Inter-State Council** — ZCs are statutory (States Reorganisation Act, 1956); ISC is set up by President under Article 263.
11. **Finance Commission** recommends (advisory), not decides — but recommendations almost always followed.
12. **GST Council = 101st Amendment (2016)**, Article 279A — Centre 1/3rd weight, states 2/3rd weight.
