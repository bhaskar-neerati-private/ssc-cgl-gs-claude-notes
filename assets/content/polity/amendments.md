## Parliament vs. Courts: The Story of Indian Amendments

India's Constitution has been amended 106 times (as of 2023). But this is not a list of bureaucratic tweaks — it is the record of a running battle between two powerful forces: a Parliament that wanted to pursue social transformation without judicial interference, and a Supreme Court that asserted its right to protect the Constitution's core from unlimited parliamentary power.

Most major amendments follow this pattern: **Court rules against government → Parliament amends to override → Court strikes back, or Parliament accepts**. Understanding this dialectic is what makes constitutional amendments a story rather than a data dump.

The procedure itself is worth understanding first — because not all amendments are equal in how they are made.

---

## The Amendment Procedure (Article 368)

### Three Modes of Amendment

| Type | Procedure | Used for |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Simple majority** | Majority of members present and voting in each House | Certain provisions outside Article 368 — e.g., creating new states (Art 3), admitting states (Art 2), Second Schedule, citizenship provisions |
| **Special majority** | Majority of **total membership** + **2/3rd of members present and voting** in EACH House | Most constitutional amendments — FR, DPSP, structure of government, etc. |
| **Special majority + State ratification** | Special majority in Parliament PLUS ratification by legislatures of **at least half of states** | Federal provisions: election of President, executive power of Union/States, distribution of legislative powers, SC and HC, representation in Parliament |

**No joint sitting for constitutional amendments** — unlike ordinary bills, if one House rejects an amendment, there is no mechanism for a joint sitting. Both Houses must independently pass it.

**President's assent** (after 24th Amendment 1971): The President **must** give assent to a Constitutional Amendment Bill — cannot withhold it. This was established after the Golak Nath controversy.

## Part I: The Parliament-Court Battle over Fundamental Rights

### The 1st Amendment (1951) — The Opening Shot

Barely a year into the Constitution's operation, Parliament faced a problem: the courts were striking down land reform laws as violations of Article 19(1)(f) (right to property) and Article 14 (equality). The government needed to redistribute land — but courts kept blocking it.

**1st Amendment's response**:
- Added the **Ninth Schedule** — laws placed in this schedule are immune from judicial review on grounds of violating Fundamental Rights
- Added "reasonable restrictions" clauses in Article 19 — made it easier to justify restrictions on freedom of speech in the interest of "public order" and "relations with foreign states"
- Also allowed reservations in Articles 15 and 16

**Result**: Parliament created a judicial review-proof zone for land reform laws. This set the pattern for the decades ahead.

---

### Golak Nath Case (1967) — The Court Pushes Back

The Supreme Court ruled (6:5 majority) in *I.C. Golak Nath v State of Punjab* that **Parliament cannot amend Fundamental Rights at all** — FRs are beyond the amending power of Parliament. This was a direct challenge to Parliament's authority.

Parliament's response was swift and sweeping.

---

### 24th Amendment (1971) — Parliament Strikes Back

- Declared that Parliament **CAN amend any provision of the Constitution**, including Fundamental Rights, using the Article 368 procedure
- Made it mandatory for the **President to give assent** to Constitutional Amendment Bills (removing the President's discretion)
- Overruled Golak Nath directly

### 25th Amendment (1971) — Protecting Land Reform

- Inserted **Article 31C**: Any law giving effect to DPSP Articles 39(b) and 39(c) — distribution of material resources and prevention of concentration of wealth — shall NOT be void even if it abridges Articles 14 (equality) or 19 (freedoms)
- Response to bank nationalisation case (*R.C. Cooper v Union of India*) which had struck down bank nationalisation on FR grounds

---

### Kesavananda Bharati (1973) — The Basic Structure Doctrine

Parliament thought it had won. The Supreme Court had other ideas.

In the landmark *Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala* (1973), a 13-judge Constitution bench ruled (7:6):
- Parliament **CAN** amend any provision of the Constitution — upholding the 24th Amendment
- BUT Parliament **CANNOT destroy the basic structure** of the Constitution
- The Basic Structure includes: supremacy of the Constitution, republican/democratic form, secular character, separation of powers, federal character, unity and integrity of India, judicial review

This is perhaps the most important constitutional ruling in Indian history. Parliament can amend but cannot destroy.

---

### 42nd Amendment (1976) — The Emergency-Era "Mini-Constitution"

Passed during the Emergency (1975-77) under Indira Gandhi, the 42nd Amendment was the most sweeping amendment in Indian history:

- Added **"Socialist", "Secular", "Integrity"** to the Preamble
- Added **Part IVA — Fundamental Duties** (Article 51A — 10 duties)
- Moved **Education, Forests, Protection of wild animals and birds** from State List to **Concurrent List**
- **Expanded Article 31C**: Now ANY law giving effect to ANY DPSP (not just Art 39b/c) would be immune from FRs — effectively making DPSPs superior to FRs
- Declared constitutional amendments **non-justiciable** — courts could not question any amendment
- Extended Lok Sabha and state assembly terms from 5 to **6 years**
- Reduced the scope of judicial review drastically

In effect, the 42nd Amendment tried to make Parliament omnipotent and the judiciary irrelevant.

---

### 44th Amendment (1978) — The Restoration

When the Janata Party came to power after the Emergency ended (1977), it passed the 44th Amendment to undo much of the 42nd:

- **Right to Property removed from Fundamental Rights** → became a legal right under **Article 300A** ("No person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law") — no longer an enforceable FR
- Restored Lok Sabha and state assembly terms to **5 years**
- **Article 352**: Changed "internal disturbance" to **"armed rebellion"** — raised the threshold for Emergency proclamation
- Emergency proclamation must be approved by **Cabinet in writing** before President acts
- Parliament must approve Emergency by **special majority** (not simple majority as earlier)
- **Lok Sabha can revoke Emergency** by simple majority resolution
- **Articles 20 and 21** (right to life, protection against double jeopardy/self-incrimination) made **non-suspendable even during Emergency**

---

### Minerva Mills Case (1980) — Courts Win the Last Round

The Supreme Court struck down the 42nd Amendment's expansion of Article 31C as violating the Basic Structure. Also struck down the provision making constitutional amendments non-justiciable. The court reaffirmed: **judicial review is part of the Basic Structure; Parliament cannot remove it**.

The Basic Structure doctrine — Kesavananda → Minerva Mills — remains the constitutional bedrock that no amendment has successfully overcome.

## Part II: Anti-Defection, Voting Age, and the 10th Schedule

### 52nd Amendment (1985) — Anti-Defection Law

Added the **Tenth Schedule** to the Constitution — the anti-defection law.

- An elected member is disqualified if they voluntarily give up party membership, vote against party directions, or abstain from voting contrary to party direction
- Decided by the **Speaker/Chairman** of the House
- **Exception**: Merger of two-thirds of a party's members into another party does not count as defection
- Criticized for reducing legislators to party automatons

**91st Amendment (2003)** tightened the anti-defection law: persons disqualified for defection cannot hold **any ministerial position** until they win a fresh election.

---

### 61st Amendment (1989) — Voting Age Lowered

- Reduced voting age from **21 to 18** years (Article 326)
- Made approximately 3.5 crore new voters eligible overnight
- India thus moved to universal adult franchise at 18, aligning with most democracies

---

## Part III: Social Justice Amendments

### 86th Amendment (2002) — Right to Education

- Inserted **Article 21A**: "The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years" — making education a **Fundamental Right**
- Added 11th Fundamental Duty (Article 51A(k)): "to provide opportunities for education to his child or ward between the age of six and fourteen years"
- Changed Article 45 (DPSP): now provides for early childhood care and education for children **below six years** (shifted the 6-14 mandate to Article 21A)
- Implemented through the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act (RTE Act), **2009**

### 103rd Amendment (2019) — EWS Reservation

- Added **Article 15(6)** and **Article 16(6)**
- Provides **10% reservation** for Economically Weaker Sections (EWS) in educational institutions and public employment
- First time economic criterion (income/property) used for reservation (all previous reservations were caste-based)
- Upheld by Supreme Court (3:2) in *Janhit Abhiyan v Union of India* (2022)

### 106th Amendment (2023) — Women's Reservation

- Added **Articles 330A** and **332A**
- Provides **33% reservation for women** in Lok Sabha, state Vidhan Sabhas, and the Delhi Legislative Assembly
- To be implemented after the next **delimitation** of constituencies (which will be based on the first census after the Act)

## Part IV: Structural Amendments

### 73rd & 74th Amendments (1992) — Local Bodies

*(Detailed coverage in Local Self-Government lesson)*

- 73rd: Panchayati Raj — Part IX, Eleventh Schedule (29 subjects)
- 74th: Municipalities — Part IX-A, Twelfth Schedule (18 subjects)

### 91st Amendment (2003) — Cabinet Size Cap

- Council of Ministers (Union and State) capped at **15% of Lok Sabha/Vidhan Sabha strength**
- Minimum **12 ministers**
- Added Articles 75(1A) and 164(1A)

### 99th Amendment (2014) — NJAC (Struck Down)

- Created the **National Judicial Appointments Commission** (NJAC) — a 6-member body to replace the Collegium system for appointing SC and HC judges
- Composition: CJI (Chair) + 2 senior SC judges + Union Law Minister + 2 eminent persons
- **Struck down in 2015** by a 4:1 SC majority — held that NJAC violated judicial independence, which is part of the Basic Structure

### 101st Amendment (2016) — GST

- **Goods and Services Tax** — the most significant tax reform since independence
- Inserted **Article 246A**: Both Parliament and state legislatures have power to make laws on GST
- Inserted **Article 269A**: Centre's share of GST on inter-state trade
- Inserted **Article 279A**: GST Council — PM/FM chairs; states have 2/3rd voting weight; 3/4th majority required

### 104th Amendment (2019) — Lok Sabha Seat Freeze Extended + Anglo-Indians

- Extended reservation of seats for SCs and STs in Parliament and state assemblies to **2030**
- **Abolished** the provision for nominating **Anglo-Indians** to the Lok Sabha (2 seats) and state assemblies — those seats had been unfilled for decades

## Exam Traps

1. **42nd Amendment added "Socialist, Secular, Integrity" to Preamble** — NOT the original Preamble.
2. **42nd Amendment added Fundamental Duties** — 10 duties (11th added by 86th Amendment).
3. **44th Amendment removed Right to Property from FRs** — it became Article 300A (legal right, not FR).
4. **61st Amendment = voting age 18** (not 52nd or 73rd).
5. **86th Amendment = Article 21A (RTE)** — NOT in original Part III; added in 2002.
6. **99th Amendment (NJAC) was struck down in 2015** — currently Collegium system continues.
7. **101st Amendment = GST** (2016), Article 279A = GST Council.
8. **104th Amendment abolished Anglo-Indian nominated seats** in Parliament and state assemblies.
9. **103rd Amendment = EWS 10% reservation** (economic criterion — first time).
10. **106th Amendment = Women's 33% reservation** — not yet implemented pending delimitation.
11. **Special majority + state ratification is needed** for federal provisions — not all amendments need state ratification.
12. **No joint sitting for constitutional amendments** — unlike ordinary bills.
