## The rulebook has chapters — and an appendix at the back

Go back to Lesson 1's idea: the Constitution is essentially the rulebook for a newly-freed 300-million-person "company." Every serious rulebook is organised into **chapters** (so you can find the rule you need) and usually has an **appendix** at the back with detailed reference tables that would clutter the main chapters if stuffed inside them.

That's exactly what **Parts** and **Schedules** are:
- **Parts** = the chapters of the main rulebook (Part III is the "Rights" chapter, Part V is the "How the Union government works" chapter, and so on).
- **Schedules** = the appendix at the back — detailed lists, tables, and forms that support the chapters but would be too bulky to write inline.

You already know the headline numbers from Lesson 1 and Lesson 3: originally **22 Parts and 8 Schedules**, now roughly **25 Parts and 12 Schedules**. This lesson is about what's actually *inside* each chapter and each appendix entry — because "which Part/Schedule deals with X" is one of the most direct, no-ambiguity question types SSC asks.

## Walking through the chapters (Parts) — the story version

Think of this as a guided tour through the rulebook, chapter by chapter, in the order they actually appear:

- **Part I — The Union and its Territory** (Art. 1–4): opens with the very sentence from Lesson 3 — "India, that is Bharat, shall be a Union of States." Chapter 1 of any rulebook usually defines who the company even is.
- **Part II — Citizenship** (Art. 5–11): who counts as a citizen at the moment the rulebook kicks in, and how citizenship works going forward.
- **Part III — Fundamental Rights** (Art. 12–35): the "signed contract" chapter from Lesson 3 — enforceable rights. Gets its own full lesson next.
- **Part IV — Directive Principles of State Policy** (Art. 36–51): the "sincere promise" chapter — non-justiciable goals for the state.
- **Part IVA — Fundamental Duties** (Art. 51A): the late arrival from Lesson 3 — added in 1976, the only Part with an "A" suffix you'll meet this early (a hint that it was *inserted* into an already-existing structure, not part of the original plan).
- **Part V — The Union** (Art. 52–151): the biggest administrative chapter — President, Vice-President, Parliament, the Union Judiciary all live here.
- **Part VI — The States** (Art. 152–237): the mirror image of Part V, but for state governments — Governor, State Legislature, State High Courts.
- **Part VIII — The Union Territories** (Art. 239–242): UTs don't get full statehood, so they get their own dedicated rulebook section.
- **Part IX — The Panchayats** and **Part IXA — The Municipalities**: the "third tier" from Lesson 3, added by the 73rd and 74th Amendments in 1992 — again, note the "A" suffix marking a later insertion.
- **Part IXB — Co-operative Societies**: an even later addition (97th Amendment, 2011) — the right to form cooperative societies.
- **Part XI — Relations between the Union and the States** (Art. 245–263): the legal machinery behind the "quasi-federal" character from Lesson 3.
- **Part XIV — Services under the Union and the States**: the rules for the civil services (your UPSC/SSC-recruited bureaucracy lives under this chapter, structurally speaking).
- **Part XV — Elections** (Art. 324–329A): home of the Election Commission's constitutional basis.
- **Part XVI — Special Provisions for Certain Classes**: reservation provisions for SC/ST/OBC.
- **Part XVIII — Emergency Provisions** (Art. 352–360): the "three red buttons" from Lesson 3, all housed in one chapter.
- **Part XX — Amendment of the Constitution** (Art. 368): the single Article that controls how every other chapter in this rulebook can ever be changed.
- **Part XXII — Short title, commencement, Hindi text and Repeals** (Art. 393–395): the very last chapter — basically the rulebook's own closing credits.

*Mnemonic for the "A" Parts (IVA, IXA, IXB, XIVA): every Part with a letter suffix was bolted on after 1949 — Fundamental Duties (1976), Panchayats-adjacent Municipalities (1992), Cooperative Societies (2011), Tribunals (1976). If you see a lettered Part, your brain should immediately say "later amendment, not original."*

## The appendix at the back — and one genuinely entertaining story

Now for the Schedules — and here's where one of them has a backstory worth remembering on its own merit, not just as a fact to memorize.

- **1st Schedule** — the literal list of States and Union Territories and their territorial boundaries. The "company org chart," basically.
- **2nd Schedule** — salaries, allowances, and privileges of the President, Governors, Speaker, Judges, CAG, and other high officials. The "executive pay scale" appendix.
- **3rd Schedule** — the exact wording of Oaths and Affirmations that officials must recite when taking office.
- **4th Schedule** — how many Rajya Sabha seats each State and UT gets.
- **5th Schedule** — special rules for administering Scheduled Areas and Scheduled Tribes (mainly applicable across several states, excluding the northeast).
- **6th Schedule** — special rules for Tribal Areas specifically in **Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, and Mizoram** — a separate, more autonomous system from the 5th Schedule.
- **7th Schedule** — the famous **three Lists**: Union List (Centre legislates), State List (States legislate), Concurrent List (both can legislate). This is the legal backbone of the "federal in form, unitary in spirit" idea.
- **8th Schedule** — the officially recognised languages of India. Started at 14, now stands at **22** — Sindhi was added in 1967 (21st Amendment), Konkani/Manipuri/Nepali in 1992 (71st Amendment), and Bodo/Dogri/Maithili/Santhali in 2003 (92nd Amendment). The list keeps growing — a living appendix, not a frozen one.
- **9th Schedule** — here's the story worth remembering: this Schedule was created by the **very first Constitutional Amendment in 1951**, specifically to shield certain land reform laws from being challenged in court as violating Fundamental Rights (especially the Right to Property). Think of it as a special "no lawsuits allowed" stamp the government could place on a law. It's controversial precisely *because* it tries to escape judicial review — and the Kesavananda Bharati Basic Structure doctrine from Lesson 2 eventually limited how far this escape hatch could go.
- **10th Schedule** — the **Anti-Defection Law**, added by the **52nd Amendment in 1985**. This one has a genuinely fun origin story: the term "**Aaya Ram Gaya Ram**" (literally "Ram has come, Ram has gone") was coined after a Haryana politician named **Gaya Lal** switched political parties **three times in a single day** in 1967. That farce eventually forced Parliament's hand, leading to the 10th Schedule nearly two decades later, to stop legislators from defecting for personal gain.
- **11th Schedule** — the 29 subjects/powers given to **Panchayats** (added by the 73rd Amendment, 1992) — the operational detail behind Part IX.
- **12th Schedule** — the 18 subjects/powers given to **Municipalities** (added by the 74th Amendment, 1992) — the operational detail behind Part IXA.

*Mnemonic: "9 escapes courts, 10 stops defectors, 11 empowers villages, 12 empowers cities."* — the last four Schedules, in order, each solving one specific real-world problem the framers (or later Parliaments) ran into.

Notice how many "future lesson" threads this single topic touches: Fundamental Rights (Part III) ties to the 9th Schedule's escape-hatch controversy; the 73rd/74th Amendments (Part IX/IXA, 11th/12th Schedules) are the same Local Self-Government topic coming later; the Union/State/Concurrent Lists (7th Schedule) are the backbone of the future Centre-State Relations lesson. ## Common exam traps

1. **Original vs current counts** — 22 Parts/8 Schedules (original) vs ~25 Parts/12 Schedules (current). Same trap pattern as Lesson 1's Article count.
2. **5th Schedule vs 6th Schedule** — both deal with tribal areas, but 5th is the general provision (most states), while 6th is specific to Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, Mizoram with greater autonomy. A favourite swap.
3. **9th vs 10th Schedule** — 9th = land reform laws shielded from judicial review (1951); 10th = anti-defection law (1985). Different purposes, different decades — don't mix them up.
4. **Which Amendment added which Schedule entry** — 1st Amendment (9th Schedule), 52nd Amendment (10th Schedule), 73rd/74th Amendments (11th/12th Schedules) — exact amendment-to-schedule pairing is a recurring question type.
5. **Number of languages in the 8th Schedule** — currently 22, not 14 (the original count) — make sure you know which era a question is asking about.
