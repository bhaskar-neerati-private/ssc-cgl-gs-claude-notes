## The Democracy Below Democracy

Gandhi wanted Gram Swaraj — village self-rule — as the foundation of independent India. He imagined a nation of self-governing villages, where power flowed upward from the grassroots, not downward from Delhi. Ambedkar was more sceptical: he worried that village republics would entrench caste hierarchies, not liberate citizens from them.

The Constitution settled this debate by putting local self-government in the **Directive Principles** — Article 40: "The State shall take steps to organize village panchayats and endow them with such powers and authorities as may be necessary to enable them to function as units of self-government." Aspirational, but not enforceable.

For **42 years** after independence, panchayats remained creatures of state law — born and dissolved at the whim of state governments, with no constitutional guarantee of elections, funds, or functions. Some states held regular panchayat elections; many didn't.

Then, in 1992, two constitutional amendments changed everything.

---

## The 1992 Watershed: Two Amendments

The **73rd Constitutional Amendment (1992)** dealt with rural local government — Panchayati Raj. The **74th Constitutional Amendment (1992)** dealt with urban local government — Municipalities. Passed together, they created a **third tier of constitutionally protected democracy** below Parliament and state legislatures.

| Feature | 73rd Amendment | 74th Amendment |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| Subject | Rural (Panchayats) | Urban (Municipalities) |
| Part added | **Part IX** | **Part IX-A** |
| Articles | **243 to 243-O** | **243-P to 243-ZG** |
| Schedule added | **Eleventh** (29 subjects) | **Twelfth** (18 subjects) |
| Came into force | **24 April 1993** | **1 June 1993** |
| National day | National Panchayati Raj Day (24 April) | — |

## Part I: The 73rd Amendment — Panchayati Raj (Rural)

### The Three-Tier Structure (Article 243B)

The Constitution mandates a **three-tier** Panchayati Raj system:

| Tier | Name (varies by state) | Level |
|------|----------------------|-------|
| First (top) | Zila Parishad / District Panchayat | District level |
| Second (middle) | Panchayat Samiti / Block Panchayat | Block / Intermediate level |
| Third (base) | Gram Panchayat | Village level |

**Exception**: States with population under **20 lakhs** may not have the intermediate (block) tier — making it two-tier for smaller states.

---

### Gram Sabha — The Foundation (Article 243A)

Before anything else: the **Gram Sabha**. It is the body consisting of all **persons registered on the electoral rolls** of a Gram Panchayat area. The Gram Sabha is not an elected body — it is the entire adult electorate of a village or group of villages.

Think of it this way: The Gram Panchayat is the "government" of the village; the Gram Sabha is the "parliament" of all village voters. The Gram Sabha meets, hears accounts, approves plans — it's the most direct form of democracy in the Indian system.

---

### Elections — Direct and Binding (Article 243C, 243E)

- **All seats** in all three tiers are filled by **direct election** by voters
- Term: **5 years**
- If a Panchayat is dissolved, elections must be held within **6 months** (this was the key reform — earlier, state governments could delay elections indefinitely)
- Delimitation of constituencies: done by the State Election Commission

---

### Reservations — The Constitutional Floor (Article 243D)

This is one of the most tested areas of this topic:

| Category | Reservation requirement |
|----------|------------------------|
| Scheduled Castes | Seats reserved in proportion to SC population in the panchayat area |
| Scheduled Tribes | Seats reserved in proportion to ST population in the panchayat area |
| **Women** | **Not less than 1/3rd of total seats** (many states have raised this to 50%) |
| OBCs | States *may* provide reservation — but it is **NOT constitutionally mandated** |

The reservation applies to **both ordinary seats AND offices of Chairpersons** at each level. And crucially: reserved seats **rotate** after each election — so the same constituency is not permanently reserved.

**Constitutional floor only**: The Constitution says "not less than 1/3rd" for women — many states (Rajasthan, Bihar, Madhya Pradesh, Uttarakhand, Himachal Pradesh) have by state law raised it to 50%.

---

### State Election Commission (Article 243K)

A **State Election Commission** (SEC) is constituted for each state — separate from the Election Commission of India (which handles Parliament and state legislature elections).

The **State Election Commissioner**:
- Appointed by the **Governor**
- Security of tenure: cannot be removed except in the **same manner and on the same grounds** as a High Court judge
- Superintendence, direction, and control of preparation of electoral rolls and conduct of all elections to panchayats and municipalities

This is critically different from the **Election Commission of India** — do not confuse them.

---

### State Finance Commission (Article 243I)

The **Governor** constitutes a **State Finance Commission** every **5 years** (mirroring the Finance Commission of India under Article 280 but at the state level).

It reviews:
- Distribution of net proceeds of state taxes, duties, tolls, and fees between state and panchayats
- Grants-in-aid to panchayats
- Measures to improve financial position of panchayats

The State Finance Commission ≠ Finance Commission of India. State Finance Commissions exist in each state; Finance Commission of India (Article 280) operates at the national level.

---

### The Eleventh Schedule (Article 243G) — 29 Subjects

The 73rd Amendment added the **Eleventh Schedule** listing 29 subjects that *may* be devolved to Panchayats by states. Key subjects include:

Agriculture, Land reform, Minor irrigation, Animal husbandry and dairying, Fisheries, Social forestry, Minor forest produce, Small-scale industries, Khadi, Village and cottage industries, Rural housing, Drinking water, Fuel and fodder, Roads and culverts, Rural electrification, Non-conventional energy, Poverty alleviation programmes, Education (primary and secondary schools), Libraries, Cultural activities, Markets and fairs, Health and sanitation, Family welfare, Women and child development, Social welfare, Welfare of weaker sections, Public distribution system, Maintenance of community assets.

**Critical caveat**: These 29 subjects are **not automatically transferred** to panchayats. It is up to each state legislature to decide which subjects to devolve, how much money to transfer, and how much staff to provide. The Constitution mandates the structure and elections — actual power devolution is voluntary for states.

---

### Compulsory vs Voluntary Provisions — The Most Important Distinction

This is why panchayati raj effectiveness varies dramatically across India:

| Type | Provisions |
|------|-----------|
| **Compulsory** (states MUST implement) | Three-tier structure; Direct elections to all seats; SC/ST seat reservations (proportional); Women's reservation (1/3rd); 5-year term; elections within 6 months of dissolution; State Election Commission; State Finance Commission |
| **Voluntary** (states MAY implement) | OBC reservations; Devolution of Eleventh Schedule subjects; Transfer of funds; Transfer of functionaries (staff) |

The compulsory provisions ensure elections and representation. The voluntary provisions are where actual governance power lies — and most states have been reluctant to fully transfer them. This gap between constitutional intent and ground reality is India's panchayati raj challenge.

## Part II: The 74th Amendment — Urban Local Bodies

### Three Types of Urban Local Bodies (Article 243Q)

| Type | Area |
|------|------|
| **Nagar Panchayat** | Transitional area (rural → urban transition) |
| **Municipal Council** | Smaller urban area |
| **Municipal Corporation** | Larger urban area |

The Governor determines which type applies to each urban area, considering population, revenue generation, density, and other factors.

---

### Wards Committees (Article 243S)

Municipal Corporations with population **3 lakh or more** must have **Wards Committees** — committees within each ward or group of wards. These function like mini-councils within larger cities.

---

### Elections and Reservations

Same structure as panchayats:
- **Direct election** to all seats; **5-year term**; election within **6 months** of dissolution
- SC/ST reservation (proportional); women (not less than 1/3rd); OBC reservation (states may provide)
- Same State Election Commission handles municipal elections

---

### The Twelfth Schedule (Article 243W) — 18 Subjects

The 74th Amendment added the **Twelfth Schedule** with **18 subjects** for municipalities, including:

Urban planning (including town planning), Regulation of land use and construction of buildings, Planning for economic and social development, Roads and bridges, Water supply, Public health (sanitation, conservancy, solid waste management), Fire services, Urban forestry, Protection of environment, Safeguarding the interests of weaker sections, Slum improvement and upgradation, Urban poverty alleviation, Provision of urban amenities and facilities (parks, gardens, playgrounds), Promotion of cultural, educational, and aesthetic aspects, Burials and burial grounds, Cattle pounds, Vital statistics (registration of births and deaths), Public amenities including street lighting, parking lots, bus stops.

---

### District Planning Committee and Metropolitan Planning Committee

Two new constitutional bodies created by the 74th Amendment:

**Article 243ZD — District Planning Committee (DPC)**:
- Consolidates plans prepared by Panchayats and Municipalities in the district
- Prepares a draft development plan for the district as a whole
- At least 4/5th of DPC members must be elected members of Panchayats and Municipalities in the district (proportional to rural-urban population)

**Article 243ZE — Metropolitan Planning Committee (MPC)**:
- For **metropolitan areas** with population **10 lakh or more**
- Prepares a development plan for the whole metropolitan area
- At least 2/3rd of MPC members must be elected members of Municipalities and Panchayats

## Historical Context: The Committees That Paved the Way

Several committees studied and recommended panchayati raj reform before the 1992 amendments:

| Committee | Year | Key Recommendation |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| **Balwantrai Mehta Committee** | 1957 | Democratic decentralisation; three-tier panchayati raj; direct elections |
| **Ashok Mehta Committee** | 1978 | Two-tier system (district + mandal); political parties may participate |
| **G.V.K. Rao Committee** | 1985 | District as the basic unit of planning; "grass without roots" problem |
| **L.M. Singhvi Committee** | 1986 | **Constitutional status for panchayats**; Gram Sabha recognition; village courts |
| **P.K. Thungon Committee** | 1988 | Constitutional provisions for regular elections and financial devolution |

The L.M. Singhvi Committee's 1986 report is particularly significant — it coined the phrase "grass without roots" and first formally recommended constitutional status. The 73rd Amendment was its legacy.

---

## The Three Fs — Why Panchayats Remain Weak

Despite the 73rd Amendment, analysts identify the **Three F problem** in panchayati raj:

1. **Functions**: Only a fraction of the 29 Eleventh Schedule subjects actually devolved by most states
2. **Funds**: States transfer insufficient money; panchayats have little own-revenue tax power
3. **Functionaries**: State department staff not transferred to panchayat control; panchayats lack staff to implement schemes

The Constitution mandated the elections framework but left the three Fs to state discretion. States with strong panchayats (Kerala, Karnataka) have voluntarily transferred more; many others have not.

## Exam Traps

1. **73rd = Rural (Panchayats), Part IX, Articles 243-243O, Eleventh Schedule (29 subjects)**
2. **74th = Urban (Municipalities), Part IX-A, Articles 243P-243ZG, Twelfth Schedule (18 subjects)**
3. **73rd came into force 24 April 1993** — not 1992 (when it was passed); 74th came into force 1 June 1993
4. **Gram Sabha ≠ Gram Panchayat** — Gram Sabha = all registered voters; Gram Panchayat = elected body
5. **State Election Commission ≠ Election Commission of India** — SEC handles panchayat/municipal elections; ECI handles Parliament/state assembly
6. **State Finance Commission ≠ Finance Commission of India** — former is state-level, every 5 years; latter is national
7. **Women: not less than 1/3rd** — many states have 50% by state law; but the Constitution says 1/3rd floor
8. **OBC reservation is VOLUNTARY** — not constitutionally mandated (unlike SC/ST which is mandatory)
9. **Devolution of 29 subjects is voluntary** — states decide how much to transfer
10. **States with population < 20 lakhs may skip intermediate tier** — two-tier permitted
11. **L.M. Singhvi Committee (1986)** recommended constitutional status — the direct predecessor to the amendments
12. **District Planning Committee = Article 243ZD; Metropolitan Planning Committee = Article 243ZE** (10 lakh+ population)
