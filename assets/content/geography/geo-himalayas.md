# The Himalayan System — Ranges, Passes & Peaks

## Born from a Collision

The Himalayas are not just mountains — they are the most consequential geological event in the history of this subcontinent. About **50 million years ago**, the Indo-Australian tectonic plate (carrying India) collided with the Eurasian plate. The **Tethys Sea** that once lay between them closed up. The sedimentary rocks at the ocean floor were crumpled and pushed upward, creating the highest mountain system on Earth. The process is still happening — the Himalayas are still rising by a few millimetres each year.

This explains several things at once: why Himalayan rivers are so erosive (young mountains, steep gradients, massive sediment loads); why the subcontinent experiences regular earthquakes (the plate boundary is still active); and why the rocks of the Himalayas contain marine fossils — ocean-bottom sediments, now at 6,000 metres.

The Himalayas are not a single range. They are a system of **parallel ranges**, each with its own character, separated by river valleys and inter-montane basins.

## The Three Main Parallel Ranges

Moving from north (inner) to south (outer):

### 1. Himadri — The Greater Himalayas

The innermost and highest range. Also called the **Inner Himalayas** or **Central Himalayas**.

- Average elevation: **~6,000 metres**
- **Continuous** — never drops below the snowline
- Width: approximately 25 km
- Permanently snow-covered; hosts most of India's major glaciers
- Contains all the peaks above 8,000 metres (the "eight-thousanders")
- The core is made of **granite** (igneous intrusion) — the hardest, most resistant rock, which is why these peaks are so high

All the famous Himalayan giants — Everest, Kanchenjunga, Makalu, Lhotse, Dhaulagiri — are in this range.

### 2. Himachal — The Lesser Himalayas

The **Middle Himalayas**, between Himadri (to the north) and the Shiwaliks (to the south).

- Average elevation: **3,700–4,500 metres**
- Width: 60–80 km
- Contains all the famous **hill stations**: Shimla, Mussoorie, Nainital, Darjeeling, Ooty (Nilgiris — a southern spur)
- The **Kashmir Valley** is a structural basin within this zone (between the Pir Panjal range and the main Himadri)
- Named sub-ranges: **Pir Panjal** (J&K), **Dhaula Dhar** (HP), **Mahabharat Range** (Nepal side), **Mussoorie Range**, **Nag Tibba**
- Most major Himalayan passes connect Himachal-zone valleys to the Tibetan plateau

### 3. Shiwaliks — The Outer Himalayas

The outermost and youngest (geologically) range. Also called **Sub-Himalayas**.

- Average elevation: **600–1,500 metres**
- Width: 10–50 km
- Composed of **unconsolidated sediment** — conglomerates, sandstones, mudstone (material eroded from the older ranges, re-deposited here)
- Structurally weak — prone to landslides and flash floods
- Broken by rivers that flow through them in gorges called **gorge exits**
- Between the Shiwaliks and the main Himalayan ranges (Himachal) lie flat valleys called **Duns**: **Dehradun** (most famous), Patlidun, Kotlidun — formed by structural depression

## The Trans-Himalayan Ranges

North of the Himadri (beyond India's main ranges), in the Ladakh/Gilgit-Baltistan region:

| Range | Key Feature |
|---|---|
| **Karakoram** | Contains K2 (8,611 m — 2nd highest in world); called the "Roof of the World" |
| **Ladakh Range** | Runs through Ladakh between Karakoram and Zaskar |
| **Zaskar Range** | Forms the southern boundary of Ladakh plateau |
| **Kailash Range** | In Tibet; contains Mount Kailash (6,638 m) — sacred to Hindus, Buddhists, Jains |

The **Siachen Glacier** (the world's largest non-polar glacier and India's highest battlefield) lies in the Karakoram Range — it is a matter of ongoing military and diplomatic sensitivity between India and Pakistan.

## The Eastern Extension — Purvanchal

After the main Himalayan arc reaches Arunachal Pradesh, it curves sharply southward to form the **Purvanchal hills** — running along India's northeastern border with Myanmar and Bangladesh.

These are geologically related to the Himalayas (same collision origin) but lower and more broken:

| Hill Range | State | Key Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Patkai Hills** | Arunachal Pradesh / Nagaland | Highest in Purvanchal arc; India-Myanmar border |
| **Naga Hills** | Nagaland | Saramati peak (3,826 m) — highest in Nagaland |
| **Manipur Hills / Barail Range** | Manipur / Assam-Nagaland border | Run east-west, unusual orientation |
| **Mizo Hills (Lushai Hills)** | Mizoram | Blue Mountain (Phawngpui) — highest in Mizoram (2,157 m) |

The Purvanchal hills form a natural barrier between India's northeast and Myanmar, creating a relatively isolated region with distinct ecology and tribal cultures.

## Major Himalayan Peaks

### The "Highest" Confusion — Three Different Answers

This is the most tested tricky area in SSC geography on this topic:

| Question | Answer | Location |
|---|---|---|
| Highest peak in the **world** | **Everest / Sagarmatha** — 8,849 m | Nepal-China border (NOT in India) |
| Highest peak **in India** (on border) | **Kanchenjunga** — 8,586 m | India-Nepal border (Sikkim) |
| Highest peak **entirely within India** | **Nanda Devi** — 7,816 m | Uttarakhand |
| Highest peak in **South India** | **Anamudi** — 2,695 m | Kerala (Western Ghats) |
| Highest peak in **Karakoram** (India-controlled claim) | **K2 (Godwin-Austen)** — 8,611 m | Gilgit-Baltistan (Pakistan-administered) |

**Why three different "highest in India" answers?**
- Everest is the world's highest but NOT in India — it is on the Nepal-China border
- Kanchenjunga's summit lies on the **India-Nepal border** — so it counts as India's highest when border peaks are included; 3rd highest in the world; stands in Sikkim
- Nanda Devi's summit lies **entirely within India** (in Uttarakhand's Chamoli district) — used when the question asks for peaks that are wholly Indian
- K2 is in the Karakoram range in Pakistan-administered territory — India claims it as part of J&K, Pakistan controls it

## Major Mountain Passes

Passes are the strategic lifeblood of the Himalayas — historically used for trade and invasion; today used for military logistics and limited civilian trade.

### Western Sector (J&K and Ladakh)

| Pass | State/Region | Connects | Altitude | Key Note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Karakoram Pass** | Ladakh | India-China (Xinjiang) | 5,540 m | Historic Silk Route; now unmanned |
| **Zoji La** | J&K | Srinagar ↔ Kargil/Leh (NH-1) | 3,528 m | Critical for Ladakh supply; snow-blocked in winter; Z-Morh tunnel under construction |
| **Banihal Pass** | J&K | Jammu ↔ Kashmir Valley | 2,832 m | Jawahar Tunnel (1956) pierces this pass |

### Himachal Pradesh

| Pass | Connects | Altitude | Key Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rohtang Pass** | Manali (Kullu) ↔ Lahaul-Spiti | 3,978 m | Famous for tourists; snow-blocked ~6 months; **Atal Tunnel** (opened Oct 2020) bypasses it |
| **Bara Lacha La** | HP/Ladakh border, Manali-Leh highway | 4,890 m | One of the highest motorable passes |
| **Shipki La** | HP — Sutlej River crossing into Tibet | 3,933 m | Trade route; Sutlej enters India here |

### Uttarakhand

| Pass | Connects | Altitude | Key Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lipulekh Pass** | Uttarakhand ↔ Tibet (China) | 5,334 m | Used by pilgrims going to Kailash Mansarovar; also claimed by Nepal |
| **Mana Pass** | Uttarakhand ↔ Tibet | 5,540 m | Near Badrinath; Saraswati River (mythological) |

### Sikkim and Northeast

| Pass | State | Connects | Key Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nathu La** | Sikkim | India ↔ Tibet (China) | Altitude 4,310 m; closed after 1962 war; reopened for trade 2006; part of historic Silk Route through Silk Road via Chumbi Valley |
| **Jelep La** | Sikkim | Alternate route to Tibet | Less used than Nathu La |
| **Bomdi La** | Arunachal Pradesh | Tawang area ↔ Assam | Key strategic pass; 1962 Sino-Indian War; altitude ~2,217 m |
| **Diphu Pass** | Arunachal Pradesh | India-Myanmar-China trijunction | Easternmost strategic pass |

## Glaciers of India

| Glacier | Location | Notable |
|---|---|---|
| **Siachen** | Karakoram, Ladakh | Largest glacier in India; ~70 km long; world's highest battlefield; controlled by India in a dispute with Pakistan |
| **Gangotri** | Uttarakhand (Uttarkashi) | Source of Bhagirathi River → Ganga; has been retreating significantly |
| **Zemu** | Sikkim (East Himalayas) | Largest glacier east of Karakoram; associated with Kanchenjunga |
| **Milam** | Uttarakhand (Pithoragarh) | Source of Goriganga (tributary of Kali/Sharda) |
| **Pindari** | Uttarakhand (Bageshwar) | Popular trekking destination |

**Retreating glaciers:** Most Himalayan glaciers are retreating due to climate change. Gangotri has retreated ~22 km since 1780. This has serious implications for the perennial water supply of Himalayan rivers.

## Why the Himalayas Matter — Geographic Consequences

**Climate barrier:** The Himalayas block cold Central Asian winds from reaching the Indian subcontinent in winter — without them, India's winters would be far colder (like Mongolia or Kazakhstan at similar latitudes). They also trap the Southwest Monsoon, forcing it to rise and precipitate over India.

**River source:** All the major northern rivers of India (Indus, Ganga, Brahmaputra and their tributaries) originate in Himalayan glaciers — meaning they are **perennial** (flow year-round), unlike the peninsular rivers that depend purely on monsoon rainfall.

**Biodiversity zones:** The altitude gradient from Terai (tropical forests) to Alpine meadows to permanent snow creates one of the most diverse ecological gradients on Earth within a short horizontal distance.

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Everest (8,849 m) is NOT in India** — it is on the Nepal-China border. Never call it India's highest.
2. **Kanchenjunga (8,586 m) = highest peak in India** (on Sikkim-Nepal border; 3rd highest in world).
3. **Nanda Devi (7,816 m) = highest peak entirely within India** — in Uttarakhand, NOT on a border.
4. **K2 (8,611 m)** is in the Karakoram, in **Gilgit-Baltistan** (Pakistan-administered); India claims it as part of J&K.
5. **Anamudi (2,695 m) = highest peak in Peninsular India** (Western Ghats, Kerala) — NOT a Himalayan peak.
6. **Siachen Glacier = largest glacier in India** (and in the Himalayas, after polar ice caps; Karakoram range; ~70 km long).
7. **Gangotri Glacier = source of Bhagirathi** (which becomes Ganga at Devprayag after meeting Alaknanda). NOT the "source of Ganga" directly.
8. **Banihal Pass** (J&K) — **Jawahar Tunnel** goes through it, connecting Jammu to Srinagar.
9. **Rohtang Pass** (HP) — **Atal Tunnel** (Rohtang Tunnel) opened **October 2020**, bypassing it for year-round Manali-Lahaul connectivity.
10. **Nathu La** (Sikkim, 4,310 m) — India-China trade route; closed 1962; reopened **2006**.
11. **Zoji La** (J&K, 3,528 m) — connects Srinagar to Leh; critical military route; snow-blocked in winter.
12. **Shipki La** (HP) — on the Sutlej River; the Sutlej **enters India through Shipki La**.
13. **Lipulekh Pass** (Uttarakhand) — Kailash Mansarovar pilgrimage route; **also claimed by Nepal** (territorial dispute).
14. **Bomdi La** (Arunachal Pradesh) — associated with **1962 Sino-Indian War**; strategically critical.
15. **Himadri = Greater Himalayas** (highest, continuous, granite core); **Himachal = Lesser/Middle** (hill stations, Kashmir Valley); **Shiwaliks = Outer/Sub-Himalayas** (lowest, unconsolidated, Duns here).
16. **Duns** (flat Shivalik valleys) — Dehradun is a Dun. The term "Dun" specifically refers to these inter-montane basins in the Shiwaliks, NOT to any valley in the Himalayas generally.
