# Location, Extent & Physiographic Divisions of India

## Where India Sits on the Planet

India occupies a pivotal position on the globe — and understanding its coordinates is not just geography trivia; it explains the climate, the monsoon, the time zone, and the agricultural calendar.

India lies entirely in the **Northern Hemisphere** and the **Eastern Hemisphere**. Its latitudinal spread is **8°4'N to 37°6'N** and its longitudinal spread is **68°7'E to 97°25'E**. That spread of about 30 degrees in latitude means that while Kanyakumari at the southern tip has a tropical climate, Ladakh in the north is frigid and high-altitude — the same country.

The east-west spread of about 29 degrees creates a time difference of nearly 2 hours between the easternmost and westernmost points. To avoid every city running on a different clock, India uses a single **Standard Time** — based on **82°30'E**, called the **Indian Standard Time (IST) meridian**. IST = **UTC + 5 hours 30 minutes**. This meridian passes through **Mirzapur** (near Prayagraj), Uttar Pradesh — which is why Mirzapur is called India's time-keeping city.

The **Tropic of Cancer (23°30'N)** cuts through the middle of India — passing through **8 states**:
**Gujarat → Rajasthan → Madhya Pradesh → Chhattisgarh → Jharkhand → West Bengal → Tripura → Mizoram**

Mnemonic: **"Go Rajput Men, Come Join West Team Mizoram"** — or simply count from west to east along the Tropic.

## India's Size and Rank

- **Area:** 3.28 million sq km (32,87,263 sq km) — **7th largest country** in the world
- **Rank order (top 7 by area):** Russia → Canada → USA → China → Brazil → Australia → **India**
- India accounts for about **2.4%** of the world's total land area
- India is the **2nd most populous** country (approximately 1.4 billion people — may have surpassed China by 2023)
- North-South extent: approximately **3,214 km**
- East-West extent: approximately **2,933 km**
- Total **coastline: 7,516.6 km** (including island coastlines); mainland coastline alone is approximately **6,100 km**
- Total **land boundary: approximately 15,200 km**

## The Five Physiographic Divisions

India's landscape is not uniform — it is a continent in miniature, with five radically different types of terrain that each have different geology, climate, soils, and economic character.

### Division 1: The Northern Mountains

The young fold mountains that form India's northern and northeastern arc — the Himalayas and their extensions. They cover approximately **10.6% of India's total area**.

**Geological age:** Young (Cenozoic, formed about 50 million years ago when the Indo-Australian plate collided with the Eurasian plate). They are still rising.

The Himalayas have **three parallel ranges** running roughly northwest to southeast:
- **Himadri (Greater Himalayas / Inner Himalayas):** The highest, most continuous range. All peaks above 6,000 m. Perpetual snow. Average elevation ~6,000 m. Contains Everest, K2 (in Pakistan-administered Kashmir), Kanchenjunga.
- **Himachal (Lesser Himalayas / Middle Himalayas):** Between Himadri and Shiwaliks. Contains famous hill stations: Shimla, Mussoorie, Nainital, Darjeeling, Ooty (in the Nilgiris — a southern spur). Kashmir Valley lies in a structural basin here. Average elevation 3,700-4,500 m.
- **Shiwaliks (Outer Himalayas / Sub-Himalayas):** Lowest and outermost. Average elevation 600-1,500 m. Formed of unconsolidated sediment. Prone to earthquakes.

**Trans-Himalayan ranges** (north of the main Himalayas, in Ladakh/J&K): Karakoram, Ladakh Range, Zaskar Range.

**Eastern extensions (Purvanchal):** Where the Himalayas curve southward into India's northeast. Include: **Patkai Hills, Naga Hills, Manipur Hills, Mizo Hills (Lushai Hills)** — running along the border with Myanmar. Arakan Range continues into Myanmar.

### Division 2: The Northern Plains

The great alluvial plain formed by the **Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra** rivers and their tributaries over millions of years. This is the most fertile, most densely populated region of India.

**Area:** covers approximately **43%** of India's total area — the largest division.

**Formation:** The Himalayan rivers carried enormous sediment loads as the mountains rose; this sediment was deposited in the trough between the rising Himalayas and the stable Peninsular Plateau, creating the plain over millions of years. The alluvial deposits are up to **2,400 metres deep** in some places.

**Four sub-zones (from foothills to floodplains, going south):**
- **Bhabar:** Immediately below the Shiwaliks. A narrow belt (8-16 km wide) of pebbles, gravel, and boulders. Rivers disappear underground here — the coarse sediment swallows the water.
- **Terai:** South of Bhabar. Fine alluvium. Underground rivers re-emerge here. Wet, marshy, heavily forested (now largely cleared). Jim Corbett National Park, Kaziranga — these are Terai landscapes.
- **Bangar:** Old alluvium (kankary/calcareous deposits), slightly elevated ground above flood level. Does not flood regularly. Better for settlement.
- **Khadar:** New alluvium. Lower ground along active floodplains. Highly fertile (fresh silt deposited by floods). Floods regularly — the cost of extreme fertility.

**Regional divisions of the plains (west to east):**
Punjab-Haryana Plains → Ganga Plain (UP, Bihar) → Bengal Delta → Brahmaputra Plain (Assam)

Also: Rajasthan Plain in the west (extends into the Thar Desert).

### Division 3: The Peninsular Plateau

The **oldest landmass** of India — a piece of ancient **Gondwana land** (the supercontinent that included South America, Africa, Arabia, Antarctica, and Australia, before they drifted apart). The plateau is geologically stable, with ancient rocks (Archean and Proterozoic) that have resisted folding.

**Area:** approximately **27.7%** of India's total area.

**Two main parts:**
- **Central Highlands:** North of the Narmada River. Includes the **Aravalli Range** (world's oldest fold mountains — mostly worn down to stumps), **Vindhya Range**, **Satpura Range**, **Malwa Plateau**, **Chota Nagpur Plateau** (mineral-rich), **Bundelkhand**, **Baghelkhand**.
- **Deccan Plateau:** South of the Narmada. Bounded by the Western Ghats on the west and Eastern Ghats on the east. Triangular in shape, tilted east (so most peninsular rivers flow east into the Bay of Bengal). Formed of **Deccan Trap basalt** (volcanic lava from about 65 million years ago — same time as dinosaur extinction). The black soil (Regur) formed from this lava is famously fertile.

**Western Ghats (Sahyadri):**
- Run parallel to the west coast for ~1,600 km from the Tapti River in the north to Kanyakumari in the south
- Average elevation: 1,000-1,500 m, with peaks up to 2,695 m
- **Continuous** range (no major gaps — exception: Palakkad/Palghat Gap in Kerala/Tamil Nadu, through which the railway and road connect the coasts)
- Act as a climate barrier — rainfall on the west (Malabar) coast is very high; east side is in a rain shadow
- Highest peak: **Anamudi (2,695 m)** — highest peak in peninsular India, in Idukki district, Kerala

**Eastern Ghats:**
- Discontinuous — broken by rivers flowing east
- Lower elevation (average 600 m) than Western Ghats
- Run along the eastern coast from Odisha to Tamil Nadu
- Highest peak: **Mahendragiri (1,501 m)** in Odisha/Andhra Pradesh

### Division 4: The Coastal Plains

India's long coastline has two very different coast types on either side of the peninsula:

**West Coast (Arabian Sea side):**
- **Narrow** (10-25 km wide), **straight**, fewer natural harbours because it's being actively eroded
- From north to south: **Kutch coast → Kathiawar (Saurashtra) coast → Konkan coast (Maharashtra/Goa) → Malabar coast (Karnataka/Kerala)**
- Malabar coast is famous for backwaters (Kerala's distinctive lagoons and canals)
- Mumbai and Goa are on the west coast

**East Coast (Bay of Bengal side):**
- **Wider** (80-100 km), with large **deltas** formed by eastern-flowing rivers (Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, Cauvery)
- From north to south: **Northern Circars (Odisha/Andhra Pradesh) → Coromandel Coast (Tamil Nadu)**
- Chennai (Madras) is on the Coromandel Coast
- More prone to cyclones than west coast

### Division 5: The Islands

**Andaman & Nicobar Islands (Bay of Bengal):**
- 572 islands (only 38 inhabited)
- Of volcanic and sedimentary origin
- Capital: **Port Blair** (South Andaman)
- **Barren Island** — India's only active volcano, in Andaman Sea
- **Narcondam Island** — dormant volcano
- Ten Degree Channel separates the Andaman group from the Nicobar group
- Duncan Passage separates South Andaman from Little Andaman
- Indira Point (southernmost tip of India) is on Great Nicobar Island

**Lakshadweep (Arabian Sea):**
- 36 islands (only 11 inhabited; 10 have settlements)
- **Coral origin** (atolls and reefs)
- Capital: **Kavaratti**
- Smallest Union Territory by area (~32 sq km)
- Highest point: just 4-5 metres above sea level — vulnerable to sea level rise
- Minicoy is the southernmost island of Lakshadweep

## Exam Traps — 18 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Standard meridian = 82°30'E** (not 82°E, not 83°E) — passes through **Mirzapur, UP**.
2. IST = **UTC + 5:30** (5 hours 30 minutes ahead of Greenwich, NOT 5 hours).
3. **Tropic of Cancer passes through 8 states** — Gujarat, Rajasthan, MP, Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, West Bengal, Tripura, Mizoram. Odisha is NOT on the Tropic (common error).
4. India is **7th largest by area** — Russia, Canada, USA, China, Brazil, Australia come first.
5. **Southernmost point of India = Indira Point** (Great Nicobar Island), NOT Kanyakumari. Kanyakumari = southernmost mainland point.
6. **Longest land border = Bangladesh (~4,156 km)**, NOT Pakistan.
7. **Shortest land border = Afghanistan (~106 km)**.
8. **Sri Lanka and Maldives have no land border** with India — only maritime (sea) borders.
9. **Bhabar → Terai → Bangar → Khadar** (north to south, foothills to floodplains) — the sequence is tested. Rivers disappear in Bhabar; reappear in Terai.
10. **Western Ghats = continuous; Eastern Ghats = discontinuous** — this is the key difference between the two.
11. **Anamudi (2,695 m)** = highest peak in peninsular India (Western Ghats, Kerala). NOT the highest in India overall (that's Kanchenjunga for India-administered peaks).
12. **Gondwana land = Peninsular Plateau** — the oldest landmass, part of the ancient southern supercontinent.
13. **Palakkad Gap (Palghat Gap)** = the only major break in the Western Ghats (in Kerala/Tamil Nadu border) — used by railways/roads.
14. **Lakshadweep = coral islands; Andaman & Nicobar = volcanic + sedimentary**.
15. **Barren Island = India's only active volcano** (Andaman Sea). Narcondam = dormant volcano.
16. **Ten Degree Channel** separates Andaman from Nicobar Islands. **Duncan Passage** separates South Andaman from Little Andaman.
17. **Kavaratti** = capital of Lakshadweep; **Port Blair** = capital of Andaman & Nicobar.
18. **Total coastline = 7,516.6 km** (including islands); mainland only = ~6,100 km. The shorter number refers to mainland, the larger to all of India.
