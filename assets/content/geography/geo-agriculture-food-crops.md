# Agriculture: Food Crops, Kharif-Rabi & Green Revolution

## India and Agriculture — The Scale

Agriculture and allied sectors contribute about **18–20% of India's GDP** but employ roughly **45–50% of the workforce** — the largest employer in the country. India is among the top global producers of rice, wheat, pulses, cotton, sugarcane, and several other crops. Understanding the seasonal framework is the entry point to everything else.

---

## The Three Agricultural Seasons

### Kharif (Monsoon/Autumn Crop)

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Sowing | June–July (onset of SW Monsoon) |
| Harvesting | October–November |
| Climate needed | High temperature, high moisture, heavy rainfall |
| Key crops | **Rice, Maize, Cotton, Jute, Groundnut, Bajra, Jowar, Tur/Arhar (Pigeon Pea), Soybean, Sugarcane** |

**Memory aid — Kharif crops: "Rice Maize Cotton Just Got Better Jute Soya Sugar"** (Rice, Maize, Cotton, Jowar, Groundnut, Bajra, Jute, Soybean, Sugarcane)

### Rabi (Winter/Spring Crop)

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Sowing | October–November (after kharif harvest) |
| Harvesting | February–April |
| Climate needed | Cool, dry growing period; warm and dry for ripening |
| Key crops | **Wheat, Barley, Gram (Chickpea), Mustard/Rapeseed, Lentil (Masur), Peas** |

**Memory aid — Rabi crops: "Wheat Barley Gram Mustard Lentil Peas"** — all winter crops that don't need monsoon

### Zaid (Summer Crop)

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Sowing | March–April (gap between Rabi harvest and Kharif sowing) |
| Harvesting | June–July |
| Climate needed | Hot, dry weather; needs irrigation |
| Key crops | Watermelon, Muskmelon, Cucumber, Bitter Gourd, vegetables, Fodder |

## Food Crops — One by One

### Rice (Paddy)

India is one of the world's **top two rice producers** (with China), and the **world's largest rice exporter**.

**Growing conditions:** Hot and humid — minimum 25°C; at least 100 cm rainfall OR heavy irrigation; standing water during growing phase; clay/alluvial soil

**Season:** Primarily Kharif; also grown in Rabi and Zaid under irrigation in some states

**Three types by season (especially in Bengal/Bihar):**

| Type | Season | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Aus** | Early Kharif (May–August) | Short duration; pre-monsoon rice |
| **Aman** | Main Kharif (June–November) | Most important; long duration; main rice crop of eastern India |
| **Boro** | Rabi/Winter (Oct–March, irrigation) | Winter rice under irrigation; grown in WB, Assam, Bihar |

**Major producers:**

| Rank | State | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **West Bengal** | **Largest rice-producing state** in India |
| 2 | Uttar Pradesh | Large area under rice |
| 3 | Punjab | **Highest rice yield (productivity)** per hectare — but water-intensive |
| 4 | Andhra Pradesh / Odisha | Delta farming |

**The Rice Belt:** Eastern India — West Bengal, Assam, Odisha, Bihar, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu

**Basmati Rice:** Aromatic, long-grain variety grown in Punjab, Haryana, western UP, J&K, and Uttarakhand. Major export commodity. Geographic Indication (GI) tag held by India.

### Wheat

The second most important food grain after rice; the staple of northern India.

**Growing conditions:** Cool and dry during growth (10–15°C); warm and dry during ripening (21–26°C); 50–75 cm rainfall; well-drained loamy soil — but primarily grown under **irrigation** in India

**Season:** **Rabi** (sown October–November; harvested March–April)

**Major producers:**

| Rank | State | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Uttar Pradesh** | **Largest wheat-producing state** |
| 2 | Punjab | **Highest wheat yield (productivity)** |
| 3 | Haryana | — |
| 4 | Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan | — |

**The Wheat Belt / "Granary of India" / "Bread Basket of India":** Punjab, Haryana, and western UP — the region transformed by the Green Revolution

### Millets — India's "Nutri-Cereals"

Millets are a group of drought-resistant coarse grains grown on poor soils with low rainfall. India is the world's **largest producer of millets**. In 2023, at India's initiative, the UN declared it the **International Year of Millets (IYoM 2023)**. The Indian government rebranded them as **"Shree Anna"** (श्री अन्न) — elevating their status.

| Millet | Local Name | Key State | Special Fact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bajra** (Pearl Millet) | — | **Rajasthan (largest)**, UP, Haryana, Gujarat | Most drought-resistant cereal; grows in 25–40 cm rainfall; sandy, poor soils |
| **Jowar** (Sorghum) | — | **Maharashtra (largest)**, Karnataka, MP, AP | Dry land crop; 40–60 cm rainfall; food + fodder |
| **Ragi** (Finger Millet) | Mandua | **Karnataka (largest)**, TN, Andhra Pradesh | Rich in **calcium and iron**; staple food in South India; grows in red and laterite soils |
| Kodo Millet, Foxtail Millet | — | Tribal areas | Traditional varieties |

### Pulses (Legumes)

India is the **world's largest producer AND consumer of pulses**. Pulses fix atmospheric nitrogen — they improve soil fertility naturally (reason why pulse rotation with other crops is beneficial).

**Growing conditions:** Dry conditions; no standing water; tolerant of poor soils; moderate temperature

**Season:** Mix — some Kharif (Tur/Arhar, Urad/Black Gram, Moong/Green Gram); some Rabi (Masur/Lentil, Chana/Gram, Peas)

| Pulse | Season | Key Producer State |
|---|---|---|
| **Tur/Arhar** (Pigeon Pea) | Kharif | Maharashtra, UP, Karnataka |
| Urad (Black Gram) | Kharif | UP, MP, Rajasthan |
| Moong (Green Gram) | Kharif + Zaid | Rajasthan, Maharashtra, AP |
| **Chana/Gram** (Chickpea) | **Rabi** | **Madhya Pradesh** |
| Masur (Lentil) | **Rabi** | MP, UP |

**Largest pulse-producing state: Madhya Pradesh**

---

### Maize (Corn)

**Season:** Kharif

**Growing conditions:** Fertile soil; 60–120 cm rainfall; 21–27°C temperature; cannot tolerate waterlogging

**Major producer: Karnataka** (first), followed by MP, Rajasthan, AP, Bihar, UP

## The Green Revolution — India's Greatest Agricultural Transformation

### The Crisis That Forced It

By the early 1960s, India's food situation was desperate. Population was growing at 2.5% per year; food production was stagnating. Famine stalked Bihar and other states. India survived only on **"Ship to Mouth"** — food aid under America's **PL-480 programme** (Public Law 480), which shipped surplus American wheat to India. This was humiliating and economically dependent.

The 1965–66 drought nearly caused mass starvation. Something radical had to change.

### The Solution: HYV Seeds + Irrigation + Fertilisers

**1966:** India imported **18,000 tonnes of High-Yielding Variety (HYV) Mexican dwarf wheat seeds** (Lerma Rojo 64, Sonora 64 — developed by Norman Borlaug's team) and planted them in Punjab and Haryana.

**Result:** Wheat production in Punjab jumped from ~5 million tonnes in 1964-65 to ~17 million tonnes by 1971-72. India declared self-sufficiency in food grains by **1978**.

### Key People

**M. S. Swaminathan (Dr. Monkombu Sambasivan Swaminathan):**
- **"Father of the Green Revolution in India"**
- Indian agricultural scientist and geneticist; worked at IARI (Indian Agricultural Research Institute), New Delhi
- Led the adaptation and implementation of HYV seeds in India; collaborated with Norman Borlaug
- Later advocated for farmers' rights and sustainable agriculture; headed the National Commission on Farmers (2004–06); the "Swaminathan Report" recommended MSP at C2 + 50%
- Padma Vibhushan, World Food Prize, and many other awards
- Passed away in September 2023

**Norman Borlaug:**
- American agricultural scientist; developed dwarf, disease-resistant, high-yielding wheat varieties at CIMMYT (Mexico)
- **Nobel Peace Prize, 1970** — "Father of the Green Revolution" globally
- His wheat varieties (IR varieties) were the seeds that transformed Punjab's farms

### What the Green Revolution Used

| Input | Details |
|---|---|
| **HYV Seeds** | Dwarf wheat (Mexican varieties); IR-8 rice ("Miracle Rice" — developed at IRRI, Philippines) |
| **Irrigation** | Expansion of tube wells in Punjab/Haryana; canal expansion; groundwater exploitation |
| **Chemical Fertilisers** | Nitrogenous (urea), phosphatic; massive increase in consumption |
| **Pesticides** | DDT and other chemicals (later found to cause ecological damage) |
| **Credit** | Agricultural credit through cooperative banks and nationalised banks |

### Results and Legacy

**Positive:**
- India achieved **food self-sufficiency** by 1978; eliminated famine
- Wheat production tripled; rice production nearly doubled
- Punjab and Haryana became India's "Granary" — producing 50%+ of rice and wheat procured nationally
- **Food Corporation of India (FCI)**, established 1965, built massive buffer stocks

**Negative:**
- **Water crisis in Punjab:** Massive over-extraction of groundwater for paddy irrigation; water table declining by 50 cm per year in some districts; long-term agricultural crisis
- **Soil degradation:** Excess fertilisers made soils alkaline; micronutrient depletion
- **Biodiversity loss:** Traditional seed varieties abandoned; mono-cropping replaced diverse crop mixes
- **Regional inequality:** Green Revolution concentrated in Punjab-Haryana-western UP; eastern India, dryland areas, tribal farming largely bypassed
- **Farmer debt crisis:** Input-intensive farming increased farmer debt
- **Chemical pollution:** Pesticide runoff contaminated water bodies

### Second Green Revolution (Concept)

Government programmes aimed at:
- Extending productivity gains to **eastern India** (Bihar, WB, Odisha — the "neglected rice bowl")
- Focus on **pulses, oilseeds, millets** — crops that missed the first revolution
- **Evergreen Revolution (Swaminathan's concept)** — sustained productivity increases without ecological damage

## Key Agricultural Institutions and Policies

| Institution/Policy | Year | Key Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Food Corporation of India (FCI)** | 1965 | Procures, stores, and distributes food grains; maintains buffer stocks; manages PDS |
| **ICAR** (Indian Council of Agricultural Research) | 1929 | Apex body for agricultural research and education in India; headquartered in New Delhi |
| **IARI** (Indian Agricultural Research Institute) | 1905 (Pusa, Bihar; shifted to Delhi 1936) | "Pusa Institute"; premier agricultural research institute; developed many HYV seeds |
| **Minimum Support Price (MSP)** | Annual | Government-declared floor price for 23 crops; protects farmers from price crash |
| **National Food Security Act (NFSA)** | 2013 | Legal right to food; subsidised grain (5 kg/person/month at ₹1-3/kg) to ~67% of population |
| **PM-KISAN** | 2019 | Direct income support of ₹6,000/year to small and marginal farmers |
| **NABARD** (National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development) | 1982 | Provides agricultural credit; refinancing apex institution |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Cotton = Kharif** crop — NOT Rabi. Many students confuse cotton with winter crops. Cotton needs warmth and is monsoon-season grown.
2. **Wheat = Rabi**; **Rice = Kharif** (primarily). Both are food grains but opposite seasons.
3. **West Bengal = largest rice producer** (by volume); **Punjab = highest rice YIELD/PRODUCTIVITY** (tonnes per hectare). Two different metrics — very frequently mixed up.
4. **UP = largest wheat producer**; **Punjab = highest wheat YIELD/PRODUCTIVITY**. Same pattern as rice.
5. **MS Swaminathan = Father of Green Revolution in INDIA**; **Norman Borlaug = Father of Green Revolution globally** (Nobel 1970). Never mix the two.
6. **Bajra = most drought-resistant crop**; grows in 25–40 cm rainfall; Rajasthan is largest producer.
7. **Ragi = rich in calcium AND iron** (both) — a nutritional fact tested in SSC Science sections too.
8. **Madhya Pradesh = largest producer of pulses AND soybeans** — MP dominates the pulse map.
9. **India = world's largest producer AND consumer of pulses** — both facts, same country.
10. **International Year of Millets = 2023** (IYoM 2023) — at India's initiative (PM Modi championed it); UN General Assembly resolution; millets = "Shree Anna".
11. **FCI (Food Corporation of India)** was established in **1965** — the same year India faced the food crisis that led to the Green Revolution. Both events in 1965 are connected.
12. **Green Revolution first succeeded with WHEAT** (not rice) — wheat in Punjab-Haryana was the first dramatic win; rice HYV (IR-8) came slightly later and was less spectacular.
13. **Punjab's biggest environmental problem from Green Revolution = water table depletion** — paddy requires enormous water; Punjab grows paddy in a semi-arid state using tube wells — deeply unsustainable.
14. **Boro rice** (Rabi rice grown under irrigation in winter) is important to West Bengal and Assam — a Rabi crop that is RICE, not wheat. This surprises many students.
15. **NABARD was established in 1982** (from the recommendations of the Shivaraman Committee); it's the agricultural credit apex body — NOT a commercial bank. NABARD refinances rural banks.
16. **Sugarcane = both Kharif and a long-duration crop** (12–18 months) — it spans multiple seasons; UP is largest producer, but Tamil Nadu and Karnataka have the highest productivity.
