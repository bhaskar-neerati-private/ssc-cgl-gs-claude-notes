# Agriculture: Cash Crops, Plantation Crops & Oilseeds

## The Landscape of Commercial Agriculture

Food crops feed India's people. Cash crops and plantation crops fund India's farmers, fill its factories, and drive its exports. The distinction matters: a cash crop is grown primarily to sell, not to eat at home. Plantation crops are large-scale, long-term commercial enterprises — tea estates, rubber groves, coffee plantations — many of which were established by the British and continue today.

---

## Cash Crops

### Cotton — "White Gold"

**Season:** Kharif (sown June–July; harvested October–December)

**Soil:** Black cotton soil (Regur) — perfect moisture retention keeps the cotton plant hydrated through dry spells between rains

**Climate:** 21–30°C; 50–100 cm rainfall; long frost-free growing season (frost kills cotton bolls)

**India's status:** India has the **world's largest area under cotton cultivation** — more cotton acreage than any other country. India competes with China and the USA for the top production spot annually.

**Bt Cotton (2002):** India commercially approved Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis) cotton in 2002 — a GM variety with a bacterial gene making it resistant to the bollworm pest. Over 90% of India's cotton area is now under Bt cotton. India went from cotton importer to exporter after Bt adoption.

| Rank | State | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Gujarat** | **Largest cotton producer** in India |
| 2 | Maharashtra | Vidarbha region; also site of severe farmer debt crisis |
| 3 | Telangana / AP | — |
| 4 | Rajasthan, Karnataka | — |

**Cotton textile cities:**
- **Ahmedabad ("Manchester of India")** — cotton textile hub of western India
- **Mumbai** — original mill district (now decommissioned; replaced by mixed development)
- **Coimbatore ("Manchester of South India")** — cotton textiles; Tamil Nadu
- **Tirupur** — knitwear and hosiery capital of India; "T-Shirt City"
- **Surat** — synthetic textiles; man-made fibres

### Jute — "Golden Fibre"

Called the "Golden Fibre" for its golden-yellow colour and economic importance.

**Season:** Kharif (sown March–May in pre-monsoon warmth; harvested July–September)

**Climate:** Hot and humid; 150–200 cm rainfall; temperature 25–35°C; alluvial deltaic soil (Ganga-Brahmaputra delta)

**India's status:** India and Bangladesh are the world's top two jute producers (historically traded places for first rank).

| Rank | State | Share |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **West Bengal** | **~80% of India's jute production** |
| 2 | Bihar | — |
| 3 | Assam, Odisha | — |

**Jute industry:** Concentrated along the **Hugli River** in West Bengal — dozens of jute mills in a 100-km stretch (Kolkata, Rishra, Serampore, Barrackpore, Uluberia). This was the "Jute Belt" of British India.

**Uses:** Gunny sacks/bags (grain storage, packaging), carpet backing, burlap fabric, rope, geotextiles (erosion control). Jute is making a comeback as the world shifts away from single-use plastics — eco-friendly packaging alternative.

### Sugarcane

**Season:** Long-duration crop — 10 to 18 months; planted in February–March or June–July; harvested the following year. Spans Kharif and Rabi seasons.

**Climate:** Hot and humid; 75–150 cm rainfall; deep, well-drained fertile soil; temperature 20–26°C

**India's status:** India is the **world's second largest sugarcane producer** (after Brazil) and frequently competes with Brazil for the title of **world's largest sugar producer**.

| Rank | State | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Uttar Pradesh** | **Largest sugarcane and sugar producer** |
| 2 | Maharashtra | Pune-Satara-Solapur-Kolhapur belt; cooperatives |
| 3 | Karnataka | — |
| 4 | Tamil Nadu | **Highest sucrose content/yield per hectare** |

**Sugarcane by-products** — all commercially important:
- **Bagasse** — fibrous residue after juice extraction; used for paper manufacturing and co-generation of electricity in sugar mills
- **Molasses** — thick syrup residue after crystallisation; used for industrial alcohol (ethanol), country liquor, and vinegar
- **Press mud** — filter cake; used as organic fertiliser

### Tobacco

India is the **world's third largest tobacco producer** (China and Brazil lead).

**Season:** Rabi in most states; Kharif in Andhra Pradesh

**Climate:** Well-drained, sandy loam soil; 60–100 cm rainfall; 20–30°C

| Rank | State | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Andhra Pradesh** | **Largest tobacco producer**; Guntur region famous for Flue-Cured Virginia (FCV) tobacco |
| 2 | Karnataka | — |
| 3 | Gujarat, MP | — |

**Guntur (AP):** Asia's largest tobacco auction/market centre. FCV tobacco from Guntur is exported worldwide and is premium grade.

**Types of tobacco:**
- **Flue-Cured Virginia (FCV):** Premium cigarette tobacco; grown in AP (Guntur, Krishna districts); light coloured; exported
- **Burley:** Air-cured; used in pipe tobacco and cigarettes
- **Bidi tobacco:** Small-leaf chewing/smoking variety; grown across India

## Plantation Crops

### Tea

India is the **world's second largest tea producer** (China is first) and one of the largest consumers — India drinks most of its own tea.

**Growing conditions:** Sloping terrain with good drainage (prevents waterlogging); 150–300 cm rainfall; cool temperatures (15–25°C); acidic soil (pH 4.5–5.5); mountain/laterite soils

**Major producing states and their character:**

| State | Region | Tea Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Assam** | Brahmaputra valley; Brahmaputra north and south bank districts | **Largest tea-producing state**; strong, malty, CTC tea; "Breakfast Tea" |
| West Bengal | **Darjeeling** hills; Terai | **Most famous**; "Champagne of Teas"; GI-tagged; muscatel flavour; grown at 1,800–3,000 m altitude; orthodox leaf tea |
| Tamil Nadu | Nilgiris; Ooty, Coonoor | Nilgiri tea; frost-affected in winter; distinctive flavour |
| Kerala | Munnar; Wayanad | High-grown tea; green and orthodox varieties |
| Karnataka | Coorg (Kodagu) | Coffee + tea region |

**Key facts:**
- **Dibrugarh (Assam)** is called the **"Tea City of India"** — centre of Assam's tea industry
- **Guwahati Tea Auction Centre** = **world's largest tea auction centre** by volume
- Kolkata (Calcutta Tea Auction) — another major auction; premium teas
- **Darjeeling tea** = first Indian product to receive a **Geographical Indication (GI) tag** (2004) — internationally recognised as authentic only if grown in Darjeeling hills

### Coffee

**Growing conditions:** Hilly terrain (shade-grown); 150–200 cm rainfall; 15–28°C; laterite and red soils; shade trees required

**Types grown in India:**

| Type | Quality | Altitude | Flavour |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Arabica** | Higher value; finer | 900–1,800 m | Mild, aromatic; used in specialty coffee |
| **Robusta** | Lower value; robust | 300–900 m | Stronger, more bitter; used in espresso blends and instant coffee |

| Rank | State | Share |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Karnataka** | **~70% of India's coffee** |
| 2 | Kerala | ~20% |
| 3 | Tamil Nadu | ~10% |

- **Coorg (Kodagu) and Chikmagalur** — the twin hearts of Karnataka's coffee industry
- **Bababudan Hills (Chikmagalur)** — where coffee was first introduced to India in the 17th century
- **Coffee Board of India** headquarters: **Bengaluru**
- India mainly grows shade-grown coffee under the rainforest canopy — environmentally sustainable; commands premium in global markets

### Rubber

**Growing conditions:** Hot and humid; >200 cm rainfall; 25–34°C; well-drained laterite soil; no frost at all

**India's status:** World's fourth or fifth largest natural rubber producer

| Rank | State | Share |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Kerala** | **~91% of India's natural rubber** |
| 2 | Tamil Nadu | — |
| 3 | Karnataka | — |
| 4 | Northeast (Tripura, Assam) | Emerging producers |

- **Rubber Board of India** headquarters: **Kottayam (Kerala)** — known as "**Rubber City of India**"
- India's rubber is almost entirely consumed domestically — primarily by the tyre industry (MRF, Apollo Tyres, CEAT, JK Tyre)
- **Tripura** is a rapidly growing rubber producer in the northeast

### Coconut

India is the **world's third largest coconut producer** (Indonesia 1st, Philippines 2nd).

**Growing conditions:** Coastal, tropical; sandy loam soil; salt-tolerant; 27°C+; 100–200 cm rainfall; loves sea coast

| Rank | State | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Kerala** | **Largest coconut producer** in India |
| 2 | Karnataka | — |
| 3 | Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh | — |

- **"Kerala" = "Kera" (coconut palm) + "alam" (land) in Malayalam** — "Land of Coconuts"
- **Uses:** Coconut oil (edible + cosmetic), copra (dried kernel), coir fibre (from husk — mats, rope, geotextiles), toddy (fermented sap), coconut water, coconut milk, desiccated coconut
- **Coir Board of India** headquarters: **Kochi (Cochin)**
- **Alappuzha (Alleppey, Kerala)** = "**Coir Capital of the World**"

## Oilseeds

India is an **oil-deficit country** — it produces significant amounts of oilseeds but still imports large quantities of edible oil (especially palm oil from Malaysia and Indonesia; sunflower oil from Ukraine/Russia).

| Oilseed | Season | Largest Producer | Global Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Groundnut (Peanut)** | **Kharif** | **Gujarat** | "King of Oilseeds"; major edible oil; grows in light, sandy soil; 50–75 cm rainfall |
| **Mustard/Rapeseed** | **Rabi** | **Rajasthan** | Major edible oil in North India; sown Oct-Nov in cool/dry conditions |
| **Soybean** | **Kharif** | **Madhya Pradesh** | Protein-rich; MP = "Soya Pradesh"; also feeds India's animal feed industry |
| **Castor** | Kharif | **Gujarat** | India = **world's largest castor oil producer**; industrial use (lubricant, biodiesel, polymers) |
| **Sunflower** | Kharif + Rabi | Karnataka, AP | Good source of polyunsaturated fat; imported also |
| **Sesame (Til)** | Kharif | West Bengal, UP, Rajasthan | Oldest oilseed known to humanity; used in sweets, tahini |
| **Linseed (Flaxseed)** | Rabi | UP, MP | Linseed oil for paint, varnish, linoleum (industrial) |

**Technology Mission on Oilseeds (TMO), 1986** — major policy boost to oilseed production; also called **"Yellow Revolution"** for edible oils.

### Verghese Kurien — The Milkman of India

The story of the White Revolution deserves its own telling. In 1946, the milk cooperative movement started in **Anand, Gujarat** by farmers led by **Tribhuvandas Patel** — who wanted to break free from exploitative middlemen. Verghese Kurien, a young dairy engineer, arrived in Anand in 1949 to run the government creamery. He stayed for decades.

Under Kurien's leadership:
- **Amul (Anand Milk Union Limited)** became India's most successful cooperative and one of the world's largest dairy organisations
- **Operation Flood** (1970–1996) — the world's largest dairy development programme — created a "flood" of milk across India; connected rural dairy farmers to urban consumers through a network of cooperatives; made India the world's largest milk producer
- **NDDB (National Dairy Development Board)** — founded by Kurien; headquarters in Anand, Gujarat
- Kurien received: **Padma Vibhushan, Ramon Magsaysay Award, World Food Prize, Padma Bhushan**
- Called **"Father of the White Revolution"** and **"Milkman of India"**

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Gujarat = largest cotton producer** (NOT Maharashtra; Maharashtra = 2nd). Very common wrong answer.
2. **West Bengal = largest jute producer** (~80% of India's jute). No other state comes close.
3. **UP = largest sugarcane producer; Tamil Nadu = highest sugar yield/hectare**. Production vs. productivity trap again.
4. **Brazil = world's largest sugarcane producer** (India = 2nd); NOT India.
5. **Andhra Pradesh (Guntur) = largest tobacco producer; Guntur = Asia's largest tobacco market**.
6. **Assam = largest tea-producing state** (volume); **Darjeeling (West Bengal) = most famous/premium** (Champagne of Teas). Assam ≠ best quality; Darjeeling ≠ largest quantity.
7. **Guwahati Tea Auction Centre = world's largest** tea auction (by volume).
8. **Darjeeling tea = first Indian product to get GI tag (2004)** — geographical indication.
9. **Karnataka = largest coffee producer (70%+)** — NOT Kerala, NOT Assam (Assam has no coffee).
10. **Kerala = largest rubber producer (91%+); Rubber Board HQ = Kottayam**.
11. **Gujarat = largest groundnut AND castor producer; India = world's largest castor oil producer**.
12. **Rajasthan = largest mustard/rapeseed producer; MP = largest soybean producer**.
13. **Jute = "Golden Fibre"; Black Pepper = "King of Spices"; Cardamom = "Queen of Spices"** — three epithets tested regularly.
14. **White Revolution = Operation Flood = Verghese Kurien = Amul = NDDB = Anand (Gujarat)**. All five are the same story — one chain of association.
15. **Tea Board HQ = Kolkata; Coffee Board HQ = Bengaluru; Rubber Board HQ = Kottayam; Spices Board HQ = Kochi; Coir Board HQ = Kochi** — four Kerala-based boards + one WB board (Tea) + one Karnataka board (Coffee).
16. **India = world's largest producer, consumer, and exporter of spices** — all three superlatives; black pepper was historically why Europeans sailed to India.
