# Climate, Monsoon, Seasons & Rainfall Distribution

## India's Climate — The Basics

India has a **tropical monsoon climate**. The word **monsoon** comes from the Arabic **"Mausim"** — meaning season. It describes a seasonal reversal of winds: from the sea to the land in summer (bringing rain), and from the land to the sea in winter (dry).

**Factors that control India's climate:**

| Factor | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Latitude** | Tropic of Cancer divides India — south is tropical, north is subtropical |
| **Himalayan Barrier** | Blocks cold Central Asian winds in winter; forces monsoon winds to rise and rain in summer |
| **Distance from Sea** | Coastal areas = moderate; interior = extremes (hot summer, cold winter) |
| **Altitude** | Temperature falls ~6.5°C per 1,000 m rise; hill stations are cooler |
| **Jet Streams** | Upper atmosphere wind belts that shift seasonally; control monsoon timing |

## Jet Streams and Monsoon Control

**Sub-tropical Westerly Jet Stream** (SWJS):
- A fast-moving air current at 8–12 km altitude, flowing west to east
- In winter, it splits around the **Tibetan Plateau**: the southern branch passes over the Indian subcontinent
- This southern branch drives **Western Disturbances** into northwest India
- In summer, the SWJS shifts northward of the Himalayas, allowing the monsoon to advance into India

**Western Disturbances:**
- Extratropical cyclones that originate in the **Mediterranean Sea** and the west
- They travel east along the southern branch of the westerly jet stream
- Bring **winter rainfall** to **J&K, Himachal Pradesh, Punjab, Uttarakhand, and sometimes Delhi**
- Crucial for **Rabi crops** (especially wheat) — the winter rain fills in what the monsoon doesn't provide in northwest India
- Snowfall in the Himalayas during winter is largely due to Western Disturbances

**Easterly Jet Stream:**
- Develops in summer at about **14°N latitude** over peninsular India
- Flows **east to west** (opposite direction from westerlies)
- Associated with the **onset and intensification** of the Southwest Monsoon
- When the Easterly Jet Stream is strong, the monsoon is vigorous

## The Southwest Monsoon — Mechanism

The SW Monsoon brings **80–90% of India's annual rainfall** and controls agricultural cycles for over a billion people. Understanding its mechanism is essential.

**Why the monsoon comes (step by step):**

1. By **May–June**, the Indian landmass heats up intensely — especially the **Thar Desert / Rajasthan region** where temperatures reach 45–50°C
2. This creates a powerful **low-pressure zone** over northwest India
3. The **ITCZ (Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone)** — a belt of low pressure that normally sits near the equator — is pulled northward over India
4. The **Southeast Trade Winds** of the Southern Hemisphere, drawn toward this low pressure, cross the equator
5. As they cross into the Northern Hemisphere, the **Coriolis effect** deflects them to the right
6. These deflected winds become the **Southwest Monsoon** — blowing from the southwest, moisture-laden from the Indian Ocean

### The Two Branches of the SW Monsoon

**Branch 1: Arabian Sea Branch**

- Strikes the **Western Ghats** head-on → forced to rise → cools → heavy rainfall on the **western (windward) slopes**
- Result: **Konkan, Malabar Coast, Coorg, Mangalore, Mumbai** receive 2,000–4,000 mm+ of rain
- The **eastern Deccan** is in the **rain shadow** → dry (Bangalore gets only ~900 mm, Pune ~700 mm)
- Part of this branch moves northward and brings rain to Gujarat, MP, Rajasthan (weakened)
- Critical trap: Aravallis run **parallel** to the Arabian Sea branch — they do NOT block it → Rajasthan stays dry even though it has a mountain range

**Branch 2: Bay of Bengal Branch**

- Moves northeast → hits the **Garo, Khasi, Jaintia Hills** of Meghalaya
- These hills act like a funnel, forcing winds sharply upward → **orographic rainfall** of extraordinary intensity
- **Mawsynram (Khasi Hills, Meghalaya)** = **highest average annual rainfall in the world** (~11,871 mm or ~1,187 cm)
- **Cherrapunji (Sohra, Meghalaya)** — just 15 km from Mawsynram; still holds records for **highest rainfall in a single month and a single year**
- The branch then turns west, moves along the Ganga plains, reaching UP, Bihar, then Delhi
- A sub-branch moves northward into Assam and the northeast

## Monsoon Calendar

| Event | Approximate Date/Period |
|---|---|
| **Onset at Kerala** | Around **June 1** |
| Spreads across Maharashtra | June 10–15 |
| Reaches Delhi | ~**June 29 – July 1** |
| Covers all of India | By **July 15** |
| **Withdrawal begins** (northwest India) | Late September |
| Withdrawal from peninsular India | October–November |
| Complete withdrawal | December |

**Burst of Monsoon:** The dramatic, sudden onset of monsoon with heavy rains — the sky darkens, temperatures drop, and heavy rain begins. It marks the official arrival of the monsoon season.

**Break in Monsoon:** During the monsoon season, there are periods of weak or no rainfall — called **monsoon breaks**. During a break:
- The monsoon trough shifts northward to the Himalayan foothills
- Northeast India (Assam, Meghalaya) gets heavy rain during breaks
- The Ganga plains get very little rain

## The Four Seasons of India

### Season 1: Cold Weather Season (December – February)

- High pressure over northwest India (cold, dense air)
- Dry, clear days; cold nights; fog in the Indo-Gangetic Plains
- **Western Disturbances** bring rain/snowfall to J&K, HP, Uttarakhand
- **Cold waves** sweep through Bihar, UP, Delhi — caused by cold winds blowing from Siberia/Central Asia via northwestern routes
- South India remains warm; coastal areas mild

### Season 2: Hot Weather / Summer Season (March – May)

The temperature builds relentlessly across the subcontinent.

**Loo:**
- Hot, dry, dusty winds blowing in the **afternoons** across the **Indo-Gangetic Plains** (Rajasthan, UP, Bihar, Haryana, Delhi)
- Blows from **west to east**
- Temperature can touch 45–48°C; can be fatal
- Most intense in May–June just before the monsoon arrives

**Mango Showers (Blossom Showers):**
- Pre-monsoon showers in **Kerala and Karnataka** (May–June)
- Local convectional thunderstorms
- Help ripen mangoes; also help the flowering of coffee plants — hence called "blossom showers" in Karnataka
- Locally called **Mango Showers** in Kerala

**Kalbaisakhi (Norwesters):**
- Violent, local **thunderstorms** that lash **West Bengal and Northeast India** in April–May
- Come from the **northwest** (hence "norwesters")
- Bring brief but intense rain; often accompanied by hail
- "**Kal Baisakhi**" — meaning "the calamity of the month of Baisakh" in Bengali
- Beneficial for **jute and tea cultivation** (pre-monsoon moisture)
- Also benefit mango and litchi crops

**Andhi (Dust Storms):**
- In Rajasthan and parts of UP/Bihar in summer
- Dust-laden winds before thunderstorms

### Season 3: Advancing Southwest Monsoon (June – September)

- India's main rainy season
- Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal branches together cover the country
- Peak rainfall: July–August in most parts
- Wettest months for most of India
- Economic lifeline — agriculture, groundwater recharge, river flow

### Season 4: Retreating / Northeast Monsoon (October – December)

As the SW Monsoon withdraws from the subcontinent, the wind direction reverses:
- Winds now blow from the **northeast** (from land to sea)
- These are the **Northeast (NE) Monsoon winds** — dry over land, but pick up moisture after crossing the **Bay of Bengal**
- The **Coromandel Coast (Tamil Nadu, southern AP)** receives its main rainfall in October–December from the NE Monsoon
- Chennai gets ~60% of its annual rain from the NE Monsoon
- Bay of Bengal churns up **tropical cyclones** during this period — November is peak cyclone month
- Rest of India is dry; temperature falls; the transition season to winter

## Rainfall Distribution

### Very High Rainfall (>200 cm annually)

- **Meghalaya** — Mawsynram, Cherrapunji: 1,000–1,200 cm
- **Assam and Northeast** — 150–300 cm
- **Western coast (Konkan, Malabar)** — 200–400 cm (western slopes of Western Ghats)
- **Andaman & Nicobar Islands** — 250–300 cm

### Moderate Rainfall (100–200 cm)

- West Bengal, Odisha
- Eastern UP, Bihar
- Parts of Tamil Nadu and Karnataka

### Low Rainfall (50–100 cm)

- Much of peninsular Deccan (rain shadow of Western Ghats)
- Parts of Rajasthan east of the Thar

### Very Low Rainfall (<50 cm)

| Region | Annual Rainfall | Type |
|---|---|---|
| **Thar Desert (Rajasthan)** | ~10–20 cm | Hot desert; least rainfall in India |
| **Jaisalmer** | ~8–12 cm | Driest station in India (hot) |
| **Ladakh (Leh)** | ~7–14 cm | Cold desert; rain shadow of Himalayas |
| Interior Deccan | 40–60 cm | Rain shadow of Western Ghats |

**Most important comparison:** India's **wettest place** = Mawsynram; India's **driest hot place** = Jaisalmer/Thar; India's **driest cold place** = Ladakh/Leh.

## Types of Rainfall in India

| Type | Mechanism | Where in India |
|---|---|---|
| **Orographic (Relief)** | Moist winds hit mountains → rise → cool → rain on windward side; dry on leeward | Western Ghats (west coast very wet; Deccan dry); Meghalaya hills |
| **Convectional** | Land heats → air rises → cools → afternoon/evening thunderstorms | Interior India in summer; common in the plains |
| **Cyclonic (Frontal)** | Collision of warm and cold air masses (fronts); also tropical cyclones | Western Disturbances (NW India, winter); Bay of Bengal cyclones (Oct-Nov) |

## El Niño, La Niña and Indian Monsoon

**El Niño (ENSO — El Niño Southern Oscillation):**
- Abnormal **warming** of the central and eastern Pacific Ocean surface
- Disrupts the Walker Circulation (atmospheric pressure pattern)
- **Effect on India:** Weakens the Southwest Monsoon → **below-normal rainfall → drought**
- Major drought years linked to El Niño: 1987, 2002, 2009, 2015

**La Niña:**
- Opposite of El Niño — abnormal **cooling** of central Pacific
- **Effect on India:** Strengthens SW Monsoon → above-normal rainfall → sometimes floods
- 2010–11, 2020–21 La Niña years associated with above-normal Indian monsoon

**Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD):**
- Difference in sea surface temperature between the **western Indian Ocean** (near Somalia/East Africa) and the **eastern Indian Ocean** (near Indonesia/Sumatra)
- **Positive IOD** = western Indian Ocean warmer, eastern cooler → favourable for Indian monsoon (more moisture over India)
- **Negative IOD** = eastern warmer → unfavourable (weakens monsoon)
- IOD can partially offset El Niño effects on India's monsoon

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Monsoon = from Arabic "Mausim"** (season). Not Portuguese; not Sanskrit.
2. **SW Monsoon onset: Kerala around June 1** — NOT July 1 (that's when Delhi gets it).
3. **Mawsynram (Meghalaya) = highest average annual rainfall** in India AND the world — NOT Cherrapunji (Sohra). Cherrapunji still holds single-month and single-year records.
4. **Cherrapunji and Mawsynram are in Khasi Hills, Meghalaya** — NOT Assam or Arunachal Pradesh.
5. **Western Disturbances** come from the **Mediterranean/west** along the westerly jet stream; bring **winter rain to NW India**; vital for **wheat (Rabi crop)**.
6. **Loo** = hot, dry winds blowing **west to east** in Indo-Gangetic plains in summer afternoons. Dangerous; can be fatal.
7. **Kalbaisakhi (Norwesters)** = violent thunderstorms in **West Bengal and Northeast India** in **April–May**; blow from the **northwest**. Good for jute and tea.
8. **Mango Showers / Blossom Showers** = pre-monsoon showers in **Kerala and Karnataka** (May–June). Help ripen mangoes; help coffee flowering.
9. **Coromandel Coast (Tamil Nadu)** gets its rain from the **NE Monsoon** (October–December) — OPPOSITE of rest of India which gets it from SW Monsoon (June–September).
10. **Aravallis are parallel to Arabian Sea monsoon winds** → do NOT block them → Rajasthan stays dry despite having a mountain range. This is counterintuitive and very frequently tested.
11. **Rain shadow = leeward side of mountain barrier** — Eastern Deccan is in rain shadow of Western Ghats; Ladakh is in rain shadow of Himalayas.
12. **El Niño = weakens Indian monsoon → drought**; La Niña = strengthens monsoon → floods.
13. **ITCZ** = Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone; shifts north over India in summer, drawing monsoon winds inland.
14. **Easterly Jet Stream** forms in summer over peninsular India (14°N); associated with SW Monsoon **onset** — not to be confused with westerly jet (which is winter).
15. **Bay of Bengal is more active for cyclones** than Arabian Sea — Bay of Bengal cyclones form in Oct-Nov and hit the Coromandel Coast; this is also why Chennai is cyclone-prone.
16. **Positive IOD = favourable for Indian monsoon**; Negative IOD = unfavourable. Indian Ocean Dipole is India-specific (unlike El Niño which is Pacific-based).
