# Natural Vegetation & Forest Types of India

## India's Forest Cover — The Big Numbers

India's total forest and tree cover is approximately **24.56%** of its geographical area (Forest Survey of India, 2021). The National Forest Policy 1988 set a target of **33%** — India is still well below it.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Forest cover target (National Forest Policy 1988) | **33%** of geographical area |
| Actual forest + tree cover (FSI 2021) | ~24.56% |
| Most forested state **by area** | **Madhya Pradesh** |
| Most forested state **by % of state area** | **Mizoram** (~84%) |

India's forests are classified using the **Champion and Seth Classification** — primarily based on rainfall, temperature, and altitude.

---

## Type 1: Tropical Evergreen Forests

**Rainfall needed:** More than 200 cm annually; high temperature; no pronounced dry season

**Where:**
- Western Ghats western slopes — Kerala, Coorg/Kodagu (Karnataka), Maharashtra (Sahyadri)
- Andaman & Nicobar Islands
- Northeast India — Assam, Meghalaya, Arunachal Pradesh, Manipur (where rainfall is very heavy)

**Why "evergreen":** Different trees shed their leaves at different times of the year — so the forest always has some trees in full leaf. It never looks bare, unlike deciduous forests.

**Characteristics:**
- Multi-layered, dense canopy (trees at different heights — emergent layer at 40–60 m, then canopy, understory, shrub, ground)
- Extremely high biodiversity; heavy undergrowth, creepers, lianas, ferns
- Commercial exploitation is difficult — dense, remote, protected
- High humidity throughout the year

**Key trees:** Mahogany, rosewood, ebony (*Diospyros*), rubber, bamboo, teak (in wetter pockets)

**Products:** Hard, dense timber (mahogany, ebony — high-value but restricted); rubber (Kerala's economy); spices (black pepper, cardamom, nutmeg)

## Type 2: Tropical Moist Deciduous Forests

**Rainfall needed:** 100–200 cm; distinct dry season

**Where:** Eastern slopes of Western Ghats, parts of MP, Odisha, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Maharashtra, UP eastern foothills (Terai), Assam (parts)

**Why "deciduous":** Trees shed leaves in the dry season (March–May) to conserve water — forest looks bare briefly — then new leaves burst out just before the monsoon arrives.

**Commercial significance:** The **most economically important forest type** in India — accessible, with high-value timber.

**Dominant trees:**

| Tree | Key Facts |
|---|---|
| **Teak** (*Tectona grandis*) | "**Queen of all timbers**"; golden-brown, durable, termite-resistant; best for furniture and ship-building; found in MP, Maharashtra, AP, Karnataka |
| **Sal** (*Shorea robusta*) | Very hard, heavy, fire-resistant; used for railway sleepers, heavy construction; dominant in Odisha, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, UP |
| Bamboo | Multi-use: construction, paper pulp, food (shoots), furniture |
| Shisham (Sheesham) | *Dalbergia sissoo*; second most important timber; furniture, sports goods |
| Sandalwood | *Santalum album*; Karnataka (state-owned trees); perfume, incense, carving |
| Mahua | Madhya Pradesh/tribal areas; flowers used to make country liquor; seeds for oil |
| Amla | Indian gooseberry; Vitamin C; Ayurveda |

**Teak vs. Sal — where they dominate:**
- **Teak:** Centre and South — MP, Maharashtra, AP, Karnataka, Kerala
- **Sal:** North and East — Odisha, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, UP (Terai), West Bengal

## Type 3: Tropical Dry Deciduous Forests

**Rainfall:** 70–100 cm; longer dry season than moist deciduous

**Where:** Large parts of the peninsula — UP, MP, Maharashtra, Karnataka, AP, parts of Rajasthan (eastern)

**Characteristics:**
- More open, less dense than moist deciduous
- Trees shed leaves for longer
- More grasses in the understory — transitions towards savanna/grassland
- Teak and Sal still found but less dominant; more drought-tolerant species

**Key trees:** Teak, Sal, Palash (*Flame of the Forest* — bright orange flowers), Neem, Peepal, Bael, Tendu (leaves used to wrap beedis — a major forest produce income for tribals in MP, Chhattisgarh)

---

## Type 4: Tropical Thorn Forests (Scrub Forests)

**Rainfall:** Less than 70 cm — the driest forested zones

**Where:** Rajasthan (Thar Desert and its fringes), parts of Gujarat, semi-arid Haryana, parts of UP, interior Deccan (parts of Karnataka, AP, Maharashtra)

**Characteristics:**
- Sparse, widely spaced trees and shrubs
- Thorns and spines (to reduce water loss and deter grazing)
- Very long roots (to access deep groundwater)
- Small, thick, waxy leaves — minimise transpiration
- Grasses and xerophytes fill the gaps between shrubs

**Key plants:**

| Plant | Notable Feature |
|---|---|
| **Khejri** (*Prosopis cineraria*) | **State tree of Rajasthan**; incredibly drought-resistant; nitrogen-fixer; every part is useful (leaves = camel fodder, pods = food, wood = fuel) |
| Babul (*Acacia nilotica*) | Thorny; gum arabic source; common in dry zones |
| Ber (*Ziziphus mauritiana*) | Indian jujube; thorny shrub/small tree; edible fruits |
| Euphorbia | Succulent; cactus-like; stores water |
| Date Palm | Gujarat/Rajasthan; fruit crop in arid zones |

**The Bishnoi Connection:** The **Khejri tree** is sacred to the **Bishnoi community** of Rajasthan. In 1730, at Khejarli village, **363 Bishnois led by Amrita Devi Bishnoi** sacrificed their lives hugging Khejri trees to prevent them from being felled by soldiers of the Maharaja of Jodhpur. This event — predating the modern Chipko Movement by 243 years — is considered the world's earliest recorded tree-protection movement.

## Type 5: Montane Forests (Himalayan Forests)

The Himalayas create distinct vegetation zones as altitude rises and temperature falls:

| Altitude | Forest Type | Key Trees | Location |
|---|---|---|---|
| Up to 1,000 m | Sub-tropical broadleaf | Sal, bamboo, dense undergrowth | Himalayan foothills, Terai-Bhabar zone |
| 1,000–2,000 m | Sub-tropical pine forests | **Chir Pine** (*Pinus roxburghii*), oak | Lower Himalayas — HP, Uttarakhand, J&K |
| 2,000–3,000 m | Temperate forests | **Deodar**, oak (*Quercus*), rhododendron, maple, chestnut | Middle Himalayas |
| 3,000–4,000 m | Sub-alpine forests | Silver Fir (*Abies*), Spruce (*Picea*), juniper, birch | Upper Himalayas |
| 4,000 m+ | Alpine meadows (Bugyals) | Grasses, sedges, low shrubs; no trees | Valley of Flowers, Bedni Bugyal |
| Above snow line | Glaciers | — | — |

**Deodar (*Cedrus deodara*):**
- "Deodar" = **"Deva" (divine) + "Dar" (wood)** = **Wood of the Gods**
- **State tree of Himachal Pradesh**
- Found at 2,000–3,000 m in the western Himalayas
- Beautiful, fragrant; used for temple construction, furniture, railway sleepers historically

**Shola Forests** (Southern Montane Forest):
A unique forest type found only in the **Nilgiris, Anaimalai, Palani Hills, Cardamom Hills** (Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka). Dense, low-canopy forests grow in the moist hollows of valleys; rolling montane grasslands cover the slopes. This alternating pattern is called "shola-grassland mosaic." Exceptional biodiversity — Nilgiri Tahr, Gaur, lion-tailed macaque, Nilgiri flycatcher.

## Type 6: Mangrove Forests (Littoral / Tidal Forests)

**Where:** Tidal estuaries, deltas, coastal mudflats — wherever rivers meet the sea in sheltered bays
- **Sundarbans (West Bengal)** — largest mangrove forest in India and the world
- Andaman & Nicobar Islands
- Odisha (Bhitarkanika — 2nd largest in India)
- AP, Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Kerala coasts
- Gujarat (Kori Creek)

**Why mangroves are special — 3 adaptations:**
1. **Pneumatophores (breathing roots):** Vertical aerial roots that grow upward from the mud, above the waterline, to absorb oxygen (which is absent in waterlogged anaerobic mud)
2. **Stilt roots (prop roots):** Arched roots that emerge from the trunk and branches and anchor in the mud — stability in soft, shifting substrate
3. **Vivipary:** Seeds germinate while still attached to the parent plant — the seedling (propagule) is already well-developed when it drops into the tidal water, allowing it to establish quickly

**The Sundarbans:**
- **World's largest mangrove forest** — shared between India (West Bengal, ~4,264 sq km) and Bangladesh (larger portion)
- UNESCO World Heritage Site; Project Tiger reserve; Biosphere Reserve
- Home to the **Royal Bengal Tiger** (famed swimmers; adapted to hunting in the tidal channels)
- Also: estuarine crocodile, Irrawaddy dolphin, Olive Ridley sea turtles
- Named after the **Sundari tree** (*Heritiera fomes*) — the dominant mangrove species

**Bhitarkanika (Odisha):** India's second largest mangrove ecosystem; Ramsar site; largest nesting site of the Olive Ridley sea turtle in the world (mass nesting called **Arribada**)

## Biosphere Reserves — The Key 12

India has 18 government-notified biosphere reserves. The ones with UNESCO recognition or SSC frequency:

| Biosphere Reserve | State(s) | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Nilgiri** | TN, Kerala, Karnataka | **India's first BR (1986)**; largest in peninsular India; includes Mudumalai, Nagarhole, Wayanad, Silent Valley |
| **Sundarbans** | West Bengal | World's largest mangrove; Bengal tiger; UNESCO WHS |
| **Nanda Devi** | Uttarakhand | UNESCO WHS (with Valley of Flowers); high Himalayas |
| **Valley of Flowers** | Uttarakhand | UNESCO WHS; alpine meadow; Brahmakamal (state flower of Uttarakhand) |
| **Gulf of Mannar** | Tamil Nadu | **India's first marine BR**; coral reefs; dugongs |
| **Manas** | Assam | UNESCO WHS; Project Tiger; Golden Langur |
| **Nokrek** | Meghalaya | Red panda; citrus species diversity centre |
| **Great Nicobar** | Andaman & Nicobar | Southernmost; leatherback sea turtles; Shompen tribe |
| **Agasthyamalai** | Kerala, Tamil Nadu | Western Ghats; Kani tribe; high endemic biodiversity |
| **Simlipal** | Odisha | Elephant; tiger; waterfalls (Barehipani, Joranda) |
| **Kanchenjunga** | Sikkim | Highest biosphere reserve; snow leopard |
| **Dehang-Dibang** | Arunachal Pradesh | Mishmi hills; clouded leopard |

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Tropical DECIDUOUS forests (not evergreen)** have teak and sal — the commercially valuable timbers. Evergreen forests have mahogany/ebony but are harder to exploit.
2. **Teak = "Queen of all timbers"** — NOT "King." The phrase is tested exactly.
3. **Sal dominates in northeast/east** (Odisha, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh); **Teak dominates in centre/south** (MP, Maharashtra, AP, Karnataka). They do NOT have the same distribution.
4. **Khejri = state tree of Rajasthan** — NOT Neem, NOT Babul (wrong answers appear regularly).
5. **Mangroves have pneumatophores** (aerial roots for breathing) and **vivipary** (seeds germinate on parent plant). These are unique adaptations not found in other forest types.
6. **Sundarbans = world's largest mangrove forest** (not "largest forest in India" — which is a different question); named after **Sundari tree**.
7. **Bhitarkanika (Odisha) = second largest mangrove in India** AND the largest Olive Ridley nesting beach (Gahirmatha Beach) where mass nesting (Arribada) occurs.
8. **Nilgiri Biosphere Reserve = India's FIRST biosphere reserve (1986)**. India's first marine biosphere reserve = Gulf of Mannar.
9. **Madhya Pradesh = most forest cover by area** (absolute sq km); **Mizoram = highest by % of state area**.
10. **National Forest Policy 1988 target = 33%** of geographical area. Current coverage ≈ 24%. India is still short.
11. **Deodar = state tree of Himachal Pradesh**; "Deva + Dar" = Wood of Gods. Found 2,000–3,000 m in western Himalayas.
12. **Shola forests** = montane forests in the Nilgiris/Western Ghats Southern Hills; unique valley-forest + hilltop-grassland pattern. NOT found in the Himalayas.
13. **Sandalwood trees in Karnataka are state property** — all sandalwood trees (including on private land) belong to the Karnataka government. Felling requires permission.
14. **Valley of Flowers (Uttarakhand)** = UNESCO WHS; alpine meadows; Brahmakamal (Saussurea obvallata) — state flower of Uttarakhand — blooms here.
15. **Tropical thorn forests** = rainfall below 70 cm; Thar Desert area; Khejri, Babul, Euphorbia — NOT teak or sal (which need more water).
16. **Vivipary in mangroves** = seeds germinate while still on the parent tree; this gives seedlings a head start in the harsh tidal environment. The term "vivipary" means "live birth" (like in animals) — an analogy that helps remember it.
