# Northern Plains, Peninsular Plateau, Ghats & Coastal Plains

## The Great Plain — Cradle of Civilisation

The **Northern Plains** stretch approximately **2,400 km** from the Punjab plains in the west to the Brahmaputra valley of Assam in the east, and about 240–320 km from north to south. They cover roughly **43% of India's total area** and hold the vast majority of its population. This is not coincidence — the plain is built from millennia of alluvial deposition by three river systems (Indus, Ganga, Brahmaputra), creating the deepest and most fertile soils on Earth (alluvial deposits up to 2,400 metres deep in some places).

The sub-zones (Bhabar → Terai → Bangar → Khadar) were covered in the previous lesson. The regional divisions of the plains, moving west to east:

| Region | States | Key Character |
|---|---|---|
| Punjab-Haryana Plain | Punjab, Haryana, Delhi | Doabs (land between two rivers); water from Indus tributaries |
| Rajasthan Plain | Rajasthan | Transitions into the Thar Desert westward |
| Ganga Plain | UP, Bihar, Jharkhand | Most extensive; most fertile; Ganga and tributaries |
| Bengal Delta | West Bengal, Bangladesh | World's largest delta; Ganga-Brahmaputra; Sundarbans |
| Brahmaputra (Assam) Valley | Assam | Also called Assam Valley; flood-prone |

**Doab:** "Do" (two) + "ab" (water/river) — land between two rivers. The Punjab has multiple doabs between its five rivers (Sutlej-Beas Doab, Beas-Ravi Doab, Ravi-Chenab Doab, etc.).

## The Peninsular Plateau — Two Halves

The Peninsular Plateau is divided by the **Narmada River** into two major parts:
- **Central Highlands** (north of Narmada)
- **Deccan Plateau** (south of Narmada)

### Central Highlands — North of Narmada

#### Aravalli Range
The **oldest fold mountains** in India — and among the oldest in the world. They run **northeast to southwest** across Rajasthan, from Delhi in the northeast to Palanpur (Gujarat) in the southwest, a length of about 800 km.

- Mostly worn down to rounded hills and ridges (billions of years of erosion)
- **Guru Shikhar (1,722 m)** — highest peak in the Aravallis, in Rajasthan
- **Mount Abu** — the only hill station in Rajasthan; site of the famous Dilwara Jain temples; located in the Aravallis
- The Aravallis act as a watershed: rivers to the east drain into the Ganga system; rivers to the west drain into the Luni and then into the Rann of Kutch or Arabian Sea

#### Vindhya Range
A large, complex hill range running **east-west** south of the Ganga Plain, roughly parallel to the Narmada River.
- Historically significant as the cultural divide between North India (Aryavarta) and South India (Dakshinapatha)
- Not very high (400-600 m mostly) but stretches for ~1,050 km
- Rivers draining north from Vindhyas join the Ganga; rivers draining south join the Narmada

#### Satpura Range
Runs **east-west**, south of the Vindhyas and north of the Narmada River — making the Satpuras the southern wall of the Narmada rift valley.
- **Dhupgarh (1,350 m)** — highest peak in the Satpura Range; located near **Pachmarhi** hill station in Madhya Pradesh (the only hill station in MP)
- The Satpuras form the watershed between Narmada (drains west) and Tapti/other rivers draining south
- Dense forests; Satpura Tiger Reserve is important

#### Chota Nagpur Plateau
The mineral heartland of India — a high plateau covering parts of **Jharkhand, Bihar, West Bengal, and Odisha**.
- Called the **"Ruhr of India"** (after the industrial Ruhr region of Germany) — for its extraordinarily rich mineral deposits
- Contains: coal (Jharia, Raniganj), iron ore, copper, bauxite, mica, manganese, uranium
- The **Damodar River** flows through it — the Damodar Valley Corporation (DVC) is India's first multi-purpose river valley project (modelled on the Tennessee Valley Authority, USA)
- **Rajmahal Hills** — volcanic basalt hills in the northeastern part

#### Other Central Highland Features
- **Malwa Plateau** — between Aravallis and Vindhyas; mostly covered in black basaltic soil (fertile); drained north by Chambal and other Ganga tributaries
- **Bundelkhand** — UP/MP border; rocky; Ken and Betwa rivers (Betwa is a Ken tributary flowing into Yamuna)
- **Baghelkhand** — MP/Chhattisgarh; upper Son River basin
- **Chhattisgarh Plain** — flat basin of the upper Mahanadi; called the "Rice Bowl of Central India"

### Deccan Plateau — South of Narmada

The great triangular plateau of peninsular India, bounded by:
- **Western Ghats** on the west
- **Eastern Ghats** on the east
- **Satpura-Vindhya ranges** on the north

**Tilt:** The Deccan Plateau tilts **gently east** — which is why most peninsular rivers flow eastward into the Bay of Bengal. This eastward tilt is a key geographic fact.

**Deccan Trap:** The plateau is largely covered by **basaltic lava flows** from about 65 million years ago (associated with the Deccan Traps — massive volcanic outpourings possibly linked to the Chicxulub meteor impact that caused dinosaur extinction). This lava weathered into the fertile **black cotton soil (Regur)**.

Sub-regions of the Deccan Plateau:
| Sub-region | States | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Maharashtra Plateau | Maharashtra | Black soil (Regur); cotton growing |
| Karnataka Plateau (Mysore Plateau) | Karnataka | Iron ore; forest; Bababudan Hills (coffee origin) |
| Telangana Plateau | Telangana, AP | Semi-arid; Krishna-Godavari basin |
| Chhattisgarh Plain | Chhattisgarh | Flat Mahanadi basin; rice cultivation |

## Western Ghats (Sahyadri)

The Western Ghats are one of the world's eight "hottest hotspots" of biological diversity — a **UNESCO World Heritage Site (2012)**. They run for approximately **1,600 km** along India's west coast, from the **Tapti River** (or the Tapi estuary near Surat) in the north to **Kanyakumari** in the south.

**Character:** Continuous, steep western escarpment (drops sharply to the coast); gentle eastern slope. Act as a massive climatic barrier forcing the Southwest Monsoon to rise and deposit heavy rain on the western (windward) side, leaving the eastern Deccan in a rain shadow.

**The only major gap: Palakkad Gap (Palghat Gap)**
Located on the Kerala-Tamil Nadu border, near Palakkad city. This ~30 km wide gap in the Western Ghats allows:
- The Northeast Monsoon to enter Kerala from the east
- A railway line (Palakkad-Shoranur-Thrissur) and road connecting the two coasts
- A drier corridor: Coimbatore and Palakkad cities are noticeably drier than surrounding areas

**Other passes (not major natural gaps, but transport corridors):**
- **Thal Ghat** — Igatpuri, Maharashtra; used by Mumbai-Nashik railway (Central Railway main line)
- **Bhor Ghat** — Khopoli, Maharashtra; used by Mumbai-Pune railway (now bypassed by tunnel)

**Key peaks in Western Ghats:**

| Peak | Height | Location | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anamudi** | **2,695 m** | Idukki district, Kerala (Anaimalai Hills) | **Highest in peninsular India** |
| **Dodabetta** | 2,637 m | Nilgiris, Tamil Nadu | Highest in Nilgiris; highest in Tamil Nadu |
| Meesapulimala | 2,640 m | Kerala (Idukki) | 2nd highest in Kerala |
| Mullayanagiri | 1,930 m | Chikmagalur, Karnataka | Highest in Karnataka |
| Kalsubai | 1,646 m | Ahmednagar, Maharashtra | Highest in Maharashtra |

**Major hill ranges within the Western Ghats system:**
- **Nilgiri Hills** (TN-Kerala border) — "Blue Mountains"; tea; Ooty (Udhagamandalam) hill station; Mudumalai National Park
- **Anaimalai Hills (Annamalai)** — contains Anamudi; Eravikulam National Park; Rajamalai
- **Cardamom Hills** — southernmost section in Kerala; spice cultivation
- **Bababudan Hills** — Karnataka; where coffee was first introduced to India (by Baba Budan, 17th century, who brought seeds from Yemen)

## Eastern Ghats

The Eastern Ghats are a **discontinuous** range of hills running along India's east coast, from Odisha in the north to Tamil Nadu in the south — a total length of about 1,600 km, same as the Western Ghats, but fundamentally different in character.

**Why discontinuous:** Major east-flowing rivers (Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, Cauvery) cut through them as they descend to the Bay of Bengal — creating wide river valleys that break the hill chain.

**Characteristics:**
- Average elevation: ~600 m (much lower than Western Ghats)
- Isolated ranges and hill groups with different local names
- On the leeward side of the Western Ghats (rain shadow); lower rainfall
- Less biodiverse than Western Ghats

**Named hill groups within Eastern Ghats (north to south):**
| Hills | State | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Rajmahal Hills | Jharkhand | Actually part of Chota Nagpur; basaltic |
| Nallamala Hills | Andhra Pradesh | Along Krishna River; forest |
| Erramalai Hills | AP | Also called Vellikonda Range |
| Shevaroy Hills (Servaroyan) | Tamil Nadu | Salem district; coffee |
| Javadi Hills | Tamil Nadu | Vellore district |
| Kalrayan Hills | Tamil Nadu | Salem/Villupuram |
| Palani Hills | Tamil Nadu | Kodaikanal hill station; Perumal Peak (~2,518 m) is its highest point |
| Mahendragiri | Odisha/AP border | One of the highest points in Eastern Ghats (~1,501 m) |

**Highest peak in Eastern Ghats:** This is contested — **Jindhagada peak (1,690 m)** in Visakhapatnam district, Andhra Pradesh is now widely cited as the highest. Mahendragiri (1,501 m) on the Odisha-AP border is also frequently cited in older sources. For SSC, know that **the Eastern Ghats' highest point is in Andhra Pradesh** and the range is much lower than the Western Ghats.

## Coastal Plains — West vs. East

### West Coast (Arabian Sea Side)

| Section | States | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Kutch Coast** | Gujarat (north) | Rann of Kutch — salt desert; tidal flat |
| **Kathiawar/Saurashtra** | Gujarat | Peninsula; Gir Forest (Asiatic lion); Gulf of Kutch, Gulf of Khambhat |
| **Konkan Coast** | Maharashtra, Goa | Narrow strip; laterite soil; Mumbai's famous natural harbour; Goa's beaches |
| **Malabar Coast** | Karnataka, Kerala | Backwaters; coconut/spice; Kochi (one of India's finest natural harbours) |

**West coast characteristics:**
- **Narrow** (10–25 km), steep drop from the Ghats
- Rivers are **short and fast** (only 50–100 km long), carry little sediment — form **estuaries** (not deltas)
- **Better natural harbours** because the coast is more sheltered
- Kerala's famous **backwaters (kayals)** — networks of lagoons, lakes, and canals running parallel to the coast; Vembanad Lake is the largest; Ashtamudi Lake, Kayamkulam Lake
- **Higher rainfall** (windward of Western Ghats; 2,000–3,000 mm+)

### East Coast (Bay of Bengal Side)

| Section | States | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Northern Circars** | Odisha, AP | Between Mahanadi and Krishna-Godavari deltas; hilly coast |
| **Godavari-Krishna Delta** | Andhra Pradesh | Fertile delta plains; rice granary of AP |
| **Coromandel Coast** | Tamil Nadu | Flat; sand bars; no backwaters; gets Northeast Monsoon |
| **Cauvery Delta** | Tamil Nadu | "Granary of South India"; fertile rice fields |

**East coast characteristics:**
- **Wider** (80–100 km in places)
- Rivers are **long and slow** (Godavari ~1,465 km; Krishna ~1,400 km), carry heavy sediment — form **large deltas**
- **Fewer natural harbours** (straight coast, few protected bays) — Chennai (Madras) has an artificial harbour
- **More cyclone-prone** (Bay of Bengal generates more frequent and intense cyclones than the Arabian Sea)
- Important coastal lakes: **Chilika Lake** (Odisha) and **Pulicat Lake** (AP-TN border)

## Important Coastal Lakes

**Chilika Lake (Odisha):**
- India's **largest coastal lagoon** (also largest lake by area in India, though it is brackish/saline)
- ~1,100 sq km; separated from the Bay of Bengal by a narrow sand bar
- A **Ramsar Site** (wetland of international importance)
- Famous for migratory birds from Central Asia and Siberia; Irrawaddy dolphins
- Connects to the sea through a narrow mouth near Satapada

**Pulicat Lake (AP-TN border):**
- India's **second largest brackish water lagoon** (~460 sq km)
- Sriharikota island (ISRO's satellite launch station) lies on the sand bar separating Pulicat Lake from the Bay of Bengal

**Vembanad Lake (Kerala):**
- Largest lake in Kerala and **longest lake in India** (~96 km long); largest lake in West India
- Part of the Kerala backwater system
- Kumarakom bird sanctuary is on its banks
- Also a Ramsar site

## Exam Traps — 18 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Aravallis = oldest fold mountains in India** — NOT the Himalayas (which are the youngest/newest).
2. **Guru Shikhar (1,722 m)** = highest peak in the Aravallis, in Rajasthan.
3. **Mount Abu** = only hill station in Rajasthan; located in the Aravallis.
4. **Chota Nagpur Plateau = "Ruhr of India"** — for its exceptional mineral wealth (coal, iron ore, copper, bauxite).
5. **Damodar Valley Corporation (DVC)** — India's **first multi-purpose river valley project**; in Chota Nagpur.
6. **Deccan Plateau tilts east** — hence peninsular rivers flow eastward into the Bay of Bengal (not west).
7. **Western Ghats = Sahyadri** (the other name, especially in Maharashtra/Karnataka context). UNESCO World Heritage Site (2012).
8. **Palakkad Gap (Palghat Gap)** = only major break in the Western Ghats; used by railway and road connecting Kerala to Tamil Nadu.
9. **Anamudi (2,695 m)** = highest in Western Ghats AND highest in peninsular India. In Idukki district, Kerala.
10. **Dodabetta (2,637 m)** = highest in Nilgiris AND highest peak in Tamil Nadu.
11. **Mullayanagiri (1,930 m)** = highest peak in Karnataka; in Chikmagalur (a coffee district).
12. **Kalsubai (1,646 m)** = highest peak in Maharashtra (Sahyadri/Western Ghats).
13. **Eastern Ghats = discontinuous** (broken by rivers); Western Ghats = continuous (only Palakkad Gap).
14. **West coast rivers** = short, fast, form **estuaries** (not deltas); **East coast rivers** = long, slow, form **deltas**.
15. **Chilika Lake = largest lagoon in India** (Odisha; Ramsar site; migratory birds; Irrawaddy dolphins). Do NOT call it a freshwater lake — it is **brackish** (saline).
16. **Pulicat Lake** is where **Sriharikota (ISRO launch station)** is located — on the sand bar separating the lake from the sea.
17. **Vembanad Lake** = longest lake in India (~96 km); largest in Kerala; part of Kerala backwaters.
18. **East coast (Bay of Bengal) = more cyclone-prone** than west coast (Arabian Sea) — because Bay of Bengal is warmer, shallower, and more enclosed.
