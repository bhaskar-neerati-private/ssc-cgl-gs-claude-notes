# Soils of India — Types, Distribution & Characteristics

## Why Soils Vary Across India

India's extraordinary soil diversity is the product of five factors working together: **parent rock**, **climate**, **topography**, **vegetation**, and **time**. The Himalayas give mountainous soils; the Deccan's volcanic basalt weathers into black cotton soil; tropical wet-dry zones bake out laterite; the great rivers deposit alluvium across the plains. Same country, completely different geology beneath each step.

For SSC, the classification that matters is the traditional **8-type system** — each type has a fixed geography, colour, key nutrient status, and signature crop.

---

## 1. Alluvial Soil — India's Breadbasket Soil

**Coverage:** ~43% of India's total land area — **the most widespread soil in India**

**Where:** The entire Indo-Gangetic Plain (Punjab → Haryana → UP → Bihar → West Bengal → Assam), the Brahmaputra valley, and coastal river deltas (Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, Cauvery)

**Formation:** Deposited over millions of years by the Indus, Ganga, Brahmaputra, and their tributaries, carrying sediment washed down from the Himalayas.

**Characteristics:**
- Extremely fertile; rich in lime and phosphoric acid
- **Deficient in nitrogen** — the one consistent limitation
- Good water retention; texture ranges from sandy to clayey
- Supports the densest agricultural populations in the world

### Two Sub-types: Khadar vs. Bangar

| Sub-type | Location | Age | Character | Fertility |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Khadar** | Near river channels; flood plains | New alluvium | Lighter, sandier; renewed by floods annually | More fertile |
| **Bangar** | Away from river channels; elevated terraces | Old alluvium | Darker; contains **kankars** (calcareous nodules of CaCO₃) | Slightly less fertile |

**Crops:** Wheat, rice, sugarcane, jute, oilseeds, cotton, pulses — virtually all of India's food grain

## 2. Black Soil (Regur / Cotton Soil)

**Coverage:** ~15% of India's land area

**Other names:** Regur (from Telugu *Reguda*), Cotton Soil, Tropical Chernozem

**Where:** The **Deccan Plateau** — Maharashtra (most extensive), Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, parts of Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu

**Formation:** Derived from the weathering of **basaltic lava** of the Deccan Trap (65 million year old volcanic outpourings). The basalt weathers into extremely fine-grained, mineral-rich soil.

**Characteristics:**

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| Colour | Black/dark grey (from titaniferous magnetite — iron + titanium compounds in basalt, plus humus) |
| Moisture retention | **Extremely high** — holds moisture for months after the rains end |
| **Self-ploughing (self-mulching)** | **Unique to black soil.** Develops deep cracks in the dry season → organic matter from the surface falls in → when wetted again, the soil swells and seals the cracks. This keeps the soil naturally mixed and aerated |
| Chemistry | Rich in **calcium carbonate, magnesia, potash, lime** |
| Deficiency | **Nitrogen and phosphoric acid** (and humus at the surface) |
| Working | Very **sticky when wet**; very **hard when dry** — difficult to plough at the wrong time |
| Depth | Very deep in western Maharashtra; shallower towards AP and TN |

**Crops:** **Cotton** (hence the name Cotton Soil — best in the world for it), wheat, sugarcane, jowar, linseed, tobacco

**Why cotton loves black soil:** Cotton needs moisture for a long growing season. Black soil's exceptional moisture-retention provides water through the long dry gaps between irrigations.

## 3. Red Soil

**Coverage:** ~18% of India — **second most widespread soil**

**Where:** Peninsular India — Tamil Nadu, parts of Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, eastern Maharashtra, parts of Madhya Pradesh

**Formation:** Weathering of ancient **crystalline and metamorphic rocks** (gneiss, schist, granite) over billions of years.

**Characteristics:**
- **Red colour = iron oxide (Fe₂O₃)** — ferric oxide in the dry, well-drained, oxidised form
- In **waterlogged valleys**, the colour turns **yellow** — because iron oxide hydrates (Fe₂O₃·H₂O = yellow/ochre)
- **Porous and friable** (crumbles easily) — poor water retention
- Generally **poor in lime, phosphate, nitrogen, and organic matter**
- Less fertile than alluvial and black soils; responds well to fertilisers and irrigation
- Found on **upland hills** (shallow, thin red soil) and **valley floors** (deeper, more fertile, yellowish)

**Crops:** Groundnut, pulses, millets (jowar, bajra), rice (in valley areas), wheat (in northern zones), potato

**Memory hook:** Red soil = RED from **R**ust (iron oxide), **E**astern and **D**eccan peninsular India.

## 4. Laterite Soil

**Coverage:** Moderate — Kerala, TN hills (Nilgiris, Palani), Karnataka (coastal belt), Odisha, Jharkhand, northeastern states, parts of Maharashtra

**The word "laterite"** comes from the Latin **"later"** = **brick** — because when moist, laterite can be cut into blocks that harden on exposure to air, making excellent bricks. Ancient Kerala temples were built from laterite blocks.

**Formation:** Forms in **tropical regions with alternating wet and dry seasons**. Heavy monsoon rains leach out silica, lime, magnesia, and bases from the soil. What remains is an enriched residue of **iron and aluminium hydroxides** — which harden and form a crust on the surface.

**Characteristics:**
- **Red-brick colour** due to iron and aluminium oxides
- Found on **hill tops** and areas with good drainage (leaching is most intense here)
- **Infertile** — silica and bases washed away; very deficient in lime, nitrogen, potash
- **Acidic** (pH 4.5–6) due to loss of bases
- Hard crust when dry; soft enough to cut when moist
- Requires heavy fertilisation for cultivation

**Crops:** **Tea** (Darjeeling, Assam, Nilgiris), **coffee** (Coorg, Chikmagalur), **rubber** (Kerala), coconut, cashew — plantation crops that tolerate acidic, leached conditions; these are NOT food grain crops

## 5. Desert / Arid Soil

**Where:** Rajasthan (Thar Desert), parts of Gujarat (Rann of Kutch), parts of Haryana and Punjab (semi-arid)

**Characteristics:**
- **Sandy and coarse** — loose particles, very low in organic matter
- Poor water retention; blown easily by wind
- **High salt content** in some areas (soluble salts brought to the surface by capillary action as water evaporates)
- Contains **kankar** (calcareous nodules of CaCO₃) at depth — similar to Bangar alluvial soil
- Red-to-brown colour (iron in oxidised form)
- Nitrogen and humus very low; phosphate is present in some areas

**Under irrigation:** Desert soil becomes productive — the Indira Gandhi Canal (from Sutlej-Beas rivers) has transformed vast areas of Rajasthan's desert into agricultural land growing wheat, cotton, and oilseeds.

**Crops (natural):** Bajra (pearl millet), barley — drought-resistant crops

---

## 6. Mountain / Forest Soil

**Where:** Himalayas and their foothills, Nilgiris, Eastern Ghats hills, Western Ghats upper slopes

**Characteristics:**
- **Immature soil** — thin, with visible parent rock material
- Rich in **humus** (organic matter from decomposing forest litter)
- **Loamy** in valleys; pebbly and thin on slopes
- **Acidic** — especially under coniferous (pine/deodar) forests where needles decompose acidically
- Poor in lime and potash
- Dark coloured at high altitudes (above 3,000 m) where decomposition is very slow

**Crops:** **Tea** (Darjeeling, Assam — mountain soils), **coffee** (Coorg/Kodagu), **spices**, **apple, apricot, cherry, walnut** (Himachal Pradesh, J&K/Ladakh), potatoes

---

## 7. Saline and Alkaline Soil (Usar/Reh/Kallar)

**Where:** Parts of Rajasthan, Punjab, Haryana, UP, Gujarat, Maharashtra, Bihar — especially waterlogged areas and regions with poor drainage

**Regional Names:**

| Name | Region |
|---|---|
| **Reh** | UP, Punjab |
| **Kallar** | Punjab |
| **Usar** | UP (most common name) |
| **Chopan** | Gujarat |
| **Khar** | Maharashtra |
| **Rakar** | Rajasthan |

**Formation:** In arid/semi-arid climates, water evaporates and leaves behind salts at the surface. Also forms in waterlogged areas where poor drainage allows salt accumulation.

**Characteristics:**
- Excess sodium, calcium, magnesium salts
- White/grey surface (salt efflorescence)
- Hard, dense, impermeable; very poor for agriculture
- Alkaline pH (>8.5 for sodic/alkaline soils)

**Reclamation methods:**
- **Gypsum treatment** — most important; gypsum (CaSO₄) replaces sodium ions with calcium → reduces alkalinity
- Proper drainage/leaching with water
- Growing salt-tolerant crops (sesbania, dhaincha) as green manure first

## 8. Peaty / Marshy Soil

**Where:** Kerala (Kuttanad — "Rice Bowl of Kerala"), coastal West Bengal, Odisha delta, parts of Bihar (flood-prone), parts of Uttarakhand

**Formation:** In areas with **heavy waterlogging**, organic matter accumulates faster than it decomposes (anaerobic/oxygen-poor conditions prevent breakdown). The result is a very dark, spongy, organic-rich soil.

**Characteristics:**
- **Highest humus/organic matter content** of all soil types — can be 40–50% organic
- Dark/black colour
- Highly **acidic** (pH 3.5–5.5)
- Waterlogged for most of the year
- Low in phosphate and potash despite being rich in organic matter

**Crops:** **Rice (paddy)** — the primary crop everywhere peaty soil is found; also jute (West Bengal)

## Master Comparison Table

| Property | Alluvial | Black (Regur) | Red | Laterite |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Colour | Light grey/yellowish | Black/dark | Red (iron oxide) | Brick-red |
| Parent material | River deposits | Basaltic lava | Crystalline rocks | Leached tropical |
| Coverage | **43% (most)** | ~15% | **~18% (2nd)** | Moderate |
| Fertility | Very high | High for cotton | Moderate-low | Low |
| Key nutrient deficiency | Nitrogen | Nitrogen + Phosphorous | N, P, lime, humus | Nearly everything |
| Key crops | All food crops | **Cotton**, wheat | Groundnut, millets | Tea, coffee, rubber |
| Moisture retention | Good | **Excellent** | Poor | Poor |
| **Self-ploughing** | No | **YES (unique)** | No | No |
| Acidic/Alkaline | Near neutral | Slightly alkaline | Neutral-acidic | **Acidic** |
| Special character | Khadar/Bangar sub-types | Cracks in dry season | Yellow in valleys | Hardens into bricks |

## Soil Conservation

**India loses ~5,334 million tonnes of topsoil annually to erosion.** Key erosion types:

| Erosion Type | Where | Cause |
|---|---|---|
| **Gully erosion** | Chambal valley (MP/Rajasthan), Yamuna ravines | Running water carves deep channels (gullies) in soft soil |
| **Sheet erosion** | Indo-Gangetic plains, peninsular India | Thin layer of soil washed off across large area |
| **Wind erosion** | Rajasthan (Thar Desert), coastal sandy areas | Strong winds carry away loose sandy topsoil |
| **Landslides** | Himalayas, hilly states | Slope failure carries soil downhill |

**Conservation methods:**
- **Contour ploughing** — ploughing along contour lines (perpendicular to slope) to reduce runoff
- **Terrace farming** — cutting hill slopes into steps to prevent runoff
- **Strip cropping** — alternating strips of crops and grass/legumes to break wind/water flow
- **Afforestation / Shelterbelts** — planting trees as windbreaks (e.g., in Rajasthan)
- **Check dams** — small dams that slow runoff and trap silt

## Exam Traps — 16 Things SSC Will Test

1. **Alluvial soil = most widespread in India (~43%)** — NOT black soil (which is most important for cotton but not the most extensive).
2. **Black soil = self-ploughing / self-mulching** — develops cracks in dry season; unique to Regur; no other Indian soil does this.
3. **Black soil = rich in lime, calcium, magnesia, potash; DEFICIENT in nitrogen and phosphorous** — remember the deficiency.
4. **Red colour of red soil = iron oxide (Fe₂O₃)**. In waterlogged valleys, it turns **yellow** (hydrated form of iron oxide).
5. **"Laterite" = from Latin "later" (brick)** — can be quarried and used as building material; ancient Kerala temples are built from it.
6. **Laterite is INFERTILE** despite being in high-rainfall, lush regions. Irony: the very heavy rainfall that makes the region green also leaches the soil bare.
7. **Laterite forms on HILLTOPS** (intense drainage, intense leaching) — valleys have red or alluvial soil, not laterite.
8. **Khadar = newer, more fertile** (near river channels); **Bangar = older, has kankars** (away from channels). Khadar > Bangar for farming.
9. **"Regur" is from Telugu** — not Hindi, not Sanskrit. Name for black/cotton soil.
10. **Tea grows best in laterite and mountain soils** (both acidic, well-drained, hilly) — NOT in alluvial or black soil.
11. **Peaty soil = highest organic matter/humus** of all soil types; forms in waterlogged, anaerobic conditions; Kuttanad (Kerala) is the prime example.
12. **Gypsum (CaSO₄) is used to reclaim Usar/alkaline soils** — the only treatment option frequently asked in SSC.
13. **Black soil is best for cotton; alluvial soil grows everything** — both are fertile but for different reasons and different crops.
14. **Chambal valley = gully erosion** (ravines/badlands); famous for dacoits historically because the ravines provided cover.
15. **Desert soil contains kankar (CaCO₃ nodules)** — a fact shared with Bangar (old alluvial) soil; both have calcareous concretions.
16. **Mountain/forest soil = rich in humus but poor in lime and potash** — the humus-lime paradox (organic matter present; minerals leached/absent).
